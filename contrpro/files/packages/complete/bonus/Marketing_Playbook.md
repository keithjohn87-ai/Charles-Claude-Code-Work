# The Contractor Marketing Playbook

**For independent contractors and small construction businesses, 1–10 people, who do the work and want more of it.**

*ContrPro Complete &middot; v1.0 &middot; 2026*

---

## How to use this playbook

This is not a marketing-agency pitch. It is a working playbook written for the owner-operator who is in the field 50+ hours a week, who has been burned by at least one SEO consultant, and who wants concrete, measurable ways to generate more qualified leads without paying $5,000/month to a retainer.

Two cardinal rules. Read them twice before you do anything else in this document.

**Rule 1 — Track every lead source for 90 days before you optimize anything.** You cannot improve what you cannot measure. The single most expensive mistake contractors make is spending money on marketing channels without knowing which ones produced jobs. Use the lead-tracking column in the `Job_Costing_Spreadsheet.xlsx` (already in your ContrPro package) and fill it in for every lead, every estimate, every closed job, for at least 90 days. Then — and only then — make decisions about where to spend more money and where to cut.

**Rule 2 — Never run more than 3 marketing channels at once until one of them is profitable.** New contractors try Facebook ads and Google Ads and HomeAdvisor and door hangers and a billboard all at the same time. Three months later they have spent $4,000 and cannot tell which channel produced the two jobs they got. Pick three channels max. Run them for 90 days. Cut the worst, double down on the best, then add a fourth.

The rest of this playbook explains what those channels are, how each one actually works for contractors specifically (not generic small businesses), what they cost in time and money, and how to measure them. The sections are roughly ordered from highest-ROI to lowest, but trade and market vary — what wins in residential roofing in Atlanta is not what wins in commercial mechanical in Cleveland.

A note on time investment. The whole foundation in this playbook can be set up in 4 to 8 weeks of part-time work by the owner-operator or a $20/hr admin. Maintenance after that is 2 to 5 hours per week. If you cannot find 2 to 5 hours per week for marketing, your business will eventually run out of leads and you will go back to whoever called you last. That is a slow way to die.

A note on your reputation. Every section of this playbook assumes you are doing good work, returning calls, showing up on time, and not stiffing your subs and suppliers. If any of those things is not true, no marketing in the world will save you. The fastest way to make marketing work harder is to make the work better.

## 1. Positioning: how to be the contractor people remember

Most contractors describe themselves with the same three words: *quality, integrity, experience.* That is not positioning. That is wallpaper. If a homeowner reads ten contractor websites in a row and they all say the same thing, the homeowner will pick the one with the most reviews or the lowest bid because nothing else distinguishes you.

Positioning is the answer to one question: **"When a customer chooses you over the next contractor, what is the actual reason?"** It has to be something specific, true, and ownable.

Specific positioning angles that actually win for trades:

- **Response time.** "We answer the phone in 5 minutes during business hours and you get a same-day estimate appointment within 24." If you can really do that, say it. Most contractors can't, so it differentiates.
- **Specialty trade depth.** Not "we do everything" — "we do *this one thing* and we are the best in this metro at it." Foundation contractors who specialize in helical pier installation. Roofers who specialize in slate restoration. Electricians who specialize in EV charger installation in older homes with insufficient panel capacity. Specialists charge more and book farther out.
- **Customer segment.** Commercial-only. Residential-only. Insurance-claim-only restoration work. Historic-property work. High-end custom homes ($1M+). Homeowner-association repeat-vendor work. Property-management portfolio work. When you pick one segment and own it, your marketing gets cheaper because it gets more specific.
- **License tier or certification.** "Master plumber on every job" is real differentiation in a market where most plumbers send a journeyman or apprentice. Manufacturer certifications (Mitsubishi Diamond Contractor for HVAC; CertainTeed Master Shingle Applicator for roofing; NICET-certified for fire suppression) are tangible and verifiable.
- **Scope of warranty.** "10-year workmanship warranty" beats "1-year." If you can stand behind it, lead with it.
- **Veteran-owned, woman-owned, family-owned, generational.** These are not gimmicks. For certain customer segments — federal projects, certain corporate procurement — the certified status is a buying criterion. For residential, "third-generation electrical contractor since 1962" carries real signal about staying power.

The "one sentence" exercise. Write down, in one sentence, the answer to: "I am a ___________ who specializes in ___________ for ___________ in ___________." Then test it on three customers and three referral partners. If they nod and say "Oh, you should call X for that, then" — you have positioning. If they stare blankly, you have wallpaper.

A few worked examples for the most common ContrPro trades:

- **HVAC (Division 23 Mechanical):** "We are a Mitsubishi Diamond Contractor that specializes in ductless mini-split installations for older homes without existing ductwork in the metro Nashville market." Specific. Verifiable. Differentiated.
- **Plumbing (Division 22):** "We are a master-plumber-on-every-job residential service company that specializes in 24-hour emergency drain and water-line work in greater Atlanta." Tells the customer exactly what kind of plumber to call you for and what kind not to.
- **Electrical (Division 26):** "We are a residential electrical contractor that specializes in EV charger installations and panel upgrades for homes built before 1990 in southeast Michigan." A homeowner Googling "EV charger installer for 1970s home" finds you instead of the generic "electrician near me."
- **Residential GC:** "We build mid-range whole-home renovations ($150K–$500K) for working families in Hamilton County who want one contractor managing the whole project." Filters out tire-kickers and architects looking for a $1.5M custom build.
- **Roofing (Division 07):** "We are an insurance-claim-experienced roofing company that specializes in storm-damage replacement using GAF Master Elite shingles in the Dallas-Fort Worth market." Owns a customer segment (insurance-claim) and a category (Master Elite installer).
- **Concrete (Division 03):** "We are a stamped-and-decorative concrete contractor that specializes in residential pool decks, patios, and driveways in greater Phoenix." Doesn't try to be a commercial slab-on-grade contractor too. Different sale, different equipment, different pricing model.

What does NOT count as positioning:

- "Quality and integrity"
- "Family owned and operated"  *(unless paired with something specific — see above)*
- "Free estimates"  *(everyone offers them)*
- "Licensed and insured"  *(table stakes — you cannot legally operate without these)*
- "Best prices in town"  *(a race to the bottom and unverifiable)*
- "Customer satisfaction is our #1 priority"  *(literally what every business says)*

If your positioning sentence could be lifted off your website and pasted onto five competitors' websites without anybody noticing, you do not have positioning. You have a tagline. Re-do the exercise.

## 2. Lead source #1 — Word of mouth and the referral system

For most trades, in most markets, in most years, **word of mouth and referrals are still the highest-margin lead source.** The cost is near zero, the close rate is 60–80% (versus 10–20% for cold leads), the customer is pre-sold, and the price-shopping is minimal. If you do nothing else in this playbook, build a referral system.

Most contractors think they have a referral system. They do not. They have a vague hope that satisfied customers will mention them at cookouts. That is not a system. A system is a deliberate, repeatable set of actions that turn happy customers into multiple referrals each.

Here is the system, step by step.

**Ask at the right moment.** The moment to ask for a referral is the exact moment a customer says some version of "thank you, this looks great" — typically at the final walkthrough, when you are handing over the keys, the warranty packet, and the final invoice. The script is short, conversational, and not pushy:

> "Thank you, that means a lot. I'm glad you're happy. We grow our business almost entirely through referrals from customers like you — would you mind telling a neighbor or coworker about us if it ever comes up? And if you'd be willing to leave us a quick review on Google, I'll text you the link when I get back to the office."

Do not skip the ask out of awkwardness. Customers who are happy with the work want to help. If you do not ask, you do not get.

**Send the handwritten thank-you note.** Within 48 hours of project completion, mail the customer a real handwritten note (not an email, not a text) on company letterhead or a branded notecard. Three or four sentences. Thank them for their business. Reference one specific thing about the project. Sign it personally. Drop a business card in the envelope. Cost: $1 of stationery and 65 cents of postage. The retention and referral lift is enormous because 95% of contractors do not do this. You will be remembered.

**The referral incentive structure.** Pay people to refer you. The most effective structure is a two-way reward:
- Person who refers gets $50 (gift card or cash) when the referred lead becomes a closed job
- Referred customer gets $50 off their first invoice
- Both rewards are paid at the same time, automatically, without requiring the referrer to ask

For repeat referrers, tier the reward: $50 for the first referral that closes, $100 for the second, $150 for the third in the same calendar year. A real estate agent who sends you 5 closed roof inspections a year is worth a lot more than $50 a piece — pay them like it.

Operationalize the program with a one-page document you give every customer at project completion. Include: how the program works, the payout amounts, what counts as a "closed job," and a way to refer (your phone number, a referral link on your website, a referral card to hand out). The Job_Costing_Spreadsheet has a "Lead Source" column — capture the referrer name there for every job so payouts are automatic at the end of each month, not a memory exercise.

**The post-job referral request template.** One week after final payment clears, send (text or email — text is better for residential):

> "Hey [Customer], it's [Your Name] from [Company]. I just wanted to follow up — everything still going smoothly with the [project]? If you have any questions or any small punch-list items, just let me know. And if you'd be willing to leave us a Google review, here's the link: [shortened review link]. Thanks again for trusting us with the work."

Two weeks after that, if no review has landed:

> "Hi [Customer], following up one more time on the Google review — it makes a big difference for a small company like ours when good customers leave honest reviews. If you have 60 seconds: [link]. If you'd rather not, no problem at all and thanks again."

Do not send a third reminder. Some customers will never leave a review and that is fine.

**Real estate agents and property managers.** For residential trades, real estate agents and property managers are the highest-value referral partners after past customers. An agent has 5–25 closings per year, every closing potentially generates 1–3 trade referrals (inspector findings, repair requests from buyers, post-closing maintenance), and a property manager managing 50 doors will generate 100+ trade calls per year. The play is simple: be the first call by being the most reliable.

Identify the 10 highest-volume agents in your service area. (Public MLS data, Zillow agent rankings, or a courthouse-records search of recent closings will tell you who they are.) For property managers, search the rental listings on Zillow and Apartments.com for your service area and note which property management companies show up most often. Then, for each, do one thing: introduce yourself in person. Drop off a small thank-you-for-considering-us package: a branded notepad, a magnet with your number, a one-page tear sheet of services and turnaround times. Ask if you can be on their preferred-vendor list.

Then deliver. Every referral from an agent or property manager gets:
- Phone call returned within 1 hour (not 4, not 24 — 1)
- Estimate appointment scheduled within 24 hours
- Estimate delivered same-day or next-business-day from walkthrough
- Work performed within the agent's required timeline
- Invoice sent the same day work is completed

If you can do all five of those things every single time, the agent will use you exclusively within 60 days. If you cannot, they will move on quietly.

**GC-to-sub and sub-to-GC referrals up the food chain.** If you are a sub, your highest-value relationships are the 5–10 GCs in your market who run jobs in your trade. If you are a GC, your highest-value relationships are the 10–20 reliable subs in each trade you manage. These relationships work in both directions: GCs send work to favorite subs, and subs refer GCs to homeowners or commercial owners who ask "do you know a good contractor for the whole project?" Treat your GC and sub network as a referral channel: send work their way when you can, and ask them to do the same.

**The lapsed-customer reactivation campaign.** Every 18 months, contact every customer you have ever served with a check-in: "How is the [roof/HVAC system/finished basement] holding up? Any maintenance or punch-list items I can take care of?" For HVAC, plumbing, roofing, and exterior trades, this generates real follow-on work. For one-time install trades (concrete, foundation), it generates referrals. The cost is one hour of admin time per 100 customers and a postage stamp or text message. The yield is 5–15% repeat or referral business.

## 3. Lead source #2 — Google Local Services Ads (LSA)

For most residential trades, Google Local Services Ads (LSA) is the single highest-volume paid lead source available in 2026. It deserves close attention because the mechanics are different from regular Google Ads and most contractors set it up wrong.

**What LSA actually is.** LSA is a pay-per-lead (not pay-per-click) advertising program. Your ad appears at the very top of Google search results for service-related queries in your service area. When a customer clicks the ad, they either call you directly or submit a contact form. You pay only when a real lead comes through — not for clicks that don't convert. Typical lead cost ranges from $25 to $75 for most trades; specialty high-ticket work (roofing, foundation, large remodel) can run $100–$200 per lead.

**The "Google Guaranteed" badge.** To advertise on LSA, your business must qualify for the Google Guaranteed badge, which requires:
- Verified business license in your trade and service area
- Verified general liability insurance ($1M minimum, sometimes $2M depending on trade)
- Background check on the business owner and any field workers (Google partners with a background-check provider; takes 1–2 weeks)
- Verified business location (Google sometimes mails a postcard with a verification code)
- Active Google Business Profile (see Section 4)

The badge takes 2–4 weeks to obtain. It carries real trust weight with consumers because Google is essentially saying "if this contractor screws you over on a job we sent them, we'll refund you up to a guarantee cap" (typically $2,000, with some variation).

**Setup, step by step.**
1. Go to ads.google.com/local-services-ads. Sign in with the same Google account you use for your Google Business Profile.
2. Confirm service category (HVAC, plumbing, electrical, roofing, etc.) and sub-categories. Be precise — bidding on services you don't actually offer wastes money on bad leads.
3. Enter service area by ZIP code. Start narrow (5–10 ZIPs around your office) and expand once you understand lead volume and quality.
4. Upload license, insurance, and background-check authorizations. Wait 2–4 weeks for Google verification.
5. Set weekly budget. Start at $200/week. After the first 30 days, adjust based on what you've learned about lead quality and close rate.
6. Add business hours. LSA only sends leads during your stated hours; after hours, leads see a "this business is currently closed" message. If you want 24/7 emergency leads, set 24/7 hours and have phone coverage.
7. Add photos: same set as your Google Business Profile (see Section 4) — minimum 25, ideally 50.
8. Turn on the listing.

**The 5-minute response rule.** LSA's lead-quality dispute system is keyed off response time. Google tracks how quickly you answer the phone or respond to form submissions, and slow response time tanks your ranking and lead flow. Industry data is consistent: lead-to-close rate **triples** when response is under 5 minutes versus 1 hour. Set up:
- A dedicated cell number for LSA leads, ringing on the owner's or admin's phone
- A text-message auto-response if the call goes to voicemail: "Thanks for calling [Company] — we're with another customer right now. Reply with your address and the issue and we'll call you back within 15 minutes."
- An email auto-response for form submissions with the same content

**Lead-quality disputes.** Not every lead Google sends is real. Wrong-number calls, spam, bot submissions, customers outside your service area, or jobs you don't do are all dispute-eligible. Disputed leads get credited back to your account if Google agrees. Dispute every bad lead — Google's automated review usually approves disputes within 24 hours. Contractors who never dispute pay for leads that should have been free.

**Reviews — Google-mandated cadence.** LSA leans hard on review velocity for ranking. After every closed job, send the Google review request via the LSA dashboard's review tool (it generates a custom link tied to your LSA profile). Aim for 1–3 new reviews per week. Reviews that come in through the LSA review tool count differently from reviews left organically — both count, but LSA-tool reviews are weighted slightly higher.

**Service area and category strategy.** Start narrow. A 10-mile radius around your shop is plenty for most trades. Expand only after you have proven you can profitably close LSA leads at a healthy ratio. Bidding on every category Google offers in your trade will burn money on leads outside your true sweet spot.

**Common LSA mistakes that cost money:**
- Turning LSA on before you have phone coverage to answer in 5 minutes
- Bidding on services you don't actually offer ("I'll just see what comes in")
- Setting a $1,000/week budget on day one
- Ignoring the dispute process and paying for spam leads
- Letting the listing sit static — no new photos, no review responses, no profile updates for months
- Advertising in ZIP codes that are 45 minutes from your shop (drive time eats your margin)

**LSA budget framework for the first 90 days:**
- Weeks 1–2: Verification underway. No spend.
- Weeks 3–4: Listing live. $150/week budget. Track every lead in `Job_Costing_Spreadsheet.xlsx` with source = "LSA," cost paid, lead quality (good/bad/spam), close outcome.
- Weeks 5–8: Adjust budget based on lead quality. If close rate is 30%+ on real leads, bump to $300/week. If close rate is under 15%, audit your phone-answer process and your estimate close rate before bumping spend.
- Weeks 9–12: Hold or expand. If LSA has a positive ROI (revenue per closed LSA job &gt; total LSA spend / number of closed jobs), expand the budget. If not, kill it and try a different channel.

## 4. Lead source #3 — Google Business Profile (GBP)

If LSA is the highest-volume paid channel, **Google Business Profile is the highest-leverage free channel**. It is also, paradoxically, the most under-invested-in lead source for contractors because it is "free" and most contractors set it up once and forget it.

A fully optimized GBP shows up in the "map pack" — the three local business results Google displays above organic search results for queries like "plumber near me" or "roofing contractor [city]." Map-pack visibility produces real lead flow at zero per-lead cost. The investment is upfront optimization (4–8 hours) and ongoing maintenance (1 hour per week).

**Step 1 — Claim and verify.** If you haven't already, go to business.google.com and claim your business listing. If a listing already exists (Google often auto-creates them based on public records), claim it via the verification flow (postcard, phone, or video verification depending on category and history).

**Step 2 — NAP consistency.** Your business Name, Address, and Phone number must be **identical** everywhere they appear online: GBP, your website, Yelp, Facebook, your truck wraps, your business cards, every directory listing. Inconsistent NAP confuses Google and demotes your map-pack ranking. Use your full legal business name (matches your license and insurance) and one phone number you actually answer.

**Step 3 — Category selection.** Pick one **primary** category that exactly matches your trade ("Roofing Contractor" or "HVAC Contractor" or "Electrician"). Add up to 9 **secondary** categories for additional services you legitimately offer. Do NOT pile on irrelevant categories — Google's algorithm understands category relationships and will demote you for category stuffing.

**Step 4 — Service-area definition.** For trades that travel to the customer (most), set your service area as a list of ZIP codes or cities you actually service. Do not enter a 100-mile radius. Be honest about where you actually go.

**Step 5 — Business description.** 700-character limit. Plain English. Mention what you do, who you serve, what makes you different. Include 2–3 keywords (your trade + your city) but write it for humans, not for the algorithm. Example for a residential plumber:

> "Master-plumber-on-every-job residential service company serving greater Nashville since 2009. We specialize in 24-hour emergency drain and water-line repair for single-family homes built before 1990. Master plumber license #XXXXX. Fully insured. Free estimates on all replacement work."

**Step 6 — Photos.** This is where most contractors fall short. Upload 25 minimum, ideally 50. Categories to cover:
- Exterior: branded vehicles, shop, signage, team photos in front of trucks
- Interior: shop, warehouse, organized tool layouts (yes, customers look at this)
- Team: clean, professional photos of crew members in branded uniforms
- Equipment: specialized equipment that signals competence in your trade
- Before-and-after: project photos showing the work, ideally 10+ pairs
- In-progress: shots of work being done, especially anything that shows expertise (proper sub-floor prep, code-compliant electrical work, manufacturer-spec material handling)
- Branded job-site photos: a yard sign in front of completed work
- Awards, certifications, team training photos

Use real photos shot on a modern phone in good light. Stock photos of generic "smiling contractor" guys are obvious and tank trust. Refresh photos quarterly — Google sees photo updates as a freshness signal.

**Step 7 — Services list.** GBP lets you add specific services with pricing where applicable. List every service you actually offer. For service-call trades, including "starting from $XX" pricing on common services helps customers self-qualify and reduces phone time on bad-fit leads.

**Step 8 — FAQ.** Add 5–10 frequently asked questions with answers. Common ones for trades: "Do you offer financing?" "Do you provide free estimates?" "What is your warranty?" "Are you insured and bonded?" "Do you work weekends?" "What areas do you serve?"

**Step 9 — Hours.** Accurate hours, including special hours for holidays. Closed during a snowstorm? Update temporarily.

**Step 10 — Attributes.** GBP lets you check off attributes like "Veteran-owned," "Women-owned," "LGBTQ+ friendly," "Wheelchair-accessible entrance," "Online estimates," "Free estimates." Check every one that's true.

**Step 11 — Posts.** Post weekly. Categories that work for trades:
- Project updates ("Just wrapped a full re-roof in West End — 30-year architectural shingles, color match to original, finished in 3 days")
- Educational ("Now is the time to schedule your fall HVAC tune-up — here's what we check")
- Seasonal ("Winter prep checklist for your home")
- Promotions ("Free estimate on all panel upgrades through the end of October")

A short post with a real photo gets more engagement than a long block of text. Aim for 50–150 words plus one good photo per post.

**The reviews game — the single biggest GBP lever.**

Reviews drive map-pack ranking more than any other factor for most trades. Google's algorithm weighs:
- **Total review count** (more is better, with diminishing returns past 50)
- **Average rating** (you want 4.7+ — anything below 4.5 hurts)
- **Review velocity** (steady stream beats a one-time burst)
- **Recency** (recent reviews count more than 5-year-old reviews)
- **Review responses** (responding to every review, positive and negative, is a ranking signal)

The review-request system. After every closed job:

1. Within 24 hours of final payment clearing, send a text to the customer:

> "Hey [Customer Name], it's [Your Name] at [Company]. Thanks again for the work — I really appreciate it. Would you have 60 seconds to leave us a quick Google review? It makes a huge difference for a small business. Here's the link: [your review-request link]"

2. If no review lands within 7 days, send one follow-up:

> "Hi [Customer Name], one more nudge on the Google review — totally understand if you're slammed, but if you have a minute: [link]. Thanks for considering it!"

3. After two attempts, stop. Some customers will never review and that's fine.

**The Google review link shortener trick.** GBP gives you a long, ugly review link by default. Get a clean short link by going to business.google.com → your profile → "Get more reviews." Google generates a g.page short link that's easy to text and customers actually click. Use that one, not the long URL.

**Responding to reviews.**
- Positive review: short, personal, mention something specific from the job. "Thanks Sarah — glad we got the laundry-room leak fixed before it hit the subfloor. Call us anytime."
- Negative review: never argue, never get defensive, never publicly debate the facts. Acknowledge, apologize for the experience even if you disagree, offer to make it right offline. "Mr. Williams, I'm sorry your experience didn't meet our standards. I'd like to make this right — please call me directly at [number] and let's talk. — [Your Name], owner."
- Customers reading reviews care more about HOW you respond to negative reviews than about the negative reviews themselves. A composed, professional, customer-first response to a complaint actually increases trust.

**Map-pack ranking factors, in priority order:**
1. **Proximity** — Where the searcher is when they search (you can't control this; it's geographic)
2. **Prominence** — Total reviews + recency + sentiment + GBP completeness + post cadence
3. **Relevance** — How well your category, services, and description match the search query

You can't move proximity. You can absolutely move prominence and relevance. That's where the 1-hour-per-week investment goes.

## 5. Lead source #4 — Nextdoor for residential trades

For residential trades in suburban and exurban markets, Nextdoor is the highest-intent free social network available. Neighbors ask each other for contractor recommendations constantly, and a Nextdoor business presence puts you in those recommendation threads.

Nextdoor is not useful for commercial-only contractors. If you don't do residential work, skip this section.

**Setup.** Create a Nextdoor Business Page at business.nextdoor.com. Verify your business address. Pick your service categories (Nextdoor's category list is similar to Google's). Add photos, services, and a description.

**The Recommendations system.** Nextdoor's central feature for businesses is "Recommendations" — neighbors leave business recommendations that show up in their neighborhood feeds. The more recommendations you have, the more often you appear when neighbors ask "who's a good [trade]?" Aim for 10+ recommendations in your first 90 days.

After every residential job in a Nextdoor-active neighborhood, ask the customer:

> "If you're on Nextdoor, would you mind leaving us a recommendation when you get a chance? Most of our work comes from neighbor recommendations and it really helps a small company like ours."

**Posting cadence.** Sparingly. Nextdoor users are intolerant of business spam. One post per month is appropriate; more than that and you'll get reported. When you do post, lead with value, not a sales pitch:
- Educational: "Spring is here — three things to check on your roof after winter"
- Local: "Wrapped a fence repair on [Street Name] today — if you're in the neighborhood and have storm damage to your fence, happy to come take a look"
- Seasonal warning: "Heads up — we've seen three burst pipes on [neighborhood] this week from the cold snap. If your pipes start ticking, here's what to do…"

**Local-events angle.** If your neighborhood Nextdoor has a community-events thread, sponsor a small thing once a year — a back-to-school supply drive, a Habitat for Humanity volunteer day. Visibility in the community feed compounds.

**Limitations.** Nextdoor is hyperlocal, so if your service area spans multiple cities, you'll need to manage presence in each neighborhood individually. The platform's reach is much smaller than Google's, so treat it as a high-quality supplement to GBP, not a primary channel.

## 6. Lead source #5 — BNI, chambers, and structured network referrals

In-person business networking is unfashionable in 2026 marketing-blog discourse. It is also still one of the highest-yield channels for contractors who treat it as a system rather than a once-a-year showing-up.

**BNI (Business Network International).** BNI chapters meet weekly for 90 minutes. The structure is one business per category (one electrician, one plumber, one HVAC, one roofer, etc.), and members are expected to bring referrals to each other every week. BNI dues run $500–$700 per year plus a per-meeting room fee. The expected ROI is 5–10x for active members.

The BNI math: a 30-member chapter that meets weekly will produce roughly 200–400 referral opportunities per year. If you close even 10% of those at an average ticket size, the channel pays for itself by month 3. The mistake is treating BNI as networking — you don't generate referrals by attending meetings, you generate them by doing the work *between* meetings: the 1-on-1 "dance card" sessions where you meet individually with other members.

The dance-card strategy: schedule 5 dance cards per month with members in complementary trades. For an HVAC contractor, the highest-value dance cards are with: residential electricians (panel upgrades pair with HVAC installs), plumbers (water heater proximity to furnaces), roofers (roof penetrations for vents), insulation contractors (energy-efficiency retrofits), and residential GCs (whole-home renovations). Use the dance card to:
- Understand their business: who their best customers are, what trades they refer
- Explain yours: what your sweet-spot job looks like, how you handle referrals, what your turnaround times are
- Identify "power partners" — members whose customer base most overlaps yours but who don't compete

After the dance card, send referrals their way when they fit. Referral reciprocity is real: members who give referrals get them back at roughly a 2:1 ratio over 12 months.

**Chamber of Commerce.** Lower intensity than BNI, higher community visibility. Chamber dues are typically $300–$800 per year. The value is being seen as a serious local business — your name on the chamber's member directory, attendance at ribbon-cuttings and business-after-hours, occasional sponsorship of community events. Chambers also often have committees (small business, governmental affairs, trade-specific) where you can build deeper relationships with other business owners over time.

Chamber referrals are slower-burning than BNI referrals but stickier. A chamber connection that turns into a referral relationship will often be a multi-year, multi-project relationship.

**Trade-association meetings.** Local plumbing associations, electrical contractor associations, roofing contractor associations, NARI (National Association of the Remodeling Industry) chapters, NAHB (National Association of Home Builders) chapters all hold regular meetings. Member contractors are not your competition for marketing purposes — they are referral partners for jobs outside their geography or their specialty. A residential-only roofer in your chapter will refer commercial roofing inquiries to you if you're known as the commercial guy.

**Tracking referrals in and out.** Every referral you receive gets logged in `Job_Costing_Spreadsheet.xlsx` with the referrer's name. Every referral you give gets logged in a simple referral log (a one-sheet spreadsheet is fine). Quarterly, review both. Send a thank-you note (or a small gift — a $25 lunch gift card is potent and inexpensive) to anyone who has referred you in the past quarter. The behavior compounds.

## 7. Lead source #6 — Branded signage, truck wraps, and physical-presence advertising

Marketing happens on the truck and on the job site. Every hour your branded vehicle is parked at a customer's house, every day a yard sign sits in front of completed work, every commercial site banner up during a build — that is advertising you already paid for. Most contractors under-invest in this category because it doesn't have a digital dashboard.

**Yard signs.** After every completed residential job, ask the homeowner if you can leave a branded yard sign in their front yard for 2 to 4 weeks. The conversation:

> "We always like to leave a sign out front for a few weeks after a job — it helps us pick up neighborhood work and we'll come pick it up whenever you'd like. Is that alright?"

Most homeowners say yes. Some will refuse. For premium locations (corner lots, high-traffic streets, busy neighborhoods), offer a small per-month payment ($25–$50) to leave the sign longer. The sign should have: company name, phone number, website, primary service line. Keep it clean, easy to read at 35 mph, and weatherproof.

Cost per sign: $25–$60 (corrugated plastic with stake) or $80–$150 (aluminum on stake) for higher-quality long-life signs. ROI: a single sign that leads to one closed job pays for 50 signs.

**Truck wraps.** A branded vehicle is a moving billboard. A full wrap costs $3,000–$6,000 depending on vehicle size and design complexity; partial wraps and lettering jobs run $500–$2,500. ROI math: if your truck is on the road 30 hours per week and gets seen by 5,000 unique impressions per week (low-end estimate for a metro market), that's 260,000 impressions per year per truck. At a billboard CPM of $5–$10, that's $1,300–$2,600 of equivalent advertising value per year per vehicle. The wrap pays itself off in 2–3 years on advertising value alone, before counting any direct lead generation.

What to put on the wrap:
- Company name (large, readable from 100 feet)
- One primary service line ("Plumbing & Drain" or "HVAC & Air Quality")
- Phone number (large, easy to read at speed)
- Website (smaller — most people Google your name from the truck, not type your URL)
- License number (required by many states for advertising on vehicles)
- One photo or graphic that signals competence (clean install photo, certification logo, simple tool illustration)

What NOT to put on the wrap:
- A list of every service you offer
- Every certification logo you've ever earned
- A bunch of stock images
- Tagline like "Your trusted partner for excellence"

Less is more. The truck has to be readable in 3 seconds at 45 mph.

**Job-site banners.** On commercial projects, a branded banner on the job-site fence is required signage in many municipalities and a free advertising opportunity everywhere. 4-foot by 8-foot vinyl banners run $80–$150. Include company name, primary service line, phone number, website, and project description ("New construction electrical: ABC Office Building"). Banners tell other GCs and developers driving by that you're capable of jobs at this scale.

**Branded apparel.** Crew in branded shirts, hats, and (in cold weather) jackets is uniform-as-marketing. Customers see a clean, branded crew and assume professionalism. Cost is $300–$500 per crew member to outfit, lasts 1–2 years. Bonus: a branded shirt at the home-improvement store on the way home from a job generates conversations and occasional leads.

**Trailer and dump-truck branding.** Same logic as truck wraps. Trailers parked at job sites are mini-billboards. A simple lettering job on the side of a trailer runs $200–$400 and pays itself off the same way truck wraps do.

**Local signage ordinances vary.** Before committing to a yard-sign program, check your municipality's signage ordinances. Some HOAs prohibit yard signs entirely. Some cities limit the duration. A quick call to the city's planning or zoning office or the local HOA management company prevents a violation notice.

## 8. Lead source #7 — Direct mail and door hangers

Direct mail still works for residential trades, especially when it's geofenced and tied to a specific customer event (like a recently completed job in the neighborhood). It does not work as a generic mass mailer.

**EveryDoorDirectMail (EDDM).** USPS's EDDM program lets you mail to every address on a postal carrier route for ~$0.20 per piece (postage), plus printing cost (~$0.10–$0.30 per piece for a standard 6x9 postcard). Total cost is $0.30–$0.50 per piece all-in, or $300–$500 per 1,000 mailers.

The lead-conversion math: industry response rates for direct mail in trades are 0.5–2%. At a 1% response rate on 1,000 mailers, you get 10 inquiries. At a 25% close rate, that's 2–3 closed jobs. If your average ticket is $4,000+, the math works. If your average ticket is $400, it doesn't.

**The "neighbor-of-recent-customer" hyperlocal play.** The single most powerful direct-mail tactic for residential trades. After completing a job, mail a postcard to the 50–100 closest neighboring addresses with a message like:

> "Hi neighbor — we just completed a [service] for one of your neighbors at [street name]. If you've been thinking about [service], we'd love the opportunity to give you a free estimate. [Photo of completed work]. Mention this card and get $100 off."

Response rates on these run 3–8% because the offer is hyperlocal, immediate, and references a verifiable nearby project. A roofer who just replaced a roof on a street can pick up 1–2 additional jobs on the same street within 60 days from this play.

**Storm-response door hangers.** For exterior trades (roofing, gutters, siding), the day or two after a hailstorm or major wind event is the highest-conversion door-hanging opportunity of the year. Print 500 door hangers in advance, kept in the truck. After a storm, drive the affected neighborhoods and hang a door hanger on every house with visible damage. Message:

> "We noticed potential storm damage in your neighborhood. We're a local roofing contractor offering free no-obligation roof inspections to homeowners affected by the [date] storm. Most insurance claims have a 1-year filing window — call now to get on our schedule. [Phone] / [Website]."

Response rates on storm-response door hangers run 5–15%. The competition will also be doing this — but speed wins. The first contractor to put a door hanger on the door usually gets the inspection.

**Seasonal cadences.** For HVAC, mailing in late spring (pre-AC season) and late fall (pre-heating season) yields better-than-average response. For roofing, post-storm and pre-spring (March–April) cadences work. For landscaping, late winter (planning season) and late summer (fall cleanup) cadences work.

## 9. Lead source #8 — Facebook and Instagram organic

Paid Facebook ads are usually a money pit for trades. The targeting is too broad, the intent is too low, and the cost-per-lead is rarely competitive with LSA or GBP. Skip paid Facebook for now.

Organic Facebook and Instagram, on the other hand, are real channels that lower-pressure and steady-cadence work for trades — especially residential trades where customers want to see what your finished work actually looks like.

**Posting cadence.** 2–3 posts per week. Less than that and the algorithm forgets you. More than that and you'll burn out and stop posting entirely.

**Content categories that work for trades.**
- **Before-and-after** (highest engagement). Two-photo or split-screen posts showing the job before and the job after. Short caption: what you did, what the homeowner wanted, time to complete.
- **In-progress walkthrough video** (90 seconds, shot on a phone). The owner or foreman walks through a job in progress, narrating what's being done. Builds trust because customers see the process, not just the polished result.
- **Finished walkthrough video** (60–90 seconds). The completed project with the owner explaining what was built and why.
- **Team / behind-the-scenes.** Photos of crew working, the shop, equipment, training days. Humanizes the company.
- **Customer testimonial reels.** A satisfied customer on camera (filmed on a phone) saying what the project was, why they hired you, and what the experience was like. Always ask permission and have them sign a brief release-of-likeness.
- **Educational.** "Three signs you need a new roof." "When to call a plumber vs. when you can DIY." Short videos or carousel posts. Positions you as the expert.
- **Seasonal.** Winter prep, spring inspection checklists, summer cooling tips, fall maintenance.

**What does NOT work on social for trades.**
- Stock photos of generic "smiling contractor"
- "Team excellence" platitudes
- Anything that looks like a polished marketing post
- Posts that are 500 words of sales copy with no photo
- Posts asking for likes and shares with no value to the reader

**The phone-on-the-dashboard mandate.** The most consistent contractor-social-media problem is "no content to post." Solve it with one rule: every truck has a phone on a magnetic dashboard mount, and every job gets at least 5 photos (start, mid, end) and 30 seconds of video. Set up a simple shared folder (Google Drive, iCloud, CompanyCam) that everyone uploads to. The owner or admin pulls one post per day from that folder.

**Hashtags and geotags.** On Instagram, use 5–10 hashtags per post: 2–3 trade hashtags (#roofing #roofer #roofreplacement), 2–3 local hashtags (#nashvillecontractor #nashvilleroofing #nashville), 2–3 specific (#shingleroof #stormdamage #roofinspection). Geotag the post with the city or neighborhood. Local discovery works through hashtags and geotags.

**Facebook groups.** Local Facebook groups ("Nashville Homeowners," "Brentwood Buy/Sell/Trade," "[Neighborhood] Community") often allow business posts on designated days or in specific categories. Read each group's rules before posting. A weekly "Recommendations Friday" post in a neighborhood group is a low-friction way to stay visible in residential markets.

## 10. Lead source #9 — Angi, HomeAdvisor, Thumbtack and the lead-aggregator question

Lead-aggregator platforms (Angi, HomeAdvisor — now both owned by Angi Inc.; Thumbtack; Networx; Porch) sell leads to contractors. The model is: a homeowner submits an inquiry through their site, the platform sells the same lead to 3–5 contractors, the contractors race to call the homeowner first.

**How they actually work.**
- Per-lead pricing. Most leads cost $20–$80 depending on category. High-ticket categories (roofing, siding, foundation) cost $100–$300.
- The same lead is sold multiple times. You are competing with 2–4 other contractors for every lead.
- "Lead score" or "match quality" features let you bid for higher-quality leads.
- Lead disputes are allowed for clearly bad leads (wrong number, customer outside service area, customer not actually shopping) but the dispute approval rate is much lower than Google LSA's.

**When they make sense.**
- Slow season — temporary patch on a lead-flow gap
- New business with no other lead sources active yet — buy time to set up LSA, GBP, referral systems
- Specific high-ticket categories where the lead cost relative to job size still pencils

**Why most contractors net-negative on them after 6 months.**
- The 5-minute response rule applies even harder here because you're racing 2–4 other contractors. If you're not the first call, the close rate drops to single digits.
- Lead quality is inconsistent. Customers submitting through Angi-style platforms are often price shoppers comparing 4 quotes.
- Costs creep up. The platform raises lead prices in your category once you're hooked.
- Reviews on the platform itself don't transfer to Google or your own site — you're building someone else's asset.

**The math test.** Track every aggregator lead in `Job_Costing_Spreadsheet.xlsx` with columns: lead source, lead cost, lead status (called / no-answer / quoted / closed / lost), close outcome, revenue. After 60 days, calculate:
- Total spent on aggregator leads
- Total revenue from closed aggregator-sourced jobs
- Net margin contribution from the channel

If net margin from aggregator-sourced jobs is positive after subtracting lead cost, keep going. If negative, kill the channel.

**Houzz** is the exception in the aggregator world for high-end residential remodeling. Houzz's audience skews higher-income and is more engaged in the design phase of a project, which means Houzz leads close at higher prices and lower price-shopping intensity than Angi-style leads. For custom-home builders, kitchen-and-bath remodelers, and high-end exterior contractors, Houzz is worth testing.

**Trade-specific platforms.** Roofr (roofing-specific), ServiceTitan ecosystem (HVAC/plumbing/electrical), CompanyCam Marketplace (general home services). These are typically better-quality leads than the generic aggregators because the audience is more qualified.

## 11. Lead source #10 — Permit data and active-project outreach

A lead source that almost no contractor uses but that produces extraordinarily high-intent leads: **active permit data**. When a homeowner pulls a permit for a major project, they have committed to spending money. When a GC pulls a permit for a commercial project, they are about to start subbing out trade work. Both events are public record, available weekly, and most contractors ignore them.

**How to get the data.**
- Most cities and counties publish permit data weekly through their planning department's online portal. Search "[city] building permit search" or "[county] building permit data."
- For broader coverage, paid services like Construction Monitor, BuildingRadar, BuildZoom, and ConstructConnect aggregate permit data nationally and let you filter by trade, project size, and geography. Pricing typically runs $100–$500/month depending on coverage.

**What to do with the data.**

For sub-tier contractors, pull weekly commercial permits in your geography filtered by trades that complement yours. Examples:
- An electrical contractor pulls every commercial permit over $500K within 30 miles, identifies the GC on each permit, and reaches out introducing themselves as a sub-tier candidate for the electrical scope.
- A landscaping contractor pulls every commercial permit for office buildings, retail strip centers, and apartments — projects that will need landscape installation as a finishing trade.

For residential trades with adjacent specialties, pull weekly residential permits in your geography filtered by relevant project types:
- A panel-upgrade electrician pulls every residential permit for additions over $50K (which usually require panel upgrades) and every permit explicitly for electrical work.
- A foundation contractor pulls every permit for new home construction and every permit for remodels involving structural work.
- A roofing contractor pulls every permit for roof replacement and every permit for additions or new construction (which need roofing).

**The introductory letter template.**

For commercial GC outreach (sub-tier contractor):

> "Dear [GC Name], I noticed you pulled a permit for the [project description] at [address] on [date]. We're [Company Name], a [trade] contractor in the [metro] market with a focus on [specialty]. We'd welcome the opportunity to be considered for the [trade] scope on this project. I've attached our prequalification packet (license, insurance, bonding capacity, recent project list). Available to meet at your office or the site at your convenience. — [Your Name], [Phone], [Email]"

For residential homeowner outreach (specialty trade):

> "Dear [Homeowner Name], congratulations on your upcoming [project] project — I noticed the permit was pulled at [date]. I'm [Your Name] with [Company Name], a [trade] contractor in [metro]. Many [project type] projects require [your trade] work to be completed before/during/after the main project. If you haven't already arranged a [trade] contractor, I'd welcome the opportunity to provide a free estimate. — [Phone], [Email]"

Send via mail (most professional) or email (faster but lower response rate). For high-value commercial GC outreach, follow up with a phone call 5 days after the letter lands.

**Privacy considerations.** Permit data is public record, but state and local laws vary on commercial use of public records for direct outreach. Check your state's rules. Some states restrict direct-mail solicitation of homeowners after certain triggering events (storm damage, foreclosure) — usually with cooling-off periods of 30–90 days.

## 12. The follow-up system: from lead to closed job

Most contractors lose more jobs to bad follow-up than to bad estimates. The work below is the difference between a 20% close rate and a 50% close rate on the same lead pool.

**The 5-touch follow-up framework.**

**Touch 1 — Initial response within 5 minutes.** When a lead comes in (phone call, web form, LSA inquiry, referral introduction), the first response happens within 5 minutes during business hours. If you cannot answer the phone, an auto-text fires immediately:

> "Thanks for reaching [Company]. We're with another customer right now. Reply with your address and the issue and we'll call back in 15 minutes."

The lead-to-close rate triples when initial response is under 5 minutes versus over 1 hour. This is consistent across every trade and every lead source.

**Touch 2 — Estimate appointment within 24 hours.** During the initial response, schedule the on-site walkthrough for the soonest possible time, ideally within 24 hours. "I can be there tomorrow at 2pm or Thursday at 10am — which works better?" Closed-ended scheduling questions close 3x more appointments than "when works for you?"

**Touch 3 — Estimate delivered same-day or next business day.** The walkthrough happens. Within the same business day or the next morning at the latest, the written estimate lands in the customer's inbox or hands. The estimate-delivery delay is the single biggest leak in most contractors' sales process. Customers who get the estimate fast feel taken seriously; customers who wait a week feel ignored and shop other contractors.

**Touch 4 — Follow-up call or text 48 hours after estimate.** If the customer hasn't responded within 48 hours of receiving the estimate, follow up:

> "Hi [Customer], just checking in on the estimate I sent on [day]. Happy to walk through any of it or revise anything if it would help. Let me know if you have questions."

**Touch 5 — Long-tail follow-up.** If the job hasn't closed after the 48-hour follow-up:
- Day 7: Short check-in. "Hi [Customer], just following up — did you have a chance to review the estimate?"
- Day 14: Value-add follow-up with new info. "Hi [Customer], wanted to share — we just completed a similar project nearby and the homeowner was kind enough to let me share before/after photos. Available for a no-pressure call if you want to discuss."
- Day 30: Soft-close. "Hi [Customer], the estimate I sent on [date] is good for 30 more days. After that I may need to revise it because of material costs. Let me know if you want to move forward or if you've decided to go a different direction — either way is fine, I just want to clean up my open estimate list."
- Quarterly for 1 year: Light-touch check-in. "Hi [Customer], just touching base — still considering [project]? No pressure, just keeping in touch."

A high percentage of "lost" jobs from initial estimate close 3 to 9 months later via long-tail follow-up. The contractor who is still around when the customer is ready to move forward usually wins the job.

**Estimate-format best practices that close more deals.**

The estimate is a sales document, not just a price quote. Format matters.

- **Itemized line items.** Use the structure in `Job_Costing_Spreadsheet.xlsx`: scope of work, materials with brand/model, labor hours, equipment, subs, overhead, profit. Organized by CSI MasterFormat divisions where applicable. Customers who see itemization trust the price more than customers who see a bottom-line lump sum.
- **Three-tier pricing options where appropriate.** Good / Better / Best. The "Best" option anchors the high end; the "Good" option gives a price-conscious customer a way to say yes. Most customers (~50–60%) pick "Better" — the middle option becomes the default by anchoring effect.
- **Photos of similar past work.** 3–5 photos of comparable completed projects. Visual proof of capability matters more than testimonials.
- **Written warranty terms.** Specific warranty: what's covered, for how long, what's not covered.
- **Payment schedule.** Clear: deposit, draws, final. Tied to milestones, not arbitrary dates.
- **Expected timeline.** Calendar dates, not vague "4–6 weeks." Start date, key milestones, completion date.
- **List of subs and crew.** For commercial work, name your subs by company and trade. For residential, identify the lead/foreman by name.
- **Copies of license and insurance.** Attach them. Don't make the customer ask.
- **3–5 customer references with phone numbers.** Real references the customer can call.

**The lost-bid debrief.** Every "no" gets a polite follow-up question:

> "Thanks for letting me know, [Customer]. Out of curiosity and just to help me improve — what made you choose the other contractor? Was it price, timeline, or something else? No pressure to share, just always trying to learn."

A surprising percentage of customers tell you. The information is free market intelligence: you learn what your real competitors are charging, what their pitch is, and where you're losing — pricing, response time, presentation, fit. Use the data to refine the next estimate.

- If closed: final invoice amount, completion date, lead source pay-out (if applicable)

Without these eight fields populated for every lead, you cannot make data-driven marketing decisions, and you will continue to spend money on channels that don't pay back.

## 14. Social proof: reviews, case studies, and testimonials

Every prospect researches you before they call. They look at Google reviews, GBP photos, Facebook page, your website, and (for commercial work) project case studies. Social proof is what turns a researcher into a caller.

**Reviews.** Set a target: 50 Google reviews in your first 12 months, 100 by year 2, 200+ by year 3. The compounding ranking benefit on map-pack visibility is real. Sources of reviews, in priority order:
- Google (highest priority — drives map-pack ranking)
- Facebook (secondary)
- Yelp (lower priority for most trades but free, and the site shows up in some searches)
- Trade-specific platforms (Houzz for remodel, BBB for some segments, Angi/HomeAdvisor if you advertise there)

The review-request system is in Section 4. Run it every job, no exceptions.

**Case studies for commercial work.** For high-ticket commercial jobs, build a case-study template. Each case study includes:
- Project description (1–2 sentences)
- Before/during/after photos (4–8 images)
- Scope of work (bullet list, organized by CSI Division)
- Timeline (start to finish)
- Customer challenge solved (the actual problem the customer hired you to address)
- Customer quote (with name, title, business — get permission)
- Project value range (a range, not a precise figure, to avoid signaling exact pricing to competitors)

Case studies live on your website (one page each), get included in commercial bid packets, and get repurposed into social posts. A library of 10–20 case studies is a powerful sales asset.

**Video testimonials.** A 90-second customer interview shot on a phone, edited lightly, posted to YouTube/Instagram/website, included in sales decks. The structure:
- Customer says their name and what kind of project you did
- Customer says why they hired you (what they were looking for)
- Customer says what the experience was like
- Customer says whether they'd recommend you and why

Always get a signed release-of-likeness before posting any video featuring a customer. A simple one-page release ("I authorize [Company] to use my photo, video, and likeness for marketing purposes") is sufficient.

**Where to use social proof:**
- Website homepage (3–5 featured reviews + 1–2 video testimonials)
- Estimate cover page (1–2 quotes from similar past customers)
- Sales presentations (case studies, video testimonials)
- GBP posts (highlight a recent review weekly)
- Social media (case study + before/after carousel)

## 15. Seasonal marketing cadences by trade

Different trades have different annual rhythms. Marketing spend should match the rhythm: heavier in pre-season to fill the calendar; lighter (but never zero) in off-season; pivoting to maintenance and indoor work when the primary season slows.

| Trade (CSI Division) | Peak Season | Pre-Season Marketing Push | Off-Season Strategy |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Roofing (Division 07)** | Apr–Jun, Sep–Oct | Mar (pre-spring), Aug (pre-fall) | Storm-response stays year-round; insurance-claim work spikes after weather events |
| **HVAC AC (Division 23)** | May–Jul | Apr (pre-AC season) | Heating maintenance Oct–Dec; preventive maintenance contracts |
| **HVAC Heating (Division 23)** | Oct–Jan | Sep (pre-heat season) | AC maintenance Mar–Apr; equipment replacement marketing in shoulder seasons |
| **Landscaping** | Mar–May, Aug–Oct | Feb (planning), Jul (fall cleanup) | Snow removal in northern markets; holiday lighting Nov–Dec |
| **Concrete (Division 03)** | May–Sep | Apr (pre-pour season) | Indoor concrete work (basement floors, garage, polishing); maintenance contracts |
| **Foundation / Waterproofing** | Mar–May, Sep–Nov | Feb, Aug | Indoor inspections, basement-finishing prep, year-round emergency calls |
| **Painting (Division 09)** | Apr–Oct (exterior); year-round (interior) | Mar (exterior) | Interior painting picks up Nov–Mar |
| **Remodel / GC (Residential)** | Contract sign Jan–Mar; build May–Sep | Dec (planning), Apr (build prep) | Smaller punch-list jobs, kitchen/bath in winter |

| **Electrical (Division 26)** | Year-round; spike around holiday lighting Nov–Dec; remodel May–Sep | Spring panel-upgrade campaign | Storm-response work spikes after weather events |

**Pre-season marketing push.** 30–45 days before peak season, increase spend on: GBP posts (warming up), LSA budget (ramp from baseline to peak), direct mail (geofenced, 30 days ahead), referral campaign reminders, social media frequency.

**Off-season survival.** When the primary season slows:
- Pivot to indoor/maintenance work in the same trade
- Push commercial work (often less seasonal than residential)
- Renew maintenance contract program (for service trades)
- Schedule major equipment maintenance, training, and team development
- Plan and pre-build marketing assets for the next peak season (case studies, videos, photo library)
- For northern trades, consider snow removal as winter work or holiday lighting installation as a seasonal pivot

- Set up the 5-minute response system: LSA-dedicated number, auto-text response, phone coverage during business hours

**The "kill the channel that's not paying" rule.** Every quarterly review, kill the lowest-performing channel and either reinvest in the best-performing one or test one new channel. Resist the urge to keep marginal channels running "in case." Cash spent on a channel that doesn't close is cash that can't fund the channels that do.

## 17. Common mistakes and what they cost

**Not tracking lead source.** Cost: you cannot improve. You cannot decide where to invest. You will continue to spend money on channels that don't pay back. Fix: lead-source column on every job in `Job_Costing_Spreadsheet.xlsx`, populated for 90 days minimum.

**Slow phone response.** Cost: 60% of leads. Studies across multiple trades show that responding in over an hour costs roughly two-thirds of the leads compared to under-5-minute response. Fix: dedicated lead phone, auto-text, 5-minute response standard, phone coverage during business hours.

**Quoting on the phone without a walkthrough.** Cost: lost jobs to whoever shows up and walks the job. Customers value the walkthrough as proof you care; phone quotes signal you're not serious. Fix: only ballpark over the phone for true emergency or simple-fix work; insist on a walkthrough for any significant project.

**Not asking for reviews.** Cost: invisible on Google. A contractor with 10 reviews loses to a contractor with 80 reviews almost regardless of star rating. Fix: every closed job, every time, ask for the Google review, send the link via text, follow up once.

**Not posting on GBP.** Cost: demoted in map pack. Google interprets stale GBP profiles as low-quality and ranks them lower. Fix: weekly post, real photo, 50–150 words.

**Letting positive customer interactions end without a referral ask.** Cost: 1–3 referrals per happy customer that you simply never asked for. Fix: scripted ask at the moment the customer says "thank you, looks great."

**Spending on Facebook ads before LSA + GBP are dialed in.** Cost: 3–6 months of wasted ad spend. Facebook ads for trades convert poorly compared to LSA; the foundation work pays off first. Fix: do LSA + GBP for 6 months, measure, then test paid social only if there's budget left over and a specific reason to.

**Not having a website that loads in under 3 seconds on mobile.** Cost: half your mobile visitors bounce. Most contractor leads research on a phone. A slow site loses them before they ever see your work. Fix: use a contractor-focused site builder (most templated builders are fast by default), optimize images, minimize plugins, test mobile load time at pagespeed.web.dev.

**Not photographing every job.** Cost: no content for social, no case studies, no before-and-afters for estimates. Fix: phone-on-dashboard, 5 photos per job minimum, shared upload folder, the owner pulls one post a day from the folder.

**Treating marketing as an expense instead of an investment.** Cost: chronically underfunded marketing, lumpy lead flow, feast-or-famine business cycle. Fix: budget marketing as a percentage of revenue (3–8% for trades is the typical range), maintain it through slow months, reinvest above the baseline when revenue spikes.

## 18. Closing: the marketing flywheel

A foundation built right runs on its own.

You optimize Google Business Profile. The map-pack ranking lifts. Customers find you. They call. You answer in 5 minutes. You walk the job. You deliver a clear estimate that day. You close the job. You do good work. You ask for the review. The review lands. The review velocity improves the map-pack ranking. The next customer finds you. The flywheel turns.

Same logic on referrals. You implement the post-job ask. You send the handwritten note. You pay the referral incentive on time and without friction. The customer tells a neighbor. The neighbor calls. Same flywheel.

Same logic on LSA. You answer in 5 minutes. You close at a healthy rate. The Google algorithm sees the close pattern and gives you better leads at lower cost. The economics improve quarter over quarter.

The compounding takes 12 to 24 months to fully show up. Most contractors quit at month 4, when the first quarter of effort hasn't yet produced visible ROI. The ones who keep going build a marketing engine that, in year three, generates more leads than they can handle and lets them raise prices.

Three reminders to carry forward:

**The contractor who shows up first wins more often than the one with the slickest brand.** Speed of response, speed of estimate delivery, speed of follow-up. None of those are about marketing dollars; they're about discipline. Disciplined response beats marketing budget almost every time.

**Marketing is what happens between jobs.** The work you do in the truck — the conversation with the customer at the walkthrough, the photo of the finished install, the request for the review, the handwritten note in the mail — that is your real marketing. The Facebook ad and the LSA budget are amplifiers. The amplifier without the work behind it is noise.

**Track everything for 90 days before you optimize anything.** Then keep tracking. The data tells you the truth that your gut won't.
