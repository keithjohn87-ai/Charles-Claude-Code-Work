Section 1 — Hierarchy of Controls — De-Energize First

OSHA + NFPA 70E require: de-energize first. Energized work is the last resort, allowed only when:

  • De-energization creates a greater hazard (life-safety circuits, ventilation).
  • De-energization is infeasible (troubleshooting, voltage measurement).
  • Operation involves voltages < 50 V with no exposure to arc-flash hazard.

If any of the above apply, an Energized Work Permit (EWP) must be issued + signed.

Section 2 — LOTO Step-by-Step
  1. Identify energy sources. All of them — utility, generator, UPS, battery, control, stored mechanical / hydraulic / pneumatic.
  2. Notify affected persons. Coordinate with building operations on live-building projects.
  3. Open the disconnect / breaker. Verify position visually + by labeling.
  4. Apply lock + tag. Each worker applies their OWN lock. Multiple workers → multi-lock hasp.
  5. Test for absence of voltage. Use a known-good meter. TEST THE TESTER on a live source BEFORE the verification AND AFTER. Confirms your meter is working.
  6. Discharge stored energy. Capacitors, springs, hydraulic accumulators.
  7. Perform work.
  8. On completion: remove tools, notify affected persons, remove personal lock + tag, restore energy.
WARNING — never remove another worker's lock. Exceptions per 1910.147(e)(3) require: (a) worker not on site, (b) reasonable effort to contact, (c) verification of safety before removing, (d) inform worker before they resume. Document every supervisor-removal. Most electrocutions involve removed locks.
Section 3 — Voltage Verification ("Test-the-Tester")
  • Use a meter rated for the voltage class being tested (CAT II / III / IV).
  • Test the meter on a known-live source BEFORE the verification.
  • Verify zero voltage on the de-energized circuit — phase-to-phase, phase-to-ground.
  • Test the meter again on the known-live source AFTER the verification. Confirms the meter didn't fail to a "zero" reading during the test.
  • Document on the LOTO log + EWP.
Practice note — meter category ratings. Use CAT IV meters for utility service + outdoor work, CAT III for distribution panels + feeders, CAT II for receptacle-level work. Using an under-rated meter on high-energy equipment can cause the meter to arc-fault during a transient — the most common cause of meter-related arc-flash injuries.

Section 7 — Arc-Rated PPE Selection Matrix (NFPA 70E 130.7)

Each worker performing work within the arc-flash boundary must be equipped with arc-rated PPE at or above the incident energy. Layering: arc-rated outer layer + natural-fiber inner layer (no synthetic). PPE is verified daily before each EWP.

  • Arc-rated head protection — hard hat with AR face shield or hood.
  • Hearing protection — ear-canal inserts (foam plugs).
  • Arc-rated eyewear — safety glasses (worn UNDER face shield).
  • Arc-rated hand protection — rubber insulating gloves with leather protectors; test gloves per ASTM F496 every 6 months.
  • Arc-rated foot protection — dielectric or EH-rated leather boots.
  • Non-conductive tools — insulated to test voltage rating; visually inspect for cuts.
Section 8 — Qualified vs Unqualified Persons

OSHA 1910.332 + NFPA 70E 110.6 require workers exposed to electrical hazards be "qualified" — trained in:

  • Skills + techniques to distinguish exposed live parts from other parts.
  • Skills + techniques to determine nominal voltage.
  • Clearance distances + voltages.
  • Selection + use of test instruments.
  • Practices of safe work near energized equipment.
  • How to apply LOTO.
  • Recognition + avoidance of electrical hazards.

Training documented + refreshed per the employer's program (typically annually). An unqualified person may not enter the limited-approach boundary of an exposed energized conductor without escort + direct supervision by a qualified person.

Section 9 — NEC 110.16 Field Labeling
  • Switchboards, panelboards, industrial control panels, meter socket enclosures, and motor control centers in non-dwelling occupancies require a permanent warning label.
  • Label content: warning of arc-flash + shock hazard, equipment ID, date applied.
  • NEC 110.16(B) — service equipment ≥1200 A in non-dwelling: label must also include nominal voltage, available fault current, clearing time of OCPD, date.
  • Engineered arc-flash study labels add: incident energy at working distance, arc-flash boundary distance, required PPE category.
  • Verify labels before energizing; replace any damaged labels promptly.
Section 10 — Incident Response
  • Electrical contact victim — never touch a victim still in contact with energy. Cut power first or use insulated rescue equipment.
  • Arc-flash victim — burns are the immediate hazard. Cool with water (clean), cover with sterile dressing if available, transport to ER. Eye exposure to arc light → ophthalmology evaluation even if no immediate symptom.
  • Trapped worker in energized enclosure — emergency call, follow site EAP. Coordinate with controlling contractor on rescue without secondary contact.
  • Investigation — document the incident under OSHA 1904.39 if hospitalization within 24 hours. Preserve scene + meters / tools / PPE.
WARNING — second-victim is the rescuer. Most secondary injuries are would-be rescuers who entered without their own PPE + verification. Train every worker on the "cut power first / use insulated rescue equipment" rule in toolbox talks before the first energized-work day.
