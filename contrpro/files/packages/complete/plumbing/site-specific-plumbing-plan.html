Practice note — code-edition mismatch. The construction documents may reference one code edition (e.g. IPC 2021) while the AHJ has adopted a newer edition (e.g. IPC 2024 with state amendments). Section 3 — Inspection Sequence (UG / Rough-In / Top-Out / Final)

The following sequence is typical for new commercial construction. Confirm with the AHJ plumbing inspector at the pre-construction meeting. No subsequent work covers inspected work until the inspector signs off.

Backflow Initial Test After installation, before placing in service Certified tester (state-licensed) tests each RPZ / DCV / PVB per ASSE/CSA; submits report to water purveyor + AHJ
Water-Heater / Boiler Before insulation / drywall over T&P discharge piping T&P relief discharge routed per IPC 504.6; seismic strap (high-zone); combustion air per IFGC Ch 9; vent connector
Final All fixtures installed + system commissioned Fixtures secure + functional; faucets meet flow limits; hot-water delivery time; trap seals; backflow report on file; disinfection certificate on file
Practice note — re-inspection fees. Most AHJs charge a re-trip fee ($75–$250) for each failed inspection. The two most common failures: (1) work not ready when the inspector arrives, and (2) field deviations from the stamped drawings without an approved RFI. Mitigation: walk every system the day before the scheduled inspection; if anything is unclear, reschedule rather than risk a fail.

Section 5 — Cross-Connection Control Plan

Every potable-water system has cross-connection hazards. The contractor must identify each, select the correct backflow-prevention device, and arrange initial + annual testing by a state-licensed cross-connection control specialist.

5.1 Hazard classification
Hazard LevelDefinitionRequired Device
High Contains substances that, if backflowed, would cause illness, death, or other serious harm (sewage, chemicals, processed water) RPZ (Reduced-Pressure-Zone Principle Backflow Preventer) — ASSE 1013
Low Contains substances objectionable but non-toxic (food, beverage) DCV (Double Check Valve Assembly) — ASSE 1015
Air-gap acceptable Direct discharge with air gap of 2× pipe diameter or 1 inch, whichever larger Physical air gap — IPC 608.13.1
5.2 Typical assignments (verify against project conditions)
  • Building water service entry — RPZ or DCV per water purveyor's policy. In most modern jurisdictions, commercial buildings require RPZ at the meter.
  • Boilers / hydronic systems — RPZ (high hazard — chemicals/glycol).
  • Cooling-tower make-up — RPZ (high hazard — biocides + scale inhibitors).
  • Lawn-irrigation system — PVB (Pressure-Vacuum Breaker) ASSE 1020 or DCV depending on chemical injection.
  • Fire-protection sprinkler — DCV without chemical additives; RPZ with chemical additives or recycled water.
  • Carbonators / soda machines — ASSE 1022 stainless-steel double-check; coordinate with foodservice vendor.
  • Wall hydrants / hose bibbs — integral vacuum breaker per IPC 608.15.4.1.
  • Hand-held showers / kitchen sprayers — integral backflow preventer per fixture standard.
WARNING — backflow on potable water. Failure to install the correct backflow device, or installing it backwards, has caused documented illness and death incidents. The plumbing contractor is responsible for installing the device per manufacturer's instructions AND arranging initial testing by a licensed cross-connection control specialist BEFORE placing the system in service. Most jurisdictions require an annual test thereafter, with the report submitted to the water purveyor.
Practice note — orientation. RPZs must be installed horizontally with the relief port pointing down and a minimum 12-inch clearance below the relief port (some manufacturers require more — read the instructions). The relief port WILL discharge during normal operation; route the discharge to an indirect waste with an air gap, not directly to the sanitary sewer. Cold-climate installations require freeze protection — heat tape or an interior location.

6.1 Test procedure (typical)
  1. Verify all openings capped + test caps rated for the test pressure.
  2. For air tests over 5 psi: clear personnel from the test radius (failures can launch fittings — see warning below). Tape barricade.
  3. Apply test pressure with calibrated gauge. Record start time + pressure.
  4. Soap or leak-detect every joint visually. Look + listen.
  5. At end of duration, record finish pressure. No drop = pass.
  6. Document on the Pressure Test Log XLSX. Photo the gauge + system at start + finish.
WARNING — air vs. water for testing. Air testing is faster + leaves no water in the system, but air is compressible and a failed fitting under air pressure can launch into a worker. ASME B31.9 and NFPA 54 require additional precautions for air testing including isolation, barricades, and relief valves on the test apparatus. Water testing is preferred for any test pressure above 50 psi. If air is required, follow the manufacturer's test-cap rating and use a relief valve set 10% above the target test pressure.
Practice note — failed test diagnosis. A test that fails by a few psi is usually a fitting, joint, or test cap leak. A test that fails fast is usually a missing cap or a transposed fitting. Soap-bubble each joint methodically — start from the test source and work outward.
Section 7 — Disinfection of Potable Water Systems (AWWA C651)

Domestic water piping must be disinfected before being placed in service. Most AHJs require a disinfection report on file before issuing the Certificate of Occupancy.

7.1 Standard procedure (Method 1, continuous-feed)
  1. Flush the system thoroughly to remove debris.
  2. Introduce chlorine to achieve 50 mg/L (50 ppm) free chlorine residual at the farthest fixture. Verify with a DPD-1 test strip or colorimeter.
  3. Hold for 24 hours minimum.
  4. Test all dead-ends + outlets for ≥10 mg/L residual at end of hold.
  5. Flush the entire system until residual drops to ambient (≤1.0 mg/L typically).
  6. Submit two bacteriological samples taken 24 hours apart to an accredited lab. Both must show no coliform OR per-jurisdiction equivalent.
  7. Submit the lab report + chain-of-custody to the AHJ + water purveyor.
Practice note — chlorine source + handling. Granular calcium hypochlorite (HTH) or sodium hypochlorite (bleach) are the standard sources. Read the SDS and provide PPE — eye protection minimum, full face shield + chemical-resistant gloves for HTH. Never mix bleach with any acid or ammonia-based cleaner — produces toxic chlorine gas. Ventilate the work area. Dispose of dechlorinated water per the water purveyor's discharge permit; uncontrolled discharge to a storm sewer or surface water is a federal Clean Water Act violation.
WARNING — Legionella risk in stagnant systems. Long construction durations between rough-in and occupancy create dead-leg stagnation that can incubate Legionella. ASHRAE 188 requires a Water Management Plan for many building types. Coordinate with the engineer of record for systems sized above ASHRAE 188 thresholds and document flushing frequency between disinfection + occupancy.
Section 8 — Excavation & Trenching (29 CFR 1926 Subpart P)

Underground plumbing work involves trenching that triggers OSHA 1926 Subpart P. The most-cited deaths are cave-ins; the most-cited violation is failure to identify a Competent Person.

8.1 Required before any excavation
  1. Call 811 minimum 2 business days before digging — locate all utilities.
  2. Document the locate ticket + paint markings before breaking ground. Photograph.
  3. Designate a Competent Person for each shift — trained per 1926.651(k).
  4. Pre-plan the soil classification (Type A / B / C per 1926 App A).
  5. Select the protective system (sloping / shoring / shielding) appropriate to depth + soil.
  6. Confirm spoil pile ≥2 ft from edge of excavation.
  7. Provide ladders or steps in any trench ≥4 ft, max 25 ft of lateral travel.
8.2 Protective systems — quick reference
DepthSoil Type A (cohesive, hard clay)Soil Type B (cohesive, sandy clay)Soil Type C (granular, water-saturated)

5 – 20 ftSlope 3/4:1 or engineered shoringSlope 1:1 or engineered shoring/shieldSlope 1-1/2:1 or engineered shield (trench box)

WARNING — confined-space crossover in trenches. A trench ≥4 ft deep with hazardous atmospheres (e.g. tied into a sewer line with H2S) triggers permit-required confined-space rules in addition to Subpart P. Carry a 4-gas meter on any sewer tie-in trench and follow Section 9 of this plan.
Practice note — trench box vs. sloping. Trench boxes (shields) save time but require the contractor to enter only the protected zone within the box. Workers reaching outside the box into unsloped soil are unprotected. Sloping is cheaper for short runs but consumes large surface area at depth — verify property-line setbacks before committing.
Section 9 — Confined-Space Entry (1910.146 / 1926 Subpart AA)

Plumbing work frequently encounters permit-required confined spaces: existing sanitary or storm manholes, sumps, sewage ejector pits, tanks, boiler rooms with limited egress. Document the program below for every entry.

9.1 Entry decision tree
  1. Identify the space. Is it large enough to bodily enter, with limited or restricted means of entry/exit, NOT designed for continuous occupancy? → confined space.
  2. Does it have ANY hazard (atmospheric, engulfment, configuration-trap, electrical, mechanical)? → permit-required confined space (PRCS).
  3. If PRCS: STOP. Implement the permit program.
9.2 Permit-required entry minimums
  • Written entry permit signed by the Entry Supervisor before any entry.
  • Atmospheric testing in this order: oxygen → flammable gases → toxics. Re-test continuously while occupied (or every 15 min minimum).
  • Authorized entrant(s), attendant(s) outside, and entry supervisor — all trained per 1910.146(g) within the prior 12 months.
  • Communication system between entrant and attendant maintained throughout.
  • Retrieval system (full-body harness + retrieval line + mechanical advantage) unless retrieval would increase risk.
  • Rescue services available — verify response time + capability before entry.
  • Lock-out / tag-out of all energy sources that could enter the space.
WARNING — most confined-space deaths are rescuers. OSHA data: over 60% of confined-space fatalities are would-be rescuers who entered without their own PPE + air monitoring. NEVER enter a space to rescue a downed worker without your own air supply + harness + a second attendant. Train every plumber on this in toolbox talks before the first PRCS-eligible job.
Section 10 — Hot Work (Welding, Soldering, Sweating)

Most plumbing operations include some hot work — soldering copper, sweating Pro-Press in tight areas, gas-piping welding, brazing of medical gas. The General Contractor's site-specific Hot Work Permit program controls.

  • Obtain a Hot Work Permit from the GC before each shift. Don't pre-pull or rubber-stamp.
  • Clear 35 ft of combustibles or cover with welding blankets / FR tarps. Sweep up dust + lint within the radius.
  • Fire watch designated for the duration of hot work PLUS a minimum 30-min cool-down (60 min on some AHJ amendments).
  • Fire extinguisher (Class A + B + C) within 10 ft of the work.
  • Suspend hot work and notify GC if smoke alarms are disabled and not re-armed within 30 min of work completion.
  • Brazing of medical gas: NITROGEN PURGE during the braze per NFPA 99 5.1.10.5. No exceptions.
  • Outdoor wind: cease hot work above 15 mph wind or during dust events.
Practice note — soldering near membrane roofs. Roofing torches + soldering near asphaltic or single-ply membranes is a documented ignition risk. Coordinate with the roofer's mock-up specs and consider press-fit connections as a substitute. Many GCs prohibit open-flame work on the roof entirely without written exception.
Section 11 — Hazmat Encountered (Asbestos / Lead / Mold)

Renovations and tenant fit-outs in buildings constructed before approximately 1980 may expose workers to asbestos pipe insulation, lead solder, lead-based paint disturbance, and mold growth in damp envelopes. Treat each as a stop-work item until characterized.

11.1 Asbestos-containing material (ACM)
  • Common in pre-1980 commercial pipe insulation (white plaster-like wrap on hot-water and boiler-feed lines).
  • STOP work and isolate the area. Notify the GC + owner immediately.
  • Survey by a state-licensed asbestos inspector before any disturbance.
  • Removal by a licensed abatement contractor. The plumbing contractor does NOT remove ACM unless specifically licensed.
  • Federal: 29 CFR 1926.1101 (construction) + 40 CFR Part 61 Subpart M (NESHAP).
11.2 Lead solder + lead-based coatings
  • Pre-1986 domestic water solder was 50% lead. Replacement service lines + soldered connections must be lead-free per the 2014 SDWA amendment (≤0.25% lead weighted average).
  • Renovation in pre-1978 buildings that disturb painted surfaces triggers EPA RRP (Renovation, Repair, and Painting) requirements — certified renovator on site.
  • Lead solder cutout: HEPA vacuum the work area, contain dust, and document chain of custody for disposal.
11.3 Mold
  • Wet drywall + insulation around leaking pipes can incubate mold within 48–72 hours.
  • Containment + remediation per IICRC S520 or local AHJ standard before plumbing work resumes in the affected area.
Section 12 — Multi-Trade Coordination (MEP)

Plumbing rough-in conflicts with HVAC ducts, electrical conduit, fire-protection mains, and structural framing more than any other trade. Resolve before installation.

  • Coordination drawings — overlay plumbing rough on the M / E / FP / S drawings. Identify clashes before pulling permits.
  • BIM coordination meetings on projects using BIM 360 / Navisworks — attend every cycle. Document clash resolutions in writing.
  • Slab and wall openings — coordinate with concrete + framing contractors. Provide sleeve schedule, openings packet, embed locations 7–10 days ahead of pour.
  • Hangers and supports — verify structural attachment points are approved by the EOR. No drilling into post-tensioned slabs without scan + EOR sign-off.
  • Firestop coordination — UL-listed assemblies for each penetration type. Coordinate with the firestop sub on inspection witness.
  • Above-ceiling priority — typical commercial: structure → gravity (storm, sanitary) → HVAC ducts → sprinkler mains → domestic water → electrical → communications. Confirm at coordination meeting.
Section 13 — Occupied-Building / ICRA Considerations

Tenant fit-outs, hospital renovations, and educational-facility work often proceed with occupants in adjacent areas. Adjust the plan accordingly.

  • Healthcare ICRA tier — coordinate with the infection-control preventionist. Tier III or IV work requires negative-pressure containment + HEPA.
  • Water-shutdown notice — give occupants minimum 48-hour written notice for any planned water shutdown. Schedule for off-hours where feasible.
  • Sewer-cap protocols — never leave an open DWV line uncapped; sewer gases (H2S, methane) accumulate in inhabited spaces within hours.
  • Noise + odor — solder + flux smoke, no-hub coupling work, and gas purges all create odors. Coordinate ventilation + occupant notice.
  • After-hours work — verify janitorial schedule + alarm systems. Document badge access + after-hours sign-in procedure.

Section 15 — Stop-Work Triggers

Any worker on this project has Stop-Work Authority for the following conditions. Document the stop, notify the plumbing superintendent + GC, and do not resume until documented resolution.

  • Suspected asbestos, lead-based paint, or mold disturbance.
  • Confined-space entry without a valid permit + atmospheric test.
  • Trench cave-in risk — soil change, water intrusion, equipment vibration.
  • Cross-connection identified that was not previously documented.
  • Pressure-test failure that cannot be diagnosed within reasonable time.
  • Energized utility encountered unexpectedly (live water main, live gas).
  • Code-edition conflict between drawings and AHJ adopted code.
  • Backflow device discharging continuously (failed RPZ or test cock open).
  • Sewage spill exceeding 5 gallons indoors or any spill outdoors.
  • Worker injury exceeding first-aid level.
