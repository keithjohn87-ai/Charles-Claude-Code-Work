Why tolerances matter. Steel-erection tolerances aren't just a contract clause — they're the dimensional handoff to every downstream trade. Section 1 — AISC §7.13 Framework
AISC Code of Standard Practice for Steel Buildings and Bridges (current edition), Section 7.13 — Tolerances. Defines the standard tolerances applicable to structural steel erection on commercial U.S. building projects. The §7.13 tolerances are the default — they apply unless the Contract Documents specify tighter or different tolerances.

The §7.13 framework is built on three layers:

  1. Mill tolerances (ASTM A6, A500, A53, etc.) — the rolled-section out-of-straightness, sweep, and camber from the steel mill. These travel with the section as delivered to the fabricator. The erector is not responsible for these.
  2. Fabrication tolerances (AISC §6.4) — cut-to-length, hole spacing, bolt-pattern position, splice fit-up. Owned by the fabricator. The erector is not responsible for these.
  3. Erection tolerances (AISC §7.13) — column plumb, member position, floor elevation, building drift. This is the erector's domain. This guide is about Section 7.13.
Contract Documents can tighten — or override — §7.13. Some specs (especially for AESS, hospitals with surgical equipment, microelectronics fabs, MRI suites) impose tolerances well tighter than §7.13. Some specs reference §7.13 verbatim. A few specs are silent and §7.13 applies by default. Read the Division 05 12 23 spec section before bid and before survey — if the spec is tighter, the survey must measure to the tighter standard, the productivity rate slows, and the bid should have priced the premium.

Anchor rods are upstream of the erector. Per the Pre-Erection Meeting (§1926.752(a) notification), the controlling contractor delivers an anchor-rod survey BEFORE the erector starts. The erector verifies the survey on receipt; out-of-tolerance rods are remediated by the concrete sub under the EOR's direction (NEVER by the erector field-modifying the rod per OSHA §1926.755(b)). If you find a survey error in the field, RFI immediately — don't try to make a base plate work on a non-conforming pattern.

4.1 — Exterior Columns (AESS or Cladding-Critical)

For exterior columns visible in the finished structure, OR exterior columns that anchor curtainwall, the Contract Documents typically tighten this. Common tightened tolerance: 1:1000 column plumb, AND no more than 1" total deviation from column line at any elevation. Verify against the project spec.

4.2 — Plumb Direction Convention

Plumb deviation is reported as the offset at the top of the column from the theoretical plumb-vertical from the base plate, in inches, in two directions (North-South and East-West, OR X and Y of the grid). Sign convention: positive in the direction of grid increase.

Section 5 — Member Straightness & Camber
  • Beam straightness (sweep + camber): per ASTM A6 mill tolerance — typically 1/8" per 10 ft for hot-rolled W shapes. The erector preserves the as-delivered straightness; if a beam arrives twisted or hogged from shipping, document on the Daily Field Report and request fabricator replacement.
  • Specified camber preservation: beams cambered per shop drawings (typically composite floor beams) must arrive with the camber and be installed so it remains. The erector should not force-set a cambered beam in a way that loses camber.
  • Bowed columns: a column out of straightness exceeding ASTM A6 mill tolerance is a fabrication issue and should be returned to the fabricator. Field straightening requires EOR approval and is generally not advisable.
  • Twisted members: rolled section twist (per ASTM A6) typically 1:1000 of length. Twist must be checked at receipt and accommodated by the connection configuration — if twist causes mis-alignment of bolt holes, that's a fab issue, not an erection issue.

Floor-elevation survey is critical for composite-floor projects where the concrete sub follows the erector — discrepancies in deck elevation translate to over- or under-pour of slab thickness, which is a real cost line item the concrete sub will backcharge if the erector misses the tolerance.

Section 7 — Building Drift / Sway

Drift = the cumulative horizontal offset of the building's exterior columns from their theoretical column line, measured at any elevation. AISC §7.13.1.3:

Drift ItemLimit
Exterior column drift from column line — multi-story1:500 of total column height from base
Drift at corner columns — typical limit±1" plus 1/2" per story above 20 floors
Lateral envelope (worst-case 4-corner deviation)Per Contract Documents — often 1.5× single-column drift

Drift is the most critical tolerance for tall buildings with curtainwall. Curtainwall anchor positions in the field are set to the as-built drift — if drift exceeds the curtainwall sub's allowance, the anchors require shimming, the panels require trimming, or the cladding design requires modification. Document drift early; coordinate with the cladding sub at the Pre-Erection Meeting.

Section 10 — Out-of-Tolerance Protocol
  1. Discover. Survey crew measures and identifies a deviation exceeding §7.13 (or tighter spec) tolerance. Document on Tolerance Survey Log with measured value, theoretical, deviation, location, and date.
  2. Stop adjacent work. If the deviation affects an in-progress bay (e.g., a column out of plumb that subsequent beams will frame to), pause erection of dependent members until resolved. Continuing to frame to an out-of-tolerance column compounds the deviation.
  3. Investigate root cause. Common causes: anchor-rod position error (concrete sub), shipping damage to member, base-plate shim error, temperature differential (sun on one side), inadequate diagonal bracing during cure of column splice.
  4. Attempt field correction. If the column is still adjustable (no permanent splice / cap connection complete), re-plumb via diagonal bracing. Re-survey to confirm.
  5. RFI to EOR. If field correction is not possible OR the deviation exceeds what diagonal bracing can correct, issue an RFI to the EOR describing the deviation, the cause, and proposed remedy. EOR options: accept as-is (re-engineer the connection geometry, modify cladding), require correction (cut + re-erect), require additional reinforcement.
  6. Document resolution. Update the Tolerance Survey Log with the resolution (RFI #, EOR response, corrective action taken, re-survey confirmation).
  7. Notify downstream trades. If the out-of-tolerance condition affects cladding, MEP, or finish trades, notify the GC PM so coordination meetings can address it.
The deviation log is the litigation record. Out-of-tolerance steel-erection conditions are a common driver of construction defect claims — both because they sometimes cause real downstream failures (cladding leak, MEP misalignment), and because they're easy for an unhappy owner to seize on years later. A complete, signed, dated, contemporaneous Tolerance Survey Log showing each deviation, its resolution, and the EOR's acceptance is the erector's strongest defense. Conversely, gaps in the log invite hostile reconstruction of what "must have happened."
Section 11 — As-Built Survey & Sign-Off

At substantial completion of steel erection (typically just before fireproofing / cladding starts), perform a full as-built survey:

  • Plan position of every primary column at base and at top of each splice.
  • Floor-to-floor elevation at four corners + center of each floor.
  • Exterior building envelope drift profile, plotted by elevation.
  • Anchor-bolt / connection points for cladding embeds.
  • Any documented deviations that did not require remediation (EOR-accepted as-is).

Submit the as-built survey to the GC and EOR for written acceptance. EOR acceptance triggers:

  1. Cladding sub mobilization (anchors set to as-built).
  2. MEP rough-in continuation in completed bays.
  3. Retainage release per Subcontract Section 4 (typically partial release at structural Substantial Completion).
