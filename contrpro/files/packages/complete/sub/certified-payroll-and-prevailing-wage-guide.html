READ BEFORE TAKING ON PREVAILING-WAGE WORK

Davis-Bacon Act compliance and state prevailing-wage compliance are strict-liability exposures: a small payroll error becomes a debarment risk in months. This guide outlines the rules in plain language and points to the operational practices a sub needs to put in place before crew arrives on a prevailing-wage jobsite. This is an operational guide, not legal advice. Davis-Bacon enforcement (federal) and state wage-and-hour enforcement vary by jurisdiction and have changed materially over the past several years. Engage prevailing-wage counsel or a certified-payroll services firm on the first prevailing-wage project; the cost is far less than a wage-determination audit.

Compliance Guide for Subcontractors on Public-Works Projects
Companion workbook: Certified_Payroll_Tracker.xlsx
What this guide covers. When the Project is federally funded ($2,000+ in construction), the Davis-Bacon Act applies and the sub must (a) pay every laborer and mechanic on the site at least the prevailing wage and fringe benefits set forth in the wage determination, (b) submit a weekly certified payroll (Form WH-347 or equivalent) signed under penalty of perjury, and (c) keep records for three years. Most states have their own "Little Davis-Bacon" laws applying similar rules to state-funded projects, often with stricter penalties. This guide explains the rules and the operational practices the sub needs.

Section 1 — When Prevailing Wage Applies

Prevailing wage is triggered by the funding source of the project, not the type of work or the type of contractor. The two regimes a sub will most commonly encounter:

RegimeTriggerEnforcerForm
Federal Davis-Bacon Act (DBA)Federally funded construction $2,000+ (including buildings, highways, public works)U.S. Department of Labor, Wage & Hour DivisionWH-347
Davis-Bacon-Related Acts (DBRA)Federally assisted (HUD, EPA, DOT grant pass-through)USDOL or assisting federal agencyWH-347 or state form
State "Little Davis-Bacon"State-funded construction; threshold varies ($1,000 in CA; $5,000 in NJ; $0 in HI on certain work)State labor departmentState form (often modeled on WH-347)
Service Contract Act (SCA)Federal service contracts $2,500+ (not construction; included here because subs occasionally have service-component scopes)USDOLDifferent rules — not addressed in this guide

Note: Prevailing wage flows down. If the prime is a federal contract, every tier of subcontractor on the project is bound, regardless of contract value at the sub tier. The wage determination is the GC's responsibility to obtain and provide to subs; the sub's compliance is the sub's own responsibility.

"Site of the work." Davis-Bacon applies to laborers and mechanics employed "on the site of the work" — which includes the physical site of the project and adjacent secondary sites where significant project-dedicated activity occurs (e.g., a batching plant set up exclusively for the project within reasonable proximity). It does not apply to off-site fabrication shops that serve multiple projects, to deliveries by third-party suppliers, or to workers performing work primarily in the contractor's permanent office. Misclassifying a worker as "off-site" when they actually worked at the project is a common audit finding.
Section 2 — The Wage Determination ("WD")

The wage determination is a document published by USDOL (federal) or the state labor department (state) that lists, for the locality of the project, the prevailing wage rate and fringe benefit value for each classification of worker covered. The WD is incorporated by reference into the prime contract and binds every tier of contractor on the project.

WD identifying number:[e.g., GA20240001 — federal; or state ref]
Geographic scope:[County / region]
Construction type (Building / Highway / Heavy / Residential):[Type]
Effective date:[Date]
WD modification number:[Mod #]
Sub's classifications covered:[List]

Key practical points:

  1. The WD freezes at the date the prime contract is signed. Amendments to the WD published after the prime contract date do not flow down — the sub bills against the WD in effect when the prime was signed.
  2. The WD shows two columns per classification: Rate (cash wage) and Fringes (fringe benefit value).
  3. "Conformance" — if a classification of work needed on the project is not listed in the WD, the GC must request USDOL conformance (Form SF-1444) to add the classification. The sub cannot self-classify.
  4. The WD applies to laborers and mechanics actually performing physical work. Project managers, supervisors, executives, professional engineers, and bona fide office staff are not covered.
Section 3 — Worker Classifications

Every worker must be assigned to the classification that best matches the work actually performed during the workweek. If a worker performs two or more classifications during the workweek, the sub may either (a) pay the highest rate for all hours worked or (b) keep an accurate record of time spent in each classification and pay each block at its respective rate. Option (b) requires the recordkeeping rigor to support it.

Common error: a "helper" classification that is not present in the WD. If the WD does not list "helper" for a trade, every worker performing the work is treated as a journeyman of that trade, even if the worker is paid less in the sub's ordinary payroll.

ClassificationTypical scopeAudit risk

Section 4 — Fringe Benefits — Cash vs. Bona Fide Plan

The "fringes" column of the WD is a per-hour dollar value that must be delivered to the worker either (a) as additional cash wage on top of the base rate or (b) as contributions to one or more "bona fide" benefit plans (health insurance, pension, training fund, etc.). The contractor's choice; the cost to the contractor must equal at least the WD fringe amount.

Delivery methodEffect on weekly wageWH-347 reporting
All cashWorker's hourly wage on payroll = WD Rate + WD FringeReport all in column 6 (Rate) or split between Rate and Fringes columns
All in bona fide planWorker's hourly wage = WD Rate only; fringe contributions made to plansReport Rate in col 6; Fringes in col 7; identify plans on the Statement of Compliance
Mixed (typical)Part cash, part planReport Rate in col 6; cash portion + plan portion totaling at least WD Fringe in col 7

To qualify as a "bona fide" plan:

  1. Contributions must be irrevocably made to a trust or third-party administrator that is independent of the contractor.
  2. The fund cannot revert to the contractor under any circumstance.
  3. The fund must be reasonably available to the worker as a benefit (e.g., health insurance with reasonable coverage; pension with reasonable vesting).
  4. Contributions must be made for time actually worked — annualizing across hours not worked at fringe rates can shift the fringe burden among workers and is permitted within limits, but the math is technical and must be documented.
"Fringe-burdened" annualization is the most common audit finding. Many sub payrolls annualize the fringe cost (e.g., a fringe of $10/hr on Davis-Bacon and $0/hr on private work, "averaged" across the contractor's full payroll). If the contractor cannot show that the annualization captures only Davis-Bacon hours, USDOL will treat the fringe as paid only at the average rate — which may be below the WD requirement. Pay the fringe directly to the Davis-Bacon worker on Davis-Bacon hours, or carefully document the annualization arithmetic.
Section 5 — Overtime & Calculation Mechanics

Overtime on Davis-Bacon work is governed by the Contract Work Hours and Safety Standards Act (CWHSSA), which requires time-and-a-half (1.5×) for hours over 40 in a workweek. State prevailing-wage laws sometimes require time-and-a-half for hours over 8 in a workday (CA), or double-time on Sundays/holidays. The stricter of the two applies.

The premium (the 0.5× portion of time-and-a-half, or the 1.0× portion of double-time) is calculated on the base rate only — not on the fringe. The fringe is paid at straight-time for all hours, including OT hours.

Worked example — WD rate $30/hr; fringe $10/hr; 45 hours in the workweek:

HoursCalculationAmount
40 ST × $30 base40 × 30$1,200.00
40 ST × $10 fringe40 × 10$400.00 (cash or plan)
5 OT base ($30)5 × 30$150.00
5 OT premium ($30 × 0.5)5 × 15$75.00
5 OT fringe ($10)5 × 10$50.00 (cash or plan)
Total$1,875.00

Common errors:

  • Calculating the OT premium on (base + fringe), inflating the premium amount but distorting the certified payroll math.
  • Treating fringe as bonus and reducing it during OT hours.
  • Not paying the fringe at all on hours when the worker exceeds 40 per week.
Section 6 — Apprentice & Trainee Ratios

Apprentices can be paid less than the journeyman rate only if all of the following are true:

  1. The apprentice is individually registered in a USDOL Office of Apprenticeship-approved program (or state-equivalent).
  2. The program's apprentice-to-journeyman ratio is observed on the actual project site.
  3. The percentage of journeyman rate paid matches the apprentice's year/period in the program.
  4. Apprenticeship paperwork (registration number, period) is available for the worker.

If any of the above is missing, the worker must be paid the journeyman rate. The most common audit issue is the on-site ratio — even with valid registration, an apprentice on a site without a journeyman in the same trade in the required ratio is paid as a journeyman for those hours.

Apprenticeship Program Sponsor:[Program name]
USDOL OA Registration No.:[Number]
Approved Ratio (Apprentice : Journeyman):[Ratio]
Approved Wage Percentages by Period:[Year 1: 50%; Year 2: 65%; etc.]
Section 7 — Form WH-347 — Anatomy

Form WH-347 is the standardized weekly certified payroll form. It is one page per contractor per week, with one row per worker showing the worker's identifying information, classification, daily hours, total hours, rate, gross, and deductions, with a Statement of Compliance attached.

ColumnContentNotes
1Worker name and individual identifying numberLast 4 of SSN historically; current guidance permits an employee ID
2Number of withholding exemptionsOptional
3Work classificationMust match WD; if classification is split during the week, must reflect each block
4Day and date — hours worked, ST and OT for each day of the workweekShow "S" for Sunday, "M" Monday, etc.; daily hours go in the cell
5Total hoursST and OT separately
6Rate of payWD Rate + cash-fringe portion; OT rate is base × 1.5 + cash-fringe
7Gross amount earnedFor the project (and "for all projects this period" if separate)
8Deductions (FICA, withholding, other)Itemized
9Net wages paid for week

Format note: The form has changed slightly over time. Always use the current WH-347 from the USDOL website or the form authorized by the state agency, not a back-copy.

Section 8 — Statement of Compliance

The Statement of Compliance is the signed certification on the back of WH-347 (or as a separate page). It must be signed by the owner or an authorized officer of the sub each week, under penalty of perjury (18 U.S.C. § 1001 and 31 U.S.C. § 3729 — the False Claims Act). A false Statement of Compliance is a federal felony.

The Statement certifies, among other things, that:

  1. The payroll for the period contains the information required for compliance with the Davis-Bacon Act and related regulations.
  2. The information is correct and complete.
  3. Each laborer or mechanic has been paid the full weekly wages earned, without rebate either directly or indirectly except for permitted deductions.
  4. The classifications shown match the work actually performed.
  5. The wages paid are not less than the applicable wage rates and fringe benefits in the WD.
  6. Fringe benefits have been paid in cash or to approved plans.
The Statement of Compliance is the False Claims Act trigger. Even where the underlying error is small (a misclassified $100/week), a false Statement of Compliance subjects the sub and the signing officer to FCA liability — treble damages, statutory penalties of $13,946 to $27,894 per claim (2024 figures), debarment, and individual criminal exposure. Do not sign a Statement of Compliance without (a) walking the payroll line-by-line, (b) confirming classifications match site work, (c) confirming fringe payments are documented, and (d) confirming apprentice ratios are observed. If anything is unclear, the matter is escalated, not signed.

Section 12 — Pre-Mobilization Checklist
  1. ☐ Wage Determination obtained from GC and reviewed for the sub's classifications.
  2. ☐ Each crew member's classification confirmed against WD; payroll system rates updated.
  3. ☐ Fringe delivery method confirmed (cash vs. plan); plan documentation gathered.
  4. ☐ Apprentice program registrations on file; on-site ratio plan documented.
  5. ☐ Time-tracking system configured for daily and classification-level reporting.
  6. ☐ WH-347 template loaded into the Certified Payroll Tracker workbook; payroll-clerk trained on the form.
  7. ☐ Wage poster (WH-1321) and state poster delivered to jobsite for posting.
  8. ☐ Statement-of-Compliance signatory identified; signatory briefed on FCA exposure.
  9. ☐ Weekly submission cadence and method agreed with GC.
  10. ☐ Outside counsel or certified-payroll services firm engaged for the first project.
  11. ☐ Project-specific recordkeeping folder set up; retention schedule calendared (3+ years).
  12. ☐ Audit-readiness review scheduled at the 4-week mark.
