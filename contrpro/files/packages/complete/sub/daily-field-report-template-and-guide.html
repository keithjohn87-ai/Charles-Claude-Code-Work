DAILY FIELD REPORT
Template, Guide, & Recordkeeping Workflow for Subcontractor Field Operations
Companion workbook: Daily_Field_Report.xlsx
Why DFRs matter. The Daily Field Report is the single most important document a sub produces on a construction project. It is the contemporaneous record of what happened on site each day — who was there, what they did, what conditions affected work, what was delivered, what was observed, what was directed, what was disputed. In every meaningful construction dispute — backcharges, change orders, delay claims, defect claims, OSHA disputes — the DFRs of the sub and the GC are the first evidence opened. A complete DFR file is the sub's strongest defense; a thin or missing DFR file is one of the most common reasons subs lose disputes that they should have won on the merits.

Section 1 — Why the DFR Exists

A DFR serves four overlapping purposes:

  1. Project-management record. A real-time record of crew, productivity, weather, schedule, and conditions, used by the PM to manage the project, forecast remaining work, and identify problems early.
  2. Evidence preservation. The first record of an event on the project, written contemporaneously and signed by a participant — the strongest form of evidence in a construction dispute. Memory of an event a year later is impeachable; a contemporaneous DFR is not.
  3. Schedule and productivity reporting. A consolidated daily record that supports the sub's monthly productivity reports, manpower forecasts, and schedule recovery plans.
  4. Compliance documentation. Required by many Subcontracts. On prevailing-wage and federal projects, DFRs support certified-payroll classifications. On safety-sensitive projects, DFRs document toolbox talks, safety meetings, and incidents.
DFR vs. Time Card vs. RFI Log. A DFR is broader than a time card (which captures hours only) and more granular than an RFI log (which captures questions only). It is the field foreman's narrative of the day, with supporting structured data. The structure of the DFR matters because (a) it forces the foreman to think about all of the categories that matter and (b) it allows later searches by category ("show me every DFR with a delay code" or "show me every day when more than 8 crew were on this elevation"). The sub's DFR system should support both narrative and structured fields.

Section 3 — Field Discipline

A DFR system is only useful if foremen actually fill it out. The four habits the sub's PM enforces:

  1. Same-day rule. DFRs are completed and signed before the foreman leaves the site for the day. "I'll do it tomorrow morning" is the start of a DFR file with holes.
  2. Specific, not generic. "Worked on south wall framing" is not a DFR entry. "Framed exterior wall sections E-1 through E-4 at south elevation; installed 18 ga metal studs, 16" o.c., 14'-6" tall; awaiting MEP rough-in for ECS-3 routing before sheathing" is. The specific entry is what survives in a dispute; the generic entry is what loses.
  3. Photographs every day. A minimum of 6-8 photos per day, geo-tagged and time-stamped, covering the locations the crew worked. Phone cameras are universally available; the marginal cost is zero; the marginal value in disputes is enormous.
  4. Flag conditions immediately. If a condition affecting work appeared today (a missing trade, an unexpected access restriction, a wet basement, an electrical de-energization), the DFR identifies it AND the foreman raises it the same day with the GC field rep. Quiet documentation that does not result in a directive is later impeached as "you didn't think it was important enough to raise" — defeating the contemporaneous-evidence argument.
Do not edit DFRs after the fact. A DFR submitted with the date of work and then edited two weeks later is a fraud vector. The integrity of a contemporaneous record depends on its contemporaneity. If a correction or addition is required, it is made as a new dated entry referring back to the original DFR by number, not as an edit to the original. The same principle applies to digital DFRs: the system should preserve an audit trail.

Section 7 — Foreman's Pre-Sign Checklist
  1. ☐ Date and DFR number entered.
  2. ☐ Crew names, classifications, hours, and tasks all populated — no "see other" or "TBD".
  3. ☐ Crew hour totals add up to the daily timecard total.
  4. ☐ Weather entered, with impact on work flagged if any.
  5. ☐ Equipment list complete.
  6. ☐ Materials delivered logged (with delivery ticket attached or referenced).
  7. ☐ Work-performed narrative is specific — references drawings, specs, locations.
  8. ☐ Conditions affecting work flagged where applicable.
  9. ☐ Any directive / RFI / T&M issued today is captured.
  10. ☐ At least six photos taken; photo log entries populated.
  11. ☐ Open issues escalated to office in writing.
  12. ☐ Foreman signature applied.
  13. ☐ GC field rep countersignature requested (collect when possible — same-day is gold).
  14. ☐ Filed in project DFR folder (paper) and uploaded to project drive (digital) the same day.
