REVIEW BEFORE FIRST USE

This Backcharge Dispute Pack contains template correspondence and a workflow a sub uses to (a) preserve rights when a GC imposes a backcharge, (b) demand itemization and substantiation, (c) protest formally if the deduction stands, and (d) preserve lien and bond claim rights through to litigation. The letter templates parallel common commercial-construction practice and should be tailored to the specific Subcontract notice clauses, the state's mechanic's lien statutes, and the specific facts of the dispute. Do not send any of these letters unmodified. The notice deadlines in your Subcontract and the lien-filing deadlines under state law are usually short (often 7 to 21 days from the triggering event). Have construction counsel licensed in the state of the Project review the letter and the facts before sending; the cost of one hour of counsel time is recovered many times over by getting the notice right.

How to use this pack. When a GC imposes a backcharge (a deduction from your pay app for damages, repair, cleanup, supplemental labor, schedule impact, etc.), the Sub has roughly three windows to react: an immediate-objection window (typically 7-14 days under the Subcontract notice provisions), an itemization-demand window (when GC's notice lacks the substantiation required to support the deduction), and a formal-protest window (where the deduction will appear on the next pay app). Section 1 — Backcharge Anatomy

A "backcharge" in commercial construction is the contractor's mechanism for deducting cost from a sub's pay app to compensate for damage, repair, or supplemental work the contractor (or another sub) had to perform on the project that the contractor believes was the subject sub's responsibility. The mechanic of a backcharge is:

  1. Triggering event — a defect, damage, cleanup issue, schedule slip, omission, OSHA citation, or similar (the "Event").
  2. GC absorbs the cost in the first instance — paying its own crew, another sub, or a vendor to remediate.
  3. GC issues a backcharge notice to the sub, stating the amount, the date of incurrence, and the Subcontract clause invoked.
  4. GC deducts the amount from the sub's next pay app (or, if no pay app pending, invoices the sub directly).
  5. If sub objects, the dispute proceeds through the Subcontract dispute-resolution clause.

The risk to the sub: backcharges are easy for a GC to assert and hard for a sub to defend after the fact. Without contemporaneous evidence (photos, daily field reports, timecards, signed tickets), the dispute is one party's recollection against the other. The first job in any backcharge defense is to preserve the evidence; the second is to make a timely paper objection that prevents the GC from claiming the sub accepted the deduction.

Why timing matters. Most commercial Subcontracts (and the AIA A401 / ConsensusDocs 750 forms) contain a "claim waiver" clause that bars any claim by the sub not asserted within a defined period (commonly 7-21 days) of the triggering event or the sub's notice of the GC's adverse action. The clock starts when the sub knows or should know about the backcharge — often when the deduction first appears on a pay app or in correspondence. If the sub does not paper its objection by the deadline, the GC will argue the claim is waived and refuse to consider it on the merits. Calendar the deadline the day you learn of the backcharge.
Section 2 — Valid vs. Invalid Backcharges

Not every backcharge is enforceable. A defensible backcharge requires:

#Required ElementWhat it means
1Subcontract authorizationThe Subcontract must give the GC the right to backcharge — either by express clause or by the general default/cure-and-perform clauses. Without authorization, the deduction is a unilateral set-off subject to challenge.
2Notice + cure opportunityMost Subcontracts require the GC to notify the sub of the alleged defect or deficiency and give a reasonable cure period before the GC self-performs and backcharges. Where the GC skipped this step, the backcharge is procedurally defective.
3Cost itemizationThe GC must itemize the cost — labor (timecards / classifications / hours / rates), materials (invoices), equipment (rates / hours), overhead, profit. A lump-sum backcharge without itemization is challengeable.
4CausationThe cost must arise from the sub's act or omission, not from third-party factors, GC's own actions, or differing site conditions.
5Reasonable amountThe cost must be reasonable in the marketplace. A GC's self-performed labor rate billed at $145/hour when the trade rate is $65/hour invites challenge to the rate.

The sub's defense framework, in priority order, attacks each element:

  1. No Subcontract authorization to backcharge? Demand strike of the deduction in full.
  2. No notice + cure? Demand strike of the deduction or, at minimum, a credit for the labor cost the sub would have performed (cure-cost benchmark).
  3. No itemization? Demand itemization and refuse to accept the deduction until provided.
  4. No causation? Demand proof that the sub's act or omission caused the cost; document differing site conditions, third-party causation, or GC's own actions.
  5. Unreasonable amount? Demand cost substantiation; benchmark against industry rates; propose a reasonable credit.
Section 3 — Immediate Response Workflow

From the moment a backcharge notice (or a pay app deduction) is received, the sub has approximately seven discrete actions to complete within the first 14 days:

Section 4 — Evidence Preservation Checklist
  1. ☐ All photos taken during the period of the alleged defect or work — original camera files preserved, time-stamped.
  2. ☐ Daily Field Reports (DFRs) for every day from 7 days before the Event through 7 days after.
  3. ☐ Timecards / payroll showing crews on site, hours worked, classifications.
  4. ☐ Material invoices, delivery tickets, signed receipts for materials delivered during the period.
  5. ☐ Subcontract, Subcontract amendments, and any change orders affecting the disputed scope.
  6. ☐ RFIs, submittals, and responses bearing on the disputed scope or condition.
  7. ☐ Drawings, specifications, and project-manual sections governing the disputed scope (including current revision dates).
  8. ☐ Schedule (baseline + most recent lookahead) showing the sub's anticipated sequence.
  9. ☐ Email and text correspondence with GC PM, GC superintendent, GC field engineer.
  10. ☐ GC's notice of the alleged defect or deficiency, and GC's notice of the backcharge.
  11. ☐ Pay app and pay-app correspondence showing the deduction.
  12. ☐ Any third-party communications (other subs, suppliers, inspectors, owner's rep).
  13. ☐ OSHA citation or third-party inspector report, if applicable.
  14. ☐ Insurance correspondence, if the matter could be insurance-covered (property damage, bodily injury).
  15. ☐ Witness list — every crew member, foreman, or third party who was present and may be deposed.
  16. ☐ Litigation hold notice issued to internal staff — preserve all documents until further notice.
Issue a litigation hold immediately. The moment a backcharge is received, send a written instruction to every PM, foreman, estimator, and accounting staff member who touched the project: do not delete email, do not overwrite photos, do not throw away DFRs, do not destroy timecards. Construction documents are routinely destroyed in the ordinary course of business 6-12 months after a project closes; if the dispute lingers past close-out, the evidence the sub needs may be gone. A litigation hold notice is two paragraphs and a sentence about preserving electronic and paper records on the disputed project.
Letter 1 — Notice of Objection / Reservation of Rights

Send within 1-2 business days of learning of the backcharge. Send by email (for speed) with a follow-up hard copy by certified mail (for the Subcontract notice clause). This letter does not engage the merits; it only preserves the sub's right to object.

[Subcontractor Letterhead]
Date: [Date]

[GC PM Name, Title]
[GC Legal Name]
[Address]
Via: Email and Certified U.S. Mail, Return Receipt Requested
Re: Notice of Objection and Reservation of Rights — Asserted Backcharge
Project: [Project Name]
Subcontract No.: [Subcontract No.]
Backcharge Reference: [GC's backcharge notice no. or pay app line]

Dear [Name]:

This letter is sent in response to [Contractor's notice / pay app deduction / correspondence] dated [Date], in which Contractor asserts a backcharge against [Subcontractor Legal Name] ("Subcontractor") in the amount of $[Amount] for [brief description of asserted cost] (the "Asserted Backcharge").

Subcontractor objects to the Asserted Backcharge in full and reserves all rights and remedies under the Subcontract, the Prime Contract (to the extent flowing down to the Subcontract), the laws of the State of [State], and at common law.

This objection is made on grounds including, without limitation, the following:

  1. The Asserted Backcharge has not been substantiated with the itemization, evidence, and causation showing required for a valid backcharge under the Subcontract.
  2. Subcontractor disputes that the cost described in the Asserted Backcharge was caused by any act or omission of Subcontractor.
  3. Subcontractor disputes that Contractor provided Subcontractor with the notice and cure opportunity required by the Subcontract before incurring the costs underlying the Asserted Backcharge.
  4. Subcontractor disputes that the amount of the Asserted Backcharge is reasonable in light of industry-standard rates for the Work described.

By this letter, Subcontractor preserves all claims, defenses, and rights of recovery, and nothing in this letter or in any subsequent communication shall be construed as a waiver of any right or as an admission of any fact or liability. Subcontractor expressly reserves the right to:

  1. Demand full itemization and substantiation of the Asserted Backcharge.
  2. Submit a formal protest pursuant to the dispute-resolution provisions of the Subcontract.
  3. Recover any amount improperly deducted from any future pay app, plus interest as permitted by the State's Prompt Payment Act.
  4. File a mechanic's lien on the Project (and a claim against any payment bond) for amounts wrongfully withheld.
  5. Pursue all other remedies at law and in equity.

This letter is not exhaustive of Subcontractor's positions on the Asserted Backcharge; additional grounds will be set forth in subsequent correspondence as the dispute develops. Subcontractor will provide further correspondence within [7] business days regarding itemization and substantiation.

Subcontractor reserves the right to amend, supplement, or further explain its position at any time.

Sincerely,

[Authorized Signatory]
[Title]
[Subcontractor Legal Name]
cc: [Sub's outside counsel; project file]

Letter 2 — Demand for Itemization

Send within 3-5 business days of the Notice of Objection. Triggers the GC's obligation to itemize; without itemization, the backcharge is procedurally defective.

[Subcontractor Letterhead]
Date: [Date]

[GC PM Name, Title]
[GC Legal Name]
[Address]
Re: Demand for Itemization of Asserted Backcharge
Project: [Project Name] · Backcharge: [Ref]

Dear [Name]:

Further to [Subcontractor Legal Name]'s Notice of Objection dated [Date], this letter formally demands itemization of the Asserted Backcharge in the amount of $[Amount]. The notice and deduction received to date does not contain the substantiation required for a valid backcharge under the Subcontract.

For Subcontractor to evaluate the Asserted Backcharge on the merits, Contractor must provide the following itemization, with supporting documentation, within [7] business days of receipt of this letter:

  1. Labor — Names and classifications of every worker who performed the underlying work; hours worked by each (straight time / overtime); the hourly rate billed; backup timecards or daily-time records signed by the worker and their foreman.
  2. Materials — Vendor invoices for every material used in the underlying work; delivery tickets confirming receipt at the Project site; description of how each material was used.
  3. Equipment — Equipment used (owned or rented); hours used; rate basis (published rental rate or internal rate); rental invoices if rented.
  4. Subcontract — If any portion of the underlying work was performed by a lower-tier subcontractor to Contractor, that subcontractor's invoice and supporting backup.
  5. Overhead and Profit — The percentage applied (if any) and the basis under the Subcontract for charging it.
  6. Date and Location of Work — The specific date(s) and location(s) on the Project site at which the underlying work was performed.
  7. Identification of the Subcontract Provision — The specific Subcontract clause(s) Contractor invokes as authority for the backcharge.
  8. Notice Documentation — Copies of any prior notice to Subcontractor of the alleged defect, deficiency, or non-performance, including the date of the notice, the means of delivery, and the cure period (if any) given.
  9. Photographs and Field Documentation — Photos of the condition before, during, and after the underlying work; daily field reports for the day(s) the work was performed.

Until the foregoing itemization is provided, the Asserted Backcharge cannot be evaluated by Subcontractor and is not accepted, in whole or in part. Subcontractor reserves all rights as set forth in its Notice of Objection.

If Contractor declines to provide the requested itemization, Subcontractor will treat the Asserted Backcharge as procedurally invalid and will pursue the remedies stated in the Notice of Objection.

Sincerely,

[Authorized Signatory / Title / Subcontractor]
cc: [Sub's outside counsel]

Lien deadlines do not extend during dispute. A common misconception is that good-faith negotiation tolls (suspends) the state lien-filing deadline. It does not. If the lien deadline expires while the parties are still negotiating, the lien right is lost permanently. Calendar the lien deadline the day the backcharge is received and file the lien at the deadline if the matter is unresolved — the lien can be released or withdrawn later, but a lien not filed by the statutory deadline cannot be revived.

Closing reminder. Backcharge disputes are won and lost on (a) timely written objections, (b) contemporaneous evidence, and (c) state-statute compliance. Of these three, only the first is fully under the sub's control after a backcharge is received — preserve evidence reflexively while the project is live and the documentation has not yet been recycled into "ordinary course of business" destruction. Letters and tracker entries are not a substitute for construction counsel; on any backcharge exceeding [$25,000], on any backcharge involving safety or OSHA claims, or on any backcharge that may threaten the sub's lien rights, escalate to outside counsel immediately.
