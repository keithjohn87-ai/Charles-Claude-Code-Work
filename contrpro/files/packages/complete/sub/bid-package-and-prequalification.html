REVIEW BEFORE FIRST USE

This Bid Package & Prequalification Form is a master template a subcontractor uses to (1) respond to a GC's invitation to bid and (2) get onto the GC's approved bidder list. The form structure parallels common commercial industry practice and the framework of AIA Document A305 (Contractor's Qualification Statement) without reproducing AIA's copyrighted form verbatim. Tailor the financial-disclosure depth, references count, and bonding-capacity letter to the specific solicitation — over-disclosing on a small private job invites overreach, under-disclosing on a public bid risks disqualification. On public/federal work, additional certifications apply (E-Verify, EEO, Davis-Bacon, MBE/WBE/DBE/SBE, drug-free workplace, etc.); add the corresponding addendum from the solicitation. Have counsel or your bonding agent review the financial-disclosure exhibits before you release them to a GC for the first time.

How to use this package. This single document is intended to serve two related but distinct purposes. Sections 1 through 5 form the bid response a subcontractor submits in answer to a specific Invitation to Bid (cover letter, bid form, bid bond, schedule of subs/suppliers, addenda acknowledgment). Sections 6 through 13 form the prequalification submission a subcontractor submits to be added to a GC's approved bidder list (qualifications, experience, financial, safety, insurance, bonding, references, litigation, key personnel, signed certification). The two halves are bundled here because most GCs ask for both at the same time — but many GCs allow the prequalification half to be submitted once per year and incorporated by reference into each bid response. Use the Bid Submission Checklist at the end to verify the submittal is complete before transmittal.

Practice note — keep the cover letter short. The cover letter is the first thing a GC's estimator reads, and on a competitive bid day the estimator is reading a stack of them in a hurry. State the bid amount in Section 2 — not in the cover letter — so the estimator can pull all bids onto a comparison sheet quickly. Use the cover letter only to (a) identify the firm, (b) confirm the bid is binding and complete, (c) flag the bid validity period, and (d) point to where the exceptions live (Section 4). Never bury an exception in the cover letter narrative; if the bid is conditioned, the conditions belong in Section 4 where they are easy to surface.

Practice note — what "includes" and "excludes" actually do. The "Includes" and "Excludes" checkboxes in 2.2 are the most contested fields in a competitive bid. If you check that the bid "includes sales tax" and the project turns out to be in a sales-tax-exempt jurisdiction, you've left money on the table; if you don't check it and the GC's prime contract assumed your bid included sales tax, you have a margin-eating dispute on Day 1. Set firm-wide policy on what the standard bid includes and excludes, train every estimator to it, and never let a junior estimator deviate without principal sign-off. The "excludes" list should always be specific enough that a GC reading it cannot reasonably claim ambiguity: "Excludes after-hours premium time" is fine; "Excludes special conditions" is not — what is "special"?

Practice note — bid security forfeiture and the "Spearin doctrine." Bid security is forfeited only if (a) the bid is accepted within its validity period and (b) the bidder then refuses to execute the subcontract on the terms originally bid. If the GC issues a Notice of Award conditioned on terms materially different from the Bid Documents (added scope, modified payment terms, increased indemnity, etc.), Subcontractor is generally not required to execute and bid security is not forfeited. Document the deviation in writing immediately upon learning of it and decline to execute only after written notice. On the other hand, Subcontractor is bound to its bid even where the bid contains an arithmetic mistake — unless the mistake is so substantial, was promptly disclosed, and meets the state-specific "unilateral mistake" doctrine. Have counsel review before invoking unilateral-mistake withdrawal.

Practice note — what to release and what to withhold. Most GC prequalification forms request "the most recent year-end financial statement." This is overbroad. A summary like the table above is sufficient for the GC's bonding-equivalent analysis (current ratio, working capital, equity). Release the underlying full audited financials only after (a) Contract has been awarded and a confidentiality agreement is in place, or (b) the bid is on a project large enough relative to your equity that the GC has a legitimate ratio-based concern. On smaller projects or with GCs you have not previously worked with, decline and offer your bonding agent's reference letter (Section 10) as proof of financial capacity instead — that letter is the surety's underwritten opinion of your financials and is the better disclosure substitute.

Practice note — what an additional-insured endorsement does and does not do. Being listed as an "Additional Insured" on a Subcontractor's CGL gives the GC and Owner direct coverage for liability arising out of the Subcontractor's work. Critical points: (a) the endorsement form matters — CG 20 10 (ongoing) does not cover claims arising after completion; CG 20 37 (completed operations) is also required if the GC wants long-tail coverage; (b) "blanket" additional-insured endorsements that automatically cover all parties contractually entitled to additional insured status are common and acceptable, but the underlying contract language must require the AI status to trigger them; (c) the AI endorsement does not modify the indemnity in the Subcontract — coverage and indemnity are separate obligations. Confirm with the broker that the endorsement actually issued matches what the Subcontract requires; many disputes arise from a Subcontract that requires CG 20 10 + CG 20 37 but a broker that issued only CG 20 26 (an older form with narrower coverage).

Practice note — the 30-minute pre-submittal review. Before transmittal, walk the package through Section 15 with a second pair of eyes — ideally not the estimator who priced the bid. Common pre-submittal misses caught by a fresh reviewer: an addendum issued the morning of bid day that was not acknowledged in Section 4.1; a unit price typo'd as $/SF when the ITB asked for $/LF; an exception in Section 4.2 that contradicts a clarification given to the GC by email earlier in the bid cycle; a financial reference listed without the contact's current direct line; a notarization that has lapsed because the signatory's notary commission expired. None of these are sophisticated errors. All of them lose bids.
