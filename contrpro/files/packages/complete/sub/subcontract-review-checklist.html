REVIEW BEFORE FIRST USE

This Subcontract Review Checklist is a sub-perspective working document the project manager, controller, and outside counsel use to flag risk when a GC tenders a Subcontract for execution. It is organized to mirror the article structure of the ContrPro Master Subcontract Agreement (GC suite) and the AIA A401 form, so the reviewer can move clause-by-clause through the tendered agreement and resolve every risk item before signing. This is a working checklist, not legal advice. Construction-contract enforceability turns on the law of the state of the Project — anti-indemnity statutes, lien-waiver statutes, pay-if-paid enforceability, and statute-of-repose periods all vary by state. Have construction counsel licensed in the state of the Project review the tendered Subcontract before execution, especially the first time you sign with a new GC or take on a new project type.

Section 1 — Threshold Deal-Killers (Read First)

Before going clause-by-clause, screen the tendered Subcontract for the following five threshold items. Any one of these, left as drafted, is enough to justify rejecting the Subcontract or pricing a substantial risk premium. If three or more appear together, the GC is tendering a risk-shift package that should not be signed without principal-level review.

#Threshold ItemWhere to lookResolved?
1Pay-IF-paid (vs. pay-when-paid) language — owner non-payment shifts to SubArticle 4 / payment clause☐
2Broad-form (sole-negligence) indemnity from Sub to GC and OwnerArticle 8 / indemnification☐
3Unlimited or daily liquidated damages flowed down to Sub from primeArticle 3 / time + LDs☐
4Termination-for-convenience without payment for work performed + reasonable overhead/profitArticle 13 / termination☐
5Choice-of-law / venue in a state with no project connection (and Sub has no presence there)Article 14 / dispute resolution☐
Why these five. Every other risk in a Subcontract can be priced into the bid (insurance limits, retainage, warranty period). These five cannot be priced because their exposure is unbounded. Owner non-payment can wipe out the Sub's entire receivable; broad-form indemnity can transfer a six-figure jury verdict in a workplace-injury case to a Sub with a five-figure margin; unbounded LDs can convert a profitable job into a multi-month loss; termination-without-pay can leave a Sub unpaid for completed-and-installed material; an out-of-state forum can quadruple legal costs in a dispute. Walk away from any one of these without a serious GC concession.
Article 1 — Subcontract Documents (Incorporation by Reference)

Standard GC language incorporates the Prime Contract between Owner and GC into the Subcontract "by reference," binding the Sub to flow-down obligations from a document the Sub may never have seen. The risk is in the unseen — Prime Contract clauses on schedule, change orders, lien waivers, insurance, and indemnity flow through to the Sub silently.

Acceptable. "Subcontractor agrees to be bound by the Prime Contract to the extent of the Work performed by Subcontractor and to assume toward Contractor all of the obligations and responsibilities that Contractor assumes toward Owner under the Prime Contract with respect to such Work." Note the limiting phrase "to the extent of the Work" — keep it.
Redline. Any version that incorporates the Prime Contract without the "to the extent of the Work" limiter, or that incorporates documents not yet shared with Sub. Insert: "Subcontractor has been provided a copy of the Prime Contract, with confidential commercial terms redacted, prior to execution of this Subcontract. Documents not so provided are not incorporated."
Walk-Away. Language that incorporates by reference "all documents executed between Contractor and Owner, whenever executed" — including post-Subcontract amendments to the Prime Contract that change the Sub's obligations without notice or compensation.

Article 2 — Scope of Work

The Scope of Work clause defines what the Sub will and will not do. The risk is open-ended scope expansion — "all work as shown or reasonably inferable," "complete and operational system," "all work necessary to coordinate with other trades." Each of these is industry-standard for full-service Subs but each contains escalation risk that should be priced or limited.

Acceptable. "Subcontractor shall furnish all labor, materials, equipment, and services necessary for the [Trade Description] Work as described in the Scope of Work attached as Exhibit A, the Drawings listed in Exhibit B, and the Specifications listed in Exhibit C." Tight reference to enumerated exhibits.
Redline. Language extending scope to "all work shown or reasonably inferable" without a corresponding RFI / clarification process. Insert: "Work reasonably inferable from but not expressly shown in the Contract Documents shall be performed at Subcontractor's cost only to the extent customary and necessary for completion of the Work; ambiguities shall be resolved through the RFI process before performance, and material additions shall be priced as a Change Order under Article 5."
Redline. "Coordinate with all other trades" without scope and process boundaries. Coordination is a real Sub obligation but the language should be capped: "Subcontractor shall reasonably coordinate sequencing with other trades through the schedule maintained by Contractor; Subcontractor is not responsible for delays caused by other trades or by Contractor's schedule sequencing."
Walk-Away. "Scope includes all work shown on Drawings now or as later issued." Open-ended scope expansion via revised drawings — without a Change Order process — is unbounded risk.
Inclusions vs. exclusions. The bid is the place to specify exclusions; the Subcontract is the place to make them stick. Pull every "exclusion" and "clarification" from the bid (Section 4 of the Bid Package) into Exhibit A of the Subcontract as enumerated exclusions. Anything not pulled forward will be argued by the GC as included.
Article 3 — Time of Performance & Liquidated Damages

Time-of-performance clauses tie the Sub to a project schedule and (frequently) flow LDs down from the Prime Contract. Risk: (a) "time is of the essence" + flowed-down LDs without a corresponding right to time extensions; (b) Sub bears LD risk for delay it did not cause.

Acceptable. "Time is of the essence. Subcontractor shall perform the Work in accordance with the schedule maintained by Contractor, as updated. Subcontractor is entitled to an extension of time, without cost, for delays caused by: (a) acts of Contractor or Owner; (b) acts of other Subcontractors; (c) Force Majeure; (d) differing site conditions; or (e) any cause for which Contractor receives an extension under the Prime Contract."
Redline (Critical). Flow-down of Owner-imposed liquidated damages at the daily rate of the Prime Contract without a cap and without Sub-specific delay analysis. Insert: "Subcontractor's exposure to liquidated damages is capped at [$ amount] in the aggregate, and assessment requires Contractor's prior written delay analysis demonstrating that Subcontractor's delay was the controlling-path cause of Owner-assessed LDs."
Redline. "No damages for delay" clauses (state-specific enforceability — TX, OH, MI, IL have limitations; CO has a statute restricting them on public work). Counter-insert: "Subcontractor is entitled to recover delay damages caused by Contractor or Owner to the same extent and on the same basis as Contractor is entitled to recover such damages under the Prime Contract."
Walk-Away. Uncapped LDs flowed down to Sub from a Prime Contract Sub has not reviewed. The Sub may be on the hook for $10K/day of LDs while collecting $0 of delay damages from the GC.

Article 4 — Payment Terms (Pay-When-Paid vs. Pay-If-Paid)

The single most consequential clause in any commercial Subcontract. The distinction:

ConstructEffect
Pay-When-Paid (PWP)Owner non-payment delays GC payment to Sub for a reasonable time; eventually GC pays regardless.
Pay-If-Paid (PIP)Owner non-payment shifts the risk of owner credit to Sub; if Owner never pays, Sub is never paid.

State enforceability varies. PIP is unenforceable in CA, NC, NY (effectively), DE, IL (limited), and several other states. PWP is generally enforceable everywhere. Where PIP is enforceable, surety bonding rates increase materially on the Sub's program.

Acceptable. "Contractor shall pay Subcontractor within seven (7) days after Contractor receives payment from Owner for Work properly performed by Subcontractor, provided that Subcontractor has submitted its application for payment timely and accurately. In any event, Contractor shall pay Subcontractor for properly performed Work no later than sixty (60) days after the date such Work is properly performed, regardless of whether Contractor has received payment from Owner for such Work." (Pay-when-paid with a sixty-day backstop.)
Redline (Critical). "Receipt of payment from Owner is a condition precedent to payment to Subcontractor" or "Subcontractor expressly assumes the risk of Owner non-payment" — these are PIP markers. Strike and replace with the Acceptable language. If GC refuses, demand a corresponding increase in retainage release rate or up-front mobilization payment to mitigate.
Walk-Away. PIP language combined with (a) an Owner with which Sub has no prior payment history, (b) a project funded by a non-investment-grade source (private equity, real-estate developer with thin equity), or (c) a Prime Contract with no Owner performance bond or letter of credit. The credit risk is unpriced and unhedged.

Article 5 — Changes in the Work

Changes clauses define how Sub gets paid for added work, how Sub objects to deductive changes, and how Sub preserves claims for unwritten changes (constructive change orders).

Acceptable. "Changes in the Work shall be authorized in writing by Contractor (Change Order). Pricing shall be by lump sum if priced in advance, or by time-and-material with a markup of [15%] overhead and [10%] profit on self-performed labor and material, and [5-7%] on lower-tier subcontracts. Subcontractor shall not proceed with changed Work absent a written Change Order or written Field Directive; Subcontractor shall preserve claims for constructive change by written notice within seven (7) days of the directing event."
Redline. Markup rates below industry-standard (10% overhead / 5% profit, or "no profit on materials"). Counter with bid-package or industry-standard rates above.
Redline. "Subcontractor waives any claim not submitted within X days" with X less than 14. Insert: "Notice within fourteen (14) days of the event giving rise to the claim; supporting documentation within thirty (30) days. Failure to provide notice strictly within these periods bars the claim only where Contractor demonstrates actual prejudice from the late notice." (Reasonable notice with a prejudice limitation.)
Walk-Away. "Subcontractor shall perform all work directed by Contractor regardless of cost or schedule impact; any claim for adjustment must be made after completion of the Project" — turns the Sub into an open-ended performer with no contemporaneous claim rights.

6.2 Additional Insured
Acceptable. "Contractor and Owner shall be named as Additional Insureds on Subcontractor's CGL policy on ISO Form CG 20 10 (ongoing operations) and CG 20 37 (completed operations), or equivalent. Coverage shall be primary and non-contributory."
Redline. "Sole-negligence" coverage extension — requiring the Sub's insurance to cover the GC's or Owner's own negligence. Most jurisdictions have anti-indemnity statutes that void this; many policies exclude it. Strike or limit to "to the extent of Subcontractor's negligence."
Walk-Away. A required additional-insured form Sub's carrier will not issue. Confirm with broker before signing.
6.3 Waiver of Subrogation

Mutual waiver of subrogation among Owner, GC, and Sub for losses covered by Builder's Risk insurance is industry-standard and acceptable. One-way waiver (Sub waives but GC and Owner retain) is not — redline.

Article 8 — Indemnification

Indemnification — the Sub's promise to defend and reimburse the GC and Owner for liability — is the second-most consequential clause after payment. The three forms:

FormSub's Exposure
Limited (Comparative)Sub indemnifies only to the extent of Sub's own negligence. Industry-standard, generally enforceable.
IntermediateSub indemnifies for any loss except the GC's sole negligence. Enforceable in many states; voided by anti-indemnity statute in others.
Broad-FormSub indemnifies for the GC's own negligence. Voided by anti-indemnity statute in roughly 40 states.
Acceptable. "To the fullest extent permitted by law, Subcontractor shall indemnify, defend, and hold harmless Contractor and Owner from claims arising out of bodily injury or property damage, but only to the extent caused by the negligent acts or omissions of Subcontractor or any party for whom Subcontractor is responsible." (Comparative-negligence-limited indemnity — generally enforceable.)
Redline (Critical). Any "broad-form" indemnity ("Subcontractor shall indemnify regardless of whether caused in part by the negligence of Contractor"). Strike and replace with Acceptable language. If GC will not accept, confirm with counsel that the controlling state's anti-indemnity statute voids the clause — and, even so, demand a redline to avoid litigation cost in enforcing the statute.
Walk-Away. Broad-form indemnity combined with (a) requirement to defend the GC at Sub's cost from the first dollar and (b) no insurance program large enough to cover the defense costs. Two-front exposure with no offset.
Article 9 — Lien Waivers & Flow-Down

Lien waiver clauses require Sub to execute partial and final lien waivers as a condition of payment. Risk: (a) waiving lien rights for amounts not yet paid (front-loaded waiver) and (b) waiving claims, not just liens.

Acceptable. Conditional partial waiver delivered with each pay app, waiving lien rights only "for amounts actually received" through the date of the waiver. Final unconditional waiver delivered upon receipt of final payment.
Redline. Unconditional waiver required before payment is received — particularly common in non-statutory states. Replace with the conditional/unconditional bifurcation above. In states with statutory waiver forms (CA, FL, TX, others), use the statutory form verbatim; non-statutory forms may not be enforceable as waivers.
Walk-Away. "Subcontractor waives all rights to liens on the Project as a condition of executing this Subcontract" — pre-furnishing lien waiver. Voided by statute in many states; in the rest, an immediate red flag about the GC's intent.

Article 11 — Lower-Tier Subcontractors
Acceptable. "Subcontractor may engage lower-tier subcontractors and suppliers in the performance of the Work. Lower-tier engagements over [$50,000] require Contractor's written approval, not unreasonably withheld."
Redline. "All lower-tier subcontracts subject to Contractor approval and Contractor's standard terms" — gives the GC veto on Sub's supply chain and may require flow-down terms incompatible with Sub's own subs. Limit approval to threshold dollars + scope criticality.
Article 12 — Safety & Site Rules

Standard safety obligations are non-negotiable: OSHA compliance, drug-free workplace, site-specific safety plan, attendance at GC's safety meetings, accident reporting. Risk is in over-broad cost-shifting:

Redline. "Subcontractor shall reimburse Contractor for any cost or fine arising from Subcontractor's safety performance" without limitation. Limit to citations actually issued by OSHA naming Sub or arising from Sub's controlled work.
Redline. "Subcontractor shall be responsible for site safety of all trades during Subcontractor's work hours" — only acceptable when Sub is the sole trade on site. Limit to Sub's own crew and work area.
Article 13 — Default & Termination
13.1 Termination for Default
Acceptable. Default termination on written notice + opportunity to cure ([10] business days) + Sub paid for Work properly performed through termination date, less GC's cost to complete.
Redline. Default termination without notice/cure for "any breach" — narrow to "material breach not cured within X days of written notice."
13.2 Termination for Convenience
Acceptable. "Contractor may terminate this Subcontract for convenience upon written notice. Upon such termination, Subcontractor shall be paid for: (a) all Work properly performed through the termination date at the rates set forth in this Subcontract; (b) demobilization costs; (c) cancellation costs on materials ordered and lower-tier subcontracts properly committed; and (d) a reasonable allowance for overhead and profit on the Work performed."
Walk-Away. Termination for convenience without (a) payment for Work performed at contract rates, (b) demobilization cost, or (c) overhead/profit on Work performed. The Sub becomes an option that the GC can drop at any time at zero cost.

Article 15 — Miscellaneous Provisions

The "boilerplate" article that frequently contains traps:

  1. Assignment. One-way assignment (GC can assign; Sub cannot) is industry-standard. Mutual is rare. Check that Sub's assignment to lender (for AR financing) is permitted.
  2. Set-off. GC's right to set off against Sub's pay app for amounts allegedly owed on this Project — acceptable. Set-off across other Projects between the same parties — redline (each project should stand alone).
  3. Audit. GC right to audit Sub's books on T&M work — acceptable. GC right to audit on lump-sum work — redline (no basis for it).
  4. Confidentiality. Reciprocal confidentiality of pricing and project information — acceptable. One-way binding only Sub — redline.
  5. Entire Agreement / Integration. Confirms only the signed Subcontract + Exhibits is binding. Verify that all bid Exceptions/Clarifications are pulled forward into Exhibits.
  6. Modifications. Modifications only in writing signed by both parties. Acceptable and protective for Sub.
  7. Survival. Confirm indemnity, warranties, lien waiver, and confidentiality survive termination — acceptable.
  8. Force Majeure. Mutual; verify pandemic / labor strikes / supply chain disruption are listed. Strike "regardless of foreseeability" if present.
  9. Waiver. No waiver implied by GC's failure to insist on strict performance — standard.
  10. Severability. If one clause is invalid, the rest survives — standard.

Closing reminder. This checklist is a working document, not a substitute for construction counsel. The five threshold deal-killers in Section 1 are the most common loss vectors but not the only ones. Material risks vary by trade (mechanical subs face equipment-procurement risk; steel erectors face crane and rigging risk; electrical subs face long-lead gear risk); the redline guidance above is generic to commercial Subcontracts and must be tailored to the trade and the project. On any Subcontract over [$500K], on any new GC relationship, and on any public-works project, escalate to outside construction counsel before execution.
