## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Texas anomaly — no statewide general contractor license

Texas is **the largest state in the United States that does not require a state-issued general contractor license** [1][2][3]. There is **no agency analog to California's CSLB, Tennessee's Board for Licensing Contractors, or Florida's CILB at the state level for general construction**. Instead, regulation of general building, residential, and home-improvement contracting is **delegated to municipalities** under the home-rule and general-law authority of the Texas Local Government Code. The major Texas metropolitan jurisdictions — Houston, Dallas, San Antonio, Austin, Fort Worth, El Paso, and others — each operate their own contractor registration regimes with materially different requirements, fees, insurance minimums, and renewal cycles. A contractor that works in three Texas cities frequently holds three separate municipal registrations.

The Texas state government **does, however, license five of the construction trades** through three different state agencies:

- **Air Conditioning and Refrigeration Contractors** — Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR), under Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1302 [4][5].
- **Electricians and Electrical Contractors** — TDLR, under Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1305 [6][7].
- **Plumbers (Tradesman Plumber-Limited, Journeyman, Master, Responsible Master, Plumbing Inspector)** — Texas State Board of Plumbing Examiners (TSBPE), under Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1301 [8][9].
- **Structural steel erection and structural welding** — **no state-issued contractor license**; competency is established by third-party certifications (AISC Certified Erector, AWS welder qualification under D1.1 or D1.5), municipal permit requirements, and OSHA-mandated training [10][11].
- **General building / commercial / residential general contracting** — no state license; municipal registration only.

For ContrPro's five trades, the practical consequence is that **the same project in Texas may require zero state licenses (a steel erection job in unincorporated land), one state license (a stand-alone HVAC change-out), or four state licenses** (a tenant-finish job that includes HVAC, electrical, plumbing, and structural steel) — plus one or more municipal registrations for the prime general contractor.

### 1.2 Air conditioning and refrigeration (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1302)

**Statutory authority:** Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1302 (the Air Conditioning and Refrigeration Contractor License Law) [4]. Administrative rules at **16 Texas Administrative Code Chapter 75** [5].

**Licensing agency:** Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR), Air Conditioning and Refrigeration Contractors Program, P.O. Box 12157, Austin, TX 78711; (800) 803-9202.

**License classes** [5][12]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Class A** | Air-conditioning and refrigeration systems of **any size** |
| **Class B** | Cooling systems **25 tons or less** and heating systems **1.5 million BTU/hour or less** |

**Endorsements** (a license must carry one of three endorsements, and only one) [5][12]:

| Endorsement | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Environmental Air Conditioning** | Comfort cooling for human occupancy |
| **Commercial Refrigeration** | Refrigeration of perishables, ice-making, walk-in coolers |
| **Process Cooling or Heating** | Industrial process cooling/heating (manufacturing, data centers) |

A contractor that does both comfort cooling and commercial refrigeration must hold a license with both endorsements (the endorsements stack on a single license number but each is separately tested).

**Experience requirements** under 16 TAC § 75.20 [5][12]:

- **Route 1:** At least **48 months** of practical experience in air-conditioning and refrigeration work under the supervision of a licensed Texas A/C contractor within the preceding **72 months**.
- **Route 2:** A current TDLR-issued ACR Technician Certification held for at least 12 months, plus at least **36 months** of supervised practical experience within the preceding 48 months.

**Examination:** Open-book, computer-based PSI exam. Passing score **70%**. The exam covers the Texas Plumbing License Law analog for HVAC (Tex. Occ. Code Ch. 1302 and 16 TAC Ch. 75), the International Mechanical Code, the International Fuel Gas Code, refrigerant-handling EPA Section 608, and the Texas Penal Code provisions on unlicensed practice [12][13].

**Fees** [12][14]:
- Application fee: **$115**
- Renewal fee: $115 (annual)

**Insurance — mandatory minimums** under 16 TAC § 75.100(c) [5][12]. The contractor must maintain commercial general liability insurance from a Texas-authorized insurer:

| Class | Per Occurrence | Aggregate | Products/Completed Ops |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Class A** | **$300,000** | **$600,000** | **$300,000** |
| **Class B** | **$100,000** | **$200,000** | **$100,000** |

The certificate of insurance must list TDLR as the certificate holder. **No surety bond is required at the state level**; municipal bonds may apply.

**Renewal cycle:** **1 year** (annual) — Texas is one of the few states that renews HVAC licenses annually rather than biennially [12][14].

**Continuing education:** **8 hours per year**, of which **1 hour** must be in Texas laws and rules governing A/C contractors (16 TAC § 75.65) [13][15]. The remaining 7 hours must be in TDLR-approved technical courses.

**Penalty for unlicensed practice:** Civil administrative penalty up to **$5,000 per day, per violation** (Tex. Occ. Code § 51.302). Criminal penalty: Class A misdemeanor for engaging in air conditioning contracting without a license (Tex. Occ. Code § 1302.605).

### 1.3 Electrical (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1305)

**Statutory authority:** Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1305 (the Texas Electrical Safety and Licensing Act) [6]. Administrative rules at **16 Texas Administrative Code Chapter 73** [7].

**Licensing agency:** TDLR, Electrical Safety and Licensing Program, P.O. Box 12157, Austin, TX 78711; (800) 803-9202.

**License categories** under 16 TAC § 73.20 [6][7][16]:

| License | Experience | Exam | Practical Scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Master Electrician** | **12,000 hours** OJT plus at least **2 years as a licensed Texas Journeyman Electrician** | Master exam (PSI) | Supervises all electrical work; required as the "master of record" for an Electrical Contractor license |
| **Journeyman Electrician** | **8,000 hours** of supervised OJT under a Master | Journeyman exam (PSI) | Performs electrical work under master's supervision |
| **Master Sign Electrician** | **12,000 hours** OJT in electrical sign work plus 2 years as Journeyman Sign Electrician | Master Sign exam | Master of record for Electrical Sign Contractor license |
| **Journeyman Sign Electrician** | **8,000 hours** OJT under a Master Sign Electrician | Journeyman Sign exam | Performs electrical sign work |
| **Journeyman Industrial Electrician** | **8,000 hours** OJT in an industrial facility | Journeyman Industrial exam | Restricted to a single industrial facility |
| **Residential Wireman** | **4,000 hours** OJT on one-family or two-family dwellings | Residential Wireman exam | One- and two-family dwellings only |
| **Residential Appliance Installer** | **4,000 hours** OJT plus 24 hours of classroom instruction | Residential Appliance Installer exam | Installs residential appliances; no general electrical work |
| **Maintenance Electrician** | Employment by a single owner | No exam (employer attestation) | Limited to maintenance for one employer |
| **Apprentice Electrician** | None; must be registered | No exam | Works only under direct supervision of a Master or Journeyman |
| **Electrical Contractor** (business license) | Must designate a licensed Master Electrician of record | No separate exam for business; master must hold license | Engages in the business of electrical contracting |
| **Electrical Sign Contractor** (business license) | Must designate a Master Sign Electrician of record | No separate exam for business | Engages in electrical sign contracting |
| **Residential Appliance Installation Contractor** (business license) | Designated Master Electrician or Residential Appliance Installer of record | No separate exam | Engages in residential appliance installation business |

**Fees** [6][16]:
- Master Electrician application: **$45** + PSI exam fee (~$74)
- Journeyman Electrician application: $30 + PSI exam fee
- Electrical Contractor (business): **$115** application; annual renewal
- Residential Wireman application: $30
- Apprentice registration: $20

**Insurance — Electrical Contractor business license** under 16 TAC § 73.81 [7][16]: Commercial general liability of **$300,000 per occurrence / $600,000 aggregate / $300,000 products and completed operations**, with TDLR as certificate holder. **No surety bond required at the state level**; municipal bonds may apply.

**Continuing education** under 16 TAC § 73.80 [7][17]: **4 hours per year** for renewal. The 4 hours must include instruction on the National Electrical Code (NEC), the Texas Electrical Safety and Licensing Act (Tex. Occ. Code Ch. 1305), the Texas Electrician Administrative Rules (16 TAC Ch. 73), and Electrical Safety (NFPA 70E).

**Renewal cycle:** **1 year** (annual) [6][16].

**Penalty for unlicensed practice:** Civil administrative penalty up to **$5,000 per day, per violation** (Tex. Occ. Code § 51.302). Engaging in electrical contracting without a license is also a separate Class B misdemeanor under Tex. Occ. Code § 1305.201 if the contractor performs work for which a license is required.

### 1.4 Plumbing (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1301)

**Statutory authority:** Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1301 (the Plumbing License Law) [8].

**Licensing agency:** **Texas State Board of Plumbing Examiners (TSBPE)** — note that plumbing is **not regulated by TDLR**, but by an independent agency. TSBPE: P.O. Box 4200, Austin, TX 78765-4200; (512) 936-5200; tsbpe.texas.gov.

**License types** [8][9][18][19]:

| License | Experience | Exam | Scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Plumber's Apprentice** (Registration, not license) | None — registered before counting any plumbing hours | None | Works under direct supervision of a Master or Journeyman; required to count hours toward Tradesman or Journeyman |
| **Tradesman Plumber-Limited** | **4,000 hours** as a registered Apprentice | Tradesman exam (Pearson VUE) | Plumbing on one- and two-family dwellings (limited scope) |
| **Journeyman Plumber** | **8,000 hours** of plumbing experience as an Apprentice or Tradesman, under a Responsible Master Plumber | Journeyman exam (Pearson VUE) | Full plumbing work under supervision of Master |
| **Master Plumber** | **2 years** as a licensed Texas Journeyman (or another state) — **reduced from 4 years effective September 1, 2025 by Senate Bill 947 of the 89th Legislature** | Master exam (Pearson VUE) | Performs and supervises plumbing work |
| **Responsible Master Plumber (RMP)** | Master Plumber license; designated as the responsible master for a business | None — must designate as RMP | Required for any business to engage in plumbing work in Texas; one RMP per business |
| **Plumbing Inspector** | Journeyman license held for at least 12 months | Plumbing Inspector exam | Performs plumbing inspections for political subdivisions |

**Endorsements** available to Journeyman, Master, RMP, and Plumbing Inspector licensees (each requires separate exam and CE) [9][18]:

| Endorsement | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Medical Gas Piping Installation** | Installation, repair of medical gas systems (oxygen, nitrous, vacuum) |
| **Multipurpose Residential Fire Protection Sprinkler Specialist** | Combined residential fire-sprinkler/domestic water systems |
| **Water Supply Protection Specialist** | Backflow prevention, cross-connection control, customer service inspections |

**Fees** [9][18]:
- Tradesman Plumber-Limited application: $36; certificate/pocket card: $35
- Journeyman application: $40; exam processing $25; Pearson VUE exam $128.50; initial license $75; annual renewal **$75**
- Master Plumber application/exam processing $25; Pearson VUE exam $128.50; initial license $75; annual renewal **$75**

**Insurance — Responsible Master Plumber (RMP)** under Tex. Occ. Code § 1301.3576 [20]: An RMP **must maintain commercial general liability insurance of at least $300,000 per claim**, with TSBPE as certificate holder. If a political subdivision requires a bond for plumbing permits, the subdivision **must accept a $300,000 GL certificate in lieu of a bond** under § 1301.3576(c) [20].

**Renewal cycle:** **1 year** (annual) for all plumbing licenses except Plumbing Inspector (annual January renewal) [9][18].

**Continuing professional education (CPE):** **6 hours per year** for Tradesman, Journeyman, Master, RMP. Each endorsement adds additional CPE: Medical Gas Piping requires **2 hours**; Multipurpose Residential Fire Protection Sprinkler Specialist and Water Supply Protection Specialist each require additional hours [9][18].

**Penalty for unlicensed practice:** Tex. Occ. Code § 1301.701 — Class A misdemeanor for engaging in plumbing without a license. Administrative penalty up to **$5,000 per day, per violation**.

### 1.5 Structural steel — no state license

Texas **does not issue a state contractor license for structural steel erection** [10][11]. Competency and quality control are established through:

1. **AISC Certified Erector / AISC Certified Fabricator** — quality-management certifications administered by the American Institute of Steel Construction. Required by many private specifications and most public-works contracts of significant scale.
2. **AWS welder qualification** — individual welder qualifications under AWS D1.1 (structural steel) or AWS D1.5 (bridge welding); Texas Department of Transportation specifications (TxDOT Standard Specification Item 448) require D1.5 qualifications for state-bridge welding [10].
3. **AWS Certified Welding Inspector (CWI)** — required for inspection on most public and large private projects.
4. **OSHA 10/30** — required by many GCs and public-works specifications; not state-mandated but ubiquitous as a contract requirement.
5. **Municipal permits** — structural permits issued by the city or county; no state-issued license for structural erection.

A structural steel contractor in Texas may operate **with no state-issued license at all** as long as the contractor (a) does not engage in electrical, HVAC, plumbing, or boiler-tube welding regulated under another statute, and (b) holds the project-specific certifications required by the contract.

### 1.6 General contracting — municipal registration matrix

There is **no state license for general contracting in Texas**. Municipal registration requirements vary widely.

**Houston** [21]: **No general contractor registration required**. Permits are issued job-by-job at the Houston Permitting Center, 1002 Washington Avenue. The Building Code Enforcement enforces the 2021-cycle City of Houston Construction Codes for residential and commercial construction (as of 2026). Specialty trades (electrical, plumbing, HVAC) must register with the city before pulling trade permits, but the GC itself does not register.

**Dallas** [22]: **Annual contractor registration required** with the Building Inspection Division of the Sustainable Development and Construction Department. **Registration fee $120/year**. Required of all GCs, residential repair providers, concrete/asphalt/paving contractors, demolition, fence, foundation, gas-well, landscape, pool, roof, and tree-service contractors. Must show proof of commercial general liability insurance and a Texas Sales and Use Tax Permit. Submission to Oak Cliff Municipal Center, 320 E. Jefferson Blvd., Room 118, Dallas, TX 75203.

**San Antonio** [23]: **Two distinct registration types** through the Development Services Department, 1901 S. Alamo:
- **Residential Building Contractor** — for new residential construction, structural alterations/remodeling of existing homes, structural additions, foundation repairs, and detached accessory buildings > 400 sq ft. Renewal cost **$170**; valid **2 years**. Initial in-person registration required.
- **Home Improvement Contractor** — for non-structural remodeling, conversions, modernizations, and detached accessory buildings ≤ 400 sq ft. Renewal cost **$150**; valid 2 years.

Both classes require commercial general liability insurance of **$300,000 per occurrence / $600,000 aggregate / $300,000 products and completed ops**, with the City of San Antonio listed as certificate holder.

**Other Texas cities** (Austin, Fort Worth, El Paso, Arlington, Plano, Corpus Christi) generally require municipal registration with varying fees, insurance minimums, and renewal cycles. **Every Texas job requires an independent municipal-registration check at the local building department before bidding.**

### 1.7 Workers' compensation — the Texas opt-out

**Texas is the only U.S. state in which a private employer may elect not to subscribe to workers' compensation insurance** [24][25][26]. An employer that does not subscribe to workers' comp is a **non-subscriber** ("non-sub"). Subscription is regulated under the Texas Labor Code, Title 5 (Workers' Compensation), and administered by the **Texas Department of Insurance, Division of Workers' Compensation (TDI-DWC)** [24].

**Non-subscriber obligations** [24][25]:

1. **Filing requirement.** A non-subscriber with one or more non-exempt employees must file **TDI-DWC Form 005, Employer Notice of No Coverage**, within **30 days** of hiring its first employee, within **10 days** of terminating coverage, and **between February 1 and April 30** of each calendar year [24].
2. **Notice to employees.** The employer must post in the workplace, in English, Spanish, and any other language as needed, a notice that the employer is not covered by workers' compensation. The notice must be visible to employees regularly. A written notice of no coverage must also be given to each new hire.
3. **Loss of the exclusive-remedy defense.** A subscriber is shielded from employee tort suits by the exclusive-remedy doctrine of Tex. Lab. Code § 408.001. A **non-subscriber loses this defense entirely** and is exposed to civil suit by an injured employee, in which the employer is also barred from asserting contributory negligence, assumption of risk, or fellow-servant defenses (Tex. Lab. Code § 406.033) [24][25].

**Construction-specific rules** [24][27]:

- **State public works.** Any contractor or subcontractor performing work on a project of a **governmental entity** in Texas must carry workers' compensation insurance for all employees on the project under **Tex. Lab. Code § 406.096**. Non-subscription is **not available** on government work [24][27].
- **Private commercial works.** Subscription is not statutorily mandated, but **virtually every commercial owner and general contractor in Texas contractually requires** its prime and subs to carry workers' compensation as a condition of contract award. The General Conditions of every major Texas commercial owner (HOK clients, real-estate trusts, hospital systems, school districts) include this requirement. ContrPro should treat WC as a de facto requirement for any commercial work, regardless of statutory non-subscription rights.
- **Sole proprietors and corporate officers.** Even on jobs that don't strictly require WC, a sole proprietor or corporate officer with no employees may file a non-subscriber notice to operate without a policy — but exposure to tort suit by independent contractors and on-site personnel is significant.

There is **no penalty for being a non-subscriber per se**, but failure to file the required Form 005 carries a civil administrative penalty up to **$25,000** under TDI rules [24].

### 1.8 General liability insurance — no statewide statutory minimum for general contracting

Texas imposes **no statewide minimum** general liability coverage requirement for general contracting (the absence-of-state-license point above). GL minimums arise from (a) state-trade-license rules — $300,000/$600,000 for HVAC Class A, electrical contractor, and RMP plumbing; (b) municipal registration rules (e.g., San Antonio's $300,000/$600,000/$300,000); and (c) individual project-level requirements set by owners, primes, and lenders.

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — Texas Property Code Chapter 53

Texas mechanic's lien law is at **Texas Property Code Chapter 53** [28][29]. The statute was **comprehensively rewritten by House Bill 2237 (Acts 2021, 87th Leg., Ch. 690), effective January 1, 2022, and applies to original contracts executed on or after that date** [30][31][32]. **Senate Bill 929 of the 89th Legislature (2025) further amended the deadline-calculation rules**, effective May 21, 2025 — see §5.1 below [33][34].

### 2.1 Who can claim a lien

Texas recognizes **two lien regimes**:

1. **Constitutional lien** under **Article XVI, Section 37 of the Texas Constitution** [35][36]: "Mechanics, artisans and material men, of every class, shall have a lien upon the buildings and articles made or repaired by them for the value of their labor done thereon, or material furnished therefor." This lien is **self-executing** — it arises automatically when an original contractor in direct privity with the owner furnishes labor or materials, with **no filing or notice requirement**. The trade-offs are significant: (a) only the **original contractor** has constitutional lien rights; subcontractors and suppliers have no constitutional lien; and (b) the constitutional lien is **valid only against the owner and parties with actual notice** — it does **not bind subsequent bona fide purchasers** of the property, which is fatal in any real-estate-financed project [35][36].

2. **Statutory lien** under Chapter 53 [29]: Available to original contractors, subcontractors of any tier, material suppliers, equipment lessors, design professionals (architects, engineers, registered surveyors) [37][38], and laborers. The statutory lien requires strict adherence to notice and filing deadlines but **binds subsequent purchasers and lenders** once properly filed in the county property records. Sophisticated lien claimants always perfect both: they hold a constitutional lien automatically and file the statutory lien affidavit to bind subsequent purchasers.

**HB 2237 expanded subcontractor scope:** Pre-2022, "subcontractor" was defined to extend only one tier below the original contractor. As amended by HB 2237 in § 53.001, "subcontractor" now includes any person "who furnishes labor or materials to fulfill an obligation to an original contractor or to a subcontractor of any tier" — eliminating the prior-existing gap that locked out sub-sub-subcontractors [30][31][32].

**Design professionals.** HB 2237 also extended statutory lien rights to architects, engineers, and registered surveyors who furnish a design or survey "in connection with the construction" — eliminating the prior requirement of direct contractual privity with the owner [30][31].

### 2.2 Monthly pre-lien notice — the perennial Texas trap

The Texas pre-lien notice regime is the most-litigated trap in Texas construction collections [30][31][39][40]. **Every month of unpaid labor or materials requires its own notice** — failure to send the monthly notice forfeits lien rights as to that month's work, even if subsequent months' notices are perfect.

**Statutory authority:** Tex. Prop. Code § 53.056 (Derivative Claimant: Notice to Owner and Original Contractor) [41].

**Deadlines** (HB 2237 streamlined what had been a two-notice scheme into a single notice) [30][31][39]:

| Project type | Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Commercial (non-residential)** | The **15th day of the third month** after each month in which the labor or materials were provided |
| **Residential** | The **15th day of the second month** after each month in which the labor or materials were provided |

**Trap.** A subcontractor that performs work in January, February, and March on a commercial project must send a January notice by April 15, a February notice by May 15, and a March notice by June 15. Sending a single "rollup" notice on June 15 perfects only the March lien and forfeits January and February.

**Service methods** under Tex. Prop. Code § 53.003 (as amended by HB 2237) [30][31][39]:

1. **In-person service**
2. **USPS Certified Mail** (return receipt requested)
3. **Other traceable forms of private mail** (FedEx, UPS, traceable courier)

**HB 2237 eliminated USPS Registered Mail** as a permitted method [30][31] — many practitioners' templates still reference Registered Mail and are technically non-compliant. **Email and fax are not authorized methods of service.**

**Content of notice** under Tex. Prop. Code § 53.056(d): date, project description and address, claimant's name and contact information, type of labor or materials provided, original contractor's name, claim amount, and party with whom claimant contracted (if different from original contractor). Claimants may attach invoices [41].

**Fund-trapping language.** To trigger the owner's duty to withhold under § 53.081 et seq. (the "fund-trapping" remedy), the notice **must additionally state** that the owner is on notice and that the claim may result in a lien on the property and personal liability if the owner does not withhold payment from the original contractor [42][43]. Without this fund-trapping language, the notice perfects the lien but does not trap funds.

### 2.3 Notice for specially fabricated items

A claimant supplying **specially fabricated material** (custom-fabricated items that cannot be readily resold elsewhere — typically custom millwork, fabricated steel, specialty HVAC ductwork) must give a separate notice under **Tex. Prop. Code § 53.058** [44]:

- **Deadline:** **15th day of the second month** after the month in which the claimant receives and accepts the order.
- **Service:** USPS certified mail.
- **Content:** Statement that the order has been received and accepted, and the price of the order.
- **Additional § 53.056 notice still required:** If delivery has been made or the normal delivery time has passed, the claimant must **also** send the § 53.056 monthly notice [44].

For a structural-steel fabricator working under ContrPro's typical scope, this means **two notices**: a § 53.058 fabricated-items notice when the order is accepted, plus the § 53.056 monthly notices as fabrication and delivery progress.

### 2.4 Filing the lien affidavit — § 53.052 deadlines

Once notices are in order, the lien claimant must file a sworn **lien affidavit** with the county clerk in the county where the property is located under **Tex. Prop. Code § 53.052** [45][46]:

| Claimant | Project type | Filing deadline |
|---|---|---|
| **Original contractor** | Commercial | **15th day of the fourth month** after the month in which the original contractor's work was completed, terminated, or abandoned |
| **Original contractor** | Residential | **15th day of the third month** after the month in which the work was completed, terminated, or abandoned |
| **Other claimant** (subcontractor, supplier, design professional) | Commercial | **15th day of the fourth month** after the later of (a) the month the claimant last provided labor/materials, or (b) the month specially fabricated materials should have been delivered |
| **Other claimant** | Residential | **15th day of the third month** after the same triggering events |
| **Any claimant — retainage** | All projects | **15th day of the third month** after the month in which the original contract was completed, terminated, or abandoned |

**Contents of the affidavit (§ 53.054)** [29][45]: sworn statement of the claim amount, name and address of claimant, name and address of owner, description of labor or materials, description of property sufficient for identification, name of the person by whom the claimant was employed, signature, notarization.

**Service of the affidavit (§ 53.055):** The claimant must send a copy of the recorded affidavit to the owner and (if claimant is a subcontractor) the original contractor by certified mail within **5 business days** of filing.

**Homestead — additional requirements under § 53.254** [47]: To fix a lien on a homestead, **the contract must be in writing, executed before any labor or materials are furnished, signed by both spouses if the owner is married, and filed with the county clerk** before work begins. The lien affidavit must include a conspicuous **10-point boldface notice** at the top of the page reading: **"NOTICE: THIS IS NOT A LIEN. THIS IS ONLY AN AFFIDAVIT CLAIMING A LIEN."** Failure of any of these prerequisites voids the lien on the homestead — homestead lien defects are the most common reason a Texas residential lien is unenforceable [47].

### 2.5 Statutory retainage — § 53.101

Under **Texas Property Code § 53.101**, the owner **must reserve** the following retainage during the work and for **30 days after** the work is completed [48]:

- **10 percent of the contract price** of the work to the owner, or
- **10 percent of the value of the work**, measured by the proportion of work done to work to be done.

The 10% retainage is a **statutory liability, not merely a contract right** — even if the owner-prime contract specifies a lower retainage, the **owner remains personally liable** to subcontractors and suppliers up to the 10% statutory floor under § 53.105 if the owner fails to reserve funds and a subcontractor perfects a retainage claim. This is independent of any contractual retainage that the owner-prime contract may impose, and applies on every Texas commercial project [48][49][50].

**Retainage lien claim:** A claimant filing a retainage lien must send a § 53.057 notice (specific retainage notice form) and file the lien affidavit by the **15th day of the third month** after completion, termination, or abandonment of the original contract [29][51].

**Affidavit of Completion (§ 53.106):** An owner may record an Affidavit of Completion to fix the completion date for retainage-lien purposes. The owner must serve a copy on each original contractor within 30 days of recording. The Affidavit of Completion is **conclusive evidence of the date of completion** for purposes of statutory retainage and lien deadlines unless contested [52][53].

### 2.6 Lien foreclosure — one-year deadline (commercial) and two-year for residential

Once recorded, the lien must be enforced by suit within a deadline that **HB 2237 shortened** for commercial projects [30][31][54]:

- **Commercial:** Suit to foreclose must be filed within **one (1) year** after the last day on which the claimant could have filed the lien affidavit under § 53.052 (i.e., one year after the § 53.052 filing deadline) [54].
- **Residential:** Suit must be filed within **one year** after the last day on which the claimant could have filed the lien affidavit [54].
- **Extension:** Either deadline may be extended by **written agreement between the claimant and owner, recorded in the county property records**, up to a total of **two (2) years** [54].

Missing the suit deadline **extinguishes the lien by operation of law**, and the property owner may file a motion to remove the lien from the property records under § 53.160.

### 2.7 Texas Construction Trust Fund Act — the criminal hammer

Texas is one of only a handful of states that imposes **personal criminal liability** on individual contractors and officers for misapplying construction payments. The Texas Construction Trust Fund Act is at **Texas Property Code Chapter 162** [55][56][57][58]. **For ContrPro contractors, this is the single most important statute outside the lien law itself — it transforms what would otherwise be a civil debt into individual criminal exposure for the contractor's owner or officer.**

**§ 162.001 — Trust funds:** Construction payments paid to a contractor or subcontractor under a construction contract — and loan receipts from a construction loan — are **trust funds**, held for the benefit of the artisans, laborers, mechanics, contractors, subcontractors, and materialmen who furnished the labor or material [55][56].

**§ 162.002 — Trustees:** The contractor, subcontractor, owner, officer, director, or agent of a contractor or subcontractor who **receives trust funds** or **has control or direction of trust funds** is a **trustee** of those funds. The individual is **personally liable** as a trustee — corporate-form protections do not shield officers and directors who controlled the disbursement of trust funds [55][57][58].

**§ 162.003 — Beneficiaries:** Artisans, laborers, mechanics, contractors, subcontractors, and materialmen who labor or furnish labor or material for construction or repair of an improvement on real property are **beneficiaries** of trust funds. Design professionals (architects, engineers, surveyors) are also beneficiaries [55].

**§ 162.031 — Misapplication offense:** A trustee who, **intentionally, knowingly, or with intent to defraud, directly or indirectly retains, uses, disburses, or otherwise diverts trust funds without first fully paying** all current or past-due obligations to beneficiaries, **misapplies trust funds** [55][56][57].

**Affirmative defense:** A trustee may retain trust funds **used to pay the trustee's actual expenses directly related to the construction or repair of the improvement** (§ 162.031(b)). The Texas Supreme Court has interpreted "actual expenses" narrowly — overhead and general administrative costs do **not** qualify [57][58]. Bonded retainage and amounts subject to good-faith dispute may also be retained.

**§ 162.032 — Criminal penalties** [55][56][57]:

| Offense | Threshold | Grade | Maximum penalty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Misapplication of trust funds | $500 or more | **Class A misdemeanor** | Up to **1 year** in county jail and a fine up to **$4,000** |
| Misapplication of trust funds with intent to defraud | $500 or more | **Third-degree felony** | **2 to 10 years** in state prison and a fine up to **$10,000** |
| Failure to establish or maintain a construction account (where required) | n/a | Class A misdemeanor | Up to 1 year in jail and a fine up to $4,000 |

**Civil liability:** In addition to criminal exposure, beneficiaries may sue the individual trustee personally for misapplied amounts under a civil trust-fund claim. The trustee is **personally liable, both civilly and criminally**, regardless of the corporate form of the contracting entity [57][58]. Texas courts have repeatedly upheld piercing of the corporate veil in trust-fund cases against the controlling officer or owner.

**Construction account requirement (§ 162.006).** For residential construction contracts of more than $5,000 (excluding the original homebuilder), the contractor must maintain a **separate construction account** at a financial institution and must keep records sufficient to show source and disposition of trust funds. Failure to do so is a separate Class A misdemeanor under § 162.032 [55].

**Senate Bill 841 (89th Leg., 2025) — qualified-assignee amendment:** Effective **September 1, 2025**, SB 841 amended Chapter 162 to permit a "qualified assignee" of trust-fund rights to also be a beneficiary, provided the assignment is in writing, made after payment is received, and written notice reaches the property owner and contractor within seven (7) days of the assignment [59][60]. This is technical and primarily affects factoring and assignment arrangements; it does not change the underlying criminal-liability structure.

### 2.8 Subcontractor's right to bond around lien

Texas allows a property owner to **remove a lien from the property** by filing a bond for **150% of the lien amount** in the county property records under **Tex. Prop. Code § 53.171–53.175** [29]. Once the bond is filed and notice given to the lien claimant, the lien attaches to the bond rather than the property. The bond procedure is heavily used by owners with title-closing or financing pressure who need the property cleared of liens; the claimant's remedies shift from foreclosure to a suit against the bond.

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

Texas's prompt-payment regime is split across **three separate statutes** with overlapping coverage and different penalty structures. Every payment relationship lands somewhere in the matrix below.

### 3.1 Private works — Texas Property Code Chapter 28 (the Prompt Payment Act)

**Statutory authority:** Texas Property Code Chapter 28 [61][62][63].

**Owner-to-prime payment.** Under **Tex. Prop. Code § 28.002(a)**, an owner must pay the original contractor **the undisputed portion of an invoice for properly performed work within 35 days** after the owner receives a written request for payment [61][62][63]. The owner may withhold from progress payments any disputed amount, but only up to **110% of the disputed amount** under § 28.003 (or 100% on commercial; see §3.4 below).

**Prime-to-subcontractor payment.** Under **Tex. Prop. Code § 28.002(b)**, the prime contractor must pay each subcontractor or supplier the proportionate share of the owner's payment, less retainage, **within 7 days** after the prime receives payment from the owner. The 7-day requirement cascades down: each subcontractor that receives payment must pay its own sub-subcontractors within 7 days [61][62][63].

**Interest on unpaid amounts.** Under **Tex. Prop. Code § 28.004**, interest accrues on overdue amounts at **1.5% per month** (effective **18% per annum**), beginning the day after the payment becomes overdue and continuing until the payment is made [61][62][63].

**Right to suspend work — § 28.009.** A contractor or subcontractor that has not received payment of an undisputed amount within the statutory deadline may give the owner (or, for a sub, the contractor that owes payment) at least **10 days' written notice** of intent to suspend. If payment is not received within the 10-day notice period, the contractor or subcontractor may **suspend performance without breach of contract** and may recover reasonable costs incurred to suspend and remobilize once payment is made [61][63]. The right to suspend does not apply to residential projects of four or fewer units.

**Attorney's fees.** The prevailing party in a Chapter 28 prompt-payment action may recover **reasonable attorney's fees and costs** under § 28.005 [61][62].

### 3.2 Public works — Texas Government Code Chapter 2251

**Statutory authority:** Texas Government Code Chapter 2251 (Payment for Goods and Services) [64][65][66]. Applies to payments by **state agencies** (e.g., Texas Facilities Commission, Texas Department of Transportation) and **political subdivisions** (counties, cities, school districts, water districts).

**Owner-to-prime payment.** Under **Tex. Gov't Code § 2251.021**, payment by a governmental entity is **overdue on the 31st day** after the later of (a) the date the entity receives the goods, (b) the date services are completed, or (c) the date the entity receives a correct invoice [64][66]. Certain governmental entities that meet less frequently than monthly have a 46-day window under § 2251.021(b).

**Prime-to-subcontractor payment.** Under **Tex. Gov't Code § 2251.022**, a vendor receiving payment from a governmental entity must pay its subcontractors and suppliers the proportionate amount **within 10 days** of receipt. The 10-day cascade applies down the chain [64][65][66].

**Interest on overdue payment.** Under **Tex. Gov't Code § 2251.025**, the interest rate on overdue governmental payment is the **Wall Street Journal prime rate as of the first non-weekend day of July of the preceding fiscal year, plus 1 percent**, set annually as of September 1 [64][67]. Interest accrues from the day after overdue until the payment is mailed or electronically transmitted.

**Right to suspend.** Under **Tex. Gov't Code § 2251.051**, a vendor that has not received payment of an undisputed amount may suspend performance after giving **at least 10 days' written notice** to the governmental entity. For highway contracts and certain other classes, the vendor must wait until the 20th day before suspending.

**HB 3005 (89th Leg., 2025) — bona-fide-dispute clarification.** Effective for contracts entered on or after **September 1, 2025**, HB 3005 amended Tex. Gov't Code Chapter 2251 to clarify that an **audit of a public-works project that extends more than 60 days after substantial completion is not a bona-fide dispute** for purposes of withholding payment. The amendment closes a loophole in which public entities had been deferring undisputed payments indefinitely by characterizing post-completion audits as ongoing "disputes" [68][69][70].

### 3.3 Pay-if-paid clauses — Texas Business and Commerce Code Chapter 56

**Statutory authority:** Texas Business and Commerce Code Chapter 56 (Agreement for Payment of Construction Subcontractor) [71][72].

Texas **permits pay-if-paid clauses** in subcontracts, but Chapter 56 imposes strict limits on their enforceability [71][72]:

1. **Subcontractor objection (§ 56.054).** After **45 days** from submitting a written request for payment, a contingent payee (subcontractor) may serve written notice on the contingent payor objecting to further enforcement of the pay-if-paid clause. After the objection notice, the clause is **unenforceable** as to amounts due and accruing.
2. **Contractor non-performance.** If the reason the owner has not paid the prime is the prime's own non-performance, the prime cannot rely on the pay-if-paid clause to withhold payment from a sub.
3. **Unconscionability (§ 56.054(a)).** If application of the clause would be **unconscionable**, the clause is unenforceable.
4. **Failure to provide information.** If the prime fails to provide the sub with requested information on the status of payment from the owner, the clause is unenforceable.

**Chapter 56 does not regulate pay-when-paid clauses** (clauses that merely set a timing reference but still require eventual payment regardless of owner payment) — those clauses are governed by general contract law and are generally enforceable as timing-only provisions.

**HB 2960 (89th Leg., 2025) — venue clauses.** Effective for construction contracts entered on or after **September 1, 2025**, HB 2960 amended Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 272.001 to make **out-of-state venue selection clauses in Texas construction contracts void rather than merely voidable**. Any action for a Texas project must proceed in the Texas county where the property is located. This is consequential for primes that previously relied on Delaware, New York, or California forum-selection clauses against Texas subs [73][74].

### 3.4 Good-faith dispute withholding

Both Chapter 28 (private) and Chapter 2251 (public) allow withholding amounts subject to a **good-faith dispute** [61][64]:

- **Residential private works:** Owner may withhold up to **110% of the disputed amount** under § 28.003.
- **Commercial private works:** Owner may withhold **100% of the disputed amount** (no 10% cushion).
- **Public works:** Governmental entity may withhold disputed amounts; must notify the contractor of the dispute within **21 days** of receiving the invoice (Tex. Gov't Code § 2251.042).

A "good-faith dispute" requires a reasonable basis tied to performance of the disputed work — pretextual withholding triggers Chapter 28 penalty exposure.

### 3.5 Retainage on private works — 10% statutory floor

Texas retainage on private works is governed by **Tex. Prop. Code §§ 53.101–53.108** [29][48][49][50]:

- **Statutory retainage (§ 53.101):** Owner must reserve **10% of the contract price** or **10% of the value of the work** during construction and for **30 days after completion**. This is a **statutory floor** — even if the owner-prime contract specifies a lower retainage (or no retainage), the owner remains personally liable to subcontractors up to 10% under § 53.105 [48][49][50].
- **No statewide cap on contractual retainage.** Unlike California (5% on private works since SB 61) or Tennessee (5% on most public works), **Texas does not cap private-works retainage**. The contract may set retainage at 10%, 15%, or higher; the statutory 10% is a floor, not a ceiling [29][49].
- **Owner liability for failure to reserve (§ 53.105):** If the owner does not reserve the 10% as required, the owner is personally liable to subcontractor lien claimants up to the amount that should have been reserved.
- **Retainage release timing:** Release is generally due 30 days after final completion, but the precise mechanic is contract-driven within the Chapter 53 retainage-lien framework — the subcontractor's remedy if retainage is wrongfully held is to file a retainage lien under § 53.057 by the 15th day of the third month after completion.

### 3.6 Retainage on public works — Tex. Gov't Code Chapter 2252

For state-public works, **Tex. Gov't Code § 2252.032** caps retainage on public-works contracts at **5% of the contract amount** for contracts exceeding $400,000, with further restrictions on contracts above $5 million [75]. Retainage on lower-value contracts is governed by the public entity's procurement rules.

## 4. Tax structure for contractors

### 4.1 State and local sales tax rates

- **State sales tax rate:** **6.25%** under **Tex. Tax Code § 151.051** [76][77].
- **Local sales tax:** Cities, counties, transit authorities, and special purpose districts may impose combined local sales tax up to **2.00%** under the **Local Sales and Use Tax Act** (Tex. Tax Code Ch. 321, 322, 323) [76][78].
- **Combined maximum rate:** **8.25%**. As of April 2026, this combined cap is in effect in essentially every major Texas metropolitan area (Houston, Dallas, San Antonio, Austin, Fort Worth, El Paso) [76][77].
- **Administered by:** **Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts** [77].

Texas has **no state income tax** on individuals — a structural advantage for sole-proprietor and pass-through contractors compared with California or Tennessee's Hall income tax (since repealed) [76].

### 4.2 Contractor sales-tax treatment — the lump-sum / separated decision

Texas's contractor sales-tax regime is governed by **Rule 3.291** (34 Tex. Admin. Code § 3.291) and **Comptroller Publication 94-116, Real Property Repair and Remodeling** [79][80][81]. The treatment depends on (a) the type of work (new construction vs. repair/remodel vs. maintenance), (b) the property type (residential vs. nonresidential), and (c) the contract structure (lump-sum vs. separated).

**Threshold concept — three types of work:**

| Work type | Definition |
|---|---|
| **New construction** | Building new structures; completing unfinished structures; initial finish-out of interior or exterior of a structure (Rule 3.291(a)(11)) |
| **Real property repair / remodel** | Rebuilding, replacing, altering, modifying, or upgrading any part of an existing structure |
| **Maintenance** | Scheduled, periodic upkeep that does not extend the useful life or value of the property |

**Threshold concept — property type:**

- **Residential** — family dwellings, including apartments, nursing homes, condominiums, retirement homes. Excludes hotels, motels, and short-term rentals (Rule 3.291(a)(13)) [80].
- **Nonresidential** — hospitals, offices, warehouses, retail, churches, schools, factories, and every other non-dwelling structure.

**Tax treatment matrix:**

| Property | Work type | Labor taxable? | Incorporated materials taxable? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Residential** | New construction | **No** | Yes (contractor pays at purchase if lump-sum) |
| **Residential** | Repair / remodel | **No** | Yes |
| **Residential** | Maintenance | No | Yes |
| **Nonresidential** | New construction | **No** | Yes |
| **Nonresidential** | Repair / remodel | **Yes** | Yes |
| **Nonresidential** | Maintenance | No | Yes |

**The nonresidential repair-and-remodel trap:** Labor on commercial repair and remodel is **fully taxable** at the combined state-plus-local rate. This is unusual — most states do not tax construction labor. ContrPro contractors performing tenant build-outs, retrofits, modifications, additions, restorations, or rebuilds of existing commercial property must collect and remit sales tax on the labor portion of the contract [79][80][81].

**Lump-sum vs. separated contracts:**

- **Lump-sum contract** (Rule 3.291(a)(8)): A single agreed price for both labor and materials, with no separate statement of material prices. **The contractor is the consumer of materials** and pays sales tax on materials at the time of purchase from the supplier. The contractor does **not** collect sales tax from the customer on the lump-sum amount.
- **Separated contract** (Rule 3.291(a)(14)): Labor and materials are separately stated in the contract or invoice. **The contractor is the retailer of the materials** and may purchase materials tax-free under a resale certificate, then **collects sales tax from the customer on the materials portion**. Labor for new construction or residential repair/remodel remains non-taxable; labor for commercial repair/remodel is fully taxable.

**Reclassification trap.** Rule 3.291(b)(2) provides that **simply totaling separately stated charges in the same invoice does not convert a separated contract into a lump-sum contract**. A contract requiring separated invoices is a separated contract. Cost-plus contracts are separated contracts if labor cost is separately stated from incorporated materials [79]. The Comptroller's audit teams routinely re-characterize contracts based on contract intent, not nomenclature.

### 4.3 Use tax on out-of-state materials

Materials purchased out-of-state and brought into Texas for use in a Texas project are subject to **Texas use tax** at the combined state-plus-local rate under **Tex. Tax Code § 151.101** [76][77]. The contractor must file a Texas Consumer Use Tax Return. This is a routine Comptroller audit target for multi-state contractors, particularly steel fabricators and specialty equipment installers who buy from out-of-state mills and manufacturers.

### 4.4 Franchise (margin) tax — Tex. Tax Code Chapter 171

Texas does not impose a corporate income tax in the traditional sense. Instead, every entity organized for profit and doing business in Texas (corporations, LLCs, limited partnerships, professional associations) is subject to the **Texas Franchise Tax**, also called the **margin tax**, under **Tex. Tax Code Chapter 171** [82][83].

**No-tax-due threshold (2026 report year):** **$2,650,000** in annualized total revenue. An entity with revenue at or below this threshold owes **zero franchise tax** but **must still file** a Public Information Report (PIR) or Ownership Information Report (OIR) [82][83]. The threshold was raised from $2,470,000 (2024 and 2025 report years) to $2,650,000 effective for the 2026 report year (filed by May 15, 2026) [83].

**Rate structure** for entities exceeding the no-tax-due threshold [82]:

| Rate | Applies to |
|---|---|
| **0.375%** | Entities primarily engaged in retail or wholesale trade |
| **0.75%** | All other entities (including construction contractors) |
| **0.331%** | Entities that elect the EZ Computation (revenue ≤ $20 million, simplified base) |

**Margin (tax base):** The lesser of (a) 70% of total revenue, (b) total revenue minus cost of goods sold (COGS), (c) total revenue minus compensation, or (d) total revenue minus $1 million.

**Cost-of-goods-sold for contractors:** Texas takes an idiosyncratic position that **labor performed in constructing or improving real property is includable in COGS** for purposes of the margin tax (Tex. Tax Code § 171.1012). This is a significant benefit for labor-heavy construction businesses but is the subject of routine Comptroller-level disputes about which costs qualify.

**Sole proprietorships and general partnerships:** Not subject to the franchise tax. Income is reported on personal federal returns; Texas imposes no personal income tax.

**Filing deadline:** **May 15** of each report year (for the prior calendar year's revenue) [83].

### 4.5 Texas Sales and Use Tax Permit

Any contractor making taxable sales (i.e., any contractor that signs a separated contract for commercial work, or that sells materials at retail, or that performs nonresidential repair/remodel) **must obtain a Texas Sales and Use Tax Permit** from the Comptroller (Tex. Tax Code § 151.202). The permit is free; failure to obtain one is a Class C misdemeanor with a fine up to $500 per day (Tex. Tax Code § 151.703).

## 5. Recent changes (2024–2026)

The Texas Legislature meets in regular session for 140 days every **odd-numbered year**. The most recent regular session — the **89th Legislature (2025)** — ran January 14, 2025, to June 2, 2025; approximately 6,500 bills were filed and ~1,200 passed [84][85].

### 5.1 Senate Bill 929 — lien deadlines extended past weekends/holidays, effective May 21, 2025

**SB 929 (Stats. 2025, 89th Leg.), authored by Sen. Nathan Johnson**, amended Tex. Prop. Code Chapter 53 to clarify that **any deadline under Chapter 53 that falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday is automatically extended to the next day that is not a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday** [33][34][86]. The legislation was signed by Governor Greg Abbott on **May 21, 2025**, with **immediate effect**. The change synchronizes Chapter 53 deadlines with the general deadline-calculation rule in Tex. Gov't Code § 311.014. Prior to SB 929, the Property Code language was ambiguous, and several appellate decisions had refused to extend a § 53.056 monthly-notice deadline that fell on a weekend.

### 5.2 Senate Bill 841 — qualified-assignee amendment to the Construction Trust Fund Act, effective September 1, 2025

**SB 841 (Stats. 2025, 89th Leg.)** amended Tex. Prop. Code Chapter 162 to permit a **qualified assignee** of trust-fund rights to be a beneficiary, provided the assignment is in writing, executed after payment is received, and written notice is given to the property owner and the original contractor within **7 days** [59][60]. SB 841 is intended to clarify and facilitate construction-claim assignments to factors and assignees; it does not change the underlying criminal-liability structure of § 162.031.

### 5.3 Senate Bill 947 — Master Plumber experience requirement reduced from 4 years to 2 years, effective September 1, 2025

**SB 947 (Stats. 2025, 89th Leg.)** amended Tex. Occ. Code Chapter 1301 to **reduce the Journeyman-to-Master experience requirement from four years to two years** [9][18]. The change is effective September 1, 2025. Practical effect: a Journeyman Plumber licensed in 2023 can now sit for the Master Plumber examination in 2025 rather than 2027. This is the most consequential workforce-supply change in Texas plumbing licensure in over a decade and is widely expected to accelerate qualification of Responsible Master Plumbers for plumbing-business operation.

### 5.4 House Bill 2960 — out-of-state venue clauses in construction contracts void, effective September 1, 2025

**HB 2960 (Stats. 2025, 89th Leg.)** amended Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 272.001 to make **out-of-state venue selection clauses in Texas construction contracts void rather than merely voidable** for projects located in Texas [73][74]. Effective for contracts entered on or after **September 1, 2025**. A national prime contractor that previously embedded a Delaware or New York forum clause in its Texas subcontracts must now litigate disputes in the Texas county where the project is located.

### 5.5 House Bill 3005 — public-works audit-as-dispute clarification, effective September 1, 2025

**HB 3005 (Stats. 2025, 89th Leg.)** amended Tex. Gov't Code Chapter 2251 to provide that **an audit of a public-works project that extends more than 60 days past substantial completion is not a bona-fide dispute** for prompt-payment purposes [68][69][70]. Public entities may no longer indefinitely defer payment by characterizing a prolonged post-completion audit as an ongoing "dispute." Effective for contracts entered on or after **September 1, 2025**.

### 5.6 Senate Bill 687 — liability protections extended to professional land surveyors, effective September 1, 2025

**SB 687 (Stats. 2025, 89th Leg.)** amended Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 130.002 to extend to **registered professional land surveyors** the existing limitations on indemnification and duty-to-defend provisions in construction contracts that apply to architects and engineers [87]. Effective September 1, 2025.

### 5.7 House Bill 1922 — government-contractor pre-claim accrual postmark rule, effective September 1, 2025

**HB 1922 (Stats. 2025, 89th Leg.)** clarified the accrual rule for government-contractor pre-claim reports — the cause of action accrues when the pre-claim report is **postmarked by USPS**, not when it is received [88]. Effective September 1, 2025.

### 5.8 Discrepancy resolutions

Two persistent secondary-source errors worth flagging:

- Some third-party "Texas contractor licensing guides" still report a **single notice** to the original contractor and owner regardless of project type. HB 2237 (2021) streamlined to a single monthly notice, but the **residential vs. commercial deadline difference (2nd month vs. 3rd month) remains** under § 53.056. ContrPro must keep separate calendars for residential and commercial work.
- Some commentary continues to reference USPS **Registered Mail** as a permissible Chapter 53 service method. **HB 2237 eliminated Registered Mail** effective January 1, 2022 — only Certified Mail, traceable private mail, or in-person service satisfy § 53.003 [30][31].
- Some published Master Plumber requirement summaries still report a **4-year Journeyman-to-Master experience requirement**. SB 947 reduced this to **2 years effective September 1, 2025** [9].
- Some published treatments of Texas non-subscriber workers' comp obligations omit the **state-project carve-out under Tex. Lab. Code § 406.096**. Construction work on any Texas governmental-entity project requires WC coverage; non-subscription is not available [24][27].

### 5.9 Bills proposed but not enacted (verification cross-reference)

A review of the 89th Legislature bill tracker via legiscan.com and the Texas Legislative Reference Library effective-dates table [84][85] identifies several construction-relevant bills that were filed but did not pass and should not be relied upon:

- Proposed legislation to **cap retainage on private works at 5% statewide** did not advance out of committee. Texas's 10% statutory floor remains the rule.
- Proposed legislation to establish a **statewide general contractor licensing scheme** (under TDLR) was introduced but did not pass. There remains **no Texas state license for general contracting**.
- Proposed legislation to **require workers' compensation for all construction employers** did not pass. Texas remains an opt-out state.

## 6. Practical compliance checklist for a Texas contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**

- Confirm the **state-trade-license** status for each self-performed trade:
  - HVAC — TDLR ACR license (Class A or B; one of three endorsements); GL insurance to TDLR minimums.
  - Electrical — TDLR Master Electrician of record for the Electrical Contractor business license; $300,000/$600,000 GL.
  - Plumbing — TSBPE Responsible Master Plumber of record for the business; $300,000 GL.
  - Structural steel — no state license; verify AISC certification and AWS welder qualifications per contract.
- Confirm **municipal registration** for every city in which the contractor will pull permits:
  - Dallas — annual $120 GC registration with Sustainable Development and Construction Department.
  - San Antonio — Residential Building Contractor or Home Improvement Contractor registration; 2-year cycle; $300,000/$600,000/$300,000 GL.
  - Houston — no general registration; specialty trades register before pulling trade permits.
- Confirm **workers' compensation strategy**: state public works requires WC; commercial private works almost universally requires WC by contract; sole proprietors may file non-subscriber Form 005 with TDI for jobs that don't require WC by contract.
- Confirm the **construction contract is properly drafted for trust-fund compliance**: if work is on a residential project over $5,000, plan to establish a **separate construction account** under Tex. Prop. Code § 162.006.
- **Homestead jobs:** Execute the written contract before any work begins; obtain both spouses' signatures if owner is married; file the contract with the county clerk before furnishing materials or labor (Tex. Prop. Code § 53.254).

**Within 15 days of accepting an order for specially fabricated materials:**

- Send **§ 53.058 notice** to the owner and (if applicable) the original contractor by USPS Certified Mail or traceable private courier — **by the 15th day of the second month after the order is accepted**.

**Every month — the monthly pre-lien notice cycle (Tex. Prop. Code § 53.056):**

- **Commercial:** Send the § 53.056 monthly notice on each month of unpaid work **by the 15th day of the third month** after the month in which the labor or materials were provided.
- **Residential:** Send the § 53.056 notice **by the 15th day of the second month**.
- **Service:** USPS Certified Mail, traceable private courier, or in-person. **Email and fax do not satisfy § 53.003.**
- **Fund-trapping language:** Include the statutory fund-trapping language to trigger the owner's withholding duty under § 53.081 [42].

**During the work — payment cycle:**

- **Private works (Tex. Prop. Code Ch. 28):** Owner pays prime within **35 days** of properly submitted invoice; prime pays subs within **7 days** of receipt from owner. Interest on overdue at **1.5%/month (18% APR)**. Right to suspend after **10 days' written notice** for non-residential.
- **Public works (Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 2251):** Governmental entity pays prime by the **31st day** after the later of receipt of goods/services/correct invoice; prime pays subs within **10 days**. Interest at **WSJ prime + 1%** annually. HB 3005 (effective Sep. 1, 2025) — audits past 60 days past substantial completion do not justify withholding.
- **Pay-if-paid clauses (Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code Ch. 56):** Enforceable but limited — sub may serve objection notice 45 days after written payment request; non-performance and unconscionability defenses preserved.
- **HB 2960 (effective Sep. 1, 2025):** Out-of-state venue clauses in Texas construction contracts are **void**; suit must be filed in the county where the property is located.

**Construction Trust Fund Act (Tex. Prop. Code Ch. 162) — the personal-liability hammer:**

- Every payment received under a construction contract is a **trust fund** held for the benefit of subs, suppliers, laborers, and design professionals.
- **Personal criminal liability** for the officer/director/agent who controls the funds: Class A misdemeanor for misapplication of $500+; **third-degree felony (2–10 years prison)** if with intent to defraud.
- **No corporate-veil protection** — an LLC's manager can be personally indicted.
- **Affirmative defense** for "actual expenses directly related to the construction" — narrow; overhead does not qualify.
- **Residential contracts over $5,000:** Maintain a separate construction account and records (§ 162.006).
- **SB 841 (effective Sep. 1, 2025):** Trust-fund rights may be assigned to a qualified assignee in writing with 7-day notice to owner and original contractor.

**Filing the lien affidavit (Tex. Prop. Code § 53.052):**

- **Commercial — original contractor:** File by the **15th day of the fourth month** after work completed, terminated, or abandoned.
- **Commercial — subcontractor/supplier:** File by the **15th day of the fourth month** after the later of (a) last labor/materials provided, or (b) last delivery of specially fabricated materials.
- **Residential — original contractor:** File by the **15th day of the third month** after work completed, terminated, or abandoned.
- **Residential — subcontractor/supplier:** File by the **15th day of the third month** after the same triggers.
- **Retainage claim:** File by the **15th day of the third month** after the original contract was completed, terminated, or abandoned.
- **Service of recorded affidavit:** Within **5 business days** of filing, send copy to owner (and, if claimant is a sub, also to the original contractor) by certified mail (§ 53.055).
- **SB 929 (effective May 21, 2025):** Deadlines that fall on weekends/holidays extend to the next business day.

**Lien foreclosure (Tex. Prop. Code § 53.158):**

- Commercial and residential: File suit within **one (1) year** after the last day on which the lien affidavit could have been filed under § 53.052.
- Extension by recorded written agreement up to **two (2) years**.

**Statutory retainage (Tex. Prop. Code § 53.101):**

- Owner must reserve **10% of contract price or value of work** during construction and for **30 days after completion**.
- Owner liable to subs up to 10% statutory floor under § 53.105 if reservation fails.
- **No statewide cap on contractual retainage** — Texas private contracts may withhold 10%, 15%, or more.

**Annual / biennial compliance:**

- File and pay **Texas sales/use tax** monthly or quarterly (depending on volume); collect tax on (a) materials in separated contracts, (b) all labor and materials in commercial repair-and-remodel.
- File **Texas Franchise Tax Report** by **May 15** annually. No-tax-due threshold $2,650,000 (2026); rate 0.75% on the standard margin for contractors.
- File **Form 005 non-subscriber notice** between **Feb. 1 and Apr. 30** of each year (if non-subscriber) and post worker notice in workplace.
- Renew TDLR HVAC license **annually** ($115; 8 hours CE).
- Renew TDLR Electrical Contractor license **annually** ($115; 4 hours CE per individual license).
- Renew TSBPE Plumbing licenses **annually** ($75 most types; 6 hours CPE; endorsement CPE).
- Renew Dallas GC registration **annually** ($120); San Antonio GC/Home Improvement registration **biennially**.
- Maintain commercial GL insurance at each agency-required minimum and renew certificates with each licensing body listed as certificate holder.
