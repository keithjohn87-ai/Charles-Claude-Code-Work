## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Ohio structure — no statewide General Contractor license, state-licensed trades, municipal patchwork

Ohio is **structurally distinctive** among large construction states in maintaining **no statewide general contractor license** [1][2][3][4]. Unlike North Carolina (which requires the NCLBGC license above $30,000), Georgia (which requires the State Licensing Board for Residential and General Contractors above $2,500 residential / for all general work), Florida (which requires CILB certification or registration), or California (which requires CSLB licensure above $500), Ohio leaves the licensure of general contractors to **municipal corporations** under the home-rule authority granted by **Article XVIII Section 3 of the Ohio Constitution** and **ORC Chapter 715** (Powers of Municipal Corporations) [1][3][5]. The result is a patchwork — Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati, Toledo, Akron, Dayton, and dozens of other Ohio cities each maintain their own contractor registration or licensing schemes, with little uniformity in fees, bonds, exams, or scope [3][5][6][7][8].

At the **state** level, Ohio licenses only **five specialty trades** through the **Ohio Construction Industry Licensing Board (OCILB)**, a division of the Ohio Department of Commerce, established by **Ohio Revised Code Chapter 4740** [1][2][3][9][10]:

- **Electrical** (Electrical Contractor) — ORC § 4740.06(A)(1)
- **Heating, Ventilating, and Air Conditioning (HVAC)** — ORC § 4740.06(A)(2)
- **Refrigeration** — ORC § 4740.06(A)(3)
- **Plumbing** — ORC § 4740.06(A)(4)
- **Hydronics** — ORC § 4740.06(A)(5)

The OCILB also administers **water and waste treatment** licensing under closely related authority. Importantly, **ORC Chapter 4740 licenses cover commercial work only** — by long-standing administrative interpretation, residential one-, two-, and three-family dwellings (and accessory structures) fall outside the mandatory state-license scope, and municipalities are expressly authorized to accept (or require) ORC 4740 licenses for residential work under **ORC § 715.27** [1][2][11][12][13]. The result: a state-licensed Ohio electrical contractor performing commercial work in Cincinnati holds an OCILB Electrical Contractor license; the same electrician wiring a single-family home in Cleveland needs a Cleveland city license, and on commercial Cleveland projects must hold both the OCILB license and the city registration.

For ContractorPro's five trades, the practical Ohio map is:

| Electrical contracting (commercial) | **Yes** — Electrical Contractor | OCILB | ORC § 4740.01(A)(1); § 4740.06 [1][9] | Residential typically requires municipal license; ORC 4740 permits cities to recognize state license under ORC § 715.27 [11] |

### 1.2 OCILB — statutory framework, ORC Chapter 4740

**Statutory authority:** ORC § 4740.01 through § 4740.99 [1][9][10]. **Administrative rules:** Ohio Administrative Code Chapter 4101:8 (Industrial Compliance, OCILB rules). **Licensing agency:** Ohio Construction Industry Licensing Board, Ohio Department of Commerce, Division of Industrial Compliance, 6606 Tussing Road, Reynoldsburg, OH 43068; (614) 644-3493; com.ohio.gov OCILB page [1][9].

**Scope of preemption — ORC § 4740.13.** ORC § 4740.13 provides that the trades regulated by Chapter 4740 (electrical, HVAC, refrigeration, plumbing, hydronics) are licensed at the state level for **commercial work**, and that the state license **preempts** local licensing requirements for those trades on commercial projects [1][9][11]. Municipalities cannot impose a duplicate trade-license exam or trade-license fee beyond their general registration / building-permit framework on a commercial OCILB-licensed contractor; they may, however, require **registration** (which is administrative — a roster filing) and continue to enforce local building codes through the permit process. The line between "registration" (permitted) and "licensing" (preempted) is the source of recurring municipal litigation; OCILB has historically prevailed on the preemption point.

**Residential carve-out — ORC § 715.27.** Under ORC § 715.27(B), a municipal corporation **may accept** a valid OCILB Chapter 4740 license for residential work on one-, two-, or three-family dwellings (and accessory structures) — but is not required to do so [11][12][13]. Most large Ohio cities (Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati, Dayton) operate their own residential trade licensing or competency programs in addition to or in lieu of the state license; some smaller jurisdictions accept the OCILB license outright. A contractor doing residential work statewide must verify the local rules in every jurisdiction.

### 1.3 OCILB licensing tiers and trades

Under **ORC § 4740.06**, OCILB issues one license per trade. Within each trade, the license operates as a **contractor license** held in the name of an **individual** (the qualifier); the contractor license may then be **assigned to a business entity** under ORC § 4740.07 by filing the appropriate assignment with the OCILB. Ohio does **not** maintain a separate "Master" / "Contractor" two-tier system at the state level for OCILB trades — the state issues a single contractor license per individual qualifier per trade [1][2][9][10][15]. Many municipalities, however, do operate a Master / Journeyman / Apprentice tier system for the same trades on residential and city-permitted work, which is the source of common confusion: a state OCILB Electrical Contractor license is not a "Master Electrician" license in the municipal sense, and the holder may still need to obtain a city Master Electrician card to qualify for permits in some Ohio cities.

**Five state trades** (ORC § 4740.01) [1][9][10]:

| OCILB trade | Statutory section | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Electrical Contractor** | ORC § 4740.01(A)(1) | Installation, alteration, repair, or maintenance of electrical wiring and equipment in commercial structures — preempts local electrical-trade licensing under § 4740.13 |
| **HVAC Contractor** | ORC § 4740.01(A)(2) | Installation, alteration, repair, or maintenance of heating, ventilating, and air-conditioning systems in commercial structures |
| **Refrigeration Contractor** | ORC § 4740.01(A)(3) | Commercial refrigeration — supermarkets, cold storage, walk-in coolers, food-service refrigeration |
| **Plumbing Contractor** | ORC § 4740.01(A)(4) | Installation, alteration, repair, or maintenance of plumbing — water supply, drainage, vent, fixtures — in commercial structures |
| **Hydronics Contractor** | ORC § 4740.01(A)(5) | Closed-loop hydronic heating systems, boiler installations, and related distribution |

### 1.4 OCILB application requirements (ORC § 4740.06)

Under **ORC § 4740.06(B)**, an applicant for an OCILB contractor license in any of the five trades must satisfy the following requirements [1][9][10][15][16][17]:

1. **Age.** At least **18 years** old (ORC § 4740.06(B)(1)).
2. **Citizenship / lawful presence.** U.S. citizen or lawfully admitted alien.
3. **Experience.** At least **five (5) years** of experience as a tradesperson in the relevant trade within the preceding ten years, **or** at least **three (3) years** of experience as a licensed engineer in the trade's discipline [9][15][16][17]. As of September 6, 2024, OCILB administratively expanded the documentation that satisfies the experience requirement to include **IRS W-2 forms** or **IRS Schedule C** filings — a meaningful change for self-employed tradespersons who previously struggled to document experience through employer affidavits alone [15][18]. The five-year requirement is not waived by passage of the exam; it is a separate gate.
4. **Examination.** Pass the **OCILB trade examination** administered by **PSI Services LLC** under contract with OCILB. The trade exam is open-book and references the Ohio Building Code (which adopts the IBC with Ohio amendments), the National Electrical Code (current cycle), the Uniform Plumbing Code (Ohio amendments), the International Mechanical Code, and the relevant trade-specific code books. A **separate Business and Law Examination** is also administered by PSI, covering OCILB rules, contract law fundamentals, ORC Chapter 4123 (workers' compensation), ORC Chapter 4111 (wages and hours), and ORC Chapter 4115 (prevailing wage). Minimum passing score is **70%** on each exam; current exam fees are approximately $50 to $75 per sitting [9][15][16][17].
5. **Insurance — minimum $500,000 contractor liability.** Under ORC § 4740.06(B)(4), the applicant must produce a **Certificate of Liability Insurance** demonstrating continuous coverage of **not less than $500,000** in general liability per occurrence, with the Ohio Construction Industry Licensing Board listed as certificate holder [9][15][16][17][19]. The $500,000 minimum is statewide and uniform across the five trades; project owners and large municipalities routinely require $1M/$2M or higher.
6. **Workers' compensation.** Active workers' compensation coverage through the Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation (the monopoly carrier — see § 5 below) is required as a condition of license issuance and renewal [9][20][21][22].
7. **Application fee.** $25 examination fee plus $25 license issuance fee (rates current as of 2025).
8. **Renewal.** Annual renewal; OCILB licenses expire **December 31** of each year; renewal fee approximately $60. Continuing education requirements have been progressively expanded — OCILB rules require eight hours of CE for active licensees per renewal cycle, covering code updates, business and law, and safety [15][16][17][23].

**Electrical contractor annual registration — effective January 1, 2025.** Under recent OCILB rulemaking, **all electrical contractors operating in Ohio** — residential, commercial, and specialty (temporary electric poles, electric-to-septic systems) — must register annually with the OCILB beginning **January 1, 2025**, with a registration fee of **$120** [15][18][23]. The annual-registration regime sits alongside the underlying contractor license; it is an additional reporting and fee obligation, not a substitute for the underlying license, and is the result of consumer-protection-driven legislative interest in tracking residential electrical activity across municipal jurisdictions.

### 1.5 OCILB Qualifier — ORC § 4740.07

Under **ORC § 4740.07**, an OCILB license is issued and renewed in the name of an **individual** (the qualifier — referred to in the statute as the "licensee"). The individual licensee may **assign** the license to a business entity (corporation, LLC, partnership, sole proprietorship) by filing an entity-assignment application with the OCILB [1][9][24]. Once assigned, the contractor license is held by the entity, but the individual qualifier remains the responsible person under whom the entity's work is performed; if the qualifier separates from the entity (death, resignation, termination), the entity's right to operate under the license terminates. The entity has a limited grace period to designate a replacement qualifier or the assignment is revoked.

**One qualifier per entity per trade** is the practical rule, and the qualifier must have a genuine employment or ownership relationship with the entity. The OCILB actively enforces against "license rental" schemes (paying a credentialed individual to "front" for an unqualified entity) under ORC § 4740.10, with civil-penalty exposure of up to **$5,000 per violation** and license revocation [9][24][25].

### 1.6 OCILB reciprocity — ORC § 4740.08

Ohio does **not** maintain **blanket reciprocity** with any state for OCILB trades. Under **ORC § 4740.08**, the OCILB is authorized to enter into reciprocity agreements with other states on a state-by-state, trade-by-trade basis, with the requirement that the out-of-state license be substantially equivalent to the corresponding Ohio license [1][9][26][27]. As of 2025, Ohio maintains limited reciprocity arrangements with Kentucky, West Virginia, and several other neighboring states for specific trades, but the reciprocity is **trade-specific and is conditional** — applicants must still:

- Document the **five-year experience requirement** under ORC § 4740.06(B)(3).
- Take and pass the **Ohio Business and Law Examination** (not waived by reciprocity).
- Provide a Certificate of Liability Insurance meeting the Ohio $500,000 minimum.
- Demonstrate active workers' compensation coverage through the Ohio BWC (because Ohio is a monopoly WC state, out-of-state WC coverage does **not** satisfy the Ohio requirement — see § 5).

The trade exam may be waived for reciprocal jurisdictions, but the Business and Law exam is universal. NASCLA-accredited examination credentials are accepted only for limited categories; the OCILB has not adopted NASCLA wholesale [26][27].

### 1.7 Municipal contractor licensing — the patchwork

Because Ohio has no statewide General Contractor license, GCs operating in Ohio must register with — and frequently obtain a competency-based license from — **each municipality** in which they perform work. The major-city map [3][5][6][7][8][28][29]:

#### Columbus
**Authority:** Columbus City Code **Chapter 4114** (Contractor Licensing); administered by the **Columbus Department of Building and Zoning Services (DBZS)** [3][5][28]. Columbus operates a comprehensive General Contractor licensing scheme.

- **General Contractor license:** Requires (i) $25,000 surety bond, (ii) certificate of general liability insurance with minimum $300,000 per person / $500,000 per occurrence, (iii) $300 application fee, (iv) examination administered by Columbus DBZS or its testing vendor, and (v) annual renewal at $300 per year. The license is held in the name of the individual qualifier and may be assigned to a business entity.
- **Trade contractors:** Columbus separately licenses Master Electricians, Master Plumbers, Master HVAC, and other trade-specific masters. Holding the OCILB state license generally exempts the commercial trade contractor from Columbus trade licensing under ORC § 4740.13 preemption, but Columbus continues to require a **registration** for OCILB-licensed contractors operating in the city.

#### Cleveland
**Authority:** Cleveland Codified Ordinances **Chapter 3115** (Building Code Contractor Licensing); administered by the **Cleveland Department of Building and Housing** [3][5][29]. Cleveland operates the most demanding GC licensing regime among Ohio's largest cities, with substantial bonding and competency-exam requirements.

- **GC registration:** $150 application fee, annual renewal at $120; requires notarized application, bond form with power-of-attorney attached, certificate of liability insurance, and insurance endorsement.
- **Trade licensing:** Master Plumber, Master Electrician, Master HVAC require separate Cleveland-administered competency exams or recognized credential transfer. The OCILB state license is recognized for commercial trade work but not generally as a substitute for the Cleveland Master credential on residential or city-permit work.

#### Cincinnati
**Authority:** Cincinnati Municipal Code; administered by the **Cincinnati Department of Buildings & Inspections**. Cincinnati relies primarily on **registration** rather than competency licensing for general contractors [3][5].

- **Registration:** $131.25 application fee, annual renewal at $131.25; requires certificate of liability insurance and proof of workers' compensation coverage.
- **Trade work:** OCILB-licensed trades operate under the state license for commercial work; residential trade work requires separate Cincinnati credentials in some categories.

#### Toledo
**Authority:** Toledo Municipal Code, administered by the Toledo Department of Building Inspection. Toledo licenses GCs for work above **$5,000**, with examination administered by the city covering current Ohio Building Code and Toledo municipal regulations [6][8].

#### Akron
**Authority:** Akron Codified Ordinances; administered by the City of Akron. Akron requires contractor registration and competency testing for residential and commercial work, with state trade licenses required for OCILB specialty work [6][8].

#### Dayton
**Authority:** Dayton Revised Code of General Ordinances; administered by the City of Dayton Department of Planning, Community & Economic Development [7]. Dayton operates a tiered contractor scheme:

- **General Contractor:** Annual registration; certificate of insurance required.
- **Electrical Contractor:** $10,000 city bond, certificate of liability insurance, copy of State of Ohio Electrical Contractor License (OCILB) [7].
- **Plumbing Contractor:** $10,000 city bond, certificate of liability insurance, copy of State of Ohio Plumbing Contractor License [7].
- The $10,000 bond runs January 1 through December 31 of the registration year and is filed with the city.

**The practical compliance burden.** A commercial GC working a 5-city Ohio footprint — Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati, Toledo, Akron — must maintain **five separate municipal registrations or licenses**, each with its own renewal cycle, bond posting, insurance endorsement, fee schedule, and (in some cases) competency exam. The bonds alone (Cleveland insurance endorsement + Dayton $10,000 + Columbus $25,000 + Toledo bond + Akron) aggregate to roughly $40,000 in continuous surety capacity. The renewal calendar is the single most-missed compliance obligation in Ohio multi-city practice: a contractor whose Columbus license lapses December 31 cannot pull a Columbus permit January 5 for a new job until renewal posts, and the lapse is a stop-work trigger in most jurisdictions [5][6][7][8][28][29].

### 1.8 Home Construction Service Suppliers Act — ORC Chapter 4722

Independent of OCILB and municipal contractor licensing, Ohio regulates **residential construction contracts** under **ORC Chapter 4722** (Home Construction Service Suppliers Act, often abbreviated **HCSSA**), originally enacted in 2012 and substantially expanded by **HB 50 (135th General Assembly)**, signed by Governor DeWine on June 28, 2024 and effective **September 20, 2024** [4][14][30][31][32][33][34][35].

**Statutory threshold — $25,000.** Under **ORC § 4722.02**, no home construction service supplier (the statutory term for a residential general contractor or remodeler) shall perform any home construction service the cost of which equals or exceeds **$25,000** unless the supplier enters into a **written home construction services contract** with the owner [4][14][30][31]. Below the $25,000 threshold, the HCSSA written-contract requirement does not apply (though municipal licensing, building permits, and ORC Chapter 1311 lien obligations still do).

**Required contract contents — ORC § 4722.02(B).** The HCSSA contract above $25,000 must contain [14][30][31][32]:

1. The supplier's name, physical business address, business telephone number, and taxpayer identification number.
2. The owner's name, address, and telephone number.
3. The address or location of the property where the work is to be performed.
4. A general description of the home construction service, including goods and services to be furnished.
5. The anticipated date or time period for commencement and completion.
6. The total estimated cost.
7. Any cost of installation, delivery, or other costs not covered by the total estimated cost.
8. **A copy of the supplier's certificate of insurance showing general liability coverage of not less than $250,000 per occurrence.** The HCSSA-required GL minimum is **separate from** and may be lower than the OCILB $500,000 minimum applicable to state-licensed commercial trades.

**Excess-cost notice — ORC § 4722.02(D).** If reasonably unforeseen but necessary excess costs at any time exceed **$5,000** over the course of the entire contract, the supplier must — **before performing the work giving rise to the excess** — provide the owner a notice with a written or oral estimate (depending on which the owner elected in the contract) of the excess cost and the work to which it relates [14][30][31][32]. The excess-cost notice is the principal consumer-protection layer in HCSSA practice; failure to comply exposes the supplier to recovery limitations on the excess and (under pre-HB 50 law) to Consumer Sales Practices Act treble damages.

**Cost-plus exception — ORC § 4722.02(E).** A supplier who enters into a **cost-plus contract** with the owner is exempt from the divisions (A) and (B) contract-content requirements of § 4722.02, though the certificate-of-insurance and excess-cost-notice obligations continue to apply [14][30][31].

**HB 50 — September 2024 expansion.** Before HB 50, HCSSA applied only to **new home construction**. HB 50 expanded the statutory definition of "home construction service" under ORC § 4722.01 to include **the repair, improvement, remodel, or renovation of an existing structure** in addition to new construction [33][34][35]. The expansion brought the entire residential remodeling industry — kitchen-bath remodelers, additions, finished basements, full-house renovations, exterior repairs above $25,000 — under HCSSA contract requirements. Critically, HB 50 simultaneously **removed** most HCSSA-governed work from the harsher remedial framework of the **Ohio Consumer Sales Practices Act (ORC Chapter 1345)** — most notably eliminating the threat of treble (3x) damages against contractors performing HCSSA-covered repairs and remodels above $25,000 [33][34][35]. The trade-off — bringing remodelers into a contractually demanding regime in exchange for shielding them from CSPA treble damages — was the explicit legislative bargain.

**HCSSA still applies only to 1-, 2-, 3-family.** Work on residential buildings with **four or more dwelling units** (or on condominium common areas) falls outside HCSSA, even after HB 50 — except for work on individual dwelling units within larger multifamily buildings [14][30][31].

### 1.9 Workers' compensation — Ohio is a MONOPOLY STATE

Ohio is one of **four monopoly workers' compensation states** in the United States — alongside Washington, North Dakota, and Wyoming — meaning that workers' compensation insurance **may only be purchased from the state** through the **Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation (BWC)**. Private-market workers' compensation insurance from carriers like Travelers, Hartford, Liberty Mutual, or CNA — the standard mechanism in 46 states — **is not lawfully available** in Ohio [20][21][22][36][37][38]. See § 5 below for full treatment.

### 1.10 General-liability insurance — Ohio statewide minimums

Ohio does not impose a single statewide GL minimum for general contractors (because there is no statewide GC license); GL minimums are set per-license-class and per-municipality:

| Source | Minimum GL |
|---|---|
| OCILB Electrical / HVAC / Refrigeration / Plumbing / Hydronics Contractor | **$500,000 per occurrence** [9][19] |
| HCSSA Home Construction Service Supplier (above $25,000 residential) | **$250,000 per occurrence** [14][30] |
| Columbus General Contractor | $300,000 per person / $500,000 per occurrence; $25,000 surety bond [5][28] |
| Cleveland General Contractor | Local insurance endorsement; $150 application fee [29] |
| Cincinnati Registered Contractor | Certificate of liability insurance required [3] |
| Dayton Electrical/Plumbing Contractor | $10,000 city bond + certificate of liability insurance [7] |
| Ohio Workers' Compensation | Statutory through Ohio BWC (mandatory; monopoly carrier) [20][21][22] |

Owners, lenders, and bonding companies routinely require commercial GL well above these floors — $1M/$2M is the typical commercial floor on private work; $2M/$4M on larger commercial and institutional jobs; $5M to $10M aggregate on bonded public works.

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — ORC Chapter 1311

Ohio mechanic's lien law is at **ORC Chapter 1311** (Liens), substantially rewritten by **Amended Substitute Senate Bill 153 (1991)** which created the modern Notice of Commencement / Notice of Furnishing regime, and amended periodically since [39][40][41][42][43][44][45]. Chapter 1311 is one of the most procedurally demanding mechanic's-lien statutes in the United States, distinguished by an **owner-filed Notice of Commencement** (a structural feature shared with Florida and Michigan but not the Carolinas, Texas, or New York), a **subcontractor- and supplier-filed Notice of Furnishing** with a **21-day clock** from first furnishing, and **three different affidavit filing windows** based on property type (residential 60 days, commercial 75 days, oil and gas 120 days).

### 2.1 Notice of Commencement — owner files; ORC § 1311.04

Under **ORC § 1311.04**, prior to the performance of any labor or work or the furnishing of any materials for an improvement on real property that may give rise to a mechanic's lien, **the owner, part owner, or lessee** who contracts for the labor, work, or materials **shall record** in the office of the county recorder for each county where the property is located a **Notice of Commencement (NOC)** [40][42][45][46][47].

**Contents of the Notice of Commencement (§ 1311.04(B)).** The NOC must contain:

1. A description of the property, including legal description and street address.
2. The name and address of the owner, part owner, or lessee.
3. The interest of the contracting party in the property (fee simple, leasehold, life estate, etc.).
4. The name and address of the fee owner, if different from the contracting party.
5. The name and address of the original (prime) contractor.
6. The name and address of the lender, if any.
7. The name and address of the surety on any bond covering the project, if any.
8. A description of the improvement.
9. **The name and address of the owner's designee, if any** — the party authorized to receive Notices of Furnishing and other lien-related notices on the owner's behalf.

**Recording — § 1311.04(D).** The NOC is recorded with the **county recorder** of each county in which the property is located. The recorder endorses the date and hour of filing and records the NOC in the mechanic's lien records. **Only one NOC** is filed per single improvement; any subsequent NOCs are treated as **amendments** to the original [42][45][46][47]. The NOC **expires six (6) years** after its filing date unless the NOC or an amendment specifies a different duration [42][46].

**Owner's posting and service obligation — § 1311.04(K).** The owner must:

1. **Post a copy of the NOC at the construction site** in a conspicuous place, visible to all persons furnishing labor or materials.
2. **Serve a copy of the NOC** on any subcontractor, material supplier, or laborer who serves a written request for a copy on the owner — **within ten (10) days** of the written request [40][42][45][46][47].

The site-posting obligation is the principal mechanism by which subcontractors and suppliers entering the project mid-stream learn (a) that an NOC was recorded, and (b) the identity of the owner's designee — both critical to timely serving the Notice of Furnishing under § 1311.05. **Site-posting failures by the owner expose the owner to liability for late or defective Notice of Furnishing claims by subs and suppliers**, because the 21-day clock under § 1311.05 is not triggered for a sub unable to identify the NOC through reasonable diligence [40][42][45].

**Effect of failure to file NOC.** If the owner fails to record a required NOC and a sub or supplier's lien rights are accordingly impaired by inability to serve a Notice of Furnishing, the owner is liable to the sub or supplier for any loss caused by the failure, and the omitted-NOC project is treated for lien-priority purposes as if the NOC had been filed on the date the first labor was performed or materials furnished — meaning the lien claimant's effective date is preserved by the owner's own non-compliance [40][42][45][47].

**Residential exception — owner-occupied existing single-family dwelling.** Under ORC § 1311.011 (Liens for home construction work), the **owner-occupied 1- or 2-family residential** lien regime operates somewhat differently — the buyer or homeowner who has fully paid the original contractor before receiving notice of a sub's lien may invoke a statutory full-payment defense against subcontractor liens [44][48][49]. The NOC-and-Notice-of-Furnishing system still applies to most residential work, but the consumer-side defenses under § 1311.011 effectively transfer some risk to the original contractor (who bears the duty to deliver lien releases) and to lending institutions (whose acceptance of the original contractor's lien affidavit is governed by § 1311.011(B)).

### 2.2 Notice of Furnishing — subs and suppliers serve; ORC § 1311.05 (21-day clock)

The single most-missed deadline in Ohio lien practice is the **21-day Notice of Furnishing (NOF)** under **ORC § 1311.05** [40][41][42][45][46][47][50].

**Who must serve.** A **subcontractor, materialman, or laborer** who is **not in direct privity** with the owner, part owner, or lessee (i.e., not the prime contractor and not a supplier dealing directly with the owner) must **serve a Notice of Furnishing** on the owner (or owner's designee) **within 21 days** after performing the first labor or work or furnishing the first material to the project — **provided that an NOC has been recorded** by the owner under § 1311.04 [40][41][42][45][46][47][50].

**Who is exempt from the NOF requirement.** Under ORC § 1311.05(A), **NOT** required to serve the NOF:

1. **Material suppliers in direct privity of contract with an owner, part owner, or lessee.** A supplier selling directly to the owner does not need to serve an NOF; the supplier's privity is sufficient notice.
2. **Subcontractors or material suppliers in direct privity of contract with the original (prime) contractor.** First-tier subs directly contracting with the prime are exempt from the NOF requirement, because the prime's relationship with the owner gives the owner constructive notice of the first-tier sub's involvement.
3. **Laborers** (employees) are not statutorily exempt as a class but typically are not the parties asserting Chapter 1311 lien claims in any event.

Practical translation: **second-tier and lower-tier subs and suppliers** — and any party not in privity with either the owner or the prime — must serve the NOF within 21 days of first furnishing.

**Form and content — ORC § 1311.05(C).** The NOF must contain:

1. The name and address of the person furnishing the labor, work, or materials.
2. The name and address of the person with whom the contractor / supplier has contracted (i.e., the higher-tier party).
3. A general description of the labor, work, or materials furnished.
4. The address or location of the property where the work is performed.

The NOF is served on the **owner or owner's designee** (whose identity appears on the recorded NOC) and on the **original contractor** by certified mail, personal service, or any method reasonably calculated to give actual notice [40][42][45][47].

**Consequence of late or no NOF — § 1311.05(D).** This is the trap. If the NOF is served **more than 21 days after first furnishing**, the NOF **preserves lien rights only for amounts owing for labor and work performed and materials furnished within the 21-day period immediately preceding service of the NOF** — and **does not revive any prior lien rights** for labor or materials furnished before that 21-day window [40][41][45][47][50]. Every day the NOF is late, lien rights for that day's work are extinguished forever. A sub who furnishes labor for 60 days before serving the NOF preserves liens only for the most recent 21 days; the first 39 days of work are unrecoverable through Chapter 1311.

The 21-day clock is calendar days — not business days, not "business days excluding holidays." It does not pause for weekends, supplier-side billing delays, or the time it takes a sub's office to receive the project's NOC from the GC. The sub's first-furnishing date is determined by the date of physical work performed or material delivery; warehouse "shipping" is not first furnishing — the date materials arrive at the site is.

**The practical implication.** Every Ohio sub or supplier with second-tier or lower-tier exposure on every $30,000+ project must operate a **first-furnishing tracking discipline** and serve the NOF within 21 days, no exceptions. The 21-day NOF window is to Ohio practice what the 20-day Preliminary Notice is to California or the 15-day Notice to Lien Agent is to North Carolina — the make-or-break gate that determines whether lien rights survive long enough to file an affidavit.

### 2.3 Affidavit for Mechanic's Lien — ORC § 1311.06 (60/75/120-day filing splits)

Under **ORC § 1311.06**, the affidavit for mechanic's lien is filed with the **county recorder** of the county where the property is located, within one of **three statutory filing periods depending on property type** [39][40][41][45][46][47][51][52]:

| Property type | Filing deadline from last furnishing |
|---|---|
| **One- or two-family dwelling** or **residential condominium unit** | **60 days** from date of last labor or materials |
| **Oil or gas well property** under ORC § 1311.021 | **120 days** from date of last labor or materials |
| **All other property** (commercial, industrial, multifamily 3+ unit, public-private partnership, etc.) | **75 days** from date of last labor or materials |

The 60/75/120-day distinction is unique among major construction states and is a recurring source of error — a commercial contractor accustomed to "75 days commercial" who does work on a duplex (2-family) is on a 60-day clock; a sub working on an oil-and-gas project has the longer 120-day window but must verify the property falls within § 1311.021. Last-furnishing date for the affidavit period is the same concept as for the NOF — the last day the claimant performed substantive contract work, **not** warranty or punch-list-only visits.

**Required contents of the affidavit — § 1311.06(B).** The affidavit must show [39][51][52]:

1. **The amount due** over and above all legal setoffs.
2. **A description of the property** to be charged with the lien.
3. **The name and address of the person to or for whom the labor or work was performed or material was furnished** (i.e., the higher-tier obligor).
4. **The name of the owner, part owner, or lessee**, if known.
5. **The name and address of the lien claimant.**
6. **The first and last dates** that the lien claimant performed labor or furnished materials to the improvement.

The affidavit must be sworn under oath before a notary public or other officer authorized to administer oaths.

**Recording fee.** The county recorder charges a recording fee (typically $34 for the first two pages plus $8 per additional page, plus indexing fees; rates vary by county).

### 2.4 Service of affidavit on owner — ORC § 1311.07 (30-day service window)

Under **ORC § 1311.07**, within **30 days** after filing the affidavit, the lien claimant must serve a copy of the filed affidavit on the **owner, part owner, or lessee** of the improved property or on the owner's designee [40][41][45][47][53]. Service may be by personal delivery, certified mail with return receipt requested, or any method authorized by the Ohio Rules of Civil Procedure. Failure to serve within 30 days does not automatically invalidate the lien but may be raised in defense by the owner; certain protections against subcontractor lien claims (such as the owner's full-payment-before-notice defense under § 1311.011) operate on the predicate that the owner received service in compliance with § 1311.07.

### 2.5 Lien priority — ORC § 1311.13 (relation back to NOC; laborer super-priority)

Under **ORC § 1311.13**, lien priority operates in Ohio along **two distinct tracks** [42][45][47][54]:

1. **Liens with an effective date prior to the recording of the NOC have priority over all other liens** (with limited exceptions for prior-recorded mortgages and certain government liens) — to the extent of the value of work and labor performed and materials furnished prior to the recording of the NOC. This is the relation-back component.
2. **Liens with an effective date after the NOC** have priority over subsequently-recorded mortgages and encumbrances, but yield to mortgages and other interests recorded prior to the NOC.

**Same-day mortgage / NOC rule — § 1311.13(D).** Unless otherwise stipulated in a construction mortgage, **if a mortgage and an NOC for the same improvement are recorded on the same day, the mortgage is considered recorded before the NOC** [42][45][54]. This statutory rule reverses the common-law presumption of timing parity and protects construction lenders whose closing schedules call for same-day mortgage-and-NOC recordation.

**Laborer super-priority — § 1311.13(C).** Liens filed by **laborers** have priority over all other liens — whether the labor or work was performed before or after the recording of the NOC [42][45][54]. The laborer super-priority is the strongest single feature of Ohio Chapter 1311 priority, reflecting historical wage-protection policy, and operates to ensure that direct laborer claims for wages on the job site are paid before contractor, subcontractor, supplier, or mortgage claims regardless of NOC timing.

**Duration of liens — § 1311.13(E).** A properly-filed Chapter 1311 lien continues in force for **six (6) years** after the affidavit is filed with the county recorder. If a foreclosure action is brought to enforce the lien within those six years, the lien continues in force until final adjudication of the foreclosure suit [42][45][54].

### 2.6 Notice to Commence Suit — ORC § 1311.11 (60-day demand)

Under **ORC § 1311.11**, the owner or any party with an interest in the property may serve a **Notice to Commence Suit** on the lien claimant, requiring the claimant to file a foreclosure action within **60 days** of service [45][55]. If the claimant fails to commence suit within the 60-day window, the lien is **discharged by operation of law** and the claimant loses the right to enforce. The Notice to Commence Suit is the standard owner-side tool to force the claimant to "fish or cut bait" — to file the foreclosure action or release the lien.

### 2.7 Bonding off the lien — ORC § 1311.11

ORC § 1311.11 also authorizes the property owner (or party with an interest) to **bond off** the lien by depositing with the county recorder a **cash deposit or surety bond** in an amount equal to **125% of the lien claim** (or a different amount specified by court order). Upon bond posting, the lien is discharged from the real property and the bond stands as substitute security for the claim. The lien claimant's suit then proceeds against the bond rather than against the property, allowing the owner to close on a sale or refinance without lien encumbrance [42][45][55].

### 2.8 Public works — bond and lien-on-funds remedies under ORC § 1311.25 through § 1311.32

Subcontractors and suppliers on Ohio **public construction projects** have **no lien rights against publicly owned real property** (the state and its political subdivisions cannot be subjected to a mechanic's lien). Their recovery vehicles are (i) claims on the prime's **payment bond**, and (ii) claims against project funds retained by the public authority pursuant to **ORC § 1311.25 through § 1311.32** [56][57][58][59].

**Public-works Notice of Furnishing — § 1311.252.** On public projects, subcontractors, laborers, and material/equipment suppliers must serve the public-works NOF within **21 days** of first furnishing — the same 21-day window as the private-project NOF, but operating against the prime contractor and the public authority rather than the owner's designee [56][57][58][59].

**Affidavit for claim against public funds — § 1311.26.** Within statutory time limits, the claimant files an affidavit identifying the amount claimed, the labor or materials furnished, and the prime contractor on the project. Upon receipt, the **public authority detains** from the principal contractor (up to the balance remaining in the contract) an amount that does not aggregate in excess of the claims [57][58][59].

**Escrow of detained funds — ORC § 153.63.** The detained funds are deposited in an **escrow account** under ORC § 153.63, to be released at the times and in the amounts ordered by a court of competent jurisdiction or by agreement of the parties [57][58][59][60][61].

**Performance and payment bonds — ORC § 153.54.** Under ORC § 153.54, the prime contractor on most Ohio public-works projects must furnish a performance bond and a payment bond, both in 100% of the contract amount. Subcontractor and supplier claims on the payment bond run from last furnishing and are subject to one-year suit limitations under § 1311.32 and related statutes [57][58][59][60].

### 2.9 Lien waivers — ORC § 1311.32 and § 4113.62

Ohio enforces written lien waivers as long as they identify the specific work, time period, and amount waived. Both **conditional progress-payment waivers** (effective upon receipt of payment) and **unconditional final waivers** (effective immediately) are valid. **Advance lien waivers** — those executed before labor or materials are furnished — face an additional layer of scrutiny under **ORC § 4113.62** (Construction Contract Provisions Against Public Policy), which generally protects subs' statutory lien and bond rights against pre-claim waiver attempts:

- **§ 4113.62(A)** voids any construction-contract provision that **waives rights under a surety bond**.
- **§ 4113.62(D)** voids any construction-contract provision that **waives delay damages** when the cause is a proximate result of the owner's or contractor's act or failure to act.
- **§ 4113.62(E)** protects a subcontractor's right to **file a mechanic's lien or payment bond claim** despite a pay-if-paid clause — pay-if-paid clauses may control internal payment-timing between prime and sub but cannot extinguish the sub's statutory remedies against the property or bond [62][63][64].

### 2.10 Home-construction lien special rules — ORC § 1311.011

Residential home-construction work operates under additional consumer-protection layers in **ORC § 1311.011** [44][48][49]:

- **Full-payment defense by homeowner.** A buyer of a new home (or a homeowner contracting directly for home construction or improvement) is protected against subcontractor liens if (i) the homeowner has paid the original contractor the full amount due under the home construction or home purchase contract, and (ii) the homeowner did not receive a copy of the affidavit of mechanic's lien under § 1311.07 before making that payment. This is a substantive defense, not merely a procedural one — the unpaid sub's recovery against the homeowner is limited.
- **Lending-institution duties — § 1311.011(B).** A lending institution making payments under a home construction contract may accept the original contractor's lien affidavit (certifying the contractor has paid or will pay all subs and suppliers) and rely on it, unless the affidavit is fraudulent on its face. If, however, the lending institution has received written notice of a subcontractor's claim of right to a mechanic's lien, the institution's failure to obtain a release from the noticing sub is prima facie evidence of gross negligence — exposing the lender to liability to the unpaid sub.
- **Original contractor duties — § 1311.011(C).** The original contractor working on home construction is required to obtain lien releases from all subs and suppliers before final payment and must deliver those releases to the homeowner or lender, on demand. Failure to deliver lien releases is a contract-default trigger and may expose the original contractor to damages including attorney fees under § 1311.16 and § 1311.32 [44][48][49].

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

Ohio prompt-payment law operates in two parallel tracks: **private projects** under ORC § 4113.61, and **public projects** under ORC § 153.13 (state contracts), ORC § 153.14 (estimates and payment), and parallel provisions for local public authorities under Title 7. The two tracks share concepts but differ materially in retainage caps, payment-cycle timing, and escrow obligations.

### 3.1 Private project prompt payment — ORC § 4113.61

For private construction contracts, **ORC § 4113.61** governs both owner-to-prime and prime-to-subcontractor payment timing [62][63][64][65][66].

**Prime to subcontractor — § 4113.61(A)(1).** Within **ten (10) calendar days** after the prime contractor receives a progress payment from the owner that includes funds owed to a subcontractor or material supplier, the prime must pay each subcontractor and material supplier its proportionate share of that progress payment [62][63][64][65][66]. The 10-day clock runs from the prime's receipt of the owner payment — not from the prime's invoice approval, not from the prime's internal accounting cycle, not from the close of the prime's payment week.

**Subcontractor to lower-tier sub.** The same 10-day rule applies in serial fashion down the contracting chain: each subcontractor that receives payment must pay its own subs and suppliers within 10 days of receipt.

**Interest penalty for late payment — § 4113.61(B).** A contractor (or higher-tier subcontractor) who fails to pay within the 10-day window is liable to the unpaid subcontractor or supplier for **interest at 18% per annum** on the amount due, computed from the date payment was due [62][63][64][65][66]. The 18% rate is statutory and non-negotiable — contract provisions purporting to substitute a lower rate are unenforceable to the extent inconsistent with § 4113.61.

**Attorney fees — § 4113.61(B)(1).** In addition to interest, the prevailing party in an action to recover late payment is entitled to **reasonable attorney fees** unless the court determines that an award of fees would be inequitable [62][63][64][65][66]. The attorney-fees provision is fee-shifting in favor of the unpaid party and is the principal reason Ohio prompt-pay litigation by subs against primes is generally cost-effective despite the small per-incident dollar amounts.

**Pay-if-paid clauses.** As discussed at § 2.9 above, pay-if-paid clauses in Ohio subcontracts are **enforceable as to internal payment timing** if drafted with sufficient clarity per the Ohio Supreme Court's decision in *Transtar Electric, Inc. v. A.E.M. Electric Services Corp.*, 140 Ohio St.3d 193 (2014) — but cannot extinguish the subcontractor's statutory mechanic's lien rights, payment bond rights, or prompt-pay-statute rights under § 4113.62(E) [62][63][64].

### 3.2 Public project prompt payment — ORC § 126.30 and § 153.14

For state public-works projects, periodic payment obligations are governed by **ORC § 126.30** (the State Prompt Payment Act for state agencies) and **ORC § 153.14** (estimates and payment by the contracting authority on public improvements) [57][58][60][67]:

- **Public owner to prime.** State agencies must pay the prime within **30 calendar days** of receipt of a properly completed invoice or pay estimate; longer than 30 days triggers interest under § 126.30 at the rate of approximately 3% per annum or the rate set by the Director of Budget and Management.
- **Local public authorities.** Local public-works contracting is governed by analogous Title 7 provisions and by contract, generally with 30- to 45-day owner-to-prime payment cycles.
- **Prime to subcontractor on public works.** The 10-day pass-through rule under § 4113.61(A)(1) applies to public projects as well as private — once the prime receives the public owner's payment, the prime must pay the sub within 10 days.

### 3.3 Retainage — public works under ORC § 153.13 (8% cap; 50% completion release)

Ohio public-works retainage operates under **ORC § 153.13** and related provisions, with substantial revision in recent sessions [57][58][60][61][67][68].

**Maximum retainage — § 153.13.** On public construction projects (except residential projects of 12 units or fewer), the maximum retainage that may be withheld from each progress payment is **eight percent (8%)** [60][61][67][68].

**50% completion — escrow trigger and end of retainage growth — § 153.13.** From the date the contract is **50% complete** (evidenced by payments equal to at least 50% of the contract price), and on projects exceeding **$15,000** in total cost, all funds retained under §§ 153.12 and 153.14 must be deposited into an **escrow account** designated by ORC § 153.63 [60][61][67][68]. After 50% completion is reached, **no further funds may be retained** — the public authority must pay each progress payment in full, with no additional retainage withholding.

**Release of retainage at substantial completion — § 153.13.** When the major portion of the project is substantially completed, occupied, in use, or otherwise accepted, and there exists no other reason to withhold retainage, the retained percentages held in connection with that portion must be released from escrow and paid to the contractor, withholding only the amount necessary to assure completion. The public entity must release retainage **within 30 days** of substantial completion or acceptance [60][61][67].

**Penalties for late retainage release.** If the public entity fails to release retainage within the 30-day window, or fails to deposit retainage in the escrow account at the 50% trigger, interest accrues at **8% per annum** [60][61][67]. This 8% interest penalty is in addition to any other prompt-pay interest accruing under § 4113.61 or § 126.30.

**Pass-through to subs.** Once the prime receives released retainage from the public authority, the prime must release retainage to its subs and suppliers within the 10-day prompt-pay window under § 4113.61, subject to the prime's own retainage-release schedule with each sub under the subcontract [62][63][64].

### 3.4 Retainage — private works

Private-works retainage in Ohio is **contract-based**, not statutorily capped. Owners and primes typically negotiate retainage of 5% to 10% during construction, with a 50%-completion reduction or release common (modeled on the public-works template). Retainage release timing is governed by the contract; § 4113.61 prompt-pay rules govern payment timing once retainage is contractually due, with the 10-day prime-to-sub pass-through and 18% interest penalty applying [62][63][64][65][66].

## 4. Tax structure

### 4.1 Sales tax — ORC Chapter 5739 (state 5.75% + county/transit local rates)

**State base sales tax rate:** Ohio imposes a state-level sales and use tax of **5.75%** under **ORC § 5739.02** [69][70][71][72]. Counties and transit authorities may impose additional local sales tax under ORC §§ 5739.021, 5739.023, and 5739.026, with combined local rates ranging from **0.75% to 2.25%** depending on jurisdiction.

**Combined sales-tax rates (2025):** Combined state-plus-local rates range from **6.50% to 8.00%** depending on county [69][70][71][72]:

| Range | Counties |
|---|---|
| 6.50% (lowest combined) | Various rural counties with no county or transit add-on beyond 0.75% |
| 7.25% (median) | Mid-tier counties |
| 7.50% | Lake County (effective April 1, 2025) [69][70][71] |
| 7.50% | Franklin County (Columbus area) — Central Ohio Transit Authority (COTA) tax increase effective July 1, 2025 raised the rate 0.5% across Franklin and portions of Fairfield, Union, Licking, and Delaware counties [69][70][71] |
| 8.00% (highest combined) | Cuyahoga County (Cleveland area) [69][70][71] |

Contractors must compute sales tax on materials at the **delivery / use location** rate — not the contractor's office rate — and must verify the rate in effect on the delivery date through the Ohio Department of Taxation's Sales and Use Tax Rate Tables (updated quarterly) [69][70][71][72].

### 4.2 Construction contractor as consumer — ORC § 5739.01 and Ohio Administrative Code Rule 5703-9-14

Ohio treats the construction contractor as the **consumer** of tangible personal property incorporated into real property — a structural treatment shared with most states but with distinctive Ohio-specific terminology and exemption procedures [73][74][75][76][77].

**Statutory definition — ORC § 5739.01.** Under ORC § 5739.01(B)(1), a "sale" includes the construction contract pursuant to which tangible personal property is or is to be incorporated into a structure or improvement on and becoming a part of real property — **but only** to the extent provided by ORC § 5739.03 [73][74][76]. The practical effect: when a contractor purchases materials at the supply house to install on a project, the contractor pays sales tax to the supplier at the rate in effect at the supply-house (or delivery) location. The materials, once installed and incorporated into real property, become part of the realty and are not subject to a separate downstream sales-tax event at the moment of installation.

**Construction contract treatment — Ohio Admin. Code Rule 5703-9-14.** Ohio Administrative Code **Rule 5703-9-14** governs sales and use tax on construction contracts and provides [73][74][75][76][77]:

1. A construction contractor who purchases materials or taxable services for incorporation into real property is the **consumer** of those materials or services and shall pay sales or use tax on their purchase price.
2. Except as provided in ORC § 5739.03, a construction contract pursuant to which tangible personal property is or is to be incorporated into a structure or improvement on and becoming part of real property is **not a sale** of such tangible personal property — the contractor does not "re-sell" the material to the owner for sales-tax purposes.
3. A construction contractor who also makes **substantial sales of the same types of tangible personal property** that the contractor incorporates into real property may purchase those types of tangible personal property **on the basis of resale**, excepted from sales and use tax under ORC § 5739.01(E) — but only on the resale-eligible portion.

**STEC CC — Construction Contract Exemption Certificate.** When a contractor performs work for a tax-exempt entity (governmental units, non-profits, churches, etc.) and the materials would otherwise be tax-exempt if purchased directly by the exempt entity, the contractor may purchase those materials exempt from sales tax by furnishing the supplier with **Form STEC CC** (Construction Contract Exemption Certificate) [73][74][77]. The STEC CC is issued by the Ohio Department of Taxation and must identify the exempt project, the exempt party, and the contractor's certification that the materials will be incorporated into the exempt party's real property improvement. Misuse of STEC CC — claiming exemption on materials for a non-exempt project — exposes the contractor to back-tax, penalty, and interest under ORC § 5739.31 and § 5739.13.

**Taxable services and repair, maintenance, and installation services.** Ohio sales tax also reaches certain repair, maintenance, and installation services to tangible personal property under ORC § 5739.01(B)(3), distinct from real-property construction. The line between "real-property improvement" (not a sale) and "repair / maintenance / installation of tangible personal property" (taxable service) is litigated regularly; a contractor whose scope blurs the line should obtain a written Department of Taxation Information Release confirming treatment for the specific work.

### 4.3 Commercial Activity Tax (CAT) — ORC Chapter 5751 (2024-2025 expansion of exclusion)

Ohio imposes a **Commercial Activity Tax (CAT)** on the gross receipts of business activity in Ohio, codified at **ORC Chapter 5751** [78][79][80][81][82]. The CAT is a low-rate, broad-base gross-receipts tax — not an income tax — applicable to virtually all business activity above the statutory threshold.

**Recent threshold expansion — 2024 and 2025.** The CAT exclusion thresholds were dramatically raised under tax-reform legislation [78][79][80][81][82]:

| Tax year | Annual exclusion amount | Registration / filing threshold |
|---|---|---|
| 2023 and earlier | $1 million tax base (with $150,000 minimum gross receipts to register) | $150,000 in Ohio taxable gross receipts |
| 2024 | **$3 million annual exclusion** | $3 million in Ohio taxable gross receipts |
| 2025 and thereafter | **$6 million annual exclusion** | **$6 million** in Ohio taxable gross receipts |

The **annual minimum CAT (AMCAT) was eliminated for tax periods 2024 and thereafter**. Taxpayers with Ohio taxable gross receipts below the exclusion amount have **no CAT liability and no obligation to register or file**.

**CAT rate.** Taxable gross receipts in excess of the exclusion amount are taxed at **0.26%** [78][79][80][81][82]. The tax base is gross receipts (not income), with limited deductions for certain costs of goods sold or other statutory adjustments.

**Practical impact for contractors.** Many small and mid-size Ohio contractors have **dropped below CAT liability** entirely as of January 1, 2025. A residential GC with $4 million in annual gross receipts paid CAT in 2023 but pays no CAT for 2025 onward (because $4M is below the $6M exclusion). A commercial GC with $15 million in gross receipts pays CAT on $9M (gross receipts above the exclusion), at 0.26% — a CAT liability of approximately $23,400.

**Affiliate / combined-group rules.** Combined and consolidated elected taxpayer groups (typical of multi-entity contractor enterprises with separate LLCs for site work, design, construction, and management) must aggregate Ohio taxable gross receipts across all group members to determine whether the group exceeds the $6M exclusion. Splitting business across affiliated entities does not avoid CAT [78][79][80][81][82].

### 4.4 Other taxes — overview

- **Ohio income tax (ORC Chapter 5747).** Ohio imposes a graduated personal income tax (currently 0% to 3.5% in 2025 after recent reductions) on individuals and pass-through entity owners. Construction-industry sole proprietors, partners, and S-corp shareholders report business income on the personal return.
- **Municipal income tax.** Most Ohio municipalities impose a separate municipal income tax (typically 1.5% to 2.85%) on net profits of businesses operating within the city and on wages earned within the city. The Regional Income Tax Agency (RITA) administers municipal income tax collection for many Ohio cities; the Central Collection Agency (CCA) administers for others. Contractors with multi-city Ohio operations must track payroll and net-profit allocation across cities and file separate municipal returns. The municipal-tax compliance burden is one of the most distinctive features of Ohio operations and a recurring source of penalty exposure for out-of-state contractors entering Ohio without realizing the city-level filings exist.
- **Property tax — ORC Title 57.** Real and tangible personal property taxes are administered at the county and local level; commercial construction equipment is subject to depreciation and assessment under standard property-tax rules. Ohio repealed the general tangible personal property tax on most business equipment over a multi-year phase-out completed in 2009, but real property tax applies to owned facilities, equipment yards, and other real-estate holdings.

## 5. Workers' compensation — Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation (MONOPOLY STATE)

This is the section where Ohio diverges most sharply from peer states. Ohio is one of **four monopoly workers' compensation states** in the United States, alongside **Washington, North Dakota, and Wyoming** [20][21][22][36][37][38]. Workers' compensation insurance in Ohio may **only** be purchased from the state — through the **Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation (BWC)** — and **private-market workers' compensation insurance is unavailable** in Ohio. Carriers like Travelers, Hartford, Liberty Mutual, CNA, Berkshire, and AmTrust — the dominant private-market WC carriers in 46 states — do not write Ohio statutory WC; the only path to coverage is the BWC.

### 5.1 Statutory framework — ORC Chapter 4123

Ohio workers' compensation is at **ORC Chapter 4123** (Workers' Compensation Act), administered by the **Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation** (bwc.ohio.gov), established in 1912 — one of the oldest state-run WC systems in the country [20][21][22][36][37][38]. The BWC employs approximately 1,600 staff and manages assets of approximately $22 billion across its State Insurance Fund, Disabled Workers' Relief Fund, Coal Workers' Pneumoconiosis Fund, and Self-Insured Employers' Guaranty Fund.

**Mandatory coverage — ORC § 4123.01 and § 4123.35.** Every Ohio employer with **one or more employees** must obtain workers' compensation coverage from the BWC, by either:

1. **State Insurance Fund participation** (the default route for most employers) — the BWC issues a certificate of coverage upon application, premium payment, and ongoing payroll reporting. This is by far the most common route for construction-industry employers.
2. **Self-insurance** under **ORC § 4123.35(B)** — available to employers meeting net-worth, working-capital, and claims-administration capacity thresholds set by BWC rule. Self-insurance is the route used by very large Ohio employers (the major automakers, big public utilities, large hospital systems) — typical eligibility is in the range of 500+ employees and demonstrated financial capacity to administer claims directly. Few construction-industry firms qualify [83][84].
3. **Project-based self-insurance for large construction projects — ORC § 4123.35(O)**. A self-insuring employer (such as a public utility, large industrial owner, or major institutional owner) acting as the GC or owner-controlled-insurance sponsor on a large construction project may apply to **self-insure the project**, providing workers' compensation coverage for its own employees and for the employees of subcontractors enrolled in the project self-insurance program. The BWC may waive cost and time criteria where the project cost is estimated to exceed **$50 million**. The project-based self-insurance route — sometimes called a "wrap" or "construction wrap-up" — is most commonly used on hospital, university, manufacturing-plant, or stadium projects above $50M, and provides workers' comp immunity for the GC and all enrolled subs against the negligence claims of injured workers (whose exclusive remedy is the workers' comp claim) [83][84][85].

### 5.2 Construction-industry rates and classifications

Ohio construction-industry workers' compensation rates are among the **highest** in the country, reflecting the combination of injury frequency, severity, and Ohio's experience-rated premium structure [20][21][36][37][38][86][87].

**NCCI manual classifications.** The BWC uses manual classifications based on the National Council on Compensation Insurance (NCCI) class-code system, with classification rates set by BWC administrative ratemaking. Major construction classifications include:

- **5538** — HVAC, sheet metal work
- **5183** — Plumbing
- **5190** — Electrical (interior wiring)
- **5057** — Iron and steel erection
- **5645** — Carpentry, residential
- **5474** — Painting
- **5022** — Masonry

Each classification carries a **base rate per $100 of payroll** that varies by year and is subject to BWC's annual ratemaking under ORC § 4123.34. As a monopoly carrier, the BWC sets base rates by administrative rule, then applies employer-specific experience modifiers (EMR equivalent) to compute the actual premium.

**Recent rate developments.**

- **2023 base-rate increases.** BWC adjusted base rates for several construction classifications in 2023; some commercial, transportation, construction, and agricultural manual codes saw base-rate increases of up to **20%** in 2023, reflecting elevated injury severity in those segments [36][86].
- **2025 rate cut.** Effective **July 1, 2025**, BWC implemented an overall **6% statewide private-employer rate cut** — though the reduction is not uniformly distributed across classifications, and several construction-industry classifications received smaller cuts or rate-neutral treatment [87][88].

**Premium structure — base rate × payroll × experience modifier.** A typical Ohio construction-industry premium calculation:

(NCCI Class Code base rate per $100 of payroll) × (annual payroll in hundreds) × (employer experience modifier) × (rate-plan adjustments)

For a small commercial HVAC contractor with $500,000 of annual payroll, a class-5538 base rate of (illustratively) $4.50 per $100, and a unity experience mod of 1.00, the annual base premium is approximately $22,500 before discount programs.

**Group rating and discount programs.** BWC operates several premium-reduction programs that materially lower construction-industry effective rates:

- **Group Rating Program.** Eligible employers may join a group sponsored by a trade association (e.g., Construction Employers Association, Ohio Construction Industry Coalition, Builders Exchange) and receive a group experience modification that combines all member employers' loss experience — typically yielding 20% to 40% premium discounts for participants with above-average safety records.
- **Group Retrospective Rating.** Higher-risk / sophisticated employers may participate in retrospective-rating groups, paying a deposit premium and receiving refunds or assessments based on actual group loss experience over a multi-year evaluation period.
- **Drug-Free Safety Program (DFSP).** Employers implementing approved drug-free workplace and safety programs receive premium discounts up to 7%.
- **Safety Council Program.** Active participation in BWC-recognized local safety councils provides additional premium-reduction credit.

For construction-industry contractors, group rating and DFSP participation routinely combine to cut effective premiums by 30% to 50% relative to the unbundled NCCI rate — and for that reason, virtually every viable construction-industry contractor in Ohio participates in a group through a trade association sponsor.

### 5.3 Sole proprietor, partner, and corporate officer treatment — ORC § 4123.01

Under **ORC § 4123.01(A)(2)**, the following are **excluded** from the default definition of "employee" and are not automatically covered by Ohio workers' compensation [89][90][91][92]:

- **Sole proprietors** (the individual owner of a sole proprietorship).
- **General partners** of a partnership.
- **Individuals incorporated as a corporation** (single-shareholder corporations where the individual is the only employee/officer).
- **Officers of a family farm corporation.**
- **Members of a limited liability company classified as a partnership** for federal tax purposes (treated analogously to general partners).
- **Ordained or associate ministers** of religious organizations.

**Elective coverage — ORC § 4123.01(A)(2)(d) and Ohio Admin. Code 4123-17-07.** An excluded individual may **elect coverage** by filing **Form U-3S** (Application for or Request to Cancel Elective Coverage) with the BWC, naming the person to be covered and including that person's remuneration in future payroll reports [89][90][91][92]. Once elected, coverage continues until the employer files a request to cancel.

**Payroll reporting for electing individuals.** For all individuals electing coverage, the reportable wages are subject to a **minimum and maximum** based on the statewide average weekly wage (SAWW). The minimum payroll reporting amount is **50% of the SAWW**; the maximum is **150% of the SAWW** [90][91][92]. As of 2025, the Ohio SAWW is approximately $1,180 per week, yielding a minimum reportable annual payroll of approximately $30,680 and a maximum of approximately $92,040 for an electing sole proprietor or partner.

**Corporate officers — ORC § 4123.01 and Ohio Admin. Code 4123-17-07.** Officers of an ordinary (non-family-farm) corporation are presumed **employees** and are covered by default, but the corporation may file an election to **exclude** specific corporate officers under Ohio Admin. Code Rule 4123-17-07. The exclusion is per-officer and must be filed before injury; retroactive exclusions are not recognized [89][90][91][92].

### 5.4 Subcontractor / statutory-employer exposure

Although Ohio's BWC statute does not impose an explicit "statutory employer" doctrine identical to North Carolina's ORC § 97-19 (which makes a GC liable for an uninsured sub's employees), Ohio achieves a functionally similar outcome through three mechanisms:

1. **Combined certificates and joint-and-several premium liability under ORC § 4123.35.** A GC that engages an uninsured subcontractor may be liable for the sub's BWC premium and may have its own premium recalculated to include the sub's payroll.
2. **BWC enforcement audits.** BWC routinely audits construction sites and primes to confirm all subs hold active BWC certificates; primes engaging uninsured subs are subject to audit-driven premium recapture.
3. **Negligence and respondeat-superior exposure under common law.** A GC engaging an uninsured sub whose employee is injured loses the "statutory employer" workers' compensation immunity defense and faces direct tort exposure under common-law negligence — typically a far larger exposure than the BWC premium.

The practical compliance rule: every Ohio GC must obtain a **BWC Certificate of Coverage** from every subcontractor before granting site access, must verify the certificate is current at intervals through the BWC's online certificate verification system (bwc.ohio.gov), and must refuse site access to any sub whose certificate has lapsed or whose policy is canceled.

### 5.5 Penalties for failure to carry coverage

Failure of an Ohio employer to maintain BWC coverage exposes the employer to [20][21][22][84][87]:

- **Civil penalty assessments** by BWC, typically computed as twice the premium that should have been paid plus interest.
- **Liability for the full cost of any injury claim** that would have been covered under WC, without the benefit of the WC exclusive-remedy defense — i.e., the employer is exposed to a direct tort action by the injured worker, with no statutory cap on damages.
- **Criminal exposure** under ORC § 4123.99 for willful violations.
- **Inability to obtain public-works contracts.** Ohio public authorities are statutorily prohibited from contracting with non-compliant employers; the BWC publishes lists of non-compliant employers, and public contracts include certificate-of-coverage warranties.

### 5.6 The monopoly-state operational implication

For a contractor crossing into Ohio from another state, the BWC monopoly imposes immediate operational friction:

- **Out-of-state WC policies do NOT satisfy Ohio coverage.** A Tennessee-based contractor whose Tennessee Travelers WC policy "follows" the employees onto an Ohio jobsite does **not** satisfy Ohio's mandatory BWC coverage requirement. The contractor must obtain a separate Ohio BWC certificate for the Ohio operations.
- **BWC enrollment timing.** Initial BWC enrollment requires the **U-3 application** (Application for Ohio Workers' Compensation Coverage), a security deposit (typically the equivalent of one to two reporting periods of estimated premium), and supporting payroll documentation. Allow 2 to 4 weeks for initial enrollment to complete; site work cannot begin until coverage is in place.
- **BWC and out-of-state simultaneously.** A contractor with employees who work part-time in Ohio and part-time in a private-market state must maintain **two parallel WC policies** — Ohio BWC for Ohio payroll, and the private-market policy for non-Ohio payroll. Failure to allocate payroll correctly between the two policies exposes the contractor to audit assessments from both jurisdictions.

## 6. Recent legislative changes (2024-2026)

### 6.1 HB 50 (135th GA) — Home Construction Services Act expansion; effective September 20, 2024

The most consequential 2024 change for Ohio contractors is **HB 50** of the 135th General Assembly, signed by Governor Mike DeWine on **June 28, 2024** and effective **September 20, 2024** [33][34][35]. HB 50 amended ORC Chapter 4722 to:

1. **Expand the definition of "home construction service"** under ORC § 4722.01 to include **the repair, improvement, remodel, or renovation of an existing structure** — bringing residential remodelers (kitchen-bath, additions, finished basements, full-house renovations) within the HCSSA's written-contract requirements for projects ≥ $25,000.
2. **Carve out HCSSA-covered work from the Ohio Consumer Sales Practices Act (CSPA, ORC Chapter 1345)** treble-damages regime for most contracts above $25,000 — substituting the HCSSA's contract-content and excess-cost-notice framework for the CSPA's harsher treble-damages remedy. This was the explicit legislative bargain: bring remodelers under HCSSA contract requirements in exchange for protecting them from CSPA 3x damages.
3. Modify various procedural elements of HCSSA notice and dispute resolution.

The HB 50 expansion is a major change in residential remodeling practice — every Ohio remodeler doing $25,000+ jobs must now operate with HCSSA-compliant written contracts including the $250,000 GL minimum insurance certificate, the excess-cost notice procedure, and the contract-content checklist of § 4722.02(B). Practitioners who continued to use pre-2024 informal contract templates after September 20, 2024 are exposed to HCSSA-violation defenses by homeowners.

### 6.2 OCILB administrative changes (September 2024 and January 2025)

**Experience documentation expansion — effective September 6, 2024.** OCILB administratively expanded the documentation that satisfies the five-year experience requirement under ORC § 4740.06(B)(3) to include **IRS W-2 forms** and **IRS Schedule C** filings, in addition to traditional employer affidavits [15][18][23]. This change materially lowered the documentation barrier for self-employed tradespersons applying for OCILB licensure.

**Electrical contractor annual registration — effective January 1, 2025.** OCILB rules now require **all electrical contractors operating in Ohio** — including residential, commercial, and specialty (temporary electric poles, electric-to-septic systems) — to register annually with OCILB at a **$120 fee** [15][18][23]. The annual-registration regime is additive to the underlying OCILB Electrical Contractor license and is intended to provide consumer-protection tracking across municipal jurisdictions.

### 6.3 BWC rate changes (July 1, 2025)

BWC implemented a statewide **6% private-employer rate cut effective July 1, 2025**, distributed unevenly across NCCI classification codes [87][88]. Construction classifications received smaller cuts than the statewide average, reflecting elevated injury experience in the construction industry.

### 6.4 CAT exclusion expansion (January 1, 2024 and January 1, 2025)

The Commercial Activity Tax annual exclusion increased from $1M (2023) to **$3M (2024)** to **$6M (2025 and thereafter)** under tax-reform legislation enacted in the 134th and 135th General Assemblies [78][79][80][81][82]. The change effectively eliminated CAT liability for the substantial majority of small and mid-size Ohio construction contractors.

### 6.5 Sales tax — Lake County and COTA / Franklin County increases (April 1 and July 1, 2025)

Local sales-tax rates rose in two Ohio jurisdictions in 2025 [69][70][71]:

- **Lake County:** Rate increased from 7.0% to **7.25%**, effective April 1, 2025.
- **Central Ohio Transit Authority (COTA) — Franklin County and portions of Fairfield, Union, Licking, and Delaware counties:** 0.5% increase, effective July 1, 2025.

### 6.6 No pending statewide GC licensure legislation

No bill currently pending in the 136th General Assembly (2025-2026) seriously threatens to introduce a statewide General Contractor license. The Ohio General Assembly has repeatedly considered — and repeatedly rejected — proposals to establish statewide residential GC licensure (most notably in HB 218 of the 132nd GA), and the home-rule preference for municipal contractor licensing remains the prevailing policy. ContractorPro should plan for continued operation in the no-statewide-GC regime through at least the end of the 136th GA.

### 6.7 Public-works retainage / prompt-pay reform — ongoing tracker

Several recent sessions of the General Assembly have considered minor adjustments to the public-works retainage / prompt-pay framework under ORC § 153.13 and § 4113.61. The current 8% retainage cap and 50%-completion-trigger framework has been stable since the 2010s; no consequential legislative change is pending for 2025-2026.

## 7. Practical compliance checklist — the gotchas a casual contractor would miss in Ohio

The following compliance items are the Ohio-specific traps that most distinguish Ohio practice from other large construction states. Each item below maps to a section above.

### 7.1 No statewide GC license — but every city you work in has one

- Ohio has **no statewide General Contractor license**. A GC working in 5 Ohio cities maintains **5 separate municipal registrations or licenses**, each with its own renewal date, bond requirement, insurance endorsement, and fee schedule (§ 1.7).
- Major-city baseline: Columbus $25,000 surety bond + $300 annual fee; Cleveland $150 application + $120 renewal; Cincinnati $131.25 application; Dayton $10,000 city bond for electrical/plumbing (§ 1.7).
- Municipal license **renewal calendar** is the single most-missed compliance obligation in Ohio multi-city practice; a lapse causes immediate stop-work on the city's permitted projects.

### 7.2 OCILB state license required for 5 trades on commercial work — preempts municipal trade licensing

- The five OCILB-licensed trades (electrical, HVAC, refrigeration, plumbing, hydronics) require state licensure for **commercial work** statewide under ORC § 4740.06. Residential work on 1-, 2-, and 3-family dwellings is governed by ORC § 715.27 — municipalities may (but are not required to) accept the state license for residential trade work (§ 1.1, § 1.3, § 1.7).
- OCILB requires **five years of trade experience** (or three years as a licensed engineer), passage of both the PSI trade exam and the PSI Business and Law exam, **$500,000 GL minimum**, and active Ohio BWC coverage (§ 1.4).
- **Electrical contractors must now register annually with OCILB ($120/year)** as of January 1, 2025 — separate from and in addition to the underlying contractor license (§ 1.4, § 6.2).
- One qualifier per entity per trade; license assignment from individual to entity under § 4740.07 is the standard structure (§ 1.5).

### 7.3 BWC monopoly — out-of-state WC policies do NOT cover Ohio operations

- Ohio is one of **four monopoly workers' compensation states**. Workers' compensation insurance must be purchased from the **Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation** — private-market WC carriers do not write Ohio (§ 1.9, § 5).
- A Tennessee, Indiana, Kentucky, or Pennsylvania contractor crossing into Ohio **must** enroll separately with the Ohio BWC for Ohio payroll — the home-state WC policy does not cover Ohio operations (§ 5.6).
- **Construction-industry BWC base rates are among the highest in the country.** Group rating (through a trade association sponsor) and the Drug-Free Safety Program typically combine to cut effective premiums by 30% to 50% off the unbundled NCCI base rate (§ 5.2).
- **Sole proprietors, partners, and certain officers are excluded from default BWC coverage** but may elect in via Form U-3S; elective coverage is reported at 50%–150% of the Statewide Average Weekly Wage (§ 5.3).
- **Project self-insurance under ORC § 4123.35(O)** is available for construction projects above $50M, providing workers' comp immunity for the GC and enrolled subs against the negligence claims of injured workers (§ 5.1).

### 7.4 Mechanic's lien — the 21-day Notice of Furnishing window

- The most-missed deadline in Ohio lien practice is the **21-day Notice of Furnishing** under ORC § 1311.05. Second-tier and lower subs and suppliers must serve the NOF on the owner / owner's designee within **21 calendar days** of first furnishing labor or materials — or lien rights for all work performed more than 21 days before service are **extinguished forever** (§ 2.2).
- First-tier subs in direct privity with the prime, and suppliers in direct privity with the owner, are exempt from the NOF requirement. **Everyone else needs the 21-day NOF** (§ 2.2).
- Affidavit filing windows are **60 days (residential 1-/2-family), 75 days (commercial), and 120 days (oil and gas)** from last furnishing under ORC § 1311.06 — not a uniform deadline (§ 2.3).
- After filing, the affidavit must be served on the owner within **30 days** under ORC § 1311.07 (§ 2.4).
- The owner files the **Notice of Commencement** under ORC § 1311.04 and **posts it at the job site**; subs and suppliers must obtain a copy and identify the owner's designee for valid NOF service (§ 2.1).

### 7.5 Prompt-pay — 10 days from prime's receipt; 18% interest; attorney fees shifted

- Under ORC § 4113.61, the prime contractor must pay each subcontractor and material supplier within **10 calendar days** of the prime's receipt of the corresponding owner progress payment (§ 3.1).
- Late payment by the prime triggers **18% per annum statutory interest** plus **attorney fees** to the prevailing party (§ 3.1).
- **Pay-if-paid clauses are enforceable** as to internal payment timing (per *Transtar Electric*, 2014) but cannot extinguish the sub's mechanic's lien or payment bond rights under ORC § 4113.62(E) (§ 2.9, § 3.1).

### 7.6 Public-works retainage — 8% cap, escrow at 50% completion, no further retainage thereafter

- ORC § 153.13 caps public-works retainage at **8% per progress payment**.
- At **50% completion** (and on projects above $15,000), all retained funds must be deposited in escrow under ORC § 153.63, and **no further retainage may be withheld** on remaining progress payments (§ 3.3).
- Public entity must release retainage within **30 days** of substantial completion; failure triggers 8% per annum interest (§ 3.3).

### 7.7 Residential HCSSA written contract — $25,000 trigger; post-HB 50 includes remodels

- Under ORC § 4722.02, residential work of **$25,000 or more** requires a **written Home Construction Services Contract** containing the supplier's name, business address, taxpayer ID, owner information, project description, completion timeline, total estimated cost, and a **certificate of liability insurance with minimum $250,000 GL** (§ 1.8).
- **HB 50 (effective September 20, 2024)** expanded HCSSA coverage to include **repairs, improvements, remodels, and renovations** of existing residential structures — not just new construction (§ 1.8, § 6.1).
- HCSSA-compliant work above $25,000 is generally **shielded from Ohio Consumer Sales Practices Act treble damages** — the explicit legislative bargain of HB 50 (§ 6.1).
- **Excess costs exceeding $5,000** over the original estimate require pre-work written or oral notice to the owner under ORC § 4722.02(D) (§ 1.8).

### 7.8 Sales tax — contractor is consumer; rate ranges 6.50% to 8.00%

- The construction contractor is the **consumer** of materials incorporated into real property; the contractor pays sales tax at the supply-house or delivery rate (§ 4.2).
- Combined state-plus-local sales-tax rates range from **6.50% to 8.00%** depending on county; Cuyahoga County (Cleveland area) is the highest at **8.00%** (§ 4.1).
- Use **Form STEC CC** (Construction Contract Exemption Certificate) when buying materials for incorporation into a tax-exempt entity's real property project — otherwise the contractor eats the sales tax (§ 4.2).
- **Lake County rate up to 7.25% (April 1, 2025); COTA / Franklin County area up 0.5% (July 1, 2025)** — keep delivery-location rate tables current (§ 4.1, § 6.5).

### 7.9 CAT — most small contractors are now exempt

- CAT annual exclusion increased to **$6 million** for tax year 2025 and thereafter; CAT rate on excess gross receipts is **0.26%** (§ 4.3, § 6.4).
- Many small and mid-size Ohio contractors with under $6M in annual gross receipts have **dropped below CAT liability** entirely as of January 1, 2025 (§ 4.3).
- **Combined affiliate groups** must aggregate Ohio taxable gross receipts across all members; splitting business across affiliated LLCs to avoid CAT does not work (§ 4.3).

### 7.10 Municipal income tax — the often-forgotten obligation

- Most Ohio municipalities impose a **municipal income tax** (typically 1.5% to 2.85%) on net profits earned within the city and on wages paid to workers within the city (§ 4.4).
- A contractor with operations in 5 Ohio cities must file separate municipal income-tax returns in each city, administered through RITA, CCA, or the city directly (§ 4.4).
- Municipal income tax is one of the most common compliance gaps for out-of-state contractors entering Ohio; the city-level filings exist independently of state income-tax filings.

### 7.11 Structural steel — no state license; private-spec governance

- No state license for structural-steel erection. Competency is established on commercial and ODOT projects through AISC 207 (Certified Erector) and 303 (Certified Fabricator), AWS D1.1 (building) or D1.5 (bridge) welder qualification, AWS CWI inspection, and OSHA 10/30 (§ 1.1).
- A structural-steel erector with no other self-performed scope is governed by municipal GC registration where the project is located, not by any statewide license.
