## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Arizona anomaly — every dollar of contracting requires a license

Arizona is one of the most restrictive contractor-licensing states in the country. **Under A.R.S. § 32-1151, no person may "engage in the business of, submit a bid or respond to a request for qualification or a request for proposals for construction services" as a contractor without a contractor's license issued by the Arizona Registrar of Contractors (ROC)** [1][2][3]. Unlike Texas (which has no state general-contractor license at all) or Tennessee (which has a $25,000 threshold below which no license is required), Arizona's default rule is **zero-threshold licensure**: contracting work of any dollar amount requires a license unless one of the narrow exemptions in A.R.S. § 32-1121 applies [1][4].

The ROC is established within Title 32 Chapter 10 and operates under the direction of an appointed Registrar [5][6]. Its principal office is at 1700 W. Washington Street, Suite 105, Phoenix, AZ 85007; ROC telephone (602) 542-1525; web roc.az.gov. The agency licenses, regulates, and disciplines contractors and administers the Residential Contractors' Recovery Fund.

The Arizona licensing structure rests on **three core tiers** [1][6][7]:

1. **Commercial contractors** — work performed for compensation outside residential property lines (the "B-1," "B-2," and "C-" classifications).
2. **Residential contractors** — work performed within residential property lines (the "B," "B-2 dual," and "R-" classifications).
3. **Dual-licensed contractors** — a single license authorizing both commercial and residential work in the same classification (the "KB-" general and "CR-" specialty classifications).

A contractor must hold **every classification** for the trades it self-performs. A general contractor that performs its own HVAC, electrical, plumbing, and structural steel must hold the general license plus each specialty classification. A general that subcontracts those trades to properly licensed subs may operate on its general classification alone, but the prime contractor remains legally liable for the licensure status of its subs under A.R.S. § 32-1154 [8].

### 1.2 General classifications under Arizona Administrative Code Title 4 Chapter 9

Article 1 of Arizona Administrative Code (A.A.C.) Title 4 Chapter 9 defines the scopes of work for every license classification — commercial scopes at R4-9-102, residential scopes at R4-9-103, and dual scopes at R4-9-104 [9][10][11]. The principal classifications relevant to ContrPro's five trades are summarized below.

**General commercial classifications** [1][9][10][12]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **A** | **General Engineering** — fixed works requiring engineering knowledge: highways, roads, railroads, dams, tunnels, bridges, pipelines, drainage, utilities, sewage, levees, mines, harbors |
| **A-General** | General engineering, with sub-classifications A-4 (excavating, grading, oil surfacing), A-5 (paving), A-7 (pipelines), A-9 (asphalt grading), A-11 (drilling), A-12 (sand/rock/gravel), A-14 (sewage and drainage), A-15 (underground utilities), A-16 (water and sewer), A-17 (water well drilling), A-19 (swimming pools), A-21 (waterproofing) |
| **B-1** | **General Commercial Contractor** — commercial buildings and structures of unlimited size and dollar value |
| **B-2** | **General Small Commercial Contractor** — commercial buildings and structures **not exceeding $750,000 per project** (single-job ceiling) [9][13] |

**General residential classifications** [1][11][14]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **B** | **General Residential Contractor** — new residential construction, additions, alterations, and structural repairs to one- and two-family dwellings, townhouses, condominiums |
| **B-2** | **General Residential Contractor — Remodeling and Repair** (when held as a residential B-2 rather than a commercial B-2) — non-structural remodeling and repair of residential structures |
| **B-3** | **General Residential Remodeling Contractor** — remodeling, repair, and renovation work on existing residential structures |
| **B-4** | **General Residential Engineering Contractor** — fixed-works residential engineering: roads, drainage, water lines, septic systems on residential property |

**Dual classifications** [11][15]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **KB-1** | **Dual Building — General Commercial and General Residential** — combination of B-1 + B |
| **KB-2** | **Dual Building — Small Commercial and Residential** — combination of B-2 commercial + B residential |
| **CR-[number]** | Dual specialty — combination of a commercial C-[n] license and the corresponding residential R-[n]; the licensee may perform the scope of either the commercial C- or the residential R- license under a single license number [11][15] |

The B-1, B-2, KB-1, and KB-2 examinations have been **consolidated into a single examination** by the ROC since rule-making in 2022; an applicant for any one of these four classifications sits the unified general-building exam [13][14].

### 1.3 Specialty classifications — the five ContrPro trades

For ContrPro's five self-performed trades — HVAC, electrical, plumbing, sheet metal, and structural steel — the relevant commercial C-, residential R-, and dual CR- classifications are as follows.

**HVAC / Air Conditioning and Refrigeration** [9][10][11][12][16]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **C-39 (Commercial Air Conditioning and Refrigeration)** | Installation, alteration, and repair of air-conditioning, heating, ventilation, refrigeration, and evaporative cooling systems on commercial property, including ductwork, piping, controls, and any solar-augmented HVAC equipment [9] |
| **R-39 (Residential Air Conditioning and Refrigeration)** | Same scope on one- and two-family dwellings [11] |
| **CR-39 (Dual)** | Performs the scope of both C-39 and R-39 under a single license [11] |
| **C-79 / R-79** | Earlier-cycle nomenclature for the same scope, retained for licensees on legacy renewal cycles |
| **C-58 (Sheet Metal)** | Fabrication, installation, alteration, and repair of sheet-metal products and assemblies — HVAC ductwork, roof flashings, gutters, downspouts, vents, exhaust systems; does **not** include air-conditioning installation unless the contractor also holds C-39 [9] |

A contractor performing both rooftop HVAC and the duct fabrication serving it on a commercial tenant build-out typically holds **both C-39 and C-58** — sheet-metal duct work is not within the C-39 scope; HVAC equipment install is not within C-58. ContrPro's five-trade scope therefore generates a routine multi-classification stack on any commercial mechanical job.

**Electrical** [9][11][17][18]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **C-11 (Commercial Electrical)** | Installation, alteration, and repair of electrical wiring, fixtures, appliances, apparatus, raceways, and devices on commercial property; includes high-voltage work; does not include licensed-trade-overlap work reserved to plumbers or HVAC contractors [9] |
| **R-11 (Residential Electrical)** | Same scope on one- and two-family dwellings [11] |
| **CR-11 (Dual)** | Performs the scope of both C-11 and R-11 under a single license [11] |
| **L-67 (Low Voltage Communication Systems)** | Specialty for telecommunications, security, alarm, audio-visual, and other 50-volt-or-less systems |

Note that **Arizona does not issue individual electrician (journeyman or master) licenses at the state level**. Individual electrician licensing is a municipal function in cities that require it (Phoenix, Tucson, others); the ROC licenses only the contracting business and its qualifying party. By contrast, Texas, California, and Tennessee maintain state-level journeyman and master electrician licensure regimes [17].

**Plumbing** [9][11][12][18]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **C-37 (Commercial Plumbing)** | Installation, alteration, and repair of plumbing — pipes, fixtures, water supply (within property lines), drains, vents, sanitary sewer, septic tanks and leaching lines, water-conditioning equipment, swimming-pool plumbing, gas piping, lawn sprinklers, roof leaders; medical-gas piping when properly endorsed [9] |
| **R-37 (Residential Plumbing)** | Same scope on one- and two-family dwellings [11] |
| **CR-37 (Dual)** | Performs the scope of both C-37 and R-37 [11] |
| **C-77 (Plumbing — pre-2018 nomenclature for the same scope)** | Retained for legacy licensees |

Like electrical, **plumbing is not separately licensed at the state level for journeymen and masters**. Compare this to Texas (TSBPE-issued Tradesman, Journeyman, Master, Responsible Master licenses), California (D-Series specialty contractor with no journeyman license), and Tennessee (state-issued Limited Licensed Plumber).

**Structural Steel** [9][11]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **C-2 (Commercial Welding/Structural Steel)** | Erection, alteration, repair, and welding of structural-steel framing, miscellaneous steel, ornamental steel, prefabricated steel structures, joist installation, and metal-deck installation on commercial property |
| **R-2 (Residential Structural Steel)** | Same scope on one- and two-family dwellings |

The C-2 classification covers structural-steel erection and the welding incident to erection; certified welder qualifications under AWS D1.1 are required by most project specifications but are not separately licensed by the ROC. Bridge and highway structural-steel welding for state-bid work is governed by Arizona Department of Transportation specifications layered on top of the C-2 license.

### 1.4 Qualifying party — the experience and exam gate

Every Arizona contractor license requires a designated **qualifying party** under **A.R.S. § 32-1122** and A.A.C. R4-9-105 [19][20]. The qualifying party is the individual whose experience and exam performance satisfies the licensing standard; the license itself is issued to the business entity (sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC, corporation), with the qualifying party named.

**Experience** under A.R.S. § 32-1122(D) [19][20]:
- **Minimum 4 years** of "practical or management trade experience" in the specific construction classification for which licensure is sought.
- **At least 2 of the 4 years must fall within the 10 years immediately preceding application**.
- **Up to 2 years** of technical training at an accredited college or university, or in a manufacturer-accredited training program, may substitute for an equal amount of practical experience.
- The Registrar may reduce the requirement if industry standards justify a lower threshold for a particular specialty.

**Examination** under A.R.S. § 32-1122 [19]:
- **Trade examination** — knowledge of the construction skill required by the classification.
- **Business management examination** — Arizona contractor business administration, building and safety laws, contracting business practices.
- Exams are administered by **PSI Services** (formerly the dedicated ROC vendor; current contract through PSI) on a computer-based testing platform.
- An applicant must take the exam **not more than 2 years before** filing the license application unless a waiver applies (e.g., the qualifying party previously held the same classification within the preceding 5 years).
- **Passing score: 70%** on each exam.

**Restrictions on the qualifying party** [19][21]:
- The qualifying party must not have engaged in unlicensed contracting; a conviction under § 32-1164 for unlicensed contracting creates a 12-month bar on serving as a qualifying party.
- A qualifying party may serve **only one licensee** at a time, except where (a) the licensees share at least 25% common ownership, or (b) one licensee is a subsidiary of another served by the same qualifying party. Serving as qualifying party for multiple unaffiliated entities is a disciplinary violation.
- The qualifying party must "supervise or perform" the contracting work and must not take other employment that "would conflict with the duties as qualifying party or conflict with the ability to adequately supervise" [19].

**Practical risk for ContrPro contractors:** the qualifying party doctrine concentrates personal liability on one individual. If the qualifying party leaves, dies, or is disciplined, the license is suspended until a replacement is named. The ROC offers a **180-day grace period** under § 32-1127(B) to designate a successor before license revocation.

### 1.5 Bonds — A.R.S. § 32-1152 monetary-tier table

Every Arizona contractor must furnish a **license bond or cash deposit** in an amount set under **A.R.S. § 32-1152**, calibrated to the contractor's classification (general vs. specialty), property type (commercial, residential, dual), and **estimated annual gross volume of construction work** [1][22][23][24]. The bond is in favor of the State of Arizona and protects the registrar, the State, and ultimately consumers harmed by contractor misconduct.

**General Commercial Contractor (B-1, B-2)** [22][23][24]:

| Estimated annual volume | Bond / cash deposit (range or fixed) |
|---|---|
| **$10,000,000 and over** | Not less than **$50,000** and not more than **$100,000** |
| **$5,000,000 to $10,000,000** | Not less than **$35,000** and not more than **$75,000** |
| **$1,000,000 to $5,000,000** | Not less than **$15,000** and not more than **$50,000** |
| **$500,000 to $1,000,000** | Not less than **$10,000** and not more than **$25,000** |
| **$150,000 to $500,000** | Not less than **$5,000** and not more than **$15,000** |
| **Less than $150,000** | **$5,000** fixed |

**Specialty Commercial Contractor (C-[n])** [22][23][24]:

| Estimated annual volume | Bond / cash deposit (range or fixed) |
|---|---|
| **$10,000,000 and over** | Not less than **$37,500** and not more than **$50,000** |
| **$5,000,000 to $10,000,000** | Not less than **$17,500** and not more than **$37,500** |
| **$1,000,000 to $5,000,000** | Not less than **$7,500** and not more than **$25,000** |
| **$500,000 to $1,000,000** | Not less than **$5,000** and not more than **$17,500** |
| **$150,000 to $500,000** | Not less than **$2,500** and not more than **$7,500** |
| **Less than $150,000** | **$2,500** fixed |

**General Residential Contractor (B)** [22][23][24]:
- Minimum **$5,000**, maximum **$15,000** depending on volume tier.

**Specialty Residential Contractor (R-[n])** [22][23][24]:
- Minimum **$1,000**, maximum **$7,500** depending on volume tier. A residential specialty contractor with annual volume under $375,000 is at the minimum $1,000 bond; volume above $375,000 raises the bond toward the $7,500 ceiling.

**Dual-license contractors (KB, CR)** [22][23][24]:
- Bond amount equals the **sum** of the bond that would be required for the residential portion plus the bond that would be required for the commercial portion at the contractor's estimated volume tier.

**Cash deposit alternative.** A licensee may post a **cash deposit, certificate of deposit, or marketable securities** in lieu of a surety bond, in the same statutory amount, under § 32-1152(E) [22]. Cash deposits are held by the ROC for the duration of the license plus a "tail" period to allow consumer claims.

**Premium cost.** Industry premium-to-bond ratios run roughly **0.5%–3.0%** of the bond face amount per annum for licensees with strong credit and clean history; rates can climb above 5% for licensees with claims history, weak credit, or new-entity status. A $50,000 commercial general-contractor bond at 1.5% premium runs $750 per year; the same bond for a credit-impaired applicant might run $2,500–$5,000.

### 1.6 Residential Contractors' Recovery Fund — A.R.S. § 32-1131 et seq.

Arizona operates a **Residential Contractors' Recovery Fund** under **A.R.S. §§ 32-1131 through 32-1140**, administered by the ROC [25][26][27]. The fund is the consumer-protection backstop for residential homeowners injured by a licensed residential contractor's violation of the licensing statute. It is the single most consequential structural difference between residential and commercial work in Arizona.

**Funding mechanism** under § 32-1132.01 [25][26][27]:
- Every applicant for a **residential** or **dual** contractor's license, and every renewal applicant in the same classes, pays a **biennial assessment**: currently **$370 at initial licensure** and **$270 at renewal** (reduced from a statutory ceiling of $600 by post-2020 amendments to § 32-1132.01).
- The Registrar may **reduce the assessment by 50%** when the Fund balance exceeds **$15,000,000** at fiscal year-end and **restore the assessment** when the balance falls below **$10,000,000**. As of fiscal year 2024, the Fund balance was approximately $27.6 million [27][28].
- A residential or dual contractor may **opt out** of the Recovery Fund by posting an additional **$200,000 surety bond** ("recovery-fund bond" under § 32-1132). The vast majority of residential licensees pay the assessment rather than post the $200,000 bond.

**Eligible claimants** under § 32-1132 [25][26]:
- An individual who **owns and actually occupies, or intends to occupy as a primary residence**, the damaged residential property.
- A limited liability company that owns the residential property if at least one member occupies or will occupy it as a primary residence.
- A revocable living trust that owns the property if all trustors occupy or will occupy it as a primary residence.
- A **planned community or unit owners' association** when the builder has transferred control and contractor negligence damaged common elements.
- A **lessee** with a direct or indirect contract with the contractor who occupies the property as a primary residence.

**Claim limits** under § 32-1132 and § 32-1139 [25][26]:
- **Maximum award per claim: $30,000**.
- **Maximum aggregate payments per residential contractor license: $200,000**. Once the cap is reached, no further claims may be paid against that license number — but each claimant up to the cap recovers in the order claims are perfected.
- The claim is contingent on the contractor having held an "active, valid" license at one of three points: contract signing, first payment, or commencement of work [25].

**Practical consequence for ContrPro:** the Recovery Fund is **the single most valuable consumer-protection vehicle in Arizona residential contracting** — and it does **not** apply to commercial work. Residential contractors carry an effective insurance-style backstop for their work that commercial contractors do not. The flip side: residential contractor failures concentrate consumer-protection litigation at the ROC, which has substantial discretion to suspend or revoke licenses based on Recovery Fund claim patterns.

### 1.7 Workers' compensation — required for every construction employer

Arizona workers' compensation is governed by **Title 23 Chapter 6 of the Arizona Revised Statutes**, principally **A.R.S. §§ 23-901 et seq.** [29][30][31]. Coverage is administered by the **Industrial Commission of Arizona (ICA)** at 800 W. Washington Street, Phoenix, AZ 85007; (602) 542-4661; azica.gov [31].

**Coverage requirement — A.R.S. § 23-961** [30][32]: An Arizona employer with **one or more regular employees** must secure workers' compensation coverage by one of two methods:

1. **Insuring with an authorized carrier** — the conventional path; the policy must be issued by an insurance carrier admitted by the Arizona Department of Insurance and Financial Institutions or by State Compensation Fund of Arizona (now CopperPoint Insurance, the privatized successor to the State Fund).
2. **Self-insurance** — an employer may apply to the ICA to self-insure if it can demonstrate financial capacity; minimum deposit **$100,000** plus actuarial reserve [32].

The "one or more regular employees" trigger means there is **no minimum-employee threshold and no construction-specific carve-out**: once an employer has its first W-2 employee in Arizona, coverage is mandatory [29][31]. This is materially more conservative than Texas (opt-out non-subscriber rights), Tennessee (5-employee threshold for non-construction), and Florida (1-employee for construction but 4-employee threshold for non-construction).

**Statutory employer doctrine** [31][33]: Under A.R.S. § 23-902, a contractor or upstream party that procures construction work from a person not providing workers' compensation coverage is treated as the **statutory employer** of that uninsured worker for purposes of workers' compensation. The practical effect: a general contractor that engages a subcontractor without verifying coverage is **liable for the workers' compensation claims** of the sub's injured employees as if those employees were its own. Every Arizona general contractor's pre-mobilization checklist therefore includes a **Certificate of Insurance (ACORD) from each subcontractor showing workers' compensation coverage with the GC listed as certificate holder**.

**Coverage applies to** (§ 23-901) [29][31]:
- Full-time, part-time, temporary, and seasonal employees.
- **Minors**, whether legally or illegally employed.
- **Undocumented workers** ("aliens . . . legally or illegally allowed to work for hire").
- Volunteer firefighters and law-enforcement reserves under specified conditions.
- Sole proprietors, working partners, and certain LLC members and corporate shareholders **only if the carrier accepts the election to cover them** (otherwise such persons are not "employees" under the statute).

**Penalty for failure to insure** under § 23-961(L) [32]:
- The employer is **personally liable** for the full amount of any workers' compensation claim plus a 10% penalty.
- A civil penalty of up to **$10,000** per violation, plus the State's prosecutorial costs.
- **Loss of common-law defenses**: under § 23-907, an uninsured employer cannot raise contributory negligence, assumption of risk, or fellow-servant defenses in a tort action by the injured worker. The employer is essentially strict-liable for the injury.

### 1.8 General liability insurance — no statutory state minimum

Unlike Texas (which mandates GL minimums in trade-license rules) or Florida (no state mandate but municipal triggers), Arizona **does not impose a statewide statutory GL insurance minimum** on contractors. GL insurance is enforced through:
- **Project-specific requirements** — owner and general-contractor contracts routinely require subs to carry $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate; many large project specs require $5M/$5M+ with excess/umbrella coverage.
- **ROC discipline** — A.R.S. § 32-1154 lists "Acts or omissions" warranting suspension or revocation, and underinsured contractor failures that injure consumers can support discipline even absent a statutory minimum.
- **Municipal registration requirements** — some cities (Phoenix, Tucson, Mesa, others) require GL coverage as a condition of municipal contractor registration on specific permit types.

For ContrPro contractors, treat $1M/$2M as a de facto floor for commercial work; $1M/$1M occurrence and aggregate as the residential floor; and confirm the actual project specs before bidding.

### 1.9 Unlicensed contracting — A.R.S. § 32-1164 penalties

Unlicensed contracting is a **Class 1 misdemeanor** under **A.R.S. § 32-1164** — the most serious misdemeanor classification under Arizona criminal law [3][34][35].

**Penalties for unlicensed contracting** [3][34][35]:
- **First offense:** Minimum fine **$1,000** plus the 83% statutory surcharge (effective minimum **$1,830**); maximum **$2,500** plus surcharge; up to **6 months in county jail**.
- **Second or subsequent offense:** Minimum fine **$2,000** plus surcharge. The minimum survives any sentence suspension.
- **Mandatory restitution** to the victim — typically calculated as the contract price minus the documented economic benefit received from work performed.
- **Mandatory payment of unpaid TPT** — under § 32-1164(C), the court must require, as a condition of probation, full payment of any **state, county, and municipal transaction privilege or use taxes** owed by the violator arising from the unlicensed work.
- **12-month bar on licensure** under § 32-1122(D)(7) — a person convicted of unlicensed contracting cannot obtain a contractor's license for 12 months after conviction.

**Civil consequences** — most consequential to ContrPro [36][37]:
- **Loss of lien rights** under A.R.S. § 33-981(C) and (E): an unlicensed contractor is **categorically barred** from claiming any mechanic's lien rights. The bar applies even if the contractor would otherwise qualify for the lien — there is no estoppel exception in Arizona.
- **Bar on suit to enforce contract** under A.R.S. § 32-1153: an unlicensed contractor may not bring suit in Arizona court to collect any compensation for any contracting work performed during the period of unlicensure. The bar is jurisdictional and can be raised at any stage.

**Enhanced charges for related conduct:**
- **Fraudulent schemes and artifices** (A.R.S. § 13-2310) — a Class 2 felony — may apply if the unlicensed contractor falsely represented being licensed to obtain the work.
- **Negligent homicide** (A.R.S. § 13-1102) — a Class 4 felony — applies if unsafe unlicensed work causes a fatal injury.

### 1.10 Handyman exemption — § 32-1121(A)(14)

**A.R.S. § 32-1121(A)(14)** — the "handyman exemption" — permits a person who is **not** licensed as a contractor to perform construction or repair work where **all** of the following conditions are met [4][38][39][40]:

1. The aggregate contract price (labor plus materials plus every other item) is **less than $1,000** as of the date last verified (May 2026). The legislature has repeatedly considered raising this threshold — HB 2092 (2024) proposed an increase to **$2,500**, and HB 2120 (2024–2025) proposed an increase to **$3,500** — but **neither bill has been enacted into law as of May 2026**; the threshold remains at **$1,000** [38][39][40]. (Note: ContrPro must confirm the statute on each fresh job — the legislature is actively considering escalation and inflation indexing.)
2. The work is **"casual or minor in nature"** — meaning the project is not a part of a larger construction project, is not a phase of a larger renovation, and does not involve structural or life-safety work.
3. The work **does not require a building permit** under the applicable local code. (This carves out essentially every job involving electrical, plumbing, mechanical, or structural work — those triggering a permit independent of dollar value.)
4. The handyman performing the work **discloses in writing in any advertisement** (and in any solicitation or proposal) that he or she is **"not a licensed contractor"** — disclosure failures convert the exemption into a violation [4][38].

**Carve-outs from the exemption** under § 32-1121(D) [4]: The handyman exemption does **not** apply to:
- **Fire-suppression and fire-safety** work, regardless of dollar value.
- **Gas piping or gas appliance installation**, regardless of dollar value.
- Work requiring a building, electrical, plumbing, or mechanical permit.

**Practical consequence for ContrPro:** the handyman exemption is irrelevant to almost every job in ContrPro's five trades — HVAC, electrical, plumbing, sheet metal, and structural steel all trigger permits and all involve life-safety-coded work. A licensed contractor that subs out a small portion of work to an unlicensed handyman risks both (a) the handyman's own § 32-1164 misdemeanor exposure and (b) the licensed contractor's discipline under § 32-1154 for aiding unlicensed contracting.

### 1.11 Other § 32-1121 exemptions

The full § 32-1121 list of persons not required to be licensed includes 18 categories [4]:
1. Federal, state, county, and municipal employees acting within the scope of public employment.
2. Public officers and court officers acting under legal authority.
3. Public utilities and licensed pipeline operators acting under franchise or operating authority.
4. Materialmen, retailers, and finished-product sellers (with embedded labor incidental to delivery — under-$1,000 thresholds in some subparts).
5. Owners performing work on their own property for their own occupancy (the "owner-builder exemption") — but not when the work is on property being prepared for sale or lease, and not when an owner-builder repeatedly relies on the exemption to construct multiple units.
6. Developers contracting with licensed contractors for construction within a development.
7. Architects, engineers, and registered land surveyors performing pre-construction work within the scope of their professional license.
8. Pest-management contractors regulated by the Office of Pest Management.
9. Condominium and apartment-complex employees performing routine maintenance for the complex (limited to 4-unit complexes for some scopes).
10. Sole-proprietor employees of an exempt owner.
11. Surety and insurance companies acting to fulfill bond obligations using licensed contractors.
12. Casual or minor workers under $1,000 contract value (the handyman exemption above).
13. Licensed professionals (e.g., licensed real-estate brokers performing inspection-related work within the scope of the real-estate license).
14. Gardeners and landscape laborers (non-structural).
15. Alarm-system installation agents licensed under the Department of Public Safety.
16. Cable, telecommunications, and broadband installers acting under their separate authorities.

### 1.12 Local authority — § 32-1101.01

Under **A.R.S. § 32-1101.01**, a city, town, or county may **not require** a contractor licensed by the ROC to also obtain a municipal occupational license or pay a municipal occupational license fee to engage in contracting within the municipal jurisdiction [41]. Cities **may** require a transaction privilege tax (sales tax) license and may require **building permits** under their adopted construction codes; what they cannot do is impose a duplicative occupational-licensing scheme on a state-licensed contractor.

This is a **state preemption** of municipal contractor licensing — materially more favorable to multi-city contractors than Texas (where Dallas, San Antonio, Houston, and Austin all maintain independent registration regimes that overlap with the state trade licenses).

### 1.13 Out-of-state contractor — temporary endorsement

A.R.S. § 32-1122.01 permits the Registrar to issue a **temporary or reciprocal license** to a contractor licensed in another state with comparable licensing requirements. Reciprocal arrangements exist (informally rather than by formal compact) with California, Nevada, New Mexico, and Utah for certain trades. The applicant must satisfy the Arizona bond and insurance requirements and pass the **Arizona Business Management Examination** (the trade exam may be waived if the home state's exam was substantively equivalent within 5 years).

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — A.R.S. Title 33 Chapter 6 and Chapter 16

Arizona mechanic's lien law is principally at **Arizona Revised Statutes Title 33 Chapter 6, Article 6 (§§ 33-981 through 33-1006)** [36][42][43]. Additional procedural rules for the stop-notice remedy are at **Title 33 Chapter 6 Article 7 (§§ 33-1051 through 33-1067)** [44].

Two structural points dominate any Arizona lien analysis and distinguish Arizona from most other states:

1. **No homestead-style total protection against statutory liens for commercial work**, but **near-total protection against subcontractor liens on owner-occupied residences** — the "owner-occupied" defense at § 33-1002 is the most consequential consumer-protection rule in Arizona construction-finance law.
2. **The 20-Day Preliminary Notice is a near-universal precondition** to lien rights — the rule applies to virtually every claimant other than wage laborers, and missing the notice deadline forfeits lien rights as to any work furnished more than 20 days before the notice is finally served.

### 2.1 Who can claim a lien — A.R.S. § 33-981

Under **A.R.S. § 33-981(A)**, lien rights extend to: "Every person who labors or furnishes professional services, materials, machinery, fixtures or tools in the construction, alteration or repair of any building, structure or improvement . . . for the work or labor done or professional services, materials, machinery, fixtures or tools furnished, whether the work was done or the articles were furnished at the instance of the owner of the building, structure or improvement, or his agent" [36][42].

This sweeps in:
- **Original contractors** in direct privity with the owner.
- **Subcontractors and sub-subcontractors** of any tier.
- **Material suppliers**, equipment lessors, and tool suppliers.
- **Design professionals** (architects, engineers, registered land surveyors) — but only when the design professional has an agreement either with the owner or with an architect, engineer, or contractor who has an agreement with the owner. There is no general lien right for design services performed without a contract chain to the owner.
- **Laborers** (wage workers).

**Agent doctrine** under § 33-981(B) [36][42]: Every contractor, subcontractor, architect, builder, or other person having charge or control of the construction "is the agent of the owner for purposes of this article, and the owner shall be liable for the reasonable value of labor or materials furnished to his agent." The agent doctrine is what gives subcontractors and suppliers a direct lien remedy against the owner notwithstanding the absence of contractual privity.

**Categorical bar on unlicensed contractor liens** under § 33-981(C) and (E) [36][37]: A person required to be licensed under Title 32 Chapter 10 who does not hold a valid license **has no lien rights**. The bar applies to both the right to file the lien and the right to enforce it; an unlicensed contractor who files a lien anyway is subject to slander-of-title and § 33-420 liability for groundless recording.

### 2.2 The 20-Day Preliminary Notice — A.R.S. § 33-992.01

The **Preliminary 20-Day Notice** is the centerpiece of Arizona lien practice. Under **A.R.S. § 33-992.01**, every person other than a wage laborer who furnishes labor, professional services, materials, machinery, fixtures, or tools to a construction project **must serve a written Preliminary 20-Day Notice** as a precondition to perfecting any subsequent lien rights [45][46][47][48].

**Who must serve the notice:** Every person furnishing labor, professional services, materials, machinery, fixtures, or tools — including original contractors in direct privity with the owner. The only carve-out is for laborers performing labor for wages [45][46].

**Recipients of the notice — four mandatory recipients under § 33-992.01(B)** [45][46][47]:
1. **The owner** or reputed owner of the property.
2. The **original contractor** or reputed contractor.
3. The **construction lender**, if any (defined as a mortgagee, deed-of-trust beneficiary, or other party providing funds for construction costs).
4. The **person with whom the claimant contracted** for the labor or materials (if different from the original contractor).

**Deadline — within 20 days of first furnishing** under § 33-992.01(E) [45][46][48]:
- The notice must be served **within 20 days after the claimant first furnishes** labor, professional services, materials, machinery, fixtures, or tools to the jobsite.
- A claimant who **misses the 20-day deadline** is **not precluded** from serving a late notice — but the lien is limited to labor or materials furnished within the **20 days preceding** the late service plus all materials furnished thereafter. Materials and labor furnished more than 20 days before the late notice are forfeited.

**Service method — three permitted methods under § 33-992.01(G)** [45][46][47]:
1. **First-class mail with a USPS certificate of mailing**.
2. **Registered mail** (USPS).
3. **Certified mail** (USPS, return receipt requested recommended but not required for the notice itself).

**Email, fax, and personal hand delivery are not authorized methods for § 33-992.01 service** — even though they may otherwise be reliable means of delivery, they do not satisfy the strict-compliance standard for preliminary notice [45]. (Note: hand delivery is sometimes accepted by practice but is not endorsed by the statute; carriers should rely only on the three statutory methods.)

**Content of the notice under § 33-992.01(C)** [45][46][47]:
1. A **general description** of the labor, professional services, materials, machinery, fixtures, or tools furnished or to be furnished.
2. The **estimated total price** of the labor, services, materials, etc.
3. The **name and address** of the person furnishing the labor or materials.
4. The **name of the person who contracted** for the labor or materials.
5. A **legal description, street address, or other description sufficient to identify the jobsite** — landmark identification is permissible but the legal description from the recorded deed is the safest.
6. The **statutory mechanic's lien warning** in **bold type** — the warning must be reproduced verbatim from the statute and warns the owner that failure to ensure subcontractors are paid may result in a mechanic's lien on the property.

**Single-notice rule under § 33-992.01(H)** [45]: A single Preliminary 20-Day Notice satisfies the requirement for **all subsequent work and materials** furnished to the same jobsite by the same claimant, **unless** (a) the estimated total price increases by 30% or more, or (b) the claimant changes the subcontractor or sub-supplier providing labor or materials (so that a different upstream chain emerges). When either trigger occurs, a **new or amended Preliminary 20-Day Notice is required** within 20 days of the change.

**Amendment for owner-supplied jobsite information under § 33-992.01(J)–(K)** [45]: The owner has 10 days from the claimant's written request to supply accurate jobsite information (legal description, lender identity, construction-loan information). A claimant who receives corrected information must file an **amended notice within 30 days** of receipt. Failure of the owner to timely provide bond or lender information **preserves** the claimant's lien rights — the claimant is not penalized for stale information the owner refused to update.

**Practical trap — first-furnishing date controls.** The 20-day clock starts at **first furnishing**, not at the project award or subcontract signing. A material supplier that delivers a single item to the jobsite on Day 1 must serve the notice by Day 21, even if the bulk of the delivery is scheduled six months later. ContrPro contractors who fail to identify their actual first-furnishing date risk a partial forfeiture of lien rights covering everything before the late-notice cutoff.

### 2.3 The owner-occupied dwelling defense — A.R.S. § 33-1002

The most consequential consumer-protection rule in Arizona lien practice is the **owner-occupied dwelling defense** at **A.R.S. § 33-1002** [49][50][51][52].

**Rule:** **"No lien provided for in this article shall be allowed or recorded . . . against the dwelling of any person by any person other than a person having executed in writing a contract directly with the owner-occupant"** [49][50].

**Effect:** On an owner-occupied one- or two-family dwelling, **only the direct contractor with a written contract signed by the owner-occupant** has lien rights. Subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, and material suppliers — **even those who fully complied with the Preliminary 20-Day Notice** — have **no lien rights** against the dwelling.

**Definitions under § 33-1002(A)** [49][50]:

- **"Dwelling"** — real property constructed (or to be constructed) with a building designed for single one-family or single two-family residential purposes, including a unit in a horizontal property regime (condominium).
- **"Owner-occupant"** — a natural person who, **before construction commences**: (a) holds legal or equitable title to the dwelling under a recorded deed or contract for conveyance, and (b) **resides or intends to reside** in the dwelling for at least **30 days during the 12-month period immediately following completion**, with **no intent to sell or lease** the dwelling.

**Waiver void under § 33-1002(C)** [49][50]: "Any provision of an agreement made or entered into by an owner-occupant which waives the provisions of this section is void." The owner-occupant cannot consent to subcontractor liens; the protection is non-waivable and runs with the dwelling.

**Practical consequence for ContrPro:**
- On any owner-occupied residence, the **only viable lien claimant is the original contractor with a written contract directly executed by the owner-occupant**. Every subcontractor and supplier on the project must look to (a) the original contractor for payment, (b) the original contractor's bond if posted under § 33-1003, or (c) a **stop-notice claim against construction funds** under the Title 33 Chapter 6 Article 7 stop-notice regime (see §2.7 below).
- A subcontractor that **records a lien against an owner-occupied dwelling has filed a wrongful lien** and is exposed to **§ 33-420 liability** — $5,000 per occurrence or actual damages (whichever is greater), plus attorney's fees, plus discipline before the ROC under § 32-1154(A)(20) for "filing a false or fraudulent claim of lien."
- For ContrPro contractors performing residential remodel work, the **structure of the contract chain dictates lien viability**. A GC that performs work directly under contract with the homeowner has full lien rights; a sub-tier mechanical contractor working under the GC has **no lien rights** against the dwelling itself, only a stop-notice right against undisbursed construction-loan funds.

The owner-occupied defense does **not** apply to:
- **Commercial property** (any non-residential improvement).
- **Investment residential property** — single-family or duplex held for rental, flipping, or sale. Owner-occupied requires the natural-person owner to occupy as a primary residence.
- **Multifamily properties of 3+ units** (apartment buildings, larger condos).
- **Property under construction by a builder/developer for resale** (the developer is not an "owner-occupant").

### 2.4 Lien priority — A.R.S. § 33-992 — relation-back to commencement of work

Arizona follows the **relation-back doctrine** under **A.R.S. § 33-992**: a mechanic's lien attaches not when it is recorded but when **labor was first commenced** or **materials were first furnished** to the property [53][54].

**Rule:** Mechanic's liens "are preferred to all liens, mortgages or other encumbrances upon the property attaching subsequent to the time the labor was commenced or the materials were commenced to be furnished." All valid mechanic's liens on a single project **share the same priority date** — the date of first visible commencement — regardless of when each individual claimant filed its lien.

**The construction-lender exception under § 33-992(B)** [53][54]: A mortgage or deed of trust securing a loan from a "construction lender" (defined at § 33-992.01(A)(1)) has **priority over mechanic's liens** if the mortgage or deed of trust is **recorded within 10 days after** labor commenced or materials were first furnished. The 10-day window is the construction-lender's protection: the lender records before, simultaneous with, or within 10 days after commencement to defeat the mechanic's-lien priority.

**Professional-services lien priority under § 33-992(C)** [53]: A lien for **professional services** (architecture, engineering, surveying) does **not attach to the property for priority purposes** until labor has commenced or materials have been furnished such that "it is apparent to any person inspecting the property that construction . . . has commenced." The rule prevents architectural and engineering services performed months in advance of any visible construction from establishing priority over construction lenders.

**Separate-improvements rule under § 33-992(D)** [53]: When the construction work consists of "improvements not included in the construction contract," each separate improvement has its own commencement date and its own priority date. A construction sequence (foundation contract, then framing contract, then finishes contract) does not share a single commencement date if the contracts are independent.

### 2.5 Filing the lien — A.R.S. § 33-993 — 120/60-day deadlines

Once notices are in order, the claimant must record a **Notice and Claim of Lien** with the **county recorder** in the county where the property is located under **A.R.S. § 33-993** [55][56][57].

**Recording deadline:**

| Trigger | Deadline |
|---|---|
| **Project completion (default rule)** | **120 days** after completion |
| **Notice of Completion recorded by owner under § 33-993(B)** | **60 days** after recordation of the Notice of Completion |
| **No building permit was issued (residential homestead work)** | 120 days after last labor or materials furnished |

**Definition of "completion" under § 33-993(C)** [55][57]:
- **Thirty (30) days after final governmental inspection and acceptance**, or
- **Sixty (60) consecutive days without labor** on the work (excluding suspension due to strikes, material shortages, or weather), or
- **The last date labor or materials were furnished** if no building permit was issued (typical for residential repairs).

The owner's filing of a **Notice of Completion under § 33-993(B)** is the most consequential lien-shortening event in Arizona practice. A recorded Notice of Completion **cuts the 120-day deadline to 60 days** — but only if the owner properly serves the notice on every party from whom a Preliminary 20-Day Notice was received, within 15 days of recordation, by certified or registered mail. **Failure to serve renews the full 120-day clock** for any party not served [55][57].

**Content of the Notice and Claim of Lien under § 33-993(A)** [55][57]: Under oath, the claim of lien must contain:
1. **Legal description of the property** sufficient for identification.
2. **Name of the owner** (or reputed owner) and the **name of the person by whom the claimant was employed** or to whom the claimant furnished labor or materials.
3. The **terms and conditions of the contract** (or a copy if written) — including the agreed scope, price, and payment terms.
4. The **amount the claimant demands** after applying all credits and offsets.
5. The **date of completion** of the work.
6. The **date the Preliminary 20-Day Notice was given** to the owner, with proof of service attached.
7. The signature of the claimant or claimant's agent.

**Service of the lien — § 33-993(D)** [55][57]: The claimant must serve a copy of the recorded lien on the property owner by certified or registered mail within a reasonable time after recording (in practice, within 5 business days is the safe-harbor rule for sophisticated practitioners). Failure to serve does not invalidate the lien per se but it estops the claimant from claiming damages for the owner's intervening transactions.

### 2.6 Foreclosure — A.R.S. § 33-998 — six-month, non-extendable deadline

**A.R.S. § 33-998** sets the suit-to-foreclose deadline at **six (6) months** after the lien is recorded [58][59][60].

**Rule:** A mechanic's lien "shall not continue for a longer period than six months after it is recorded, unless action is brought within that period to enforce the lien and **a notice of pendency of action is recorded** in the office of the county recorder" [58][59].

**The suit-and-record requirement is dual:**
1. **File the foreclosure action** in Arizona Superior Court within 6 months of lien recordation.
2. **Record a notice of pendency of action (lis pendens) under A.R.S. § 12-1191** in the same county within the same 6 months.

**Failure of either step extinguishes the lien by operation of law** — the lien is "expired" and the property owner may treat the lien as discharged. The 6-month deadline is **statutory and non-extendable**: parties cannot contractually extend it. The Arizona Court of Appeals has repeatedly refused to enforce private extensions beyond the statutory 6 months [58][60].

**Attorney's fees under § 33-998(B)** [58][59]: "In any action to enforce a lien granted under this article, the court may award the successful party reasonable attorney fees." Fee-shifting is discretionary, not mandatory, and applies to either the lien claimant or the successful defending owner.

**Cross-claim or answer doctrine under § 33-998:** Filing an answer or cross-claim asserting the lien "shall be deemed the commencement of an action" — so a lien claimant who is named as a defendant in a related foreclosure action can preserve the lien by cross-claiming within the 6-month period without filing an independent foreclosure suit.

### 2.7 Stop-notice remedy — A.R.S. § 33-1051 et seq.

Arizona's **stop-notice** remedy under **Title 33 Chapter 6 Article 7 (§§ 33-1051 through 33-1067)** is a **parallel alternative** to the mechanic's lien — and is essential where the lien itself is barred by the owner-occupied defense [44][61][62][63].

**The stop notice:** A written, signed, verified statement served on the **property owner** and/or the **construction lender** notifying them that the claimant has not been paid and demanding that they withhold construction funds in the amount of the claim from disbursements to the original contractor [44][61].

**Bonded vs. unbonded stop notice — § 33-1058** [44][61][62]:
- **Bonded stop notice** — accompanied by a surety bond equal to **150% of the claim**, issued by a surety company authorized in Arizona. A bonded stop notice **binds the construction lender** and triggers a duty to withhold construction-loan funds.
- **Unbonded stop notice** — binds only the owner; the construction lender may ignore an unbonded stop notice.

For most claimants, the **bonded stop notice** is the meaningful remedy: it freezes the construction lender's disbursements and forces the lender to interplead the disputed funds.

**Precondition — Preliminary 20-Day Notice required under § 33-1056(B)(1)** [44][61]: A claimant's right to file a stop notice is conditioned on the prior service of a valid § 33-992.01 Preliminary 20-Day Notice. A claimant that failed to serve a Preliminary 20-Day Notice has **no stop-notice rights**.

**Timing — § 33-1058** [44][61][63]:
- The stop notice must be served **before the time expires to record a mechanic's lien** — that is, before the **120th day** after completion (or **60th day** after a Notice of Completion).
- After service, the stop notice perfects a right to **proceed against withheld funds** under § 33-1062 within the time the claimant would otherwise have had to foreclose a lien.

**Demand for stop-notice service under § 33-1054** [44][63]: An owner or construction lender may serve a **written demand for service of stop notice** by certified mail in bold-face type. The claimant has **30 days** from receipt of the demand to serve the stop notice; failure to serve within 30 days forfeits stop-notice rights.

**Practical use for ContrPro:** On owner-occupied residential work where § 33-1002 bars subcontractor liens, the **bonded stop notice** is the principal collection remedy. A mechanical contractor working under a residential general contractor that has been paid by the homeowner but has not paid the sub uses the bonded stop notice to reach the construction lender's withheld funds (if a construction loan exists) or to force the owner to interplead.

### 2.8 Payment bond in lieu of lien rights — A.R.S. § 33-1003

Under **A.R.S. § 33-1003**, a property owner may **post a payment bond** for the benefit of subcontractors, suppliers, and laborers, **in lieu of subjecting the property to lien claims** [64].

When a payment bond is recorded under § 33-1003:
- The owner-occupied dwelling cannot be liened by anyone — even the direct-contract original contractor.
- Subcontractors and suppliers must instead make claims against the **payment bond**.
- The bond amount must equal the contract price between the owner and the original contractor.
- The bond is governed by Arizona surety-bond practice; claims must be perfected within the bond's statutory period (typically 12 months from the principal's last work).

For ContrPro contractors on owner-occupied residences where the owner has posted a § 33-1003 payment bond, the **bond replaces all lien and stop-notice rights** — the sub must claim against the bond surety, not the property.

### 2.9 Wrongful lien — A.R.S. § 33-420

A claimant who records a **groundless, false, or fraudulent** lien is liable under **A.R.S. § 33-420** [65]:

- The lesser of **$5,000** or **three times the actual damages** caused by the recording, plus
- **Reasonable attorney's fees and costs**.

The §33-420 liability applies in addition to ROC discipline under § 32-1154(A)(20). A common ground for §33-420 liability: a subcontractor recording a lien against an owner-occupied dwelling notwithstanding the § 33-1002 bar.

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

Arizona's prompt-payment regime is split across **two principal statutory tracks** — private works under **A.R.S. §§ 32-1181 through 32-1188** and public works under **A.R.S. §§ 32-1129 through 32-1129.07** [66][67][68][69][70]. Public-works retainage is separately governed by **A.R.S. § 34-221** (state-level public works) [71][72].

The two tracks share the same architectural skeleton — a 14-day owner approval window, a 7-day owner-to-prime payment window, a 7-day prime-to-sub flow-down, and a 1.5%-per-month statutory interest rate — but diverge in the rules on retainage, in the public-works billing-certification mechanics, and in the residential exemption.

### 3.1 Private works — A.R.S. §§ 32-1181 through 32-1188

**Statutory authority:** A.R.S. §§ 32-1181 through 32-1188 [66][68][69]. The private prompt-payment regime applies to all construction contracts on private (non-public) Arizona projects, with a narrow exception for owner-occupied dwelling construction (see § 3.4 below).

**Owner-to-prime payment under § 32-1182** [66][67][68][69]:
- The owner must **issue and pay** each progress payment to the prime contractor based on the prime's monthly billing or estimate.
- A billing is **deemed certified and approved 14 days** after the owner receives it, **unless** the owner issues a written statement detailing the specific items not certified and the reasons within those 14 days.
- After certification, the owner must **issue payment within 7 days**.

The architecture creates a hard ceiling: **21 days from billing receipt to mandatory payment** unless the owner perfects a written withholding within the first 14.

**Prime-to-subcontractor flow-down under § 32-1183** [66][68][70]:
- The prime contractor must pay each subcontractor and supplier its proportionate share of the owner's payment **within 7 days** of the prime's receipt of payment from the owner.
- The 7-day cascade applies down every tier: each subcontractor that receives payment must pay its own sub-subcontractors and suppliers within 7 days.

**Withholding — permitted grounds under § 32-1183(B)** [70]: A contractor may **withhold payment certification** for any of the following specified reasons:
1. Unsatisfactory job progress.
2. Defective construction work or defective materials not remedied.
3. Disputed work or disputed materials.
4. Failure of the subcontractor to make timely payments for labor, equipment, and materials.
5. Damage caused by the subcontractor or supplier to the owner or to another contractor.
6. Reasonable evidence that the subcontract cannot be completed for the unpaid balance of the subcontract price.
7. Reasonable evidence demonstrating that the subcontractor will probably be unable to complete the subcontract.
8. Third-party claims that may result in liability to the contractor or owner.

**Written statement requirement under § 32-1183(D)** [70]: When withholding payment, the contractor must **prepare and issue a written statement within 14 days** that explains in reasonable detail the reasons for withholding. Failure to issue a timely written withholding converts the withholding into a default approval, and the entire claim is due.

**Interest — § 32-1184** [66][68][70]:
- Late payments accrue interest at **1.5% per month** (effective **18% per annum**) on the unpaid balance from the day after the payment was due until payment is made.
- The interest rate cannot be reduced by contract; it is a statutory minimum.

**Suspension and termination rights under § 32-1185** [66][73]:
- A **contractor** may suspend performance or terminate the contract for the owner's failure to make timely certified payment, after **at least 7 calendar days' written notice** to the owner (unless the contract specifies a shorter notice period).
- A **subcontractor** may suspend or terminate for the owner's failure to pay AND the contractor's flow-through failure, after **at least 3 calendar days' written notice** to the contractor and owner [73].
- A **subcontractor** may suspend or terminate for the contractor's failure to pay despite owner payment, after **at least 7 calendar days' written notice** [73].
- A **subcontractor** may suspend or terminate when the owner refuses to certify or approve billing for reasons unrelated to the subcontractor's work, after **at least 7 calendar days' written notice** [73].

A party suspending under § 32-1185 is **not deemed in breach** and is entitled to recover reasonable mobilization, demobilization, and remobilization costs on return to work after payment is made [73].

**Attorney's fees under § 32-1185(F)** [73]: The prevailing party in a prompt-payment dispute under §§ 32-1181 through 32-1188 may recover **reasonable attorney's fees and costs** — fee-shifting is mandatory for the prevailing party.

### 3.2 Public works — A.R.S. §§ 32-1129 through 32-1129.07

**Statutory authority:** A.R.S. §§ 32-1129 through 32-1129.07 [66][67][68][69]. Applies to construction contracts entered into by a **public agent** — defined to include any Arizona state agency, county, city, town, school district, special-purpose district, university, or other political subdivision.

**Owner (public agent) to prime payment under § 32-1129.01** [66][67][74]:
- The public agent must make progress payments to the prime contractor based on the prime's billing or estimate.
- A billing is **deemed certified and approved 14 days** after the public agent receives it, unless the agent issues a **written statement within 14 days** detailing the items not certified.
- After certification, the public agent must **pay within 7 days**.
- (The architecture is identical to the private-works track in § 32-1182.)

**Prime-to-sub flow-down under § 32-1129.02** [66][68][74]:
- Same **7-day flow-down** rule as for private works: a contractor receiving payment from the public agent must pay subcontractors and suppliers within 7 days.

**Interest under § 32-1129.01(M)** [66][67][74]: **1.5% per month** on overdue amounts — same rate as private works.

**Suspension and termination under § 32-1129.04** [66][67]: Parallels § 32-1185 for the private regime — 7-day notice for contractors, 3- or 7-day notice for subcontractors depending on the cause.

**Owner-occupant residential exemption under § 32-1129.07** [66][75]: The Arizona public-works prompt-payment regime does **not apply** to construction of a one- or two-family dwelling for an owner-occupant. The private-works regime under §§ 32-1181 et seq. likewise has an owner-occupant carve-out — the practical effect is that **prompt-payment statutory remedies do not run against an owner-occupant for work on the owner-occupant's primary residence**. The owner-occupant is free to negotiate any payment terms; contractors must rely on contract law and lien/stop-notice remedies (subject to the § 33-1002 residential restriction).

### 3.3 Public-works retainage — A.R.S. § 34-221

**A.R.S. § 34-221** governs retainage on public-works contracts in Arizona [71][72][76].

**Retainage rate — § 34-221(D)** [71][72][76]:
- The **public agent must withhold 10%** of each progress payment as retainage.
- **At 50% project completion**, retainage **reduces to 5%** at the contractor's request, "if the contractor is making satisfactory progress on the contract and there is no specific cause or claim requiring a greater amount to be retained" [71][72]. The reduction is contractor-initiated; it does not happen automatically — the contractor must request the reduction at the 50% milestone.
- **At 50%, the previously withheld retainage is reduced by half** — i.e., one-half of the amount retained to that point is released to the contractor on request.

**Substitute securities — § 34-221(C)** [71][72][76]: In lieu of retainage withholding, the contractor may elect to substitute:
- Money-market accounts at Arizona-licensed banks
- Demand deposit accounts
- Time certificates of deposit at Arizona-licensed banks
- U.S. Treasury securities or U.S.-guaranteed securities
- State of Arizona securities, county securities, municipal securities, or school-district securities
- Savings-and-loan shares of associations authorized in Arizona

The substituted securities must equal 10% of all progress estimates and the contractor is **entitled to receive all interest or income** earned by the substituted securities as it accrues.

Subcontractors and suppliers under the prime contractor are entitled to provide their own substituted securities to the prime, on terms similar to those between the prime and the public agent, in lieu of subcontract retainage.

**Final release of retainage — § 34-221(F)** [71][72]:
- Retainage (or substituted security) must be **returned within 60 days after final completion and acceptance** of the work, provided the contractor has furnished:
  - **Satisfactory receipts** for all labor and materials billed, and
  - **Waivers of liens** from any persons holding claims against the work.
- If the public agent fails to release retainage within the 60-day window without justification, the **1.5% per month** statutory interest under § 32-1129 begins to run.

**Job-order, CMAR, and design-build exemptions** [71][76]: Under A.R.S. § 34-609, the standard 10/5 retainage rule **does not apply** to:
- **Job-order contracts** — no retention required (§ 34-609(7)).
- **Construction-manager-at-risk (CMAR) contracts** — public agent may elect no retainage.
- **Design-build contracts** — public agent may elect no retainage.

These exemptions reflect the modern Arizona public-works procurement methods and substantially reduce retainage exposure on alternative-delivery projects.

### 3.4 Private retainage

Arizona **does not statutorily cap private-works retainage**. The 10% public-works rule under § 34-221 has no statutory analog for private projects. Practical norms:
- **Owner-prime retainage:** Industry standard 5%–10% per progress payment; the contract sets the rate.
- **Prime-to-sub retainage:** Limited under § 32-1183(B) — a contractor cannot withhold from a subcontractor an amount of retainage **exceeding** the retainage actually withheld by the owner from the contractor for that subcontractor's work. A sub-retainage cap above the owner's retainage is unenforceable.
- **Release timing:** Contract-driven; the prompt-payment statute requires release within 7 days of the prime's receipt of the corresponding retainage release from the owner.

### 3.5 ADOT prompt-pay — Title 28 § 28-411

For **Arizona Department of Transportation (ADOT) highway and bridge contracts**, **A.R.S. § 28-411** applies as a parallel prompt-payment regime [77]. The ADOT-specific rules generally track the Title 32 Chapter 10 architecture (14-day approval, 7-day payment, 7-day flow-down) but include design-professional-specific provisions added by amendment in 2015. ContrPro contractors performing ADOT highway, bridge, or related transportation work should track § 28-411 alongside § 32-1129.

## 4. Tax structure for contractors — Arizona Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT)

### 4.1 The TPT — what it is and is not

Arizona's **Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT)** is the single most distinctive feature of doing business as a contractor in Arizona [78][79][80][81][82][83]. It is **not** a sales tax. It is a **tax on the vendor for the privilege of doing business in Arizona**, calculated on the vendor's **gross receipts**, separately classified by business activity. The contractor — not the customer — is the taxpayer.

**Functional similarities to sales tax:**
- The contractor is permitted to pass the TPT through to the customer as a separate line item on the invoice.
- The rate is collected on each transaction.
- The receipts are reported to and remitted to the Arizona Department of Revenue (ADOR).

**Functional differences from sales tax:**
- The contractor remains liable for the TPT even if the customer fails to pay the pass-through amount.
- The TPT is measured against the contractor's gross income from the contract, not the price negotiated with the customer.
- There is **no resale exemption** for materials incorporated into real property under the Prime Contracting classification — the contractor pays TPT on its gross income whether or not it has paid sales tax on its material purchases.

**Administered by:** **Arizona Department of Revenue (ADOR)**, 1600 W. Monroe Street, Phoenix, AZ 85007; (602) 255-3381; azdor.gov [83].

### 4.3 Prime Contracting Classification — A.R.S. § 42-5075

The **Prime Contracting Classification (business code 015)** is the core construction-industry classification under **A.R.S. § 42-5075** [78][79][81][84]. It applies to:

- **Contracting** — modification of any building, highway, road, railroad, manufactured building, or other structure, project, development, or improvement of real property [78][79][81].
- **Dealership of manufactured buildings**.

**Tax base — 65% of gross income under § 42-5075(B)** [78][79][81][84]:

The Prime Contracting tax base is **65% of the gross proceeds of sales or gross income** derived from the contract. The other **35% is a standard statutory deduction** — generally understood as a proxy for labor cost, which Arizona does not tax under the prime contracting classification.

The contractor computes the TPT by:
1. Determining **gross income** from the construction contract (the total price the customer pays).
2. Applying any **statutory deductions** under § 42-5075(B) (see § 4.4 below).
3. Applying the **35% standard deduction** to the post-statutory-deduction base.
4. Multiplying the resulting **65% tax base** by the combined state + county + city TPT rate.

**Numerical example.** A Phoenix commercial contractor performs a $1,000,000 modification project (new tenant build-out) for a private owner. The contractor's gross income is $1,000,000. After the 35% standard deduction, the tax base is $650,000. At a combined Phoenix prime contracting rate of approximately 8.6% (5.6% state + 0.7% Maricopa + ~2.3% Phoenix), the TPT is **$650,000 × 8.6% = $55,900**, which is the contractor's tax liability for the project. The contractor typically passes this through to the customer as a separate line on the invoice, with proper TPT classification.

### 4.4 Statutory deductions under § 42-5075(B)

A.R.S. § 42-5075(B) lists numerous deductions that reduce gross income **before** the 35% labor deduction is applied [78][79][81][84]:

1. **Land sales** at fair market value (excluded from contracting base).
2. **Groundwater measuring devices and monitoring wells** required by ADWR rules.
3. **Furniture, furnishings, and fixtures** not permanently incorporated into the structure.
4. **Aerospace and aviation work in qualifying military reuse zones** (with prior letter of qualification from ADOR).
5. **Environmental remediation contracts**.
6. **Installation of machinery or equipment with independent functional utility** (e.g., process equipment that does not become part of the real-property improvement).
7. **Post-construction pest-control treatment**.
8. **Development fees** paid by the contractor for off-site public infrastructure.
9. **Mixed-waste processing facilities** on landfills.
10. **Sales of manufactured buildings** as personal property (taxed under a separate classification).
11. **Energy storage equipment** and certain renewable-energy installations (rolling expansions of this carve-out).

A contractor failing to track and claim these deductions overpays TPT — the deductions are not automatic, and the ADOR will not refund overpaid TPT older than 4 years (the standard refund statute of limitations under A.R.S. § 42-1118).

### 4.5 The MRRA exclusion — A.R.S. § 42-5075(P) and (Q)

**Maintenance, Repair, Replacement, or Alteration (MRRA)** activities are **specifically excluded from the Prime Contracting Classification** under **A.R.S. § 42-5075(P) and (Q)** [85][86][87]. The MRRA carve-out is the single most consequential prime-contracting distinction under Arizona TPT law.

**MRRA defined:**
- **Maintenance** — scheduled, periodic activity that does not extend the useful life or value of the improvement (cleaning, lubrication, inspection, calibration).
- **Repair** — restoration of damaged or worn property to its prior functional state without modification.
- **Replacement** — like-for-like substitution of a component within an existing improvement (e.g., a failed water heater replaced with an equivalent unit).
- **Alteration** — modification or upgrade of an existing improvement that does not constitute "modification" of the real property as a whole. ADOR uses dollar thresholds: under certain rules, an alteration that does not exceed 25% of the property's value (or comparable thresholds) is presumed alteration rather than modification.

**Tax treatment of MRRA contracts:**
- **Prime contractor performing an MRRA project: not subject to Prime Contracting TPT.** The gross receipts from an MRRA contract are excluded from the § 42-5075 prime contracting classification [85][86][87].
- **Materials: contractor is the consumer.** The MRRA contractor pays **retail TPT** on materials purchased for the project at the point of sale. If materials are purchased exempt (e.g., across state lines from an out-of-state vendor), the contractor must **remit the retail TPT equivalent** on the cost of those materials using ADOR business code **315** [85][87].
- **Form 5000M** — non-TPT-licensed MRRA contractors may purchase materials tax-free using ADOR Form 5000M (Arizona Resale Certificate for Non-TPT Licensed Contractors), but only where a statutory deduction otherwise exists for the tax-exempt purchase and the materials will be used in an MRRA project [85][87].

**Modification vs. MRRA — the distinction:**
- **"Modification"** means new construction, improvement, movement, wreckage, or demolition of any building, highway, road, railroad, excavation, manufactured building, or other structure or improvement. New construction and major tenant build-outs are modification work — subject to Prime Contracting TPT.
- **"MRRA"** means maintenance, repair, replacement, or alteration of existing real property where the work does not constitute modification. A water-heater swap, a roof patch, a parking-lot crack-seal — these are MRRA work and not subject to prime contracting TPT.

The ADOR offers extensive **Contracting Guidelines** at azdor.gov to draw the line case-by-case [85][86][87]. Misclassification is one of the most common ADOR audit findings against contractors — prime-contracting classification claimed but actually MRRA (with overpaid TPT and underpaid material tax), or MRRA classification claimed but actually prime (with underpaid prime contracting TPT). ContrPro contractors should obtain written ADOR rulings on borderline classifications for high-value contracts.

### 4.6 Form 5005 — Contractor's Certificate

The **Arizona Form 5005 (Contractor's Certificate — Prime Contracting or Tax Equivalent on MRRA)** is the central document that allocates tax liability between a prime contractor and its subcontractors [88][89]. Issued by the prime contractor and provided to each subcontractor under its control, Form 5005:

- **Validates the subcontractor's exemption** from Prime Contracting TPT on the underlying transaction (the subcontractor is not a "prime contractor" for the project and is not the taxpayer).
- For **MRRA projects**, validates the subcontractor's exemption from the retail-TPT-equivalent liability on materials incorporated into the project.
- **Establishes the prime contractor's liability** for the Prime Contracting TPT (or MRRA-equivalent tax). The prime contractor that issues the Form 5005 assumes the tax liability for the subcontracted scope.

**Effect:** A subcontractor that holds a valid Form 5005 from its prime contractor is **not subject to Prime Contracting TPT** on its receipts from the subcontract. The subcontractor's gross income is recorded but excluded from the TPT base. Without a Form 5005, the subcontractor may itself be treated as a prime contractor on its scope and liable for the full prime contracting TPT.

**Limitations:**
- The Form 5005 **does not entitle the subcontractor to purchase materials tax-free from a materials vendor**. The subcontractor still pays retail TPT at point of sale on materials it directly purchases.
- The Form 5005 may be issued as a **blanket certificate** for an ongoing relationship or as a **project-specific certificate**. Blanket certificates must specify exceptions and have a defined end date or termination event.
- The Form 5005 may have **retroactive effect** if signed within **4 years** of project completion, matching the standard ADOR refund / assessment statute of limitations.

### 4.7 Subcontractor exemption — A.R.S. § 42-5075(E)

Under **A.R.S. § 42-5075(E)**, a subcontractor is **not subject to Prime Contracting TPT** on the gross income from a contract that is within the control of a properly registered prime contractor [78][79][81]. The exemption operates as follows:

- The subcontractor must demonstrate that the work was **within the scope of a contract held by a prime contractor**.
- The **prime contractor remains liable** for the Prime Contracting TPT on the **prime contractor's gross income** (including amounts paid to the subcontractor).
- The subcontractor must hold the Form 5005 from the prime to substantiate the exemption on audit.

The subcontractor is **not exempt from TPT** in two situations: (a) where the subcontractor is, in substance, a co-prime (e.g., a multi-prime delivery method where each "subcontractor" actually contracts directly with the owner); (b) where the prime contractor fails to register or fails to remit TPT — in which case the subcontractor may itself be the taxpayer of last resort under collection-priority rules.

### 4.8 Use tax — A.R.S. § 42-5155

Materials purchased **out of state** and brought into Arizona for use in an Arizona project are subject to **Arizona Use Tax** at the **combined state + county + city** TPT-equivalent rate under **A.R.S. § 42-5155** [78][79][82]. This is a routine ADOR audit target for multi-state contractors — particularly structural-steel fabricators, mechanical-equipment installers, and electrical-equipment installers who buy from out-of-state mills, manufacturers, and distributors. The use tax exists to neutralize the incentive to source materials from low-or-no-sales-tax states.

**Practical compliance:** Out-of-state purchases trigger a Use Tax filing obligation on the **TPT-1 form**. The use tax is in addition to the Prime Contracting TPT (or MRRA materials retail-TPT-equivalent) that already applies to the project. Failure to file is a Class 2 misdemeanor under A.R.S. § 42-1127 and exposes the contractor to **double-tax assessment** plus penalties on audit.

### 4.9 TPT licensing — A.R.S. § 42-5005

Every contractor making sales subject to TPT — including every prime contractor performing modification work and every MRRA contractor purchasing materials — must obtain a **Transaction Privilege Tax License** from ADOR under **A.R.S. § 42-5005** [78][83]. The TPT license is the contractor's registration for TPT purposes; it is separate from the ROC contractor's license.

**Fees:** State TPT license — $12 application, no annual renewal fee. Each Arizona city in which the contractor will engage business may also require a city TPT license through ADOR's consolidated city-tax program; city fees range from $0 to $50 per year depending on the city's rate structure.

**Penalty for unlicensed operation:** Class 2 misdemeanor under A.R.S. § 42-1127 plus daily civil penalty until license is obtained.

### 4.10 No state corporate income tax distinction; Arizona has a state income tax

Unlike Texas (no income tax, franchise/margin tax instead) or Tennessee (no broad income tax), Arizona imposes a **state corporate income tax** under A.R.S. Title 43 and a **state personal income tax** (currently a flat **2.5%** as of 2026) on residents and on Arizona-source income of nonresidents. The contractor's federal income, after Arizona apportionment, is the basis for Arizona corporate or personal income tax — this is a routine compliance burden but is not contractor-specific.

## 5. Recent changes (2024–2026)

The Arizona Legislature is bicameral and meets in regular session each year beginning the second Monday of January, typically running 100–120 days. The 56th Legislature (2023–2024) and 57th Legislature (2025–2026) have considered numerous construction-industry bills; the survey below identifies what was enacted versus what was proposed but not enacted, focusing on changes that affect ContrPro's compliance posture.

### 5.1 HB 2092 (2024) — handyman exemption increase — NOT ENACTED

**HB 2092** (56th Legislature, 2nd Regular Session) proposed to increase the handyman exemption threshold under A.R.S. § 32-1121(A)(14) from **$1,000 to $2,500**, and to raise the minimum civil penalty for unlicensed contracting from $200 to $500 per violation [38][39][40]. The bill was introduced by Representative Hendrix (LD 14) on December 21, 2023, and progressed through committee but **did not pass the full legislature**. The threshold remains at **$1,000** as of May 2026.

### 5.2 HB 2120 (2024–2025) — handyman exemption to $3,500 with inflation indexing — NOT ENACTED

**HB 2120** proposed to increase the handyman exemption threshold to **$3,500** across multiple sections of Title 32 Chapter 10 (handyman, materialman, finished-product seller, single-undertaking, etc.) and to direct the Registrar to annually adjust the threshold for inflation [38]. The bill carried over into the 2025 session and remained pending at the close of the 57th Legislature 1st Regular Session in June 2025. Status: **not enacted** as of May 2026.

**Practical posture for ContrPro:** Continue to treat **$1,000** as the operative handyman threshold for compliance and contracting decisions. Track HB 2120 and successor bills in the 57th Legislature 2nd Regular Session — there is bipartisan interest in raising the threshold, and an inflation-adjusted increase is plausible in the 2026 or 2027 session.

### 5.3 SB 1087 (2025) — Recovery Fund assessment adjustment — VETOED

**SB 1087** (57th Legislature, 1st Regular Session, 2025) proposed to amend A.R.S. § 32-1132.01 to formalize the **Recovery Fund assessment-reduction trigger**: when the Fund balance exceeds $15,000,000 at fiscal year-end, the biennial assessment would automatically reduce by 50% (to $185 initial / $135 renewal from the current $370 / $270); restoration would occur when the Fund balance fell below $10,000,000 [27][28]. **Governor Hobbs vetoed the bill on April 23, 2025**. The Recovery Fund assessment remains at **$370 (initial) / $270 (renewal)** through the 2026 biennial cycle.

**Practical posture:** No change in residential or dual contractor licensing fees. The $200,000 surety bond opt-out under § 32-1132 remains available.

### 5.4 ADOR TPT rate adjustments — City of Maricopa rate increase

**Effective October 1, 2025**, the **City of Maricopa** (Pinal County) increased its general TPT rate by **0.5%**, raising the city's prime contracting (business code 015) rate from approximately 2.0% to **2.50%**. The combined Maricopa prime contracting rate as of January 2026 is approximately **8.8% (5.6% state + 1.1% Pinal County + 2.5% city + ~0.5% applicable other)** [82][83].

**Practical posture:** ContrPro contractors with active work in the City of Maricopa (Pinal County) must update bid templates and invoice-classification routines. Refer to the ADOR monthly TPT Rate Tables for any subsequent rate changes.

### 5.5 No major construction lien or prompt-pay legislative changes in 2025

A review of the 57th Legislature 1st Regular Session (January 13 – June 27, 2025) bill tracker [90][91][92] identifies **no enacted amendments** to:
- Title 33 Chapter 6 (mechanic's lien law) — §§ 33-981 through 33-1006.
- Title 33 Chapter 6 Article 7 (stop-notice law) — §§ 33-1051 through 33-1067.
- Title 32 Chapter 10 Article 5 (private prompt-pay) — §§ 32-1181 through 32-1188.
- Title 32 Chapter 10 Article 2 prompt-pay subsections — §§ 32-1129 through 32-1129.07.
- Title 34 Chapter 2 (public-works contracts) — § 34-221.

The 2025 session focused on housing-policy reforms (HOA/CC&R restrictions on design mandates), small-claims-court jurisdictional thresholds, and planned-community lien foreclosure thresholds — none of which affect construction-industry prime contracting.

### 5.6 Bills proposed but not enacted — verification

A review of the 57th Legislature 1st Regular Session bill tracker [90][91] identifies the following construction-relevant bills filed but not enacted, which should not be relied upon as law:

- Proposed legislation to raise the **handyman exemption** to $3,500 (HB 2120) and to $2,500 (HB 2092 carryover) — both not enacted.
- Proposed legislation to **reduce Recovery Fund assessments** (SB 1087) — vetoed.
- Proposed amendments to **mechanic's lien deadlines** — multiple proposals filed; none advanced past committee.
- Proposed legislation to **cap private retainage** at 5% — did not advance past committee.

### 5.7 Discrepancy resolutions

Several persistent secondary-source errors are worth flagging:

- Some published guides reference an **$1,000 to $3,500 handyman exemption increase** as if enacted. **HB 2120 did not pass**; the threshold remains $1,000.
- Some published treatments of the **Recovery Fund assessment** continue to reference the historical $600 biennial maximum. The current statutory amount is **$370 (initial) / $270 (renewal)** under post-2020 amendments, subject to the 50% reduction trigger that has applied since the Fund balance exceeded $15 million.
- Some commentary refers to **Arizona's "small claims" jurisdiction** as applicable to mechanic's lien foreclosures. **It is not** — mechanic's lien foreclosure must be filed in Arizona Superior Court regardless of amount; the small-claims jurisdiction is limited to specified actions under A.R.S. Title 22 Chapter 5.
- Some "Arizona contractor licensing guides" cross-reference a **state-level electrician journeyman license** that does not exist. Arizona does **not** issue state-level individual electrician or plumber licenses — those are municipal or unregulated. The ROC licenses the contracting business and its qualifying party only.

## 6. Practical compliance checklist for an Arizona contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**

- **Confirm ROC license status** for the contractor's classification(s):
  - Verify license number on roc.az.gov; verify "active" status, no suspension or revocation, current bond, and no open complaints.
  - Confirm **every self-performed trade has its own classification** — a B-1 with self-performed HVAC requires C-39 (and C-58 for ductwork); self-performed electrical requires C-11; self-performed plumbing requires C-37; self-performed steel requires C-2.
  - Confirm the **qualifying party's status** — the QP must be active, named on the license, and not concurrently QPing for unrelated entities.
- **Confirm the bond amount** matches the contractor's estimated annual volume tier under § 32-1152 — under-bonding is a discipline violation.
- **Confirm GL and Workers' Compensation insurance** are in force.
  - GL: no statutory state minimum; project-specific minimums via contract.
  - WC: required for every employee under § 23-961; verify carrier authorization, no premium delinquencies, and current certificate.
- **Verify subcontractor licenses and WC coverage** before issuing any subcontract.
  - **Statutory employer doctrine**: GC is liable for an uninsured sub's worker injuries under § 23-902. Require ACORD with GC named as certificate holder; require ROC license number; verify status on roc.az.gov.
- **Confirm TPT license status** — TPT license must be active for the contractor; for MRRA work, the contractor must be prepared to pay retail TPT on materials.
- **Confirm city TPT license** for every city in which the contractor will perform work — each city's TPT is administered through ADOR's consolidated city-tax program.
- **Owner-occupied dwelling analysis:** Determine whether the project property is an **owner-occupied one- or two-family dwelling** under § 33-1002. If yes:
  - Only the direct-contract contractor has lien rights — subcontractors and suppliers have none.
  - The stop-notice remedy under § 33-1051 et seq. is the principal collection backup for subs.
  - If the owner posts a § 33-1003 payment bond, subs claim against the bond rather than file lien or stop notice.

**Within 20 days of first furnishing — the Preliminary 20-Day Notice (§ 33-992.01):**

- **Serve the Preliminary 20-Day Notice** on the owner, original contractor, construction lender (if any), and the person with whom the claimant contracted.
- **Service method:** First-class mail with USPS certificate of mailing, registered mail, or certified mail. **Not email, fax, or hand delivery.**
- **Content:** General description of work, estimated total price, claimant name/address, contracting party, jobsite description, and the bold statutory warning notice.
- **Track the first-furnishing date precisely** — the 20-day clock starts when materials are first delivered to the jobsite or labor first performed on the jobsite, regardless of when the subcontract was signed.
- **If estimated price increases by 30% or more, or the upstream chain changes, serve an amended notice** within 20 days of the change.

**During the work — payment cycle:**

- **Private works (A.R.S. §§ 32-1181–1188):**
  - Owner must approve/reject prime billing within **14 days**; pay within **7 days** thereafter.
  - Prime must pay subs **within 7 days** of receipt from owner.
  - Interest on overdue: **1.5%/month (18% APR)**.
  - Suspension rights after notice: **7 days for contractors**, **3 days for subs (joint owner+prime failure)**, **7 days for subs (prime-only failure)**.
- **Public works (A.R.S. §§ 32-1129–1129.07):**
  - Same 14-day approval, 7-day payment, 7-day flow-down architecture.
  - Owner-occupant residential dwelling: **exempt** from prompt-pay statutory remedies under § 32-1129.07.
  - ADOT-specific track under A.R.S. § 28-411 applies to highway and bridge work.
- **Retainage:**
  - Public works under § 34-221: **10% retainage** until **50% complete**, reducing to **5%** at the contractor's request thereafter; substituted securities permitted; **60-day final release** post-acceptance.
  - Private works: contract-driven; sub-retainage cannot exceed owner-retainage on the corresponding scope; prompt-pay rules require release within 7 days of receipt.
  - CMAR, job-order, and design-build contracts under § 34-609: retainage exempted or reduced.

**Lien recordation — A.R.S. § 33-993:**

- **Default deadline:** Record the Notice and Claim of Lien within **120 days** of completion.
- **Shortened deadline:** **60 days** after recordation of a Notice of Completion (provided the owner properly serves it within 15 days on every § 33-992.01 noticed party).
- **Completion defined:** 30 days after final governmental inspection; or 60 consecutive days without labor; or last date of labor/materials if no permit issued.
- **Content:** Legal description, owner identity, contract terms, demand amount, completion date, Preliminary 20-Day Notice date with proof of service, claimant signature, notarization.
- **Service:** Certified or registered mail to owner within a reasonable time after recording.

**Foreclosure — A.R.S. § 33-998:**

- **File suit and record notice of pendency of action within 6 months** of lien recording.
- **Non-extendable** by contract — parties cannot extend the statutory deadline.
- **Forum:** Arizona Superior Court for the county where the property is located.
- **Attorney's fees:** Discretionary to the prevailing party under § 33-998(B).

**Stop-notice — A.R.S. § 33-1051 et seq.:**

- **Preliminary 20-Day Notice must have been served** to support a stop notice.
- **Bonded stop notice** required to bind a construction lender — surety bond at **150% of claim**.
- **Timing:** Before the lien-recordation deadline (120 days, or 60 days if Notice of Completion).
- **Demand for service of stop notice:** If demanded by the owner or lender, must be served within 30 days of demand.

**Wrongful liens — A.R.S. § 33-420:**

- Do not record a lien against an owner-occupied dwelling unless the contractor has a written contract directly with the owner-occupant.
- Liability: lesser of $5,000 or 3× actual damages, plus attorney's fees, plus ROC discipline under § 32-1154.

**Tax compliance — Transaction Privilege Tax:**

- Confirm **business classification**: Prime Contracting (modification) vs. MRRA (maintenance/repair/replacement/alteration).
- Prime Contracting: report on TPT-1 under **business code 015**; apply 35% standard deduction; pay TPT on 65% of gross income at the combined state+county+city rate.
- MRRA: pay retail TPT on materials at purchase; report under **business code 315** for any materials acquired exempt (out-of-state, etc.).
- **Form 5005 chain:** Issue Form 5005 to each subcontractor on prime contracting projects to confirm the sub is not the taxpayer.
- **Use tax:** File and pay use tax on out-of-state materials brought into Arizona under § 42-5155.
- **City TPT:** Maintain city TPT licenses in each city of operation via ADOR consolidated city-tax program.

**Workers' compensation — A.R.S. § 23-961:**

- Carry WC insurance covering every employee from first hire.
- Verify sub WC coverage on every subcontract; require ACORD with GC as certificate holder.
- Statutory employer exposure under § 23-902 for uninsured-sub injuries.
- Industrial Commission of Arizona claims administration; carrier reports filed with the ICA.

**Annual / biennial compliance:**

- **Renew ROC contractor's license biennially** — application/license fees up to $500 (residential) or $1,500 (commercial) under § 32-1126; Recovery Fund assessment $270 for residential/dual at renewal; verify bond renewal.
- **File ADOR TPT-1 returns** monthly or quarterly based on volume; remit Prime Contracting TPT and city TPT.
- **Renew GL and WC insurance certificates** with the ROC and provide updated COI to any owner or upstream contractor required as a contractor of record.
- **Continuing education:** Arizona ROC does **not** impose mandatory continuing education for license renewal (unlike Texas, California, Florida). License renewal is administrative — submit application, pay fee, post bond, maintain insurance.
