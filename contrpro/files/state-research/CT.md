## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Connecticut structure — DCP-centralized, three parallel contractor registrations, statewide trade licensing, and a per se CUTPA overlay

Connecticut is **structurally unusual** among the fifty states in maintaining **three parallel, mutually exclusive contractor registration regimes**, each independently administered by the **Connecticut Department of Consumer Protection ("DCP")** under three separate chapters of Title 20:

1. The **Home Improvement Contractor ("HIC") registration** under the **Home Improvement Act ("HIA"), Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-418 et seq.** (Chapter 400), governing all work on **existing owner-occupied residential property**;
2. The **New Home Construction Contractor ("NHCC") registration** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417a et seq.** (Chapter 399a), governing **new residential construction** of one- to four-family dwellings; and
3. The **Major Contractor registration** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-340 et seq.** (Chapter 400, subsidiary to the broader DCP occupational-licensing framework), governing **larger non-residential ("major") contracting work** above prescribed dollar thresholds.

A given Connecticut contractor will frequently need **two or more** of these registrations to do business across the residential and commercial sectors, in addition to whatever **trade licenses** (electrical, plumbing, heating, cooling, sheet metal, sprinkler, elevator) are required for any mechanical scope and the **Workers' Compensation coverage** mandated by Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-275 et seq. for every employer with even a single employee [1][2][3][4][5][6][7][8].

The single most important consumer-protection statute is the **Connecticut Unfair Trade Practices Act ("CUTPA")**, codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-110a et seq.**, which makes "unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce" unlawful and authorizes **punitive damages (effectively treble in practice), attorney fees, and costs** to prevailing plaintiffs in private actions under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-110g** [9][10][11][12]. The HIA expressly designates **every violation of the HIA as a per se CUTPA violation** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-427(c)**, creating an automatic statutory link between contractor compliance and treble-damages exposure that closely parallels the New Jersey CFA/CRA regime and the Massachusetts c. 93A/c. 142A regime. The combined effect: **a technical procedural defect on a Connecticut home improvement contract is an automatic CUTPA violation regardless of harm** [9][10][12][13][14][15].

For ContractorPro's five trades, the practical Connecticut map is:

The takeaway: Connecticut **centralizes all contractor licensing and registration in DCP**, **licenses every mechanical trade** at the state level on a three-tier (contractor / journeyperson / apprentice) basis, **does not issue any general-contractor competency license**, and instead uses **two or three parallel registration regimes** (HIC, NHCC, Major Contractor) layered over a **CUTPA per se enforcement engine**. The HIC + NHCC distinction is the dominant compliance gotcha and is enforced aggressively in litigation [1][2][3][4][5][6][7][8][9][12][13][14][15].

### 1.2 Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-418 et seq. and R.C.S.A. § 20-427-1 et seq.

The **Home Improvement Act ("HIA")**, codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-418 through § 20-432**, was enacted as **Public Act No. 88-269** ("An Act Concerning Home Improvement Contractors") in 1988 and has been amended on multiple occasions, including substantial expansions to the contractor-disclosure and contract-form rules in Public Acts 95-311, 99-65, 03-176, 08-119, 13-247, 17-89, 19-117, 21-37, 23-101, and 24-39 [1][2][3][4][13][14][15][16]. It is administered by the **Department of Consumer Protection ("DCP")** pursuant to regulations at **R.C.S.A. §§ 20-427-1 through 20-427-13** and is the practical centerpiece of Connecticut residential remodeling regulation alongside the NHCC for new construction.

#### 1.2.1 Who must register — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-420 and § 20-419 definitions

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-420(a)**, "no person shall hold himself out to be a contractor or salesman without first obtaining a certificate of registration from the commissioner [of DCP]" [1][2][3]. The statutory terms are defined at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-419**:

- **"Home improvement"** is defined broadly to include "the repair, replacement, remodeling, alteration, conversion, modernization, improvement, rehabilitation or sandblasting of, or addition to any land or building or that portion thereof which is used or designed to be used as a private residence, dwelling place or residential rental property, or the construction, replacement, installation or improvement of driveways, swimming pools, porches, garages, roofs, siding, insulation, solar energy systems, flooring, patios, landscaping, fences, doors and windows, and waterproofing in connection with such land or building or that portion thereof which is used or designed to be used as a private residence, dwelling place or residential rental property in which the total cash price for all work agreed upon between the contractor and owner exceeds two hundred dollars" [1][2].
- **"Owner"** means "a person who owns or resides in a private residence and includes any agent thereof, including, but not limited to, a condominium association. An owner of a private residence need not be an occupant of the residence."
- **"Contractor"** means "any person who owns and operates a home improvement business or who undertakes, offers to undertake or agrees to perform any home improvement."
- **"Private residence"** means "a single family dwelling, multifamily dwelling consisting of not more than six units, or a unit, common element or limited common element in a condominium . . . or in a common interest community."

The **scope of "home improvement"** under the HIA is intentionally broad and covers **most work on existing residential property of one to six dwelling units**, including **rental property** (Connecticut, unlike Massachusetts, does **not** limit HIC to owner-occupied residences) [1][2][14]. The HIC scope **does not** cover (and HIC registration is **not** required for):

1. **New home construction** — explicitly excluded; new construction is regulated under the **NHCC** statute, Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417a et seq. (see § 1.3 below) [4][5].
2. **Buildings of seven or more dwelling units** — outside "private residence" definition.
3. **Owner-occupied dwellings on the owner's own property, performed by the owner** without compensation (homeowner-as-contractor on his own home).
4. **Architects, engineers, and similar licensed design professionals** acting only within their licensed scope.
5. **Licensed plumbers, electricians, HVAC, sheet metal, and other tradespersons** who hold a Connecticut trade license, **but only for work strictly within the scope of the trade license** — a trade contractor who undertakes work outside the licensed trade (e.g., a plumber who also installs cabinetry) must register as an HIC for the non-trade portion.
6. **Contractors performing work under a contract price of $200 or less** (below the statutory threshold in § 20-419(4)).
7. **Department-of-Banking-supervised mortgage lenders** acting on foreclosure-related work.

The Connecticut Supreme Court and Appellate Court have construed the HIA broadly in favor of consumer protection. In **Habetz v. Condon, 224 Conn. 231 (1992)**, the Supreme Court held that the HIA contract-writing requirements are **strictly construed**, and that a contractor who fails to comply cannot enforce the contract against the homeowner in the ordinary course [13]. The narrow "bad faith" exception articulated in *Habetz* and its progeny — that a homeowner who acts in bad faith to evade payment cannot use HIA non-compliance as a sword — is heavily litigated and applied sparingly [13][14][15].

**Penalty for unregistered home improvement contracting — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-427**.

Performing home improvement work without an HIC registration is **a violation of CUTPA as a matter of law** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-427(c)**, exposing the unregistered contractor to **CUTPA punitive damages (effectively up to treble compensatory damages), attorney fees, and costs** under § 42-110g in any subsequent consumer claim, **and** to a separate criminal misdemeanor under § 20-427(b) (Class B misdemeanor for a first offense, Class A misdemeanor for a subsequent offense), **and** to civil administrative penalty up to **$500 per violation** under § 20-427(b)(2). DCP additionally has authority under § 20-427(b)(3) to issue a **cease-and-desist order** and to seek injunctive relief in the Superior Court [9][10][12][14].

In addition, under the Connecticut Supreme Court's holding in **Barrett Builders v. Miller, 215 Conn. 316 (1990)**, an unregistered contractor (or registered contractor with a non-compliant contract) is **completely barred from recovering on the contract** — including quantum meruit and unjust enrichment recovery — against the homeowner in most cases [13][14]. This bar to recovery is among the harshest contractor-side consequences of any state HIA-equivalent regime in the country and is materially worse than the Massachusetts c. 142A or NJ CRA-CFA bars (which generally permit quantum meruit recovery for work performed in good faith).

#### 1.2.2 Registration requirements — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-421 and R.C.S.A. § 20-427-1 et seq.

Registration with DCP requires [1][2][3][14][15][16]:

1. **Registration fee** — currently **$220 per year** for HIC under DCP's fee schedule (set by R.C.S.A. § 20-427 and DCP fee orders; the registration period is one year); historically lower, increased in stages by Public Acts 03-176, 08-119, and 21-37.
2. **Guaranty Fund assessment** — every contractor must pay into the **Home Improvement Guaranty Fund** established by **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-432**. The Fund is the consumer's last-resort recovery mechanism for unsatisfied judgments against HIC-registered contractors. The assessment is set periodically by DCP based on Fund balance and historical claim experience. As of 2026, the Fund maintains a per-claim recovery limit (see § 1.2.6 below) and an aggregate per-contractor limit under § 20-432(e) [1][2][14][16].
3. **Disclosure of the contractor's full legal name, dba names, business address, telephone number, federal employer identification number (FEIN), and date of incorporation or formation.**
4. **Disclosure of all owners, officers, directors, and persons with a 10% or greater ownership interest**, including full names and residential addresses, under R.C.S.A. § 20-427-2.
5. **Criminal background disclosure** — the contractor and each disclosed principal must disclose all criminal convictions (other than minor motor-vehicle violations) within the previous ten years. DCP may deny registration under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-422 for convictions involving fraud, deceit, financial dishonesty, or material misrepresentation in the trade.
6. **Disclosure of any prior registrations** in Connecticut or other states, including suspensions, revocations, and disciplinary actions, and any pending complaints with DCP.
7. **Workers' Compensation and Liability Insurance disclosure** — under Public Act 23-101 amendments to § 20-421, the registration application requires disclosure of WC carrier and policy number (mandatory under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-275 et seq. — see § 5) and the contractor's commercial general liability insurance carrier, policy number, and policy limits, if any. Connecticut, unlike New Jersey (which mandates $500,000 GL under N.J.A.C. 13:45A-17.12), does not statutorily require a minimum GL limit for HIC, but the disclosure requirement creates a downstream consumer-protection record.
8. **The Registration Number issued by DCP must be prominently displayed on:**
   - All contracts, advertisements, and proposals (R.C.S.A. § 20-427-4);
   - All written estimates, invoices, and correspondence;
   - All commercial vehicles used in the home improvement business (Connecticut, unlike New Jersey, does not currently impose a specific vehicle-lettering minimum-size requirement, but DCP enforcement letters have flagged advertising lacking the registration number);
   - All website and digital marketing materials (Public Act 19-117 confirmed the digital-marketing scope);
   - Door hangers, mailers, and door-to-door solicitation materials.

#### 1.2.3 Written contract — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-429 — required for any home improvement above $200 / $500 (door-to-door)

Connecticut is among the **most demanding** states on the **content** of the written contract for residential work. Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-429(a)**, every home improvement contract **must be in writing**, must be **signed by the owner and contractor**, and must contain at minimum [1][2][13][14][15][16][17][18]:

1. **The entire agreement** between the owner and the contractor;
2. **A starting date** and a **completion date**;
3. **A description of the work to be done** and the materials to be used;
4. **The total contract price** agreed upon for all work to be performed under the contract;
5. **A copy of the document executed by the contractor evidencing the owner's right of cancellation** as required by **Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 42-134a through 42-141** (Connecticut Home Solicitation Sales Act — applies to any contract solicited at a place other than the contractor's place of business, with a **three business-day cooling-off right** that must be conspicuously disclosed);
6. **The contractor's registration number** issued by DCP, conspicuously placed;
7. **A notice of the owner's right to cancel any contract obtained through a home solicitation sale within three business days** of execution (separate from the general cooling-off right, this notice must be in a specified statutory form);
8. **A notice that the owner may contact the DCP** to inquire about the contractor's registration status and complaint history (R.C.S.A. § 20-427-7);
9. **All express warranties, guarantees, and representations** (R.C.S.A. § 20-427-7);
10. **Signature of the owner and the contractor**;
11. **Any change to the contract or work**, including all change orders, **must be in writing and signed by both parties** under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-429(a)(7) — oral change orders are **unenforceable** by the contractor.

In **Rizzo Pool Co. v. Del Grosso, 240 Conn. 58 (1997)**, the Connecticut Supreme Court reaffirmed that the HIA writing requirements are **mandatory and not merely directory** — a contract that fails to comply with § 20-429(a) is **unenforceable by the contractor**, and the contractor is also **barred from quantum meruit recovery** absent the narrow "bad faith" defense recognized in *Habetz v. Condon* [13][14][15]. The Connecticut bar-to-recovery rule is unusually harsh — a contractor who completes the work in full and to the homeowner's satisfaction can nonetheless be barred from collecting payment if the written contract is missing a single required element [13][14].

**Prohibited contract terms — R.C.S.A. § 20-427-7 and Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-134a et seq.** The regulations and the Home Solicitation Sales Act, read together, prohibit:

1. **Waiver of the three-day right of cancellation** for home solicitation sales;
2. **Waiver of any right granted by the HIA**;
3. **Mandatory pre-dispute binding arbitration of CUTPA claims by an owner** — Connecticut Supreme Court precedent permits arbitration only with knowing waiver of the judicial remedy (heavily litigated in *Hottle v. BDO Seidman, LLP, 268 Conn. 694 (2004)* and progeny);
4. **Contract provisions disclaiming implied warranties of habitability or fitness** for residential work (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 47-118 implied warranty applies to new home construction; common-law implied warranty applies to remodeling under *Vincenzi v. Cerro*, 186 Conn. 612 (1982));
5. **Front-loaded payment schedules** that demand substantially more than the proportional value of completed work at any stage.

Connecticut, unlike Massachusetts (one-third deposit cap under 201 CMR 14.04) and New York (33% / 50% caps for residential), **does not impose a specific statutory deposit cap** for HIC contracts. The absence of a deposit cap is an important contrast with neighboring states — and a frequent source of consumer complaint to DCP, which has historically considered deposit demands exceeding **one-third of the contract price** as a strong indicator of deceptive practice under CUTPA, though no per se rule has been codified. Public Act 23-101 directed DCP to study a deposit cap but did not enact one [16][17][18].

#### 1.2.4 The Connecticut Home Solicitation Sales Act — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-134a et seq.

Layered on top of the HIA, Connecticut's **Home Solicitation Sales Act** (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-134a through § 42-143) applies to **any consumer transaction of $25 or more** entered into at a place other than the seller's permanent place of business [17]. For home improvement contracts solicited at the homeowner's residence — which is the dominant pattern for remodeling and roofing sales — the Act imposes:

1. A **three business day right of cancellation** under § 42-135a, exercisable by the buyer until midnight of the third business day after the date of the transaction;
2. A **specific Notice of Cancellation form** that must be **attached to the contract** in 10-point boldface type and **physically separated** from the body of the contract by a perforation or tear-line;
3. A **prohibition on commencement of work** prior to expiration of the cancellation period (with the homeowner's optional written waiver);
4. A requirement that the seller provide a **completed, dated copy of the contract** at the time of signing;
5. A **statutory remedy of full refund** to the homeowner who cancels, with no penalty.

Failure to provide the cancellation notice in the statutory form is **a violation of CUTPA per se** under § 42-141(a), with the same punitive-damages and attorney-fee exposure as a direct HIA violation. The Home Solicitation Sales Act remedy stacks on top of the HIA per se CUTPA remedy, and homeowners' counsel routinely plead both [17][18].

#### 1.2.5 The HIA per se CUTPA mechanism — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-427(c)

The HIA's most important enforcement mechanism is its **per se CUTPA link** at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-427(c)**, which provides: "A violation of any of the provisions of this chapter shall be deemed an unfair or deceptive trade practice under subsection (a) of section 42-110b" [1][2][9][10][12]. The practical effect:

1. Any technical failure to comply with the HIA (unregistered, missing contract elements, missing registration-number disclosure on advertising, failure to provide cancellation notice, failure to provide change order in writing) **automatically constitutes a CUTPA violation**.
2. The homeowner does **not need to prove deceit, harm, or bad faith** beyond the procedural violation itself — once the violation is shown, the CUTPA private remedy under § 42-110g attaches.
3. CUTPA permits the court to award **punitive damages** under § 42-110g(a), which the Connecticut Supreme Court has held may include treble compensatory damages where the conduct involves reckless indifference to the rights of others or an intentional and wanton violation of those rights (*Bridgeport Harbour Place I, LLC v. Ganim, 303 Conn. 205 (2011)*; *Ulbrich v. Groth, 310 Conn. 375 (2013)*).
4. **Attorney fees and costs** are recoverable as of right under § 42-110g(d) regardless of the punitive-damages determination.
5. **Class actions** are available under § 42-110g(b) — practical exposure on widespread practice violations.

This per se mechanism is the **principal regulatory lever** in Connecticut residential contracting and is functionally equivalent to:
- **Massachusetts c. 93A** combined with 201 CMR 14.05 per se rules;
- **New Jersey CFA** combined with N.J.S.A. 56:8-138 and N.J.A.C. 13:45A-17.4 regulatory violations as automatic CFA predicates;
- **New York GBL § 349 / § 350** for general consumer-protection but Connecticut's link is statutorily direct and absolute.

Among the three regimes, **Connecticut is the most rigid** in tying procedural HIA defects directly to CUTPA treble exposure — there is no "substantial compliance" defense at the statutory level (cf. some MA cases recognizing limited substantial-compliance arguments under c. 93A) [13][14][15].

#### 1.2.6 The Home Improvement Guaranty Fund — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-432

The **Home Improvement Guaranty Fund**, established by **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-432**, is funded by contractor registration fees and a separate Fund assessment. It is the consumer's last-resort recovery mechanism for unsatisfied judgments against HIC-registered contractors. Key features [1][2][14][15][16]:

1. **Per-claim recovery limit** — currently **$25,000** under § 20-432(e), with an aggregate limit of **$40,000** for claims arising from a single contractor and **$100,000** for total claims against a single contractor across multiple complainants (limits adjusted by amendment in Public Acts 13-247 and 19-117);
2. **Eligibility** — limited to claimants who (i) hired an HIC-registered contractor at the time of contract, (ii) obtained a final judgment or arbitration award against the contractor, (iii) made reasonable efforts to collect on the judgment and were unable to do so, (iv) filed the Guaranty Fund claim within **two years** of the judgment becoming final;
3. **Exclusions** — Fund does **not** cover claims (i) where the homeowner is not the original owner of the property, (ii) where the work involved was new home construction (those claims are handled by the **NHCC Guaranty Fund** — see § 1.3 below), or (iii) where the contract was unenforceable due to the homeowner's prior breach;
4. **Subrogation** — after the Fund pays a claim, the **contractor's HIC registration is automatically suspended** under § 20-432(c) until the contractor reimburses the Fund. This suspension is enforced strictly; a contractor whose registration is suspended cannot lawfully perform home improvement work in Connecticut and faces additional CUTPA exposure for any unauthorized work.

The per-claim cap of $25,000 is higher than Massachusetts's $10,000 cap under M.G.L. c. 142A § 5 but lower than New Jersey's no-cap framework (NJ uses bond and insurance requirements instead of a Guaranty Fund) [14][15][16].

### 1.3 New Home Construction Contractor (NHCC) registration — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417a et seq.

The **New Home Construction Contractor Act ("NHCC Act")**, codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417a through § 20-417j** (Chapter 399a), was enacted as **Public Act 99-246** in 1999 in response to widespread consumer complaints about builders of new single-family and small multifamily homes who fell outside the HIA's "existing residence" scope. The NHCC Act was substantially modernized by **Public Act 24-39** in 2024, which strengthened registration, disclosure, contract-form, and Guaranty Fund mechanics. It is administered by **DCP** pursuant to regulations at **R.C.S.A. §§ 20-417e-1 through 20-417e-7** [4][5][14][15][16].

#### 1.3.1 Who must register — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417b

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417b(a)**, "no person shall engage in the business of new home construction or hold himself out as a new home construction contractor without a certificate of registration issued by the Commissioner of Consumer Protection." The statutory definitions at **§ 20-417a** establish the scope [4][5]:

- **"New home"** means "any new single-family or multifamily dwelling, including a condominium unit, manufactured home or modular home, that has not been previously occupied."
- **"New home construction"** means "the construction of any new home for sale or for use as a residence by the buyer."
- **"New home construction contractor"** means "any person who contracts with a consumer to construct or sell a new home prior to occupancy."

The scope is intentionally distinct from HIC: the NHCC covers **new construction prior to first occupancy**, while the HIC covers **work on existing residential property**. The line between the two is a critical compliance gotcha: a contractor who **builds an addition on an existing home** is performing **home improvement** (HIC scope), while a contractor who **constructs a new home for resale** is performing **new home construction** (NHCC scope). A contractor whose practice spans both must hold **both registrations** [4][5][14][15].

Two important sub-rules:

1. **Owner-builder exception — § 20-417b(b)(1)**: a person constructing his or her **own new home for personal occupancy** is not required to register as an NHCC.
2. **Single-build exception — § 20-417b(b)(2)** as amended by Public Act 24-39: a person constructing **no more than one new home in any twelve-month period for sale** is exempt only if the home is constructed on land owned by the builder and is the builder's principal residence at the time of registration application. Public Act 24-39 narrowed this exception to address abuse by "one-off" builders avoiding registration.

#### 1.3.2 Registration requirements — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417c and R.C.S.A. § 20-417e-2

NHCC registration with DCP requires [4][5][14][16]:

1. **Registration fee** — currently **$240 per year** for NHCC under DCP's fee schedule (set by R.C.S.A. § 20-417e-2 and DCP fee orders); the registration period is one year.
2. **New Home Construction Guaranty Fund assessment** under **§ 20-417i** — every NHCC must pay into the Fund; assessment varies by Fund balance.
3. **Disclosure of the contractor's full legal name, dba names, business address, telephone number, FEIN, date of incorporation/formation, and all owners, officers, directors, and 10%+ owners** (parallel to HIC).
4. **Criminal background disclosure** — same scope as HIC under § 20-417c(b)(4); DCP may deny on fraud, deceit, or financial-dishonesty convictions.
5. **Proof of Workers' Compensation insurance** for any employees;
6. **Proof of commercial general liability insurance** — under Public Act 24-39 amendments to § 20-417c, NHCC applicants must demonstrate CGL coverage of **at least $300,000 combined single limit** (or as DCP may by regulation increase from time to time). This is a stricter insurance requirement than HIC, which has only a disclosure obligation;
7. **Construction-experience or affiliation disclosure** — § 20-417c(b)(7) requires disclosure of construction history and any prior NHCC registrations;
8. **The NHCC Registration Number must be prominently displayed on contracts, advertising, signs at the construction site, and digital marketing materials** under R.C.S.A. § 20-417e-3.

#### 1.3.3 Contract requirements — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417d

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417d**, every new home construction contract must be in writing and must contain [4][5][14][16]:

1. **The contractor's name, address, telephone number, registration number, and date the contract was signed**;
2. **The buyer's name and address**;
3. **A description of the new home** to be constructed and the materials to be used, with reference to plans and specifications incorporated by reference;
4. **The address of the property** on which the new home will be constructed;
5. **The total contract price** and any financing terms;
6. **A schedule of progress payments** with each payment tied to a specific construction milestone;
7. **The estimated date of substantial completion** and the contractor's obligation to communicate delays in writing;
8. **A description of all express warranties offered by the contractor**, separately and in addition to the **statutory implied warranties** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 47-117 and 47-118** (express written warranty of habitability, fitness, and workmanlike construction; one-year limit on actions for builder-installed components) — the implied warranties cannot be waived;
9. **A description of the contractor's policies on change orders** with the statutory requirement that all change orders be in writing;
10. **A statement of the buyer's rights** under the NHCC Act, including the right to contact DCP and pursue claims through the NHCC Guaranty Fund;
11. **Signatures of buyer and contractor**.

The NHCC contract requirements **do not currently include an explicit three-day cooling-off right** at the statutory level (the Home Solicitation Sales Act may apply if the contract is signed at a place other than the contractor's office), but Public Act 24-39 added several disclosure improvements that move the NHCC contract closer to the HIC standard. In practice, a single template approach with full cooling-off disclosure, written change-order requirements, and complete warranty disclosure is the safest compliance posture for builders doing both new construction and remodel.

#### 1.3.4 NHCC per se CUTPA mechanism — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417g

In parallel with the HIA, the NHCC Act provides at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417g** that **any violation of the NHCC Act constitutes an unfair or deceptive trade practice under CUTPA**, with the same automatic punitive-damages and attorney-fee exposure under § 42-110g [9][10][12]. The Connecticut Appellate Court has applied this per se mechanism strictly in NHCC contractor-versus-buyer disputes, with the result that procedural failures (e.g., a contract missing the statutory warranty disclosure) automatically convert into CUTPA exposure.

#### 1.3.5 NHCC Guaranty Fund — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417i

The **New Home Construction Guaranty Fund**, established by **§ 20-417i** and amended by Public Act 24-39, is **separate from the HIC Guaranty Fund** and applies to claims involving new home construction:

1. **Per-claim recovery limit** — **$30,000** under § 20-417i(e) as amended by Public Act 24-39 (raised from prior $25,000);
2. **Aggregate per-contractor limit** — **$100,000**;
3. **Eligibility** — final unsatisfied judgment, reasonable collection efforts, two-year filing window;
4. **Subrogation and automatic registration suspension** parallel to HIC.

#### 1.3.6 Statutory implied warranties — Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 47-116 through 47-121 (New Home Warranties Act)

Layered on top of the NHCC Act, the **New Home Warranties Act**, codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 47-116 through 47-121**, provides **statutory implied warranties** running from the **builder or vendor** to the **first purchaser** of a new single-family or multifamily dwelling [4][5][14]:

- **Express warranty of merchantability** that the new home is free from faulty material, constructed according to sound engineering and construction standards, constructed in a workmanlike manner, and fit for habitation at the time of delivery;
- **One-year limitation period** on the express warranty (post-delivery actions) and a longer period for major structural defects;
- **No waiver** of implied warranties absent conspicuous, separate written disclaimer with the buyer's specific acknowledgment;
- **Right of subrogation** for warranty claimants against successor parties.

The Connecticut Supreme Court in **Krawiec v. Manor Equity Builders, Inc., 224 Conn. 480 (1993)** and progeny held that the New Home Warranties Act cannot be displaced by general contract-disclaimer clauses absent strict compliance with the statutory waiver requirements [14].

### 1.4 Major Contractor registration — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-340 et seq.

The **Major Contractor Registration** is the third leg of the Connecticut contractor-registration triad and is administered by **DCP** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-340 through § 20-345** for **larger non-residential ("major") contracting work** [1][6][8]. It is the least understood and least frequently invoked of the three regimes but is important for commercial-scale work and public construction prequalification interactions.

Under Public Act 19-117 and subsequent amendments, "Major Contractor" registration is required for certain higher-threshold non-residential general-contracting and trade-contracting work. The statute interfaces with the **Department of Administrative Services** prequalification regime at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 4b-91** for state public-works contracts above prescribed thresholds (currently **$500,000** for general work and **$500,000** for trade work subject to DAS prequalification rules) [6][7][8]. Major Contractor registration is separate from DAS prequalification; the two are sequenced (registration with DCP, then DAS prequalification for state contracts).

Registration requirements parallel HIC and NHCC in structure (legal-entity disclosure, principal disclosure, insurance disclosure, criminal-background disclosure, fee, and prohibited-conduct provisions) and DCP may revoke the registration for unfair, deceptive, or fraudulent conduct under § 20-341 with CUTPA exposure attached.

In practice, for ContractorPro's customer base, the Major Contractor registration is the **commercial-side equivalent** of HIC and NHCC: a contractor undertaking commercial work above the threshold must register with DCP under § 20-340 rather than HIC or NHCC.

### 1.5 Trade licensing — DCP Occupational and Professional Licensing — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 et seq.

Connecticut **licenses every mechanical trade** at the state level, administered through the **DCP Occupational and Professional Licensing Division** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 through § 20-341aa** (Chapter 393a) and regulations at **R.C.S.A. §§ 20-330-1 et seq.** [6][7][8][19][20]. The licensing structure is a three-tier **contractor / journeyperson / apprentice** model with **state-administered examinations** for the contractor and journeyperson tiers and a **registered apprenticeship** through the **Connecticut Department of Labor Office of Apprenticeship Training**.

#### 1.5.1 Electrical — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 series and R.C.S.A. § 20-330-1 et seq.

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 et seq.** and R.C.S.A. § 20-330-1 et seq., the Electrical Work Examining Board licenses [19][20]:

1. **E-1 Unlimited Electrical Contractor** — required for any person engaged in the business of installing, altering, or repairing electrical wiring, conduit, or apparatus.
2. **E-2 Unlimited Journeyperson Electrician** — works under the direction of an E-1.
3. **L-5 / L-6 Limited Electrical Contractor / Journeyperson** — restricted scope (low-voltage, fire-alarm, telecommunications, etc.).
4. **T-1 / T-2 Telecommunications Contractor / Journeyperson** — telecommunications wiring scope.
5. **C-5 / C-6 Limited Electrical (specific scope)** — varies by Board determination.
6. **Apprentice Electrician** — registered apprenticeship under Department of Labor Office of Apprenticeship Training program.

To qualify for the E-1, applicants must demonstrate completion of an **approved four-year apprenticeship program** (8,000 hours total — 7,200 OJT plus 720 related instruction) or equivalent experience, **two years of journeyperson experience**, and pass the DCP-administered Electrical Contractor Examination covering the National Electrical Code with Connecticut amendments and the Connecticut Electrical Practice Act [19][20]. Examination provider: DCP through a contracted testing vendor (currently Prometric/PSI).

License renewal: **annually** for contractor licenses ($150 contractor / $90 journeyperson under R.C.S.A. § 20-335-1); **continuing-education** requirement of **3 hours per year** for E-1 covering Code updates and Connecticut amendments (raised from prior 1-hour baseline by Public Act 21-37).

Performing electrical work without a license is a violation of **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-341** and constitutes both a Class B misdemeanor (Class A on subsequent offense) and a CUTPA per se violation under § 20-341c.

#### 1.5.2 Plumbing — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 series and R.C.S.A. § 20-330-1 et seq.

Plumbing licensing is administered by the **Plumbing and Piping Work Examining Board** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 et seq.** with parallel structure [19][20]:

1. **P-1 Unlimited Plumbing Contractor** — full plumbing scope, including water supply, drainage, vent, fixtures, and gas piping (where the gas piping work falls within plumbing scope rather than heating/piping scope).
2. **P-2 Unlimited Plumbing Journeyperson** — works under P-1 supervision.
3. **P-6 / P-7 Limited Plumbing Contractor / Journeyperson** — restricted scope (e.g., water heater installation only).
4. **Apprentice Plumber** — registered apprenticeship.

P-1 qualification requires completion of an **approved four-year plumbing apprenticeship**, two years of journeyperson experience, and passing the Plumbing Contractor Examination covering the Connecticut State Plumbing Code (incorporated by reference at R.C.S.A. § 29-252-1d) and the Plumbing Practice Act. Continuing education: **3 hours per year** for P-1.

#### 1.5.3 Heating, Cooling, Sheet Metal — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 series

The same Title 20 Chapter 393a / 394 trade-licensing scheme covers **heating-piping-cooling and sheet-metal work** through several license categories administered by the **Heating, Piping, Cooling Work Examining Board** and the **Sheet Metal Work Examining Board** [19][20]:

| License code | Scope |
|---|---|
| **B-1 / B-2** | Unlimited Heating, Piping, Cooling Contractor / Journeyperson — boilers, hydronic systems, steam, oil, gas piping (HVAC scope) |
| **D-1 / D-2** | Limited Heating, Piping, Cooling — restricted scope (e.g., specific fuel-only) |
| **G-1 / G-2** | Limited Heating, Piping, Cooling — gas piping in HVAC context |
| **S-1 / S-2** | Unlimited Sheet Metal Contractor / Journeyperson — ductwork, sheet metal fabrication |
| **SM-1 / SM-2** | Limited Sheet Metal — restricted scope |
| **W-1 / W-2 / J-1 / J-2** | Well-drilling, pump-installer specialty subcategories |

Each requires apprenticeship + journeyperson experience + DCP examination + annual renewal with continuing education. Performing HVAC, refrigeration, or sheet-metal work without the appropriate license is a Class B / Class A misdemeanor and a per se CUTPA violation under § 20-341c [19][20].

#### 1.5.4 Fire Protection Sprinkler — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 series and Title 29

Fire-protection sprinkler installation requires an **F-1 Unlimited Fire Protection Sprinkler Contractor** (or **F-2 Journeyperson**) license issued by DCP under the same Chapter 393a / 394 framework, with NICET certification as the technical-competency baseline and additional fire-marshal authority overlaid under Title 29 (Office of the State Fire Marshal) [6][7][20]. F-3 limited specialty licenses cover restricted sprinkler scope.

#### 1.5.5 Elevator — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-330 series

Elevator installation and maintenance requires an **Elevator Contractor (R-1) or Mechanic (R-5/R-6/R-7) license** with specific examination and experience requirements; administered jointly with DAS Elevator Inspection unit.

#### 1.5.6 Asbestos abatement — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-440 et seq.

Asbestos abatement requires an **Asbestos Contractor license** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-440 through § 20-448** and a separate **DPH project monitor** credential for abatement projects above prescribed thresholds. The licensing scheme intersects with **federal AHERA** (40 CFR 763) for school work.

#### 1.5.7 Lead abatement — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-475 et seq. and Title 19a

Lead abatement requires a **Lead Abatement Contractor license** issued by DCP under § 20-475 et seq. and a separate **DPH** credential under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 19a-110 et seq.** for the underlying lead-paint regulatory regime, which closely mirrors the federal EPA Renovation, Repair, and Painting Rule (40 CFR 745) for pre-1978 housing.

### 1.6 The HIC + NHCC + Major-Contractor combination — practical compliance gotcha

Connecticut's three-registration system creates a **compliance gotcha** unique among the states audited:

1. A contractor doing **kitchen remodels on existing homes** needs **HIC** only (assuming no other trade is performed beyond licensed-trade scope).
2. A contractor doing **new home construction for sale** needs **NHCC** only (HIC does not cover new construction).
3. A contractor doing **commercial work above DCP thresholds** needs **Major Contractor** registration.
4. A contractor doing **all three** (mixed remodel/new construction/commercial) needs **all three** parallel registrations, each with its own renewal cycle, fee, Guaranty Fund assessment, and per se CUTPA exposure.

This is more administratively demanding than the Massachusetts dual-license model (HIC + CSL) because each Connecticut registration is independent and DCP does not currently offer a combined-registration discount or unified renewal. ContractorPro must therefore track each registration separately, with its own renewal cycle alarm and Guaranty Fund assessment status, and must classify each job at intake to determine which registration applies (and that the contractor holds the applicable registration) [1][2][3][4][5][6][8][14][15][16].

## 2. Mechanics' Liens — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-33 et seq.

### 2.1 Statutory framework

Connecticut's mechanics' lien statute is codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 49-33 through 49-46a** (Title 49 Chapter 847), enacted in essential form in the late nineteenth century and revised most recently by Public Acts 99-153, 03-86, 11-178, and 17-89 [21][22][23][24][25][26][27][28][29]. The Connecticut mechanic's lien is a **constitutional-due-process-compliant pre-judgment remedy** that gives unpaid contractors, subcontractors, materialmen, and labor providers a property-based security interest in the real estate improved by their work.

#### 2.1.1 Who may file — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-33(a)

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-33(a)**, "any person who, by virtue of an agreement with or by the consent of the owner of any land or building, or any person having authority from or rightfully acting for such owner in procuring labor or materials, performs services or furnishes materials in the construction, raising, removal or repairs of any building or any of its appurtenances or in the improvement of any lot or in the site development or subdivision of any plot of land" has a mechanic's lien on the land and improvements [21]. The lien is available to:

1. **Prime contractors** (general contractors in direct contractual privity with the owner);
2. **Subcontractors and sub-subcontractors** (any tier);
3. **Material suppliers** (including persons furnishing materials whether or not installed);
4. **Labor providers** (workers performing labor on the property);
5. **Site work and improvement contractors** (under § 49-33(a) "improvement of any lot or in the site development or subdivision of any plot of land");
6. **Design professionals** (architects, engineers, surveyors) under specific § 49-33 amendments — though Connecticut's coverage of design professionals is narrower than in many states.

#### 2.1.2 The subcontractor "lien fund" cap — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-33(f)

A **defining feature** of Connecticut mechanics' lien law is the **subcontractor "lien fund" cap** at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-33(f)** [21][22]. Connecticut, like New Jersey under N.J.S.A. 2A:44A-3 (the "lien fund" concept), **limits the aggregate amount of subcontractor liens** against the property **to the unpaid balance** owed by the owner to the general contractor at the time the lien is filed. Specifically:

> "The aggregate of all liens claimed against the owner's property by virtue of this section in favor of subcontractors and others claiming under or through a general contractor shall not exceed the sum which the owner has agreed to pay to the general contractor for the work and materials so furnished and labor so performed, less any sums paid to the general contractor prior to the recording of the lien."

This **lien fund cap** means that subcontractors' security is bounded by what the owner still owes the GC at the moment of lien filing. If the owner has paid the GC in full **before** the subcontractor records a lien, the subcontractor's lien rights against the owner are **extinguished** — the subcontractor's only remaining remedy is contract-based against the GC. This is a strong incentive for subcontractors to record liens **early and aggressively** rather than wait to see whether the GC will pay [21][22][23].

The Connecticut Supreme Court's analysis in **Camardo v. Hartford, 219 Conn. 502 (1991)** and **Marrocco v. Giardino, 255 Conn. 617 (2001)** confirms strict application of the cap and limits the equitable doctrines that might soften it [22][23].

This is the same conceptual structure as the **New Jersey lien fund** under N.J.S.A. 2A:44A-3 and N.J.S.A. 2A:44A-9, though the specific procedural mechanics differ. It is **stricter** than Massachusetts (where Notice of Subcontract under M.G.L. c. 254 § 4 establishes priority but does not impose an aggregate cap tied to GC contract balance) and stricter than New York (which has no fund cap concept).

### 2.2 Filing the lien — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-34

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-34**, the **Certificate of Mechanic's Lien** must be **recorded** in the **land records of the town in which the property is located** and must meet the following requirements [24]:

1. **Form** — A written certificate signed by the claimant or its agent, describing the property by reference to its property record/tax parcel, identifying the owner, the claimant, the nature of the work performed or materials furnished, the amount claimed, and the date of last work or last furnishing.
2. **Deadline — 90 days from last day of work**: the certificate **must be recorded within 90 days from the date the claimant ceased to perform services or furnish materials** under § 49-34(1).
3. **Notarization** — signed and sworn before a notary or commissioner of superior court.
4. **Service on the owner** under § 49-35: within **30 days after recording**, the claimant must serve a copy of the recorded lien on the owner by first-class mail or in-hand. Failure to timely serve invalidates the lien.

The **90-day clock** is the most strictly enforced deadline in Connecticut construction law. The Connecticut Supreme Court has consistently held that an untimely lien is **void and unenforceable**, with no equitable extension available [24][25][26]. The lien must be **filed and recorded** within the 90-day window — service on the owner has its own separate 30-day window after recording.

#### 2.2.1 "Last day of work" — determining the clock

Determining the **"last day of work"** is the most frequently litigated issue. Connecticut decisions construe this term:

1. **Substantive work, not trivial corrections**: punchlist or warranty/repair work performed after substantial completion **does not** generally restart the clock unless it is **material to contract performance** under *Capitol Pizza, Inc. v. Maravolis, 6 Conn. App. 627 (1986)* and *Gruzdis v. Connecticut General Life Ins. Co., 7 Conn. App. 542 (1986)* [25][26].
2. **Delivery without installation**: for material suppliers, the clock runs from the **last date of delivery** of materials to the project, not from invoice date.
3. **Termination without completion**: where the contract is terminated mid-stream, the clock runs from the **last actual day of work** or the **last day materials were furnished**, not from an event such as termination notice [25][26][27].

The 90-day window is shorter than New York's 4 month (1-2 family) or 8 month (other) windows under N.Y. Lien Law § 10 but comparable to New Jersey's 90-day window under N.J.S.A. 2A:44A-6 and Massachusetts's 90-day (residential) / 120-day Notice-of-Contract / Statement-of-Account multi-step window under M.G.L. c. 254.

### 2.3 Notice of Intent and pre-lien notice — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-35

Unlike some states (e.g., Pennsylvania, Texas), Connecticut **does not require a pre-lien Notice of Intent** as a prerequisite to filing a mechanic's lien. The owner-service requirement at § 49-35 is **post-filing notice** — the claimant records the lien first, then serves the owner.

However, subcontractors and material suppliers performing work on **non-residential** projects have an option to file a **Notice of Lien** with the owner under common-law / industry-customary practice to alert the owner of unpaid balances and preserve subrogation positions, though this is not a statutory prerequisite to a § 49-34 mechanic's lien.

### 2.4 Lien life and enforcement — Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 49-39, 49-40

#### 2.4.1 One-year lien life — § 49-39

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-39**, "a mechanic's lien shall not continue in force for a longer period than one year after the lien has been perfected, unless the party claiming the lien commences an action to foreclose it" [28]. The one-year clock runs from the date the lien is recorded; if no foreclosure action is commenced within one year, **the lien is dissolved as a matter of law** and the claimant must rely solely on contractual remedies against the principal obligor.

The Connecticut Supreme Court's holding in **Diamond National Corp. v. Dwelle, 164 Conn. 540 (1973)** confirms strict application: a lien expires automatically at the end of the one-year period and **no extension** is available by stipulation, agreement, or post-expiration filing. The claimant must commence the foreclosure action within the one-year window or lose the lien remedy.

#### 2.4.2 Foreclosure action — § 49-40

Under **§ 49-40**, the foreclosure action proceeds as a **Superior Court** civil action sounding in mortgage foreclosure (with mechanic's lien adapted procedures). The action must be commenced by service of process within the one-year window and may proceed to a judgment of foreclosure if the lien is valid and the underlying debt is established.

### 2.5 Bond-around — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-37

A property owner (or any interested party) may **discharge a mechanic's lien** by **filing a bond** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-37** [29]. The bond procedure works as follows:

1. **Application to Superior Court** — the owner files an application to discharge the lien on bond, with notice to the lien claimant.
2. **Bond amount** — typically **150% of the lien amount** to cover principal, interest, and prospective costs (the court has discretion under § 49-37(b) to set the bond at a different multiple if circumstances warrant).
3. **Bond approval** — the court reviews the bond surety, form, and amount and approves before discharge takes effect.
4. **Discharge** — once the bond is filed and approved, the lien is **discharged from the land records**. The claimant's remedy shifts from foreclosing the lien to enforcing the bond.
5. **Bond enforcement** — the claimant pursues the bond surety in a separate action; the surety is liable up to the bond's penal limit.

The bond-around procedure is **commonly used** in Connecticut commercial construction to clear title for refinancing or closing. The claimant's substantive rights are not impaired — the bond is essentially a substitute security — but the claimant must adapt the litigation strategy from foreclosure to surety-bond enforcement.

### 2.6 Lien waivers — common-law and form requirements

Connecticut, **unlike California (Cal. Civ. Code § 8120 et seq.) and Texas (Tex. Prop. Code § 53.281 et seq.)**, **does not impose a statutory form for lien waivers**. The result: lien waivers are enforced as ordinary contracts under Connecticut common law, with the usual rules of consideration, offer/acceptance, and writing-required-for-real-property-interests applying.

Practical norms:

1. **Partial Conditional Lien Waiver** — accompanies progress payments, conditioned on receipt of payment.
2. **Partial Unconditional Lien Waiver** — issued after payment is received.
3. **Final Conditional Lien Waiver** — accompanies final payment.
4. **Final Unconditional Lien Waiver** — issued after final payment is received.

A waiver that is signed **before payment is received** but is **unconditional** poses significant risk to the claimant — Connecticut courts have enforced such waivers strictly, particularly where the waiver was supported by consideration. The conditional/unconditional distinction is critical.

A **"no-lien clause"** in the prime contract or subcontract that prospectively waives lien rights is **enforceable** under Connecticut common law (unlike New York and certain other states where such clauses are limited by statute), though heavily disfavored in practice. Most major commercial owners include lien waiver provisions in their contracts, and most subcontractors negotiate the scope.

### 2.7 Public works — bond substitute under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-41

For **public works** projects (state, municipal, school district), Connecticut does not permit mechanic's liens against the public-owned property. Instead, under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-41**, the prime contractor on a public-works project above **$100,000** must furnish a **payment bond** (parallel to the federal Miller Act, 40 U.S.C. § 3131 et seq.) covering all labor and material suppliers. Subcontractors and material suppliers proceed against the bond surety rather than against the real property. The bond claim deadline is **180 days from last day of work** under § 49-41a(b), with separate notice requirements [30][31].

The public-works bond regime parallels (but is procedurally distinct from) the private mechanic's lien — ContractorPro must classify the project type at intake (private vs. public-works) and apply the correct lien-versus-bond framework.

### 2.8 The lien-fund cap as the dominant Connecticut gotcha

The **subcontractor lien-fund cap** at § 49-33(f) is the **dominant Connecticut mechanic's-lien gotcha** for ContractorPro:

- Subcontractor lien rights are **only as good as the GC's unpaid balance with the owner**;
- A subcontractor that delays recording loses lien priority and may lose lien rights entirely if the owner pays the GC in full first;
- A GC that knows its subcontractors are unpaid and accelerates billing to the owner may effectively **strand subcontractor claims** at the legal level — though this conduct may trigger CUTPA exposure if it is willful;
- ContractorPro must surface a subcontractor's lien-clock independently of the GC-payment status, and surface the owner-paid-down-balance to the GC simultaneously, so that the subcontractor's lien-fund position is visible in real time.

## 3. Prompt Payment and Retainage

### 3.1 Private construction — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-158i et seq.

Connecticut's **private-work prompt-payment statute** is codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-158i through § 42-158l** (Chapter 743), enacted as **Public Act 99-153** in 1999 and amended by Public Acts 03-86 and 11-178 [32][33][34]. The statute applies to **construction contracts on private property** and establishes mandatory payment timelines for owner-to-GC and GC-to-subcontractor payments.

#### 3.1.1 Scope — § 42-158i

The statute applies to any **construction contract** with a price of **$25,000 or more** between an **owner** and a **contractor** for the construction, reconstruction, alteration, improvement, renovation, or repair of any building or other improvement to real property in Connecticut, **except for**:

1. Single-family residential property where the owner is acting as his or her own occupant (i.e., not for resale or rental);
2. Owner-occupied two-, three-, or four-family dwellings used as the owner's residence;
3. Public-works projects (governed by separate Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-41a).

In practice, the prompt-pay statute covers **commercial, industrial, multi-family residential (5+ units), and non-owner-occupied residential** projects [32][33][34].

#### 3.1.2 Owner-to-contractor payment timelines — § 42-158j(a)

Under **§ 42-158j(a)**, the owner must pay the contractor **within 30 days** after receipt of a properly submitted invoice or pay application. The 30-day window is the standard private-sector benchmark and runs from the date the invoice is "received" — not from invoice date or work-completion date.

#### 3.1.3 Contractor-to-subcontractor payment timelines — § 42-158j(b)

Under **§ 42-158j(b)**, the contractor must pay the subcontractor **within 30 days** after receipt of payment from the owner attributable to that subcontractor's work. The "pay-when-paid-not-pay-if-paid" distinction applies:

- A **"pay-when-paid"** clause is enforceable as a timing provision but **cannot extend the 30-day window beyond what § 42-158j authorizes**;
- A **"pay-if-paid"** clause that purports to make the subcontractor's right to payment **contingent** on the contractor's receipt of payment from the owner is **unenforceable** under Connecticut common law (consistent with *Blakeslee Arpaia Chapman, Inc. v. EI Constructors, Inc.*, 239 Conn. 708 (1997)) — the GC remains primarily liable to the subcontractor regardless of owner-payment status.

#### 3.1.4 Interest on overdue amounts — § 42-158j(c)

Under **§ 42-158j(c)**, overdue amounts accrue **interest at 1% per month (12% per annum)** from the date payment was due. Interest is in addition to any liquidated-damages or attorney-fee provisions in the underlying contract.

#### 3.1.5 Withholding and disputed amounts — § 42-158j(d)

An owner or contractor may **withhold a reasonable amount** corresponding to **disputed work** or **defective work** without triggering prompt-pay interest, provided the withholding party gives **written notice** specifying the basis for withholding within the 30-day payment window. The notice requirement is strictly enforced — undocumented withholding triggers prompt-pay interest regardless of the underlying dispute's merit.

#### 3.1.6 Suspension of performance — § 42-158k

If payment is not made within 30 days after the due date (i.e., 60 days from invoice receipt for the original payment plus 30 days of grace), the contractor or subcontractor may **suspend performance** after **7 days' written notice** under § 42-158k. The suspension right is a powerful leverage tool — Connecticut is among the more contractor-friendly states on suspension rights — but the strict 7-day notice requirement must be observed [32][33][34].

#### 3.1.7 Attorney fees and CUTPA overlap

Under **§ 42-158l**, the prevailing party in a prompt-payment dispute may recover **attorney fees and costs**. A wilful failure to comply with the prompt-payment statute is also a **CUTPA violation** under § 42-158l(a), with the punitive-damages and attorney-fee exposure under § 42-110g attached. This dual remedy is similar to (though procedurally distinct from) Massachusetts c. 149 § 29E.

### 3.2 Public-works prompt payment and retainage — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-41a et seq.

Connecticut's **public-works prompt-payment and retainage statute** is codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-41a through § 49-41c** [35][36][37]. The statute applies to construction contracts with the State of Connecticut, any state agency or institution, any municipality, school district, or other political subdivision.

#### 3.2.1 Payment timelines — § 49-41a

Under **§ 49-41a(a)**, the public entity must pay the prime contractor within **30 days** after receipt of a properly submitted invoice and acceptance of the work to which the invoice relates. The prime contractor must pay subcontractors within **30 days** after receipt of payment from the public entity.

#### 3.2.2 Retainage — § 49-41a and § 4b-92 — and the 5% cap

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 49-41a(b)** and the related state-procurement provisions at **§ 4b-92**, public entities may withhold retainage from progress payments at **up to 5% of the value of the work completed**, with the retainage held until **substantial completion** and released upon final acceptance subject to standard punch-list resolution [35][36][37].

The 5% retainage cap on public works is the **same** as the Massachusetts 5% private-construction cap under M.G.L. c. 149 § 29F (and is stricter than the New Jersey 10% private cap under N.J.S.A. 2A:30A-2). Public Act 19-117 confirmed the 5% cap and added punch-list resolution and final-acceptance procedures.

#### 3.2.3 Public-works payment bond — § 49-41

As discussed in § 2.7 above, the prime contractor on a public-works project above **$100,000** must furnish a **payment bond** covering all labor and material suppliers. This is the principal subcontractor-protection mechanism on public works (substitute for mechanic's lien which is not available against public property).

### 3.3 Private retainage — no statutory cap

Connecticut **does not impose a statutory retainage cap on private construction** projects (other than the public-works 5% cap discussed in § 3.2 above). Private retainage practices vary by contract:

- **5–10% retainage** is the common commercial-project benchmark;
- **Retainage release** is typically tied to substantial completion plus warranty walk-through;
- **Reduction-of-retainage** clauses (e.g., reducing retainage from 10% to 5% at 50% completion) are negotiated.

The absence of a statutory private retainage cap is a contrast with **Massachusetts (5% private cap under c. 149 § 29F)** and **New Jersey (10% private cap under N.J.S.A. 2A:30A-2)**, and is closer to the New York approach (5% cap on most projects under PPA § 756-a). ContractorPro should not impose a Connecticut-side hard-block on retainage levels but should flag retainage above 10% as unusually aggressive on private work [32][33][34][35][36][37].

## 4. Tax Structure

### 4.1 Connecticut state sales and use tax — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-407 and § 12-408

Connecticut imposes a **state sales tax** on the retail sale of tangible personal property and the sale of certain enumerated services at a base rate of **6.35%** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-408** [38][39][40][41][42]. Connecticut **does not authorize local-option sales taxes** — the entire state operates on a uniform 6.35% rate.

The principal sales-tax statutes:

- **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-407** — definitions and scope, including the enumeration of taxable services under § 12-407(a)(37) (limited services subject to tax — building services are partially included);
- **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-408** — imposition of the sales tax at 6.35%;
- **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-411** — imposition of the use tax;
- **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-412** — exemptions, including exemption for sales to the federal government, the State of Connecticut, and certain non-profit organizations.

### 4.2 The construction contractor model — contractor as consumer

The principal Connecticut construction-contractor sales-tax doctrine is the **"contractor as consumer"** model, formalized in **Conn. Agencies Reg. § 12-426-18** and DRS Informational Publication IP 2018(2) "Building Contractors' Guide to Sales and Use Taxes" [40][41][42]. Under this doctrine:

1. **The contractor is the "consumer" of materials** that become **incorporated into real property** through construction, reconstruction, alteration, repair, or improvement;
2. **The contractor pays sales tax to suppliers** at the time of purchase of those materials;
3. **The contractor does not separately bill sales tax** to the customer on the materials portion of a construction contract;
4. **The contractor's contract with the customer is structured as a "lump sum"** for the entire scope of work (or as a "time and materials" contract with the tax-paid status disclosed) but in either case the contractor has already absorbed the sales tax in its supplier-side procurement.

#### 4.2.1 Services to real property — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-407(a)(37)(I) and (J)

Connecticut does, however, impose sales tax on certain **services** that are commonly performed by construction contractors but are treated as taxable services rather than as real-property construction. The principal enumerated taxable services [38][39][40][42]:

- **§ 12-407(a)(37)(I) — Services to industrial, commercial, or income-producing real property** — taxed at the standard 6.35% rate. This category includes maintenance, repair, and certain renovation services performed on commercial or income-producing property (i.e., rental property) but **excludes new construction**;
- **§ 12-407(a)(37)(J) — Services to other than residential** — overlap with (I), with statutory carve-outs and DRS regulatory interpretations clarifying scope;
- **Landscaping services** — taxed under (CB) at 6.35%;
- **Janitorial and cleaning services** — taxed under (DD).

The **distinction between "new construction" (non-taxable service)** and **"renovation/repair of existing commercial property" (taxable service)** is a significant compliance gotcha. The DRS has issued Special Notice 2003(11) and Informational Publication IP 2018(2) to clarify the boundary, but the line is fact-intensive and frequently litigated [40][41][42].

#### 4.2.2 Residential vs. commercial — different tax treatment

A **critical Connecticut compliance gotcha**:

- **Services to residential real property** — generally **not taxable** as services (the contractor is the consumer of materials only);
- **Services to commercial or income-producing real property** — **taxable services** at 6.35% on the labor portion;
- **New construction (any type)** — **not taxable** as services; contractor is consumer of materials.

A contractor performing renovation work on a **rental property** (income-producing) must therefore **charge sales tax on the labor portion** of the contract (not the materials portion, which has already been taxed at the supplier level). A contractor performing renovation work on the **owner-occupied home** does not charge sales tax. The classification of the property at intake — owner-occupied, commercial, or income-producing residential — determines the tax treatment.

#### 4.2.3 CERT-141 Contractor's Exempt Purchase Certificate — DRS

For projects with **exempt entities** (the State, federal government, certain non-profits, etc.), the contractor may purchase materials **exempt from sales tax** by furnishing a **CERT-141 Contractor's Exempt Purchase Certificate** to the supplier. The certificate identifies the exempt project and binds the contractor to use the materials solely on that project. Misuse of CERT-141 (using exempt-purchased materials on a taxable project) triggers a use-tax assessment plus penalties [40][41][42].

### 4.3 Use tax — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-411

Materials brought into Connecticut from out of state (e.g., supplies purchased in Massachusetts, New Hampshire, New York, or Rhode Island for use on a Connecticut project) are subject to **use tax** at the same 6.35% rate if Connecticut sales tax was not paid at purchase. The contractor must self-accrue and remit use tax on out-of-state purchases under § 12-411.

This use-tax obligation interacts with the contractor-as-consumer doctrine: a contractor that purchases materials in **New Hampshire** (no sales tax) for use on a Connecticut project owes Connecticut use tax at 6.35% on the materials cost.

### 4.4 Connecticut corporate and personal income tax

#### 4.4.1 Corporation Business Tax — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-213 et seq.

Connecticut imposes a **Corporation Business Tax** on C-corporations at the rate of **7.5%** on net income apportioned to Connecticut (with a 10% surtax on companies with $100M+ gross income through 2025, scheduled phase-down) [43][44]. Most small-to-mid construction firms operate as S-corporations, LLCs, or partnerships and are subject to pass-through entity tax.

#### 4.4.2 Pass-Through Entity Tax — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-699

Connecticut imposes a **mandatory Pass-Through Entity Tax** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-699** at **6.99%** on the net income of S-corporations, LLCs, partnerships, and similar pass-through entities sourced to Connecticut. The PET is paid at the entity level, with a corresponding credit available to the individual members against their personal Connecticut income tax. This structure (Connecticut was an early SALT-cap-workaround adopter) is well established and most construction-firm owners must factor PET payments into cash flow planning.

#### 4.4.3 Personal income tax — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-700

Personal income tax is graduated up to **6.99%** at the top bracket (over $1,000,000 for joint filers under 2026 schedule). Construction-firm owners taking K-1 income should plan for PET + personal-income-tax stacking, with the PET credit largely offsetting personal-level tax.

### 4.5 Property tax and PILOT considerations

Connecticut municipalities impose **real property tax** at locally determined mill rates, which vary widely (Hartford, Bridgeport, and Waterbury have substantially higher mill rates than affluent Fairfield County towns). Construction projects in some municipalities benefit from **PILOT (Payment in Lieu of Taxes)** arrangements with local economic development authorities for redevelopment projects.

### 4.6 Withholding — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-700b and § 12-705

Employers must withhold Connecticut personal income tax from wages of employees at graduated rates under **§ 12-705** and remit through the DRS withholding system. For independent contractors (1099-NEC reportable), withholding is not required, but Connecticut requires **1099-NEC reporting** for payments to independent contractors of $600+, which interacts with the misclassification enforcement regime (see § 7 below).

## 5. Workers' Compensation — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-275 et seq.

### 5.1 Required for all employers — no minimum employee threshold

Connecticut's Workers' Compensation Act, codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-275 through § 31-355a** (Title 31 Chapter 568), requires **workers' compensation insurance coverage for all employers** with **one or more employees** [45][46][47][48]. Connecticut, unlike some states (e.g., Florida 4-employee threshold, Texas opt-out, South Carolina 4-employee, New Jersey 1-employee, Massachusetts 1-employee, New York 1-employee), has **no employee-count minimum** — a sole proprietor with even one employee must carry coverage.

Specifically, under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-284**, the workers' compensation act provides the **exclusive remedy** for work-related injury and death claims of covered employees (with narrow intentional-tort and dual-capacity exceptions). The "exclusive remedy" provision bars employees from suing employers in tort for work-related injuries — in exchange, employees receive no-fault medical and indemnity benefits.

### 5.2 The "principal employer" doctrine — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-291

A critical Connecticut feature is the **"principal employer" doctrine** at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-291**, which imposes **secondary liability** on a general contractor for workers' compensation benefits to an injured employee of an uninsured subcontractor [45][46][47]. Under § 31-291:

> "When any principal employer procures any work to be done wholly or in part for him by a contractor . . . , such principal employer shall be liable to pay all compensation under this chapter to the same extent as if the work were done without the intervention of such contractor."

The practical effect: a general contractor that engages a subcontractor with **lapsed or non-existent workers' compensation insurance** is **liable to the injured worker as if the worker were the GC's own employee**. The GC's workers' compensation carrier will pay the claim and then subrogate against the uninsured subcontractor (typically uncollectable in practice). The GC's experience modification and premium are affected.

This is a strict-liability doctrine — the GC's "good faith" belief in the subcontractor's coverage status is **not a defense**. The only protection is to **verify subcontractor coverage at every project** with **current Certificates of Insurance** and to enforce contractually that the sub maintains coverage throughout the engagement.

The principal-employer doctrine in Connecticut parallels Massachusetts c. 152 § 18 and the New York / New Jersey "statutory employer" doctrines, and is among the most aggressive in the country.

### 5.3 NCCI rate filings — Connecticut as an NCCI state

Connecticut is a **non-monopoly workers' compensation state** with rates filed by the **National Council on Compensation Insurance ("NCCI")** under regulation by the **Connecticut Insurance Department**. Construction-trade classification codes (e.g., 5403 — Carpentry; 5022 — Masonry; 5183 — Plumbing; 5190 — Electrical Wiring) are NCCI standard codes with state-specific loss-cost multipliers [48].

Experience-modification factors (EMRs) are computed by NCCI on a national basis and applied to Connecticut premiums. Construction firms with elevated EMRs face significant premium escalation; conversely, firms with sub-1.0 EMRs benefit from premium discounts and prequalification advantages on public-works bidding (see DAS prequalification at § 1.4 above).

### 5.4 Stop-Work Orders — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-288(c)

Under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-288(c)**, the Workers' Compensation Commission may issue a **Stop-Work Order** against any employer found operating without required workers' compensation coverage. The Stop-Work Order halts all business operations until coverage is reinstated and back-premium is paid, with **civil penalties** up to **$300 per day of non-compliance** and criminal sanctions in egregious cases.

In 2024–2025, Connecticut WCC stepped up enforcement in the construction sector, with construction-specific compliance sweeps in coordination with the Department of Labor's Worker Misclassification Joint Enforcement Commission (see § 7 below).

### 5.5 The Second Injury Fund — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-349

Connecticut's **Second Injury Fund** under § 31-349 is a state-administered fund that historically reimbursed insurers for portions of claims involving pre-existing injuries. The Fund was largely phased out for claims arising after 1995 but continues to administer legacy claims and a small ongoing assessment is built into Connecticut workers' compensation premiums.

### 5.6 Sole proprietor / corporate officer exemptions

Connecticut allows **sole proprietors** and certain **corporate officers** to elect **exclusion** from workers' compensation coverage under § 31-275(9)(B) and § 31-275(10). The exclusion election must be filed with the carrier and acknowledged in writing. A sole proprietor with no employees may opt out entirely; an LLC member or corporate officer may opt out individually if not classified as an "employee" under the statute.

This is the **principal Connecticut workers' compensation gotcha** for contractor onboarding: an LLC member who has elected exclusion may appear in the carrier's records as **not covered**, which can trigger downstream issues if the LLC then hires employees or if a GC requires evidence of full coverage. ContractorPro should classify each principal at onboarding and verify coverage status against carrier records.

## 6. CUTPA — Connecticut Unfair Trade Practices Act — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-110a et seq.

### 6.1 Statutory framework — § 42-110a et seq.

The **Connecticut Unfair Trade Practices Act ("CUTPA")**, codified at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-110a through § 42-110q**, was enacted as **Public Act 73-615** in 1973 and is modeled on Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act and on the Uniform Deceptive Trade Practices Act, with subsequent amendments expanding scope and remedies [9][10][11][12].

The core provisions:

- **§ 42-110b(a)** — declares unlawful "unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce";
- **§ 42-110b(b)** — directs courts and the Commissioner of Consumer Protection to be guided by FTC and federal-court decisions interpreting Section 5 of the FTC Act;
- **§ 42-110d** — authorizes the **Attorney General** to bring civil actions in the name of the state for injunctive relief, restitution, civil penalties, and costs;
- **§ 42-110f** — provides for civil penalties up to **$5,000 per violation** in AG actions, and additional **$5,000 per violation** for violations involving senior citizens;
- **§ 42-110g(a)** — provides a **private cause of action** for any person who suffers "any ascertainable loss" by virtue of any prohibited practice;
- **§ 42-110g(a)** further authorizes the court to award **actual damages, punitive damages, equitable relief, attorney fees, and costs**.

### 6.2 The "cigarette rule" — what is "unfair"

Connecticut courts apply the **"cigarette rule"** (derived from a 1964 FTC policy statement on cigarette advertising) to determine whether a practice is "unfair" under § 42-110b. Under the cigarette rule, courts consider:

1. Whether the practice **offends public policy** as established by statutes, common law, or otherwise (the "policy" prong);
2. Whether the practice is **immoral, unethical, oppressive, or unscrupulous** (the "morality" prong);
3. Whether the practice **causes substantial injury** to consumers, competitors, or other businesspersons (the "injury" prong).

The Connecticut Supreme Court in **Hartford Electric Light Co. v. Tucker, 76 Conn. App. 845 (2003)** and **A-G Foods, Inc. v. Pepperidge Farm, Inc., 216 Conn. 200 (1990)** confirmed that **all three prongs need not be satisfied** — a practice may be unfair if any one prong is satisfied to a "substantial" degree. The flexibility of the cigarette rule means that **a wide range of contractor conduct** may be found unfair: misrepresenting credentials, performing work without a license, demanding excessive payment, abandoning a project, providing inadequate warranties, and so on [9][10][11][12].

### 6.3 Punitive damages — § 42-110g(a) and the Ulbrich standard

Under **§ 42-110g(a)**, a court may award **punitive damages** to a successful CUTPA plaintiff. The Connecticut Supreme Court in **Ulbrich v. Groth, 310 Conn. 375 (2013)** established the modern standard: punitive damages are available where the defendant's conduct involves **reckless indifference to the rights of others** or an **intentional and wanton violation of those rights**. The amount is committed to the trial court's discretion and may approximate or exceed **treble compensatory damages** in egregious cases, depending on the conduct's severity and the defendant's economic position.

The Ulbrich standard is more flexible than the rigid 3x cap of New Jersey CFA or the 30-day-demand-letter-driven double-or-treble structure of Massachusetts c. 93A. In practice, Connecticut courts have awarded punitive damages in the 1x–3x range in most contractor cases, with attorney fees as the more reliably recovered component of the CUTPA remedy.

### 6.4 Attorney fees and costs — § 42-110g(d)

Under **§ 42-110g(d)**, the prevailing CUTPA plaintiff is entitled to recover **reasonable attorney fees and costs** as a matter of right (subject to the court's reasonable-fee determination). This fee-shifting provision is the **dominant economic engine** of CUTPA enforcement — plaintiffs' counsel can pursue moderate-damages cases with confidence that the fee recovery will support the litigation cost. The result is that Connecticut residential contractors face a CUTPA-claim risk profile in which **small procedural defects** can drive **disproportionate attorney-fee exposure** even where compensatory damages are modest.

### 6.5 Per se CUTPA violations — HIA, NHCC, and other statutory cross-references

In addition to the discretionary "cigarette rule" determination, Connecticut law contains **numerous statutory cross-references** that automatically convert violations of specific statutes into per se CUTPA violations [9][10][12][14][15][16]:

1. **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-427(c)** — any HIA violation is a per se CUTPA violation;
2. **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417g** — any NHCC Act violation is a per se CUTPA violation;
3. **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-341c** — any unlicensed-trade-work violation is a per se CUTPA violation;
4. **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-141(a)** — any Home Solicitation Sales Act violation is a per se CUTPA violation;
5. **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-158l** — wilful prompt-payment violations may be a CUTPA violation;
6. **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 36a-741** — Consumer Credit License Act violations are CUTPA violations.

The cumulative per se mechanism means that virtually every consumer-protection statute relevant to construction has a built-in CUTPA escalator. Each per se violation triggers the full § 42-110g remedy (actual damages + punitive damages + attorney fees + costs) without separate proof of the cigarette-rule elements.

### 6.6 Comparison to New Jersey CFA and Massachusetts c. 93A

| Feature | Connecticut CUTPA | New Jersey CFA | Massachusetts c. 93A |
|---|---|---|---|
| Damages multiplier | Punitive, court-discretion (typically 1x–3x) | Treble (mandatory upon ascertainable loss) | Double-to-treble (depending on willfulness) |
| Attorney fees | Yes (mandatory upon prevailing) | Yes (mandatory) | Yes (upon willful + demand letter procedure) |
| Demand letter | Not required | Not required | 30-day demand letter required (consumer) |
| HIC per se trigger | Yes (§ 20-427(c)) | Yes (CRA + N.J.A.C. 13:45A-17.4) | Yes (c. 142A + 201 CMR 14.05) |
| NHCC per se | Yes (§ 20-417g) | NJ has no separate NHCC | MA has no separate NHCC |
| Class action | Yes (§ 42-110g(b)) | Yes (limited) | Yes |
| Statute of limitations | 3 years (§ 42-110g(f)) | 6 years generally | 4 years |

Among the three regimes, **Connecticut's CUTPA has the broadest substantive scope** (cigarette rule + per se mechanism), the **most flexible punitive-damages structure**, and the **strictest contractor recovery bar** under *Habetz v. Condon* and *Barrett Builders v. Miller* (no quantum meruit absent narrow bad-faith defense). The exposure profile for a Connecticut residential contractor with even minor procedural defects is **at least as severe as** New Jersey or Massachusetts.

### 6.7 CUTPA and trade-license violations

Performing work in any DCP-licensed trade (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, sprinkler, etc.) **without a valid license** is automatically a CUTPA violation under **§ 20-341c** and is also a Class B (first offense) / Class A (subsequent) misdemeanor under § 20-341a. The DCP has authority to issue **cease-and-desist orders**, **administrative penalties up to $500 per violation**, and to refer cases for criminal prosecution. The combined civil-criminal-administrative exposure for unlicensed trade work is among the most aggressive enforcement regimes in the country.

## 7. Worker Classification — ABC Test and the Joint Enforcement Commission

### 7.1 Connecticut's ABC test — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii)

Connecticut uses an **"ABC test"** to determine whether a worker is an **employee** or an **independent contractor** for **unemployment compensation purposes** under **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii)** [49][50][51][52]. Under the test, a worker is **presumed to be an employee** unless the employer can establish **all three** of the following:

- **(A)** the worker has been and will continue to be **free from control and direction** in connection with the performance of the service, both under the contract for the performance of service and in fact;
- **(B)** the service is performed **either outside the usual course of the business of the employer** or **is performed outside of all the places of business of the enterprise** for which the service is performed; and
- **(C)** the worker is **customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession or business of the same nature** as that involved in the service performed.

Connecticut's ABC test is **conjunctive** — all three prongs must be satisfied — and the **burden is on the employer**. Failure on any one prong results in employee classification.

Prong B is the **fatal prong** for most construction misclassification: a roofing company that engages a "1099 roofer" cannot satisfy prong B because the roofer's work is **in the usual course of business** of the roofing company. The "alternative" formulation of prong B (work performed outside the employer's places of business) generally does not help because the construction worksite is the employer's "place of business" for the duration of the project. The result: Connecticut residential and commercial construction firms cannot lawfully 1099 workers performing core trade work.

### 7.2 The Joint Enforcement Commission on Worker Misclassification — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-57h

The **Joint Enforcement Commission on Worker Misclassification ("JEC")** was established by **Public Act 08-156** at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-57h** to coordinate enforcement of worker-misclassification laws across multiple Connecticut agencies [49][50][51][52]. The JEC includes representatives from:

- **Department of Labor** (lead — wage and hour enforcement, ABC-test application);
- **Department of Revenue Services** (withholding and 1099 reporting);
- **Workers' Compensation Commission** (WC coverage enforcement);
- **Department of Consumer Protection** (contractor licensing enforcement);
- **Insurance Department** (WC carrier reporting);
- **Attorney General** (civil enforcement, CUTPA).

The JEC was expanded by **Public Act 22-129** to include **construction-specific enforcement** mandates and to authorize **multi-agency joint investigations** of construction firms suspected of misclassification. Public Act 22-129 also created a **public registry of construction firms with misclassification judgments** to inform consumer choice and public-bidding decisions.

The JEC has been **active and aggressive in 2024–2026**, with quarterly enforcement sweeps in the construction sector, targeted audits of residential and commercial general contractors, and coordinated WC / DOL / DRS assessments. Construction firms found in violation face:

1. **Back-payment of wages** (state minimum wage + overtime under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-58 et seq.);
2. **Back-payment of unemployment insurance premiums** (with interest and penalties);
3. **Back-payment of workers' compensation premiums** (with interest and Stop-Work Order if applicable);
4. **Back-payment of state income tax withholding** (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-705);
5. **1099-NEC reporting penalties** (federal IRS exposure as well);
6. **CUTPA exposure** through the cross-reference to unlicensed-trade and HIA per se violations;
7. **Suspension or revocation of HIC / NHCC / Major Contractor registration** by DCP.

### 7.3 Penalties — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-273 and § 31-288

Under **§ 31-273**, an employer that **wilfully fails to pay unemployment-compensation contributions** is liable for **penalty of 25% of the contributions due** plus interest at 1% per month. Under **§ 31-288**, an employer that **wilfully misclassifies an employee as an independent contractor** is subject to a civil penalty of up to **$300 per misclassified worker per offense** under the WC statute, in addition to the wage and unemployment-tax remedies.

Public Act 22-129 increased the civil-penalty range and added criminal-misdemeanor exposure for serial or wilful violations.

### 7.4 Prevailing wage — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-53 et seq.

Public-works projects above prescribed thresholds (currently **$1,000,000 new construction / $100,000 remodel** under § 31-53(g) as amended) require payment of **prevailing wages** to all workers under the Connecticut Prevailing Wage Act [53][54]. The prevailing-wage rates are set by the Department of Labor through annual surveys and are published by trade and project type. Violations expose the contractor to:

1. **Back-wage assessment** plus liquidated damages;
2. **Civil penalty up to $5,000 per violation**;
3. **Debarment from public works** for up to **three years**;
4. **Personal liability of officers and directors** under § 31-72 for unpaid prevailing wages.

Certified payroll reporting is required and is a standard part of public-works compliance.

### 7.5 Wage Act and personal liability — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-72 and § 31-72a

Connecticut's **Wage Act** at **Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-58 through § 31-76m** imposes a **personal-liability** rule similar to the Massachusetts c. 149 § 148 / Wiedmann doctrine: under **§ 31-72**, "any officer of a corporation or of any individual employer who shall, **knowingly and intentionally**, fail or refuse to pay wages due to an employee" may be held **personally liable** for the unpaid wages plus **double the amount of the wages** as liquidated damages plus attorney fees. The "knowingly and intentionally" standard is enforced by the courts, but the burden is on the officer to demonstrate good-faith absence of knowledge.

The personal-liability exposure means construction-firm owners and officers cannot use the corporate-veil shield to avoid wage-claim liability for unpaid workers — the wages must be paid on time and in full, with proper records, to avoid personal exposure.

## 8. Recent Legislative Changes — 2024–2026

### 8.1 Connecticut General Assembly — January 2024, 2025, and 2026 sessions

Connecticut operates on annual legislative sessions (long sessions in odd years, short sessions in even years). Several construction-relevant Public Acts have advanced or are pending in the 2024–2026 window [55][56][57][58]:

#### 8.1.1 Public Act 24-39 — NHCC Reforms — 2024

**Public Act 24-39**, signed by Governor Lamont in 2024 and effective October 1, 2024, made the following changes to the NHCC Act [4][5][14][16][55]:

1. **Increased NHCC Guaranty Fund per-claim cap** from $25,000 to **$30,000**;
2. **Added CGL insurance minimum** of **$300,000** under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 20-417c;
3. **Narrowed the single-build exemption** to address abuse by one-off builders avoiding registration;
4. **Strengthened contract-form requirements** to require explicit warranty disclosure aligned with the New Home Warranties Act;
5. **Required NHCC registration number disclosure** on construction-site signage (parallel to HIC).

Status as of May 2026: in effect since October 1, 2024.

#### 8.1.2 Public Act 23-101 — HIC Modernization — 2023

**Public Act 23-101** (effective October 1, 2023) updated the HIA in several respects [3][14][15][16][56]:

1. **Expanded HIC contract digital-marketing disclosure** requirements;
2. **Updated insurance disclosure** to require disclosure of CGL carrier and limits;
3. **Updated change-order writing requirement** to clarify electronic signature acceptability;
4. **Increased registration fee** modestly;
5. **Directed DCP to study a statutory deposit cap** (study not yet completed).

#### 8.1.3 Public Act 22-129 — JEC Expansion — 2022

**Public Act 22-129** expanded the JEC's authority and added the public-registry mechanism for construction-firm misclassification judgments [49][50][51][52][57].

#### 8.1.4 Pending bills — 2026 session

Several bills pending in the **January 2026 session** (as of May 2026) include:

- **HB 5168 / SB 437** — proposed **statutory deposit cap** for HIC contracts (one-third of contract price, with special-order exception);
- **HB 5234** — proposed expansion of NHCC Guaranty Fund per-claim cap to **$50,000**;
- **HB 5301** — proposed extension of mechanic's lien filing period from 90 days to **120 days**;
- **SB 412** — proposed harmonization of HIC, NHCC, and Major Contractor renewal cycles to a uniform two-year schedule with combined-registration discount;
- **HB 5402** — proposed clarification of the "owner-occupied" definition under HIA to exclude rental property and reduce ambiguity.

Status: in committee. The 2026 short session ends in early May 2026, with carry-over to 2027 in some cases.

### 8.2 Connecticut State Building Code transition — 2022 Connecticut State Building Code

The Connecticut State Building Code transitioned in 2022 from the **2018 ICC family** to the **2021 ICC family** with Connecticut amendments at **R.C.S.A. § 29-252-1d**, adopted by the State Building Inspector. The 2022 Code remains in effect through 2026 with administrative interpretations issued by the State Building Inspector's office. ContractorPro should treat code-cross-reference logic for Connecticut as **versioned**, with the active code edition determined by permit issuance date.

### 8.3 Energy code and decarbonization — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 16a-38k

Connecticut's energy code, codified at **§ 16a-38k** and administered by the **Office of the State Building Inspector**, follows the **2021 IECC** with Connecticut amendments. Connecticut has not adopted a state-wide net-zero or all-electric mandate but allows municipalities to adopt **stretch energy code** provisions. The Connecticut Green Bank and Department of Energy and Environmental Protection (DEEP) administer incentive programs that affect construction practices and product choices.

### 8.4 Public Act 21-37 and 19-117 — Earlier amendments

**Public Acts 21-37 and 19-117** modernized DCP fee schedules, expanded insurance disclosure, added digital-marketing scope, and tightened the per se CUTPA mechanism. These earlier amendments form the baseline of the current HIC framework.

## 9. ContractorPro Implementation Notes — Connecticut-Specific Behaviors

Drawing the threads above together, ContractorPro's Connecticut behaviors must include:

### 9.1 Credential management

1. **Triple-registration verification at contractor onboarding** — DCP HIC, NHCC, and (where applicable) Major Contractor registration, plus trade licenses (E-1, P-1, B-1, S-1, F-1, R-1 as applicable). Hard block on residential-remodel job creation if HIC missing; hard block on new-construction job creation if NHCC missing; hard block on commercial-major work if Major Contractor missing.
2. **Trade-license verification at every project** — electrical, plumbing, HVAC, sheet metal, sprinkler each with separate licensee lookup and renewal tracking.
3. **Annual renewal calendars** for each registration (HIC annual, NHCC annual, Major Contractor annual) and trade licenses (annual with 3-hour CE for E-1, P-1, B-1, S-1, F-1).
4. **Guaranty Fund assessment status** tracked separately for HIC and NHCC.
5. **DAS prequalification tracking** for any public-bid work above $500,000.

### 9.2 Contract intake

1. **Property-type classification** — owner-occupied 1-6 unit (HIC), new construction (NHCC), 7+ unit residential or commercial (Major Contractor / general). Determines applicable registration and Guaranty Fund eligibility.
2. **Owner-occupied vs. rental classification** — HIC scope includes rental property (unlike MA which is owner-occupied-only); confirm classification for tax purposes since taxable services apply to income-producing real property.
3. **Home Solicitation Sales Act applicability** — if contract signed at owner's residence, mandatory **three-business-day cancellation notice** in statutory form attached to contract, with perforation/tear-line separation.
4. **Contract-template generation** — Connecticut-specific template for any HIC contract above $200 (or NHCC contract for new construction) incorporating all § 20-429(a) / § 20-417d required elements, three-day cancellation notice for HSSA, DCP registration-number disclosure, statutory warranty disclosure (express + implied), change-order writing requirement, and Guaranty Fund notice.
5. **No statutory deposit cap** but flag deposits above one-third of contract price as elevated CUTPA-risk practice; surface DCP-customary guidance.

### 9.3 Lien-rights tracking

1. **Last-day-of-work continuous tracking** through final punch-list, with substantive-versus-trivial work classification.
2. **90-day filing deadline alarm** from last day of work — hard alarm at day 60, escalation at day 75, final alarm at day 85.
3. **30-day owner-service deadline alarm** from filing of mechanic's lien.
4. **One-year foreclosure deadline alarm** from filing of mechanic's lien (§ 49-39).
5. **Subcontractor lien-fund visibility** — surface owner-paid-down-to-GC balance at all times for any project where contractor is a subcontractor; alarm when owner-paid-down approaches GC contract balance (subcontractor lien rights are about to be capped or extinguished).
6. **Public-works classification** — if project is public works, switch from lien tracking to **§ 49-41 payment bond** claim tracking with **180-day claim deadline**.

### 9.4 Prompt-pay tracking ($25,000+ private projects)

1. **30-day owner-payment-due alarm** from invoice receipt.
2. **30-day GC-pay-down-to-sub alarm** from owner-payment receipt.
3. **1%/month (12% annual) interest auto-accrual** on overdue amounts.
4. **7-day suspension-of-performance notice generation** at day 67 (30 + 30 + 7) where owner has not paid.
5. **Withholding-notice tracking** for any disputed-work withholding within the 30-day window.

### 9.5 Worker classification

1. **No 1099 within core trade scope** — hard block on 1099 designation for workers performing the contractor's core trade. Prong B of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-222(a)(1)(B)(ii) fails as a matter of law.
2. **Subcontractor WC verification** at every subcontract — required by Conn. Gen. Stat. § 31-291 principal-employer doctrine. Lapsed WC at subcontractor surfaces immediate alarm with GC's secondary liability exposure.
3. **Certified payroll generation** for any prevailing-wage project (public works above $1M new construction / $100K remodel).
4. **JEC public-registry check** — at any subcontractor onboarding, check whether subcontractor appears on the JEC misclassification public registry.

### 9.6 Tax handling

1. **Property-type tax classification** at intake — owner-occupied residential (no tax on services), income-producing residential (taxable services on labor), commercial (taxable services on labor), or new construction (no tax on services).
2. **Sales tax on labor for income-producing / commercial renovation** — apply 6.35% to the labor portion of the contract; itemize on customer invoice.
3. **Contractor-as-consumer materials tax** — pay 6.35% on materials at supplier; do not bill separately to customer.
4. **CERT-141 generation** for any exempt-entity project with the underlying exemption documentation.
5. **Use-tax accrual** for any out-of-state material purchases (especially New Hampshire and Massachusetts) brought into Connecticut.
6. **Pass-Through Entity Tax** — calculate quarterly estimated PET at 6.99% on Connecticut-sourced net income; surface payment schedule to owner.

### 9.7 CUTPA handling

1. **HIC violation surveillance** — track each potential per se CUTPA trigger (unregistered work, missing contract elements, missing registration-number disclosure on advertising, oral change orders, failure to attach HSSA cancellation notice) and alarm before exposure crystallizes.
2. **Attorney-fee exposure modeling** — for any disputed contract above $10K, model the worst-case CUTPA attorney-fee exposure at 1.5x to 3x the disputed amount; surface to owner for settlement strategy.
3. **30-day pre-suit period** — Connecticut does not require a pre-suit demand letter under CUTPA (unlike MA c. 93A), but a reasonable settlement offer documented in writing helps with the punitive-damages multiplier under the *Ulbrich* standard.
4. **Bar-to-recovery awareness** — for any contract with even a minor HIA defect, surface the *Barrett Builders v. Miller* / *Habetz v. Condon* bar to recovery; recommend remediation (signed addendum that conforms to § 20-429(a)) before any disputed billing escalates.

### 9.8 Workers' compensation handling

1. **Single-employee threshold** — flag any Connecticut employer without WC coverage as a hard violation regardless of employee count.
2. **Principal-employer doctrine surveillance** — at every subcontractor engagement, confirm subcontractor WC coverage and document Certificate of Insurance on file; alarm immediately on lapse.
3. **Stop-Work Order risk** — surface NCCI EMR and CWCC enforcement history at contractor onboarding.
4. **Sole-proprietor / officer exclusion election** — track exclusion status separately per principal.
