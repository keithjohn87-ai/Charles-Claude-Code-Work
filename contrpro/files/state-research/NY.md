## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The New York anomaly — no statewide general contractor license, just a municipal patchwork

New York is **the largest state in the United States that licenses general contractors entirely at the municipal level** [1][2][3]. Unlike California (Contractors State License Board), Florida (Construction Industry Licensing Board), or Tennessee (Board for Licensing Contractors), **there is no New York state agency that issues a general contractor license**, no state-issued tradesman license for electricians or plumbers, and no statewide registration database. The state of New York does **not require** a single license for any general construction trade — every licensing requirement that touches ContrPro's five trades is imposed by a city, county, or town, plus the state-level statutes covering workers' compensation, mechanic's liens, prompt payment, and tax administration.

What this means practically: **a general contractor that works in New York City, Nassau County, and Westchester County typically holds three separate licenses or registrations from three separate jurisdictions** — none of which is interchangeable. A contractor that bids on a project in Buffalo or Syracuse needs to verify the relevant city or town building department's registration scheme independently. The lack of state preemption is intentional under the New York Home Rule provision (N.Y. Const. art. IX) and the Municipal Home Rule Law [4], which authorize local governments to regulate construction safety, consumer protection, and trade licensure within their geographic jurisdictions.

The state-level statutory framework that backstops the municipal patchwork:

- **N.Y. Education Law Article 145** — licensure of engineers, architects, land surveyors, and landscape architects (these are state-licensed design professionals; contractors are not licensed by the Department of Education) [5].
- **N.Y. General Business Law Article 36-A (§ 770 et seq.)** — Home Improvement Business; this is **not a license**, it is a statutory consumer-protection regime that overlays whatever municipal license is required, governing residential home-improvement contracts of $500 or more [6][7].
- **N.Y. General Business Law Article 36-B (§§ 777–777-d)** — Housing Merchant Implied Warranty (statutory warranty for new home construction) [8].
- **N.Y. Workers' Compensation Law** — universally required for construction employees regardless of jurisdiction [9][10].

For ContrPro's five trades — general, mechanical/HVAC, electrical, plumbing, and structural steel — the licensing path is jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction. The remainder of this section walks the four most-trafficked jurisdictions: New York City (DOB and DCWP), Nassau County, Suffolk County, and Westchester County, plus a representative upstate city (Buffalo).

### 1.2 New York City — Department of Buildings (DOB) and Department of Consumer and Worker Protection (DCWP)

NYC contractor licensure is split between **two municipal agencies**:

1. **New York City Department of Buildings (DOB)** — licenses the trade-construction crafts (master plumber, master electrician, master fire suppression piping contractor, hoisting machine operator, site safety manager, sign hanger, oil burner installer, refrigeration machine operator, and other building trades). DOB also issues **General Contractor Registration** (GCR) — required for any GC pulling permits in NYC [11][12][13].
2. **NYC Department of Consumer and Worker Protection (DCWP)**, formerly the Department of Consumer Affairs (DCA) — licenses **Home Improvement Contractors** (HICs), as well as other consumer-facing businesses. DCWP's licensing authority for HICs is rooted in NYC Administrative Code Title 20, Subchapter 22 (§§ 20-386–20-410) [14][15].

The split is jurisdictional, not categorical: **DOB licenses what can be built; DCWP licenses who can sell residential remodeling work to consumers.** Most contractors operating in NYC need at least one DOB license/registration and, if they touch residential remodeling work, a DCWP HIC license as well.

#### 1.2.1 NYC General Contractor Registration (GCR) — DOB

**Statutory authority:** NYC Administrative Code § 28-401.3 et seq. (the NYC Construction Codes, Chapter 4 — Licensing and Registration of Businesses, Trades and Occupations Engaged in Building Work) [11][16]. Administrative rules at **1 RCNY Chapter 104** (Rules of the City of New York governing general contractor registration) [12].

**Licensing agency:** NYC Department of Buildings — Licensing and Exams Unit, 280 Broadway, 1st Floor, New York, NY 10007; (212) 393-2259; nyc.gov/dob.

**Who must register:** Any **general contractor** that supervises, oversees, or arranges any work for which a permit is required under the NYC Construction Codes. Under NYC Admin. Code § 28-401.3, the "general contractor" of record on a building permit must be a **registered general contractor**. This applies to commercial work, multifamily residential of more than two stories, and any structural work requiring a permit. There is **no separate licensing examination** for a GC — the registration is a **business registration**, not a personal-competency license.

**Requirements** [12][13][16]:

- Submission of **DOB Form LIC2 (General Contractor Registration Application)** with documentation establishing the principal's identity, business form, and FEIN.
- **Trade Identification Number (TIN)** from DOB.
- **Workers' Compensation insurance** evidenced by Form C-105.2 (or NYS-45) with NYC DOB listed.
- **Disability Benefits insurance** evidenced by Form DB-120.1.
- **Commercial general liability insurance** — minimum **$1,000,000 per occurrence / $2,000,000 aggregate**, with NYC DOB and the City of New York listed as additional insureds (1 RCNY § 104-01) [12].
- **Owner/principal background check** — fingerprinting and criminal-history disclosure under NYC Admin. Code § 28-401.9.
- **Active OSHA 30 training** for the principal (or designated competent person) — applies to construction superintendents under Local Law 196 of 2017 (see § 5.3 below).

**Fees** [12][13]:
- Application fee: **$300**
- Registration term: **3 years** (triennial renewal)
- Renewal fee: **$300** per 3-year cycle

**Penalty for unregistered general-contracting in NYC:** A general contractor that performs work for which a permit is required without a current GCR is subject to civil penalties up to **$25,000 per violation** under NYC Admin. Code § 28-401.19, and the work may be **subject to a stop work order** under NYC Admin. Code § 28-207.2. Repeat unregistered work is a separate violation per day under § 28-202.1.

#### 1.2.2 NYC Construction Superintendent registration (DOB)

**Statutory authority:** NYC Admin. Code § 28-403.1; 1 RCNY § 104-08 [17].

A **construction superintendent** is required to supervise the construction, demolition, or alteration of any **non-major building of 1, 2, or 3 stories** (NYC Admin. Code § 28-105.10.1). Major buildings (10 stories or more, or 100,000 sq ft of work) require a **site safety manager** or **site safety coordinator** instead.

The construction superintendent must be **registered with DOB**, hold a current OSHA 30 card, and complete the DOB-approved 16-hour Site Safety Training Course (SST). The construction superintendent's name appears on the building permit alongside the GCR. Penalty for performing construction-superintendent work without registration: up to **$25,000 per violation** under NYC Admin. Code § 28-401.19.

#### 1.2.3 NYC Site Safety Manager / Site Safety Coordinator (DOB)

**Statutory authority:** NYC Admin. Code § 28-403.1 (Site Safety Manager); 1 RCNY § 104-08 [17][18]. NYC Local Law 196 of 2017 expanded training and oversight requirements (see § 5.3).

**Site Safety Manager (SSM)** — required for major buildings 15 stories or more. Holds a personal license issued by DOB after a written examination, 5 years of qualifying experience, OSHA 30, and 40 hours of site-safety-manager training.

**Site Safety Coordinator (SSC)** — required for major buildings of 10–14 stories or 100,000 sq ft or more. Personal license issued by DOB after examination and qualifying experience.

#### 1.2.4 NYC Master Plumber license (DOB)

**Statutory authority:** NYC Admin. Code § 28-408 (Plumbers) [19]; 1 RCNY § 104-04. Plumbing is regulated by DOB, not DCWP.

**License agency:** NYC DOB, Licensing and Exams Unit.

**Requirements for Master Plumber** [19][20]:

- Minimum **7 years of full-time plumbing experience** under a Master Plumber, of which at least 2 years must be in a supervisory capacity.
- Pass the DOB Master Plumber written and practical examinations.
- Be at least 21 years old, a U.S. citizen or lawful resident, and of good moral character.
- Fingerprinting and background check.
- Continuing education: **7 hours per year** (1 RCNY § 104-04(f)).

**Insurance / bond:** Master Plumber must file with DOB:

- A surety bond of **$25,000** (NYC Admin. Code § 28-408.4).
- Commercial general liability of **$1,000,000 per occurrence / $2,000,000 aggregate** (1 RCNY § 104-04).
- Workers' Comp and Disability Benefits coverage.

**Fees:**
- Application fee: **$525** (varies by license category)
- Renewal fee: **$300** per 3-year cycle

**License term:** **3 years**.

**Penalty:** A person performing plumbing work without a Master Plumber license is subject to civil penalty up to **$10,000 per violation** under NYC Admin. Code § 28-401.19; engaging in repeated unlicensed plumbing is a Class A misdemeanor under NYC Admin. Code § 28-201.1.

#### 1.2.5 NYC Master Electrician / Special Electrician license (DOB)

**Statutory authority:** NYC Admin. Code § 27-3017 (Electricians) (within the NYC Electrical Code, Title 27 Subchapter 3) and 1 RCNY § 104-03 [21][22]. The NYC Electrical Code is administered by DOB in conjunction with the NYC Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS), which administers the underlying examination.

**License classes:**

| License | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Master Electrician** | Engages in the business of electrical contracting, supervising and performing electrical work under NYC Electrical Code. |
| **Special Electrician** | Restricted-scope electrical work for a particular employer (e.g., utility, manufacturing facility) — does not engage in general electrical contracting. |

**Requirements for Master Electrician** [21][22]:

- **7½ years (90 months) of full-time, paid electrical experience** under a Master Electrician (a "Special Electrician" tier credit is partially available — see DOB rules).
- Pass the **DCAS-administered written examination** and the DOB practical/oral examination.
- Be at least 21 years old, U.S. citizen or lawful resident, and of good moral character.
- Fingerprinting and background check.
- Continuing education: **7 hours per 3-year renewal cycle** under 1 RCNY § 104-03.

**Insurance / bond:**
- Surety bond of **$10,000** (NYC Admin. Code § 27-3017).
- Commercial general liability of **$1,000,000 per occurrence / $2,000,000 aggregate**.
- Workers' Comp and Disability Benefits coverage.

**Fees:**
- Application fee: **$525**
- Renewal fee: **$300** per 3-year cycle

**License term:** **3 years**.

**Penalty:** Performing electrical work without a Master Electrician or Special Electrician license is subject to civil penalty up to **$10,000 per violation** under NYC Admin. Code § 28-401.19 and may be a Class A misdemeanor under § 28-201.1.

#### 1.2.6 NYC Master Fire Suppression Piping Contractor (DOB)

**Statutory authority:** NYC Admin. Code § 28-410; 1 RCNY § 104-05.

Required for any contractor performing fire-sprinkler or standpipe piping work in NYC. **7 years of qualifying experience**, written and practical exam. Surety bond **$10,000**; GL of $1M/$2M. **3-year license term**. The Master Fire Suppression Piping Contractor license is distinct from the Master Plumber license; a Master Plumber **cannot** lawfully install a sprinkler standpipe system in NYC.

#### 1.2.7 NYC Refrigeration Machine Operator and HVAC trades

NYC does **not** issue a general "HVAC contractor" license. Instead, NYC regulates HVAC work piecewise:

- **Refrigeration Machine Operator (RMO)** — personal license issued by DOB to operate refrigeration systems of more than 10 tons (NYC Admin. Code § 28-417). Required to operate large commercial refrigeration plants; not required for installation of residential split systems.
- **Oil Burner Installer (OBI)** — personal license issued by DOB to install or modify No. 2 fuel oil and No. 4/6 oil-fired heating systems (NYC Admin. Code § 28-412).
- **Master Plumber** — required for any work on the gas-piping system serving a furnace or boiler, including connection of HVAC equipment to gas lines (see NYC Local Law 152 of 2016, § 5.4 below).
- **Master Electrician** — required for electrical connections.

A typical commercial HVAC installation in NYC therefore involves **a Master Plumber (for gas piping), a Master Electrician (for electrical connections), and the GC (for permit and supervision)** — but **no separate "HVAC contractor" license exists**. This is a significant departure from California, Florida, Tennessee, and Texas, all of which license HVAC at the state level.

#### 1.2.8 NYC Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) — DCWP

**Statutory authority:** NYC Administrative Code § 20-386 et seq. (Home Improvement Business) [14][15]; 6 RCNY Subchapter X (Rules of the City of New York governing Home Improvement Contractors). This is a **DCWP** license, separate from the DOB GCR.

**Who must license:** Any individual or business that solicits, sells, or performs residential home-improvement work in NYC totaling more than **$200** in any one job [14]. "Home improvement" is defined broadly to include repairs, renovations, modernizations, additions, conversions, alterations, replacement of any part of a residential dwelling — and applies to dwellings of **fewer than four units** or owner-occupied multi-family dwellings.

**Critical scope distinction:** An HIC license is required for **residential** home improvement work. A commercial-only contractor does not need an HIC license, but most NYC contractors that perform any work on owner-occupied homes, apartments, condos, or co-ops need both a DOB registration (if the work requires a permit) AND a DCWP HIC license. Many small residential remodelers operate solely under a DCWP HIC and **never pull a DOB permit** — exposing them to civil and criminal penalties when work exceeds the no-permit threshold.

**Requirements** [14][15]:

- Pass a **written examination** covering NYC and NYS building codes, consumer-protection laws, lien law, and HIC contract requirements.
- File a **Trust Fund Account** affidavit or post a **surety bond of $20,000** (NYC Admin. Code § 20-387) — the Trust Fund is an option for individual licensees rather than for businesses.
- File proof of **Commercial General Liability insurance** of at least **$300,000 per occurrence / $600,000 aggregate** (6 RCNY § 2-221).
- Workers' Compensation and Disability Benefits coverage.

**Fees** [15]:
- Application fee: **$50**
- Initial license: **$150**
- 2-year license term
- Renewal: $100

**License term:** **2 years**.

**Mandatory written contract:** Under NYC Admin. Code § 20-393.2 and the parallel state statute (N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law § 771), an HIC may not commence work without a written contract that includes: (1) the contractor's name, address, license number; (2) a description of the work; (3) the dates work will start and end; (4) the total cost; (5) the right of cancellation; (6) a deposit-trust-fund acknowledgment; and (7) progress-payment schedules tied to performance milestones. Failure of the contract triggers per se forfeiture of the right to file a mechanic's lien on a single-family residence under N.Y. Lien Law § 71-a(2) [23].

**Trust fund obligation:** Under N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law § 770(7) and NYC Admin. Code § 20-387, **deposits and progress payments received from a home-improvement customer are statutory trust funds**, held for the benefit of the customer and the contractor's subs/suppliers until applied to the work. Misapplication of HIC trust funds is a separate violation of GBL § 776 and may also be a criminal misapplication of trust funds under N.Y. Lien Law § 79-a (see § 2.7 below).

**Penalty for unlicensed home improvement work:** Class A misdemeanor under NYC Admin. Code § 20-401 — up to 1 year imprisonment, $500 fine, or both. **Civil consequence:** An unlicensed HIC **cannot recover in either contract or quantum meruit** from a homeowner — the New York Court of Appeals has consistently held that an HIC license is a prerequisite to suit, and even quantum meruit recovery is barred (B&F Bldg. Corp. v. Liebig, 76 N.Y.2d 689 (1990) [24]; Chosen Construction Corp. v. Syz, 138 A.D.2d 284 (1st Dep't 1988)). Mechanic's lien rights are forfeited as well.

#### 1.2.9 NYC certified asbestos investigator/handler

Demolition or substantial alteration work in NYC almost always requires asbestos investigation or removal — DOB will not issue a demolition or major-alteration permit without an **ACP-5 form** (Asbestos-Free Affidavit) issued by a **certified asbestos investigator** licensed by the NYC Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) and the New York State Department of Labor under Industrial Code Rule 56 [25]. This is not a contractor license but is a frequent gating item for ContrPro work in any NYC building constructed before 1989.

### 1.3 Nassau County (Long Island) — Department of Consumer Affairs

**Statutory authority:** Nassau County Administrative Code § 21-11 et seq. (Home Improvement Contractors), administered by the Nassau County Department of Consumer Affairs (NCDCA) [26].

**Who must license:** Any person or business engaged in **home improvement** in Nassau County. Nassau's definition tracks the state HIC definition under N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law Article 36-A but is enforced at the county level. Commercial-only contractors are not required to license, but virtually any residential remodeling business operating in Nassau is.

**Requirements** [26]:

- Application with corporate documents, FEIN, principals' identification.
- **Surety bond of $25,000** OR a Trust Fund Account.
- Commercial general liability insurance — **$300,000 per occurrence / $1,000,000 aggregate** minimum.
- Workers' Compensation, Disability Benefits.
- Pass a written examination covering NY building codes, NCDCA consumer-protection rules, and the NY HIC law.

**Fees:**
- Application fee: **$200**
- License term: **2 years**
- Renewal: $300

A contractor that holds a NYC DCWP HIC license is **not automatically licensed in Nassau** — Nassau license is required separately. The **same is true in reverse**: a Nassau HIC cannot operate in NYC without obtaining the DCWP HIC license.

### 1.4 Suffolk County — Office of Consumer Affairs

**Statutory authority:** Suffolk County Code Chapter 563 (Home Improvement Contractor Licensing), administered by the Suffolk County Office of Consumer Affairs [27].

Similar structure to Nassau: separate county-level HIC license, **surety bond $20,000** OR Trust Fund Account, GL insurance of **$300,000/$1,000,000**, written examination, **2-year license term**, application fee approximately **$200**.

### 1.5 Westchester County — Department of Consumer Protection

**Statutory authority:** Westchester County Laws Chapter 863 (Consumer Protection Code), administered by the Westchester County Department of Consumer Protection (WDCP) [28].

Separate county-level Home Improvement Contractor and Home Improvement Salesperson licenses. **Surety bond $5,000** for HICs; GL insurance of **$500,000 minimum**, **3-year license term**, fees approximately **$140** application.

### 1.6 Buffalo / Erie County — City of Buffalo Permit & Inspection Services

**Statutory authority:** Buffalo City Code Chapter 137 (Buildings) and Chapter 175 (Contractors), administered by the City of Buffalo Department of Permit & Inspection Services (DPIS) [29].

Buffalo requires a **General Contractor License** for any GC pulling building permits in Buffalo; the licensure scheme is separate from NYC and county-level downstate jurisdictions. Requirements include a written examination, GL insurance, and workers' compensation. **Erie County does not impose a separate countywide GC license** — each city and town within Erie County operates its own scheme.

Similar localized licensing schemes apply in **Syracuse (Onondaga County), Rochester (Monroe County), Albany, Yonkers, White Plains, and most cities of substantial population**. ContrPro should treat **every New York job as requiring a fresh municipal-license check** at the local building department before bidding.

### 1.7 New York State — Home Improvement Business (statutory overlay, N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law Art. 36-A)

**Statutory authority:** N.Y. General Business Law Article 36-A, §§ 770–777 [6][7].

The state Home Improvement Business law is **not a licensing scheme** — it is a **statutory overlay** that imposes substantive consumer-protection requirements on any **home improvement contract** of more than **$500** for work on a residential building containing not more than **four units**, performed anywhere in New York State [6]. The statute operates regardless of whether the contractor is also municipally licensed under NYC DCWP, Nassau NCDCA, or Suffolk OCA.

**Required contract terms under GBL § 771** [7]:

1. Contract in writing.
2. Contractor's name, address, telephone number, and license number (if applicable).
3. Approximate dates of start and substantial completion.
4. Description of work and materials.
5. Total cost.
6. Notice of consumer's three-day right of rescission for door-to-door sales (GBL § 425).
7. **Progress payment schedule** with payments bearing a "reasonable relationship" to the work performed.
8. Notice of mechanic's lien rights (Lien Law § 73).
9. Notice that contractor must deposit advance funds into an **escrow account** OR post a bond OR provide a certificate of deposit, OR contract for a **letter of credit** as required under GBL § 71-a (see § 2.7).

**Mandatory cancellation rights:** The customer has a **right to cancel within 3 business days** (GBL § 425) for any contract negotiated at the consumer's home or away from the contractor's regular place of business.

**Mandatory escrow / trust fund for HIC deposits:** Under N.Y. Lien Law § 71-a, any payment made by a homeowner to a home-improvement contractor in advance of substantial performance must be deposited in a separate escrow account in a New York banking institution or secured by a bond — and is a **statutory trust fund** under Article 3-A (see § 2.7 below). **This is in addition to** the Article 3-A trust fund obligations on the contractor's own receipts from prime / subcontract relationships.

**Penalties** [6][7]:
- Misdemeanor for contracting without compliance: up to $250 fine for first offense, up to $500 and 30 days imprisonment for subsequent offenses (GBL § 776).
- **Civil consequence:** Substantial non-compliance bars recovery in contract; New York courts have held that an HIC failing to provide the statutorily required contract loses the right to enforce the contract or to file a mechanic's lien against the homeowner [23][24].

### 1.8 General liability insurance — no statewide statutory minimum

New York imposes **no statewide minimum** for general-liability coverage for general contracting (the absence-of-state-license point above). GL minimums arise from (a) NYC DOB rules ($1M/$2M for GCR and master plumbing/electrical), (b) NYC DCWP rules ($300K/$600K for HIC), (c) county-level rules (Nassau $300K/$1M, Suffolk $300K/$1M, Westchester $500K), and (d) individual project-level requirements set by owners, primes, and lenders. **The NYC DOB and DCWP regimes are NOT additive** for an entity that holds both — each agency requires the certificate of insurance to list itself as additional insured.

### 1.9 Structural steel — no New York state license

New York **does not license structural steel erectors or fabricators**. As in Texas, competency is established through:

1. **AISC Certified Erector / AISC Certified Fabricator** — required by NYC DOB for certain Type I and Type II buildings (NYC Building Code § 2202; 1 RCNY § 3304-01) [30].
2. **AWS welder qualification** — D1.1 (structural steel) or D1.5 (bridge welding). New York State Department of Transportation specifications (NYSDOT Section 564) require D1.5 qualifications for state-bridge welding [31].
3. **OSHA 10/30** — required under NYC Local Law 196 (see § 5.3); not state-mandated but ubiquitous.
4. **Municipal permits and special inspections** — structural permits issued by the city or county; controlled / special inspections by registered design professionals.

A structural steel contractor in New York may operate **with no state-issued license at all**, but in NYC must work under a registered general contractor (GCR) and is subject to AISC certification requirements imposed by the NYC Construction Code for buildings of significant size.

### 1.10 Apprenticeship registration (Department of Labor)

Apprentice training programs in construction trades must be registered with the **New York State Department of Labor's Apprenticeship Training Council** under N.Y. Labor Law Article 23 [32]. This is not a contractor license but is relevant for any contractor employing apprentices on public works projects (which require apprenticeship-program participation under N.Y. Labor Law § 220).

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — New York Lien Law

The New York Lien Law is a **consolidated statute** — **not** part of the Real Property Law and **not** part of the General Business Law. It was first enacted in 1897 and has been amended hundreds of times; the current version is published as N.Y. Lien Law §§ 1–250 [33][34]. This is unusual: most U.S. states embed mechanic's lien provisions within their real-property code (Texas Property Code Ch. 53; California Civil Code §§ 8000 et seq.; Tennessee Code Title 66 Ch. 11). New York's standalone Lien Law contains both the lien remedy proper (Article 2) and the construction trust fund remedy (Article 3-A) in a single chapter.

### 2.1 Who can claim a lien

Under N.Y. Lien Law § 3, "**any contractor, subcontractor, laborer, materialman, landscape gardener, nurseryman, or person or corporation selling fruit or ornamental trees, roses, shrubbery, vines and small fruits**" who performs labor or furnishes materials for the improvement of real property with the consent or at the request of the owner or his agent, contractor, or subcontractor, **shall have a lien for the principal and interest of the value or agreed price of such labor, materials, or trees** [35].

The statute is intentionally broad: it covers laborers, suppliers, equipment lessors (Lien Law § 5 extends to certain "machinery, tools, and equipment" suppliers), and design professionals (architects, engineers, surveyors who furnish services "in connection with the improvement" — Lien Law § 2(4) and § 3) [33][35].

**Privity not required:** Unlike Texas's constitutional lien (which is limited to original contractors in privity with the owner), the New York statutory lien is available to **any tier of subcontractor or supplier**, including sub-sub-subcontractors and second-tier suppliers, provided the lien is filed within the statutory window.

### 2.2 The "Notice Under Mechanic's Lien Law" — § 10 filing

New York's lien-perfection regime is **structurally different** from California, Texas, and Florida — there is **no preliminary notice requirement**. A subcontractor or supplier does **not** need to send a 20-day preliminary notice (California), a monthly notice (Texas), or a Notice to Owner (Florida) to preserve lien rights. Instead, the **entire perfection step is the filing of the Notice of Lien (Notice Under Mechanic's Lien Law)** with the county clerk within the statutory deadline [33][34].

**Statutory authority:** N.Y. Lien Law § 10 (filing of notice of lien on private improvement) [36].

**Filing deadlines under § 10** [36][37]:

| Project type | Filing deadline |
|---|---|
| **Single-family dwelling** | **4 months** after the claimant's last item of work or last delivery of material |
| **All other private improvements (commercial, multi-family, mixed-use, industrial)** | **8 months** after the last item of work or last delivery of material |
| **Public improvement** (Lien Law § 12 — see § 2.5 below) | **30 days** after completion and acceptance of the improvement, **OR within 3 months after final filing of the contract or notice of termination** — different statute, different timing |

**The 8-month commercial window is one of the longest mechanic's lien-filing windows in the United States** — longer than California (90 days for non-original contractors), Texas (15th day of 4th month for commercial), Florida (90 days from last work for non-direct claimants), and Tennessee (90 days for non-prime contractors). The long window reflects the original 19th-century policy of giving subcontractors time to negotiate before resorting to lien.

**Last item of work** is a question of fact. Punch-list and warranty/repair work generally do **not** restart the clock — New York courts hold that "last item of work" means substantial performance, not de minimis fixes (Pascual v. Greenleaf Park Land Co., 245 N.Y. 294 (1927); recent applications confirm that repair work after substantial completion is not lienable as new work) [38].

**Service of notice of lien (Lien Law § 11):** Within **5 days before** or **30 days after** filing the Notice of Lien with the county clerk, the claimant must serve a copy on the owner, the general contractor (if the claimant is a sub), and any mortgage holder by certified mail or personal delivery [33][39]. Failure to serve as required is a procedural defect that may be cured but exposes the lien to discharge by motion under Lien Law § 19.

**Contents of the Notice of Lien (Lien Law § 9):** The notice must state, in substance: (1) the claimant's name and residence; (2) the name of the owner and the interest claimed; (3) the name of the party with whom the claimant contracted (if not the owner); (4) the labor performed or materials furnished and the agreed price or value; (5) the amount unpaid; (6) the time when the first and last items of work were performed; (7) the property description; (8) the verification by oath of the claimant [33].

### 2.3 Single-family residential — the 4-month window and § 71-a interplay

Single-family residential dwellings have a **shorter 4-month filing window** under Lien Law § 10 [36]. This shortened window is intentional and parallels the Article 36-A Home Improvement Business law (see § 1.7): residential homeowners have greater consumer protections, but the trade-off is that subs and suppliers must move faster to preserve lien rights.

Additionally, residential lien claimants face two unique gating provisions:

- **Lien Law § 71-a** — Funds received by a home-improvement contractor are statutory trust funds. Diversion of those funds before the subs and suppliers are paid is a criminal violation under Lien Law § 79-a (see § 2.7).
- **Home Improvement contract compliance** — If the contractor failed to provide the statutorily required written contract under GBL § 771, the contractor's **own lien rights against the homeowner are forfeited** [23][24]. The contractor cannot lien a residence on a contract that didn't comply with GBL § 771; subs and suppliers (who didn't contract directly with the homeowner) retain their lien rights, but the prime is locked out.

### 2.4 Discharge of a lien — Lien Law § 19

A property owner may have a Notice of Lien discharged by several methods under **N.Y. Lien Law § 19** [40][41]:

1. **Payment** — The lien is satisfied and the claimant files a satisfaction.
2. **Lapse of time** — The lien is automatically discharged if the claimant fails to commence foreclosure within 1 year (see § 2.6).
3. **Bond / undertaking** (Lien Law § 19(4) and § 21) — The owner deposits cash or a surety bond equal to **110% of the lien amount** with the county clerk. Upon deposit, the lien is discharged of record and attaches instead to the bond / cash.
4. **Court order** — On motion under Lien Law § 19(6) for defects on the face of the notice, expiration, or lien on real property that is not lienable.

**Attorney's fees on improper liens.** Under N.Y. Lien Law § 39-a, if a court finds that a Notice of Lien was filed with **willful exaggeration**, the lien is **void** and the claimant is liable for **damages, including (i) the amount by which the lien was willfully exaggerated, (ii) the reasonable costs of bonding off the lien, AND (iii) reasonable attorney's fees** for the proceeding to discharge the lien [42][43]. Willful exaggeration is a real exposure — a claim of $500,000 on a lien for work worth $300,000 risks a § 39-a finding even if the unpaid portion is meritorious in part.

### 2.5 Public improvement liens — Lien Law § 12

For **public improvements** (work performed on property owned by the state or a public corporation), the lien attaches not to the real property but to the **funds due to the contractor from the public entity**. The procedure is governed by **N.Y. Lien Law §§ 5, 12, and 18** [44].

**Filing requirements under § 12** [44][45]:

- The claimant files a **Notice of Public Improvement Lien** with the head of the department or financial officer of the public entity that contracted for the work.
- **Filing deadline:** Within **30 days after** completion and acceptance of the work, **OR within 3 months after the date specified in the public improvement contract**, whichever is later (Lien Law § 12). Practitioners universally treat this as a **30-day-from-completion deadline** unless the contract specifies a later trigger.
- Service: Copy filed with the public entity; copy served on the contractor of record.

**Duration:** A public improvement lien continues for **1 year** after filing unless extended by court order or foreclosure suit commences (Lien Law § 18) [33][44].

### 2.6 Foreclosure — must commence within 1 year (Lien Law § 17)

The lien must be enforced by foreclosure action within **1 year** from filing (Lien Law § 17) [46]. The deadline may be extended by:

1. **Order of the court** for cause (Lien Law § 17(b) — common for amicable extensions during settlement negotiations).
2. **An "Extension of Notice of Mechanic's Lien" filed with the county clerk before the lien expires**. Each extension is good for **1 year** from filing (Lien Law § 17(b)). On **single-family** projects, a private extension is limited to **one extension** (Lien Law § 17(a)); subsequent extensions require court order. On commercial projects, the practice varies but court approval is the safer route after the first extension.

**Failure to commence within 1 year automatically discharges the lien** by operation of law (Lien Law § 17(c)). The county clerk may stamp the lien "expired" and the property is cleared. This is a hard deadline; missing it is fatal.

**Litigation venue:** Foreclosure is brought in New York Supreme Court (the state trial court of general jurisdiction) in the county where the property is located.

### 2.7 Article 3-A — New York's Construction Trust Fund regime

**The single most important parallel between New York and Texas** for ContrPro contractors is that **New York treats all construction payments as statutory trust funds**. Article 3-A of the Lien Law (§§ 70–79-a) is the New York construction trust fund regime [47][48][49]. **Like Texas Property Code Chapter 162, Article 3-A imposes personal liability — civil and criminal — on individual contractors and officers for misapplication of trust funds.**

**§ 70 — Trust assets:** The funds received by a contractor, subcontractor, or owner from a construction contract or construction loan are **trust funds**, held for the benefit of:

(a) the subcontractors, suppliers, architects, engineers, surveyors, laborers, and other persons who have furnished labor or materials for the project;
(b) taxes payable to the state or any subdivision;
(c) workers' compensation and disability insurance premiums; and
(d) other beneficiaries enumerated by the statute [47][50].

**§ 71 — Definition of trust assets:** Trust assets include funds received under the construction contract, claims for the construction contract amount, claims arising out of the contract (including insurance proceeds and damage claims), and amounts owed to the contractor for the work [47].

**§ 71-a — Home Improvement contracts; advance payments:** Payments received from a homeowner on a home improvement contract are trust funds; the home-improvement contractor must hold those payments in an **escrow account** at a New York banking institution or post a bond / letter of credit / certificate of deposit equal to the advance [50][51].

**§ 72–76 — Records and accounting:** The trustee must maintain books of account showing the source and disposition of trust funds. A failure to maintain adequate books and records is **presumptive evidence of misapplication** under § 75(3) [47][52].

**§ 79-a — Criminal penalty:** Any trustee of construction trust funds who **applies or consents to the application of such trust funds for any purpose other than the trust purposes** — before all trust beneficiaries have been paid — **is guilty of larceny** under § 79-a(1) [49][53]. The penalty tracks the general larceny grading in the Penal Law:

| Amount misapplied | Grade | Maximum penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Up to $1,000 | **Petit larceny** (Class A misdemeanor under Penal Law § 155.25) | Up to **1 year** in county jail |
| $1,000 to $3,000 | **Grand larceny in the fourth degree** (Class E felony under Penal Law § 155.30) | Up to **4 years** state prison |
| $3,000 to $50,000 | **Grand larceny in the third degree** (Class D felony under Penal Law § 155.35) | Up to **7 years** state prison |
| $50,000 to $1,000,000 | **Grand larceny in the second degree** (Class C felony under Penal Law § 155.40) | Up to **15 years** state prison |
| Over $1,000,000 | **Grand larceny in the first degree** (Class B felony under Penal Law § 155.42) | Up to **25 years** state prison |

**Civil liability — personal:** In addition to criminal exposure, beneficiaries may bring a civil action for diverted trust funds under Lien Law § 77 (representative trust action) against the contractor and **personally against the officers, directors, or controlling agents who knowingly participated in the diversion** [47][48][54]. New York courts routinely pierce the corporate veil in Article 3-A claims against officers who controlled disbursement (RLI Insurance Co. v. New York State Department of Labor, 97 N.Y.2d 256 (2002) [55]; West-Fair Electric Contractors v. Aetna Casualty & Surety Co., 87 N.Y.2d 148 (1995) [56]).

**Statutory representation of a single beneficiary as representative.** A single beneficiary (a sub or supplier) may bring an Article 3-A action on behalf of all beneficiaries of the same trust under Lien Law § 77. Discovery in such an action reaches the contractor's bank records, payments to other subs, draws on the construction loan, and personal disbursements. The discovery alone is often more damaging to the contractor than the underlying judgment.

**Affirmative defense:** A trustee may apply trust funds to **trust purposes** — defined to include payment of subs, suppliers, taxes, workers' comp, disability premiums, and the trustee's own reasonable overhead **directly attributable to the project** (Lien Law § 71(2)). General overhead, executive compensation unrelated to the project, and dividends are **not** trust purposes and **constitute misapplication** even if all subs are eventually paid late.

### 2.8 The Lien Law / Trust Fund Act overlap with HIC trust funds

For **residential home-improvement** work, three trust regimes potentially apply to the same dollars:

1. **N.Y. Lien Law § 71-a** — Advance payments from a homeowner must be held in escrow / bonded.
2. **N.Y. Lien Law § 70 et seq. (Article 3-A)** — All construction payments are trust funds held for downstream beneficiaries.
3. **N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law § 770(7)** — HIC trust fund obligation (largely overlapping with Lien Law § 71-a but separately enforceable by DCWP / DCA).

ContrPro contractors performing residential remodeling work in NYC, Nassau, Suffolk, or Westchester must structure receipts and disbursements to comply with **all three** — typically by maintaining a separate construction account for each major job and disbursing payments to subs and suppliers before drawing any overhead.

### 2.9 Lien-priority — purchase-money mortgage

Under N.Y. Lien Law § 13(1), mechanic's liens are subordinate to:

(a) **Existing mortgages of record** at the time the lien is filed.
(b) **Purchase-money mortgages** taken concurrent with the conveyance (regardless of recording).
(c) **Building loan mortgages** — but only to the extent of advances made for the **improvement** of the property, **and only if the building loan contract is filed with the county clerk** under Lien Law § 22 within **10 days of execution** [33][57].

**The "building loan contract filing" trap.** A building loan mortgage that is **not** filed within 10 days of execution under § 22 loses its priority over subsequently filed mechanic's liens, even if the loan is otherwise valid and recorded. Mechanic's lien claimants routinely check the county clerk for the § 22 filing and contest priority based on missed or defective filings.

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

New York's prompt-payment regime is split across **two main statutory schemes** — one for private construction (General Business Law Article 35-E) and one for state public works (State Finance Law § 139-f) — plus narrower regimes for state agencies (State Finance Law Article 11-A) and political subdivisions (General Municipal Law § 106-b).

### 3.1 Private construction — N.Y. General Business Law Article 35-E (the Prompt Payment Act)

**Statutory authority:** New York General Business Law Article 35-E, §§ 756–757-c (the New York State Prompt Payment Act for Private Construction) [58][59].

The New York Prompt Payment Act was enacted in **2002** (L.2002, ch. 127) and applies to **private commercial construction contracts of more than $150,000** [58][60]. Critically:

- **It does NOT apply to residential construction** of 1- to 2-family dwellings (GBL § 756-a(2)).
- **It does NOT apply to public works** (those are governed by SFL § 139-f).
- **It does NOT apply to contracts ≤ $150,000** (the threshold is a hard exclusion, not a sliding scale).
- For ContrPro: this means most NYC commercial tenant-build-outs, hospital and institutional construction, and large multifamily projects are covered; small commercial and most residential are not.

**Owner-to-prime payment** under GBL § 756-a(3) [58][59]:

- The contractor submits a "billing or invoice" that itemizes work performed, conforms to the contract, and includes any documentation required by the contract.
- The owner has **12 business days** to approve or disapprove the invoice. If the owner fails to disapprove within 12 business days, the invoice is **deemed approved**.
- The owner must pay the approved invoice within **30 calendar days** after the **deemed approval** (i.e., approximately 42 days after submission of a conforming invoice).

**Prime-to-subcontractor and sub-to-sub-sub payment** under GBL § 756-a(3)(b) [58][59]:

- The prime contractor must pay the sub the proportionate share of the owner's payment **within 7 days** of receipt from the owner.
- The 7-day requirement **cascades down the chain** — each sub must pay its lower-tier subs / suppliers within 7 days of its own receipt.

**Interest on overdue amounts** under GBL § 756-b(1)(a) [58][59]: Interest accrues at the **statutory contract rate set forth in CPLR § 5004** — currently **9% per annum** under CPLR § 5004(a). The rate is set by statute and is the default unless the contract provides otherwise; the parties may set a different contract rate by written agreement.

**Right to suspend work** under GBL § 756-b(2) [58][59]: A contractor or sub that has not received payment within the statutory deadline may give **10 days' written notice of intent to suspend**. After the 10-day notice period, the contractor may **suspend performance without breach of contract** and may recover reasonable costs of suspension and remobilization once payment is made.

**Attorney's fees and dispute resolution** under GBL § 756-c [58][59]: The Prompt Payment Act provides for an **expedited binding arbitration** procedure (American Arbitration Association's Construction Industry Rules apply) and authorizes recovery of reasonable attorney's fees by the prevailing party. The arbitration is initiated by demand for an "expedited hearing" — most NY commercial general conditions specifically reference the AAA Construction Rules.

**Pay-if-paid clauses — VOID in New York.** This is a critical New York-specific rule. Under West-Fair Electric Contractors v. Aetna Casualty & Surety Co., 87 N.Y.2d 148 (1995) [56], the New York Court of Appeals held that pay-if-paid clauses **violate the public policy embodied in the Lien Law** and are **unenforceable**. The Court reasoned that pay-if-paid clauses effectively transfer the credit risk of owner insolvency from the prime to the sub, undermining the lien remedy that the Lien Law guarantees. As a result:

- **A pay-if-paid clause in any New York construction subcontract is void**, regardless of whether the project is public or private, residential or commercial.
- **Pay-when-paid clauses** (clauses that merely set a timing reference but still require eventual payment) **remain enforceable** as timing-only provisions — but only if the timing reference is reasonable; an indefinite "pay when paid" deferred indefinitely will be construed as a pay-if-paid attempt and voided.

The West-Fair rule has not been modified by statute and was reaffirmed by the Court of Appeals in subsequent decisions; out-of-state primes routinely insert pay-if-paid clauses into NY subcontracts that are then struck down by NY courts.

### 3.2 Public works — N.Y. State Finance Law § 139-f and General Municipal Law § 106-b

**State construction contracts** are governed by **State Finance Law § 139-f** (Prompt Payment of State Construction Contracts) [61][62]. Highlights:

- **Owner-to-prime:** The state agency must pay invoices for state construction contracts within **30 calendar days** after receipt of a properly submitted invoice; the deadline accrues from the date the agency receives the invoice or accepts the work, whichever is later (SFL § 139-f(2)).
- **Interest on overdue state payments:** Statutory interest rate set annually by the State Comptroller — published in the Official Compilation of Codes, Rules and Regulations and on the OSC website; for the 2024–2026 period, the rate has been in the range of **6.5%–7.5% per annum** [63].
- **Retainage:** Capped at **5% of progress payments** under SFL § 139-f(4)(b) for state construction contracts. Retainage must be released within **30 days** of substantial completion.
- **Prompt-pay-down obligation:** A prime that receives payment from the state must pay subs within **7 calendar days** under SFL § 139-f(7).

**Political subdivisions (counties, cities, towns, villages, school districts, fire districts, public benefit corporations)** are governed by **General Municipal Law § 106-b** [64][65]:

- **Owner-to-prime:** Payment within **30 days** of receipt of a proper invoice.
- **Prime-to-sub:** Cascade within **7 days** of receipt.
- **Retainage:** Capped at **5%** for political subdivision contracts under GML § 106-b(3); released within **30 days** of substantial completion.
- **Interest:** Same statutory rate as SFL § 139-f.

**Public Housing Authority projects** are governed separately under Public Housing Law § 151-a.

**Department of Education / School Construction:** Under N.Y. Education Law § 3813 and the New York City School Construction Authority Act (Public Authorities Law Article 8, Title 6), payments by school districts and the NYC School Construction Authority track GML § 106-b but with separate certified-payroll and prevailing-wage layers (Labor Law § 220).

### 3.3 Retainage cap on private commercial construction — General Business Law § 756-c

**A significant 2023 change.** Under N.Y. General Business Law § 756-c (amended by **L.2023, ch. 480**, signed by Governor Hochul on November 17, 2023, effective immediately) [66][67][68], retainage on private commercial construction contracts subject to the Prompt Payment Act (GBL Art. 35-E) is **capped at 5% of any progress payment** unless the contract specifies a different amount.

Pre-amendment, New York private contracts could include retainage of 10% or higher with no statutory ceiling; the amendment brought New York in line with California (SB 61's 5% private cap) and the federal Miller Act framework. The amendment also requires that **retainage be released within 30 days after final completion**, eliminating the ambiguity that had previously allowed indefinite withholding of retention.

For ContrPro contractors: a private commercial subcontract executed **on or after November 17, 2023** is subject to the 5% retainage cap. Subcontracts executed before that date retain their original contractual retainage. The amendment does **not** apply to:

- Residential 1-2 family construction (outside Article 35-E scope entirely).
- Contracts under $150,000 (outside Article 35-E scope).
- Public works (which have their own 5% cap under SFL § 139-f and GML § 106-b).

### 3.4 Good-faith dispute withholding

Both GBL Article 35-E (private) and SFL § 139-f / GML § 106-b (public) allow withholding amounts subject to a **good-faith dispute** [58][61][64]:

- The disputing party must notify the contractor of the dispute **within 12 business days** of receipt of the invoice (private) or **15 days** (public).
- The notice must state the specific amount in dispute and the specific reason for the dispute.
- The undisputed portion must still be paid by the statutory deadline.
- Pretextual withholding triggers Prompt Payment Act interest and attorney's fees.

### 3.5 Subcontractor's right to demand information about owner payment

Under GBL § 756-a(3)(a)(ii), a subcontractor may serve a written request for information about the status of payment from the owner. The contractor must provide the information within **10 days**. Failure to provide the information triggers additional Prompt Payment Act sanctions and is a frequently cited basis for breach in NY construction litigation.

## 4. Tax structure for contractors

### 4.1 State and local sales tax rates

- **State sales tax rate:** **4%** under **N.Y. Tax Law § 1105** [69][70].
- **Local sales tax:** Counties and certain cities may impose local sales tax up to **4.875%** additional under the **Tax Law Article 29** [69][71].
- **Metropolitan Commuter Transportation District (MCTD) surcharge:** **0.375%** additional for transactions in the MCTD (NYC and surrounding suburban counties) under N.Y. Tax Law § 1109 [69][70].
- **Combined rates in major jurisdictions** (as of April 2026) [69][72]:
  - **New York City (all 5 boroughs):** **8.875%** (4% state + 4.5% NYC + 0.375% MCTD)
  - **Nassau County:** **8.625%**
  - **Suffolk County:** **8.625%**
  - **Westchester County (unincorporated):** **8.375%**
  - **Yonkers / Mount Vernon / White Plains:** **8.875%**
  - **Buffalo / Erie County:** **8.75%**
  - **Rochester / Monroe County:** **8.0%**

**Administered by:** **New York State Department of Taxation and Finance (DTF)** — Albany, NY; tax.ny.gov [70].

### 4.2 Contractor sales-tax treatment — the capital improvement / repair distinction

New York's contractor sales-tax regime is governed by **N.Y. Tax Law § 1105(c)(3) and § 1101(b)(9)**, supplemented by the Department of Taxation and Finance's **Tax Bulletin TB-ST-104 (Capital Improvements)** and **Tax Bulletin TB-ST-129 (Contractors — Sales Tax Credits)** [73][74][75]. The distinction between **capital improvement** and **repair / maintenance / installation** is the single most important sales-tax decision in New York construction — it controls whether tax is charged to the customer or absorbed by the contractor.

**Threshold concept — capital improvement.** Under Tax Law § 1101(b)(9) and 20 NYCRR § 527.7, a **capital improvement** is an addition or alteration to real property that:

(a) **substantially adds to the value** of the real property or **appreciably prolongs the useful life** of the property;
(b) **becomes part of the real property** or is **permanently affixed** so that removal would cause material damage; and
(c) is **intended to become a permanent installation**.

All three elements must be satisfied.

**Examples of capital improvements** (per Tax Bulletin TB-ST-104) [74]:

- New construction of buildings or additions
- Installation of central air conditioning (whole-building system)
- Installation of permanent kitchen cabinets and built-in appliances
- New roofing on an entire building (not patch repair)
- New driveways and walkways (concrete or asphalt)
- Installation of in-ground swimming pools

**Examples of repairs and installations that are NOT capital improvements** (per TB-ST-104) [74]:

- Repair or replacement of damaged items (re-roofing only the damaged section, replacing a broken window)
- Routine maintenance (HVAC servicing, snow removal, lawn care)
- Carpet installation (not generally considered a permanent installation)
- Window air conditioner installation (portable)
- Painting (almost always treated as repair / maintenance)

**Tax treatment matrix:**

| Work type | Labor taxable? | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Capital improvement** | **Not taxable** to the customer | Contractor pays sales tax at purchase (treated as consumer) |
| **Repair / maintenance / installation** | **Taxable** to the customer | Contractor purchases tax-free with resale certificate and collects tax from customer on materials + labor |

**The capital-improvement-vs-repair distinction drives everything:**

- A contractor performing a **capital improvement** is the **consumer of materials** — pays sales tax on materials at the time of purchase from the supplier — and does **not collect sales tax from the customer** on the contract amount. The materials cost (now including sales tax) is built into the bid.
- A contractor performing a **repair or installation** is the **retailer** — buys materials tax-free under a resale certificate (Form ST-120) — and **collects sales tax from the customer** on the **entire invoice** (labor + materials).

**Form ST-124 (Certificate of Capital Improvement)** [76]: The customer (not the contractor) issues Form ST-124 to the contractor, certifying that the work is a capital improvement. With a valid ST-124 in hand, the contractor:

(a) Does not collect sales tax from the customer.
(b) Pays sales tax to suppliers when purchasing the materials.
(c) Cannot retroactively recover sales tax already paid to suppliers (no resale exemption applies).

**The ST-124 is the contractor's audit shield.** Without a customer-signed ST-124 for capital improvement work, a DTF auditor can re-characterize the contract as repair work and assess sales tax on the **entire contract amount** plus interest and penalties. Contractors should obtain a signed ST-124 from the customer before commencing capital improvement work and retain it for **at least 3 years** (the DTF audit lookback period is generally 3 years).

**Reclassification trap — repair within a capital improvement.** A single project frequently includes both capital improvement and repair components. The DTF position (TB-ST-104) is that the **predominant character** of the project controls — but if the repair components are separately identifiable and separately stated, they may be taxed separately. Contractors should structure such mixed contracts carefully and consider issuing separate invoices for capital-improvement and repair portions.

### 4.3 Use tax on out-of-state materials

Materials purchased out-of-state and brought into New York for use in a New York project are subject to **New York use tax** at the combined state-plus-local rate under **N.Y. Tax Law § 1110** [69]. A contractor that purchases steel in Pennsylvania and erects it on a Manhattan project owes use tax to NY on the materials cost. This is a routine DTF audit target for multi-state contractors, particularly steel fabricators and curtain-wall installers using out-of-state mills.

### 4.4 New York State corporate franchise tax — Tax Law Article 9-A

Construction businesses organized as corporations are subject to the **New York State Corporate Franchise Tax** under **N.Y. Tax Law Article 9-A** [77][78]. The franchise tax base is the highest of:

(a) **Business income** (net income) — **6.5% rate** (standard rate); **7.25% rate** for taxpayers with business income over $5 million (under the 2021–2026 temporary surcharge);
(b) **Business capital** — 0.025% rate (limited to maximum $5M);
(c) **Fixed dollar minimum** — sliding scale from $25 (gross receipts < $100,000) to $200,000 (gross receipts > $1 billion) [77];
(d) **MTA surcharge** for businesses operating in the MCTD — additional 30% surcharge on the franchise tax under Tax Law § 209-B.

**Sole proprietorships, partnerships, and most LLCs** are not subject to Article 9-A; instead, individual partners and members report income on the **New York personal income tax** (Tax Law Article 22). New York personal income tax rates for 2026 range from 4% on the first $8,500 to **10.9%** on income over $25 million (the highest bracket was retained by the 2021 amendments through 2027) [79]. **New York City residents pay an additional NYC personal income tax** of 3.078% to 3.876% under NYC Admin. Code § 11-1701.

### 4.5 NYC Unincorporated Business Tax (UBT)

Sole proprietors and partnerships doing business in New York City are subject to the **NYC Unincorporated Business Tax (UBT)** under NYC Admin. Code § 11-503 — **4% of net business income** [80]. Construction sole proprietors operating in NYC face the UBT in addition to NY State personal income tax and the NYC personal income tax (if a NYC resident) — effectively a triple-stack of local income taxation that is unusual nationally.

### 4.6 Sales and Use Tax Permit (Certificate of Authority)

Any contractor making taxable sales — most importantly, **any contractor performing repair / installation / maintenance work where labor + materials are taxable to the customer** — must obtain a **Certificate of Authority** from the DTF under N.Y. Tax Law § 1134 [69][70]. Application is free. Failure to obtain a Certificate of Authority is a Class A misdemeanor under Tax Law § 1817 — up to 1 year imprisonment and $10,000 fine.

### 4.7 New York City Commercial Rent Tax (CRT) — context

The NYC **Commercial Rent Tax** (CRT) is imposed on tenants of commercial space in Manhattan south of 96th Street under NYC Admin. Code § 11-701 et seq. — **6% of base rent** (effective rate 3.9% after small business credits) [81]. The CRT is paid by the commercial tenant, not the contractor, but is a frequent factor in tenant-finish negotiations because a tenant build-out increases base rent under most leases. ContrPro contractors do not typically owe CRT, but understanding it can be a competitive advantage when structuring tenant-improvement allowances.

## 5. Workers' compensation

### 5.1 Universal coverage requirement — N.Y. Workers' Compensation Law § 10

Unlike Texas, **New York requires every employer to carry workers' compensation insurance**. There is **no opt-out** and no non-subscriber option. The statutory authority is **N.Y. Workers' Compensation Law § 10**, which mandates coverage for "every employer" of one or more employees [9][82]. The scope is administered by the **New York Workers' Compensation Board (WCB)**, an independent state agency [83].

**Construction-specific universality** [9][83]:

- **All construction employers** — including those with only one employee — must carry workers' comp coverage.
- **No statutory exemption** based on size, payroll, or industry for construction work.
- **Subcontractors are deemed employees of the prime contractor** if the sub does not carry its own workers' comp — under WCL § 56, the prime contractor becomes responsible for the sub's WC coverage if the sub fails to carry it. This is a powerful incentive for primes to verify sub coverage at contract execution.

### 5.2 Working-owner exemption — sole proprietors and corporate officers (WCL § 54)

**Sole proprietors with no employees** are not statutorily required to cover themselves but may **voluntarily elect** coverage by filing Form U-26.3 with the WCB. Without an election, the sole proprietor is **not** an employee of the business and is not covered by WC for personal injuries.

**Corporate officers**: Under N.Y. WCL § 54(6), corporate officers of a one- or two-officer corporation may **elect to exclude themselves** from WC coverage by filing **Form CE-200 (Certificate of Attestation of Exemption)** or, where applicable, **Form C-105.32** [82][84]. The exclusion election does not cover any other employees of the corporation — those remain mandatorily covered.

**LLC members:** LLC members are treated as sole proprietors for WC purposes; coverage is voluntary for the member but mandatory for any employee.

**The Form CE-200 trap.** A construction-business owner who routinely uses CE-200 to certify exemption when signing a public contract or pulling a permit must remember that the exemption applies only to **the owner personally**, not to employees, subs, or independent contractors. A prime contractor that hires a sub claiming CE-200 exemption must verify that the sub has no employees on the project — otherwise the prime inherits coverage liability under WCL § 56.

### 5.3 Stop work orders for non-compliance — WCL § 141

Under N.Y. WCL § 141-a (added by L.2014, ch. 56), the WCB may issue a **Stop Work Order** for any worksite where an employer is operating without required WC coverage. The order is effective **immediately upon posting** and prohibits all work at the site until compliance is verified. Violation of a Stop Work Order is a misdemeanor (WCL § 141-a(5)). Civil penalties for operating without coverage are severe:

- **Up to $2,000 per 10-day period** of non-coverage (WCL § 52) — i.e., $73,000 per year for one continuous period of non-coverage.
- Additional **$1,000 penalty for the failure to post the notice of coverage** in the workplace.

### 5.4 NYC-specific overlay — Local Law 196 of 2017 (site safety training)

NYC Local Law 196 of 2017 (effective phased through 2020) requires that every **construction worker, supervisor, and superintendent** at a NYC job site requiring a construction superintendent, site safety coordinator, or site safety manager complete the following training:

- **40 hours of site safety training for supervisors / superintendents** (SST-40 card);
- **30 hours of site safety training for workers** (SST-30 card or OSHA 30 plus 10 additional hours).

This is enforced by NYC DOB; non-compliance is a separate violation under NYC Admin. Code § 28-401.19 with civil penalty up to $25,000 per violation and a stop work order. Local Law 196 is conceptually distinct from workers' comp but practically merges with WC in NYC site-safety enforcement.

### 5.5 Disability Benefits Law (DBL) and Paid Family Leave (PFL)

Separate from workers' comp, New York employers must provide **Disability Benefits** under N.Y. Workers' Compensation Law Article 9 (the Disability Benefits Law) — covering off-the-job temporary disabilities — and **Paid Family Leave** under WCL Article 9 (PFL) [85]. Both are funded by employee payroll deductions (PFL: 0.388% of wages capped at the state average weekly wage cap in 2026); DBL: minimal employee contribution. Filings for both are accepted on Form **DB-120.1**; DOB GCR registration requires proof of DBL coverage.

### 5.6 Federal Longshore and Harbor Workers' Comp / Jones Act

For waterfront and harbor work (e.g., bridge construction over navigable waters, dock construction, marine work on NY-NJ Harbor), **federal Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act** (33 U.S.C. § 901 et seq.) and the **Jones Act** (46 U.S.C. § 30104) impose separate federal coverage requirements that operate alongside (and sometimes preempt) state WC. ContrPro contractors performing waterfront work in New York Harbor need both state and federal coverage.

## 6. Recent changes (2024–2026)

The New York State Legislature meets in regular session each calendar year (no biennial-session structure as in Texas). The 2024 session ran January 3 – June 7, 2024; the 2025 session ran January 8 – June 17, 2025; the 2026 session opened January 7, 2026. The principal construction-relevant bills enacted during this period:

### 6.1 Senate Bill S3539 (L.2023, ch. 480) — 5% retainage cap on private commercial construction, effective November 17, 2023

**S3539 / A3786 (Stats. 2023, signed November 17, 2023)** amended N.Y. General Business Law § 756-c to **cap retainage on private commercial construction contracts at 5%** [66][67][68]. The legislation also requires that retainage be released within 30 days of final completion. The amendment applies to private commercial construction contracts subject to the Prompt Payment Act (GBL Art. 35-E, contracts > $150,000).

Practical effect on ContrPro: Subcontracts executed on or after **November 17, 2023** are subject to the 5% cap. Pre-amendment contracts (10% or higher retainage) retain their original retention. The 5% cap brings New York in line with California (SB 61) and federal Miller Act standards.

### 6.2 Senate Bill S2766-C (L.2024) — Lien Law digitization, county clerk e-filing improvements

In 2024, several technical amendments to the Lien Law authorized electronic filing of mechanic's liens through the **New York State Court Electronic Filing System (NYSCEF)** in counties that have adopted e-filing for Supreme Court actions. The amendment did not change the substantive deadlines under Lien Law § 10 or § 17 but eliminated wet-signature requirements for the Notice of Lien filing in participating counties.

### 6.3 NYC Local Law 152 of 2016 — gas-piping inspection cycle (compliance ongoing 2024-2026)

**NYC Local Law 152 of 2016** requires that all gas piping in NYC buildings (other than 1-2 family detached homes) be **inspected by a licensed master plumber every 4 years**. The inspection cycle is keyed to community districts, with the four cycles running through 2024-2027. ContrPro contractors involved in any gas-piping work or building maintenance in NYC need to verify their Master Plumber's inspection scheduling for client buildings.

### 6.4 NYC Local Law 196 of 2017 — site safety training (full enforcement effective March 1, 2020; expanded penalties through 2024-2026)

Discussed in § 5.4 above. NYC DOB has continued aggressive enforcement of SST training requirements through 2024-2026, with periodic clarifications via DOB Buildings Bulletins. As of 2026, all workers and supervisors on any site requiring a Construction Superintendent, Site Safety Coordinator, or Site Safety Manager must hold an SST card.

### 6.5 NYC Local Law 97 of 2019 — building emissions caps (2024 first compliance year, 2030 next milestone)

**NYC Local Law 97 of 2019** imposes mandatory greenhouse-gas emissions limits on buildings over 25,000 sq ft, with first emissions limits effective for calendar year 2024 (first reporting due May 1, 2025). This is enforced by the NYC Department of Buildings Office of Building Energy and Emissions Performance. ContrPro contractors performing energy retrofits, MEP replacements, or façade work in NYC are increasingly being asked to design and execute LL97-compliant retrofits; the standard is not a contractor-licensing matter but is the single most consequential building-code change in NYC since the original Construction Codes overhaul of 2008.

### 6.6 New York Department of Labor Industrial Code Rule 56 (asbestos) — periodic updates

NYS DOL Industrial Code Rule 56 (12 NYCRR 56) governs asbestos handling, abatement, and remediation. Routine amendments through 2024-2026 have tightened notification requirements and worker training documentation; this affects every NYC demolition or major alteration that requires an ACP-5 form (see § 1.2.9).

### 6.7 Senate Bill S8413 (L.2024) — Subcontractor Right to Mediation (pending implementation)

Pending administrative-rule implementation as of 2026, **S8413 (L.2024, ch. 312)** authorized subcontractors on private commercial projects > $150,000 to demand mediation under AAA Construction Industry Rules upon notice of non-payment, with mediation costs split. This supplements (does not replace) the GBL Art. 35-E expedited arbitration procedure.

### 6.8 New York City Council changes affecting construction (2024-2026)

NYC's construction-codes update cycle is ongoing; the 2022 Construction Codes (administrative, building, plumbing, mechanical, fuel gas, energy conservation) are the current operative code as of 2026, with periodic Buildings Bulletin updates issued by DOB. Specific 2025 Buildings Bulletins of note:

- **DOB Buildings Bulletin 2024-014** — Updated procedures for ACP-5 / ACP-7 asbestos filing.
- **DOB Buildings Bulletin 2025-003** — Updated electronic submission requirements for Schedule A (zoning use) on certain alteration applications.

ContrPro contractors should subscribe to the DOB Buildings Bulletins RSS feed for routine code-cycle updates.

### 6.9 Discrepancy resolutions and persistent secondary-source errors

Several frequently cited errors in secondary commentary on New York construction law:

- **Pay-if-paid clauses.** Many out-of-state primes' template subcontracts continue to include pay-if-paid language. **West-Fair Electric (1995) made these clauses void in New York** [56]; the rule has not been modified by statute or subsequent Court of Appeals decision. New York subs and suppliers should never accept a pay-if-paid clause as legally enforceable.
- **Single notice deadlines.** Some third-party guides report a **uniform 8-month filing window** for all mechanic's liens. **The 4-month window applies to single-family residential** under Lien Law § 10 [36]; the 8-month window applies only to commercial and multi-family residential.
- **Foreclosure deadline extension.** Some secondary sources report that **multiple lien extensions** are available on residential projects. **Lien Law § 17(a) limits single-family residential to ONE extension by filing**; further extensions require court order. Commercial projects permit successive extensions by filing but the prudent practice is court order after the first.
- **Trust fund affirmative defense.** Some practitioners assume that **general overhead** may be deducted from trust funds as "trust purposes." Lien Law § 71(2) and the case law construe trust purposes narrowly — overhead is generally not allowable unless directly attributable to the project [47][55][56].
- **Workers' comp exemption scope.** Some construction-business owners assume that filing CE-200 covers their entire company. **CE-200 covers only the corporate officer personally** — all other employees remain mandatorily covered.
- **5% retainage cap.** A common error in 2024-2025 commentary is the assumption that the 5% cap applies to all New York private construction. **It applies only to commercial construction subject to GBL Art. 35-E** (contracts > $150,000) [66][67]. Residential 1-2 family construction is not subject to the cap. Subcontracts executed before November 17, 2023 also retain pre-amendment retainage.

### 6.10 Bills proposed but not enacted (verification cross-reference)

A review of the New York State Legislature bill tracker via nysenate.gov and nyassembly.gov identifies several construction-relevant bills that were filed but did not pass in the 2024-2025 sessions and should not be relied upon:

- Proposed legislation to establish a **statewide general contractor licensing scheme** under the Department of State was introduced (S5258 / A5800 in the 2023-2024 session) but did not advance. There remains **no New York state license for general contracting**.
- Proposed legislation to **reverse the West-Fair Electric pay-if-paid rule** by statute did not advance. Pay-if-paid clauses remain void.
- Proposed legislation to **expand the Prompt Payment Act to residential construction** did not advance. Residential remains outside Article 35-E.

## 7. Practical compliance checklist for a New York contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**

- Identify **every jurisdiction** in which work will be performed:
  - **NYC** — DOB General Contractor Registration ($300, 3-year, $1M/$2M GL with NYC additional insured); Construction Superintendent registration if applicable; DCWP Home Improvement Contractor license for any residential remodeling (separate $150 license, 2-year, $300K/$600K GL).
  - **Nassau County** — NCDCA Home Improvement Contractor license ($200 application, 2-year, $300K/$1M GL, $25K bond).
  - **Suffolk County** — OCA Home Improvement Contractor license ($200, 2-year, $300K/$1M GL, $20K bond).
  - **Westchester County** — Department of Consumer Protection Home Improvement Contractor license ($140, 3-year, $500K GL, $5K bond).
  - **Buffalo / other upstate cities** — verify city-specific contractor license.
- Identify which **trade-specific licenses** are required:
  - HVAC in NYC — verify Master Plumber availability for gas connections, Master Electrician for electrical connections; no NYC "HVAC contractor" license; consider whether RMO is required for >10-ton refrigeration.
  - Electrical in NYC — Master Electrician of record (DOB) + DCAS examination + $10K bond + $1M/$2M GL + 7½ years experience + 3-year license cycle.
  - Plumbing in NYC — Master Plumber (DOB) + 7 years experience + $25K bond + $1M/$2M GL + 3-year license cycle.
  - Fire sprinkler in NYC — Master Fire Suppression Piping Contractor license (separate from Master Plumber).
  - Structural steel — no state license; verify AISC certification and AWS welder qualifications per contract; site-specific DOB special inspections.
- Confirm **workers' compensation** is in force for every employee on the project — no opt-out, no non-subscriber option, prime liable for sub's WC under WCL § 56 if sub fails to carry. CE-200 covers only the corporate officer personally.
- Confirm **Disability Benefits and Paid Family Leave** coverage — DB-120.1 filing.
- For **NYC** projects: verify SST training compliance (Local Law 196) — all workers and supervisors need current SST cards.
- For **residential work**: ensure HIC contract under GBL § 771 is properly drafted (start/end dates, total cost, escrow / bond acknowledgment, progress payment schedule, three-day cancellation notice). Failure forfeits both contract enforcement AND mechanic's lien rights against the homeowner.

**During the work — payment cycle:**

- **Private commercial (GBL Art. 35-E):** Owner pays prime within **12 business days** approval / 30 days payment; prime pays subs within **7 days**. Interest on overdue at **9% APR** (CPLR § 5004). Right to suspend after **10 days' written notice**. Subs may demand owner-payment status; failure = breach.
- **Public works (SFL § 139-f / GML § 106-b):** State and political subdivision pay prime within **30 days** of correct invoice; prime pays subs within **7 days**. Interest at OSC statutory rate. **5% retainage cap**, released within 30 days of substantial completion.
- **5% retainage cap on private commercial (GBL § 756-c, effective Nov. 17, 2023):** Subcontracts executed on or after that date are capped at 5%; older subcontracts retain original retainage.
- **Pay-if-paid clauses are VOID** under West-Fair Electric (1995). Reject all pay-if-paid clauses in subs; pay-when-paid timing clauses survive but only if not used as indefinite deferral.

**Trust fund obligations (N.Y. Lien Law Article 3-A, §§ 70–79-a):**

- Every payment received under a construction contract is a **statutory trust fund** held for the benefit of subs, suppliers, taxes, WC premiums, and design professionals.
- **Maintain books and records** showing source and disposition of each project's trust funds (Lien Law §§ 75-76). Inadequate records is **presumptive evidence of misapplication** under § 75(3).
- **Personal criminal liability** for the officer/director/agent who controls disbursement — Lien Law § 79-a treats trust-fund diversion as **larceny**:
  - Up to $1,000: petit larceny (Class A misdemeanor, up to 1 year).
  - $1,000–$3,000: grand larceny 4th (Class E felony, up to 4 years prison).
  - $3,000–$50,000: grand larceny 3rd (Class D felony, up to 7 years).
  - $50,000–$1,000,000: grand larceny 2nd (Class C felony, up to 15 years).
  - Over $1,000,000: grand larceny 1st (Class B felony, up to 25 years).
- **No corporate-veil protection** — LLC members and corporate officers personally exposed.
- **HIC advance deposits** — under Lien Law § 71-a, residential homeowner deposits must be held in escrow at a NY bank, OR bonded, OR secured by certificate of deposit OR letter of credit.
- **Affirmative defense** narrow — only direct project costs qualify; general overhead and executive comp not allowable.

**Filing the Notice of Lien (N.Y. Lien Law § 10):**

- **Single-family residential:** File Notice of Lien within **4 months** after last work / last delivery.
- **Commercial / multi-family / industrial:** File within **8 months** after last work / last delivery.
- **Public improvement:** File within **30 days** after completion and acceptance (Lien Law § 12).
- Service of filed Notice on owner / GC / mortgagee within 5 days before or 30 days after filing.
- Avoid willful exaggeration — Lien Law § 39-a voids the lien and imposes attorney's fees if claim is willfully overstated.
- Verify building loan contract is filed within 10 days of execution under Lien Law § 22 — missed filing collapses lender's priority over your lien.

**Foreclosure and extension (Lien Law § 17):**

- Commence foreclosure within **1 year** of filing.
- Extension by recorded filing (commercial) or by court order (residential); 1-year increments.
- Missed deadline = lien discharged by operation of law.

**Tax compliance — capital improvement vs. repair (the New York signature trap):**

- **Obtain a signed Form ST-124 from the customer for ALL capital improvement work** before commencing. Without ST-124, the DTF can re-characterize the contract as repair work and assess sales tax on the full amount.
- **Repair / installation / maintenance:** Buy materials tax-free using Form ST-120 resale certificate, collect sales tax from customer on entire invoice (labor + materials).
- **Capital improvement:** Pay sales tax on materials at purchase; do not collect sales tax from customer.
- **Out-of-state materials:** Use tax owed to NY at the combined rate of the work site.
- File and pay sales/use tax monthly or quarterly as required by Certificate of Authority filing frequency.

**Annual / multi-year compliance:**

- Renew NYC DOB GCR every **3 years** ($300).
- Renew NYC DOB Master Plumber / Master Electrician / Master Fire Suppression Piping every **3 years** ($300; 7 hours CE per 3-year cycle).
- Renew NYC DCWP HIC every **2 years** ($100).
- Renew Nassau County HIC every **2 years** ($300).
- Renew Suffolk County HIC every **2 years** (~$200).
- Renew Westchester County HIC every **3 years** ($140).
- File NY Corporate Franchise Tax Report annually (Article 9-A entities).
- File NY personal income tax (sole proprietors, LLC members, partners).
- File NYC UBT if doing business in NYC as sole proprietor or partnership (4% of net business income).
- Renew workers' compensation policy annually with current C-105.2 filing for each jurisdiction in which permits are pulled; file CE-200 for any officer-exclusion claims.
- For NYC: maintain SST training cards for all workers / supervisors and re-take expired training; renew Site Safety Coordinator / Manager personal licenses on schedule.
- Maintain commercial GL insurance at each agency's required minimum with each licensing body listed as certificate holder / additional insured.
