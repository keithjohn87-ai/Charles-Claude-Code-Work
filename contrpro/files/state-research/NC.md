## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The North Carolina structure — a state-licensed general contractor with separately-boarded trades

North Carolina, like Georgia and Florida, **does require a statewide general contractor license** for work above a statutory threshold [1][2][3]. The licensing authority is the **North Carolina Licensing Board for General Contractors (NCLBGC)**, established by the General Contractor Licensing Act, **N.C.G.S. § 87-1 et seq.** [1][4][5]. The five mechanical and electrical trades that ContrPro routinely encounters — electrical, plumbing, HVAC, fire sprinkler, and refrigeration — are licensed under **separate Articles** of Chapter 87 and by **separate state boards**:

- **Electrical contracting:** Article 4 (N.C.G.S. § 87-39 et seq.), administered by the **North Carolina State Board of Examiners of Electrical Contractors (NCBEEC)** [6][7].
- **Plumbing, Heating (HVAC), and Fire Sprinkler contracting:** Article 2 (N.C.G.S. § 87-16 et seq.), administered by the **North Carolina State Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors (NCSBPHFSC)** [8][9].
- **Refrigeration contracting:** Article 5 (N.C.G.S. § 87-52 et seq.), administered by the **North Carolina State Board of Refrigeration Examiners** [10].

Structural-steel erection has **no North Carolina state license** at all; competency is established through AISC, AWS, and project-specification routes, with NCDOT-specific welder qualifications on state bridge work [11][12].

For ContrPro's five trades, the practical map is:

| Trade | Statewide license? | Board | Statutory authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| General contracting (commercial & residential) | **Yes** — Limited, Intermediate, Unlimited; classified by scope | NCLBGC | N.C.G.S. § 87-1 et seq. [1][4][5] |
| Electrical contracting | **Yes** — Limited, Intermediate, Unlimited; SP-restricted subclasses | NC Board of Examiners of Electrical Contractors | N.C.G.S. § 87-39 et seq. [6][7] |
| Plumbing | **Yes** — P-I and P-II classifications | NC State Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors | N.C.G.S. § 87-16 et seq. [8][9] |
| HVAC | **Yes** — H-1 (heating Group 1), H-2 (heating Group 2), H-3 (heating Group 3 oil), H-3-I and H-3-II classifications | NC State Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors | N.C.G.S. § 87-16 et seq. [8][9] |
| Fire sprinkler | **Yes** — SP-FLS classification | NC State Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors | N.C.G.S. § 87-16 et seq. [8][9] |
| Refrigeration (commercial) | **Yes** — Commercial Transport, Commercial Refrigeration | NC State Board of Refrigeration Examiners | N.C.G.S. § 87-52 et seq. [10] |
| Structural steel erection | **No state license** | Local building department; AISC/AWS by contract; NCDOT-specific for bridges | Industry standard, building code |

### 1.2 The $30,000 threshold — the gateway

The North Carolina licensing statute is **threshold-driven**, and missing the threshold reading is the single most common reason an unlicensed North Carolina contractor finds itself with an unenforceable contract under settled North Carolina case law [13][14][15][16]:

- **General Contractor license required:** N.C.G.S. § 87-1(a) defines a "general contractor" as any person who "for a fixed price, commission, fee, or wage, undertakes to bid upon or to construct or who undertakes to superintend or manage, on his own behalf or for any person, firm, or corporation that is not licensed as a general contractor pursuant to this Article, the construction of any building, highway, public utilities, grading or any improvement or structure where the cost of the undertaking is **thirty thousand dollars ($30,000) or more**" [4][13][14][15].
- **Below-threshold work:** A person performing construction or improvement work where the **total cost is less than $30,000** is not required to hold an NCLBGC license under § 87-1(a). The exemption does **not** cover any work that requires a separate trade license under Articles 2, 4, or 5 of Chapter 87 — touching electrical, plumbing, HVAC, fire sprinkler, or refrigeration systems requires the appropriate trade license **regardless of project value** [4][8][6][13].
- **Owner-builder exemption:** Under N.C.G.S. § 87-1(b)(2), an owner who personally builds his or her own residence is exempt from the GC licensing requirement, but the exemption is narrow — the owner must personally perform or directly supervise the work, must not transfer the property within 12 months of completion (or be presumed to be operating as an unlicensed contractor), and cannot delegate the supervision to an unlicensed agent [4][13][17].
- **Aggregation of costs.** The $30,000 threshold is computed on the **total cost of the undertaking**, including labor, materials, equipment rentals, and subcontracted scopes — not the prime contract amount alone. Splitting a $50,000 job into two $25,000 contracts to evade licensing is an established licensing-board enforcement target and a long-recognized basis for finding unlicensed activity [4][13][17][18].

**Unenforceable-contract penalty.** North Carolina applies a settled common-law rule, rooted in *Brady v. Fulghum*, 309 N.C. 580, 308 S.E.2d 327 (1983), and its progeny, that **an unlicensed general contractor cannot enforce its contract** for any work within Chapter 87 — neither in contract nor in quantum meruit. The rule is jurisdictional in the sense that it is not subject to estoppel or waiver: even an owner who knew of the lack of licensure at the time of contract can assert the defense. The unlicensed contractor cannot sue for the contract price, cannot recover for the value of work performed, and cannot file a mechanic's lien for the unlicensed work [13][14][15][16][17][19]. The North Carolina Supreme Court has reaffirmed this rule across multiple decisions, holding the public-policy interest in licensing enforcement outweighs any equitable considerations favoring the unlicensed contractor.

**Criminal penalty.** Under N.C.G.S. § 87-13, performing work as a general contractor without the required license, or applying to be a general contractor by false or fraudulent representation, is a **Class 2 misdemeanor** [4][13][20]. The NCLBGC has the authority to refer suspected unlicensed activity to local district attorneys, and license-revocation proceedings under N.C.G.S. § 87-11 are administrative remedies separate from the criminal track.

### 1.3 NCLBGC — license classifications by monetary cap

**Statutory authority:** N.C.G.S. § 87-1 through § 87-15.6 [4][5][20]. **Administrative rules:** **21 NCAC 12** (NCLBGC Rules) [21][22][23]. **Licensing agency:** North Carolina Licensing Board for General Contractors, 5400 Creedmoor Road, Raleigh, NC 27612; mailing address P.O. Box 17187, Raleigh, NC 27619; (919) 571-4183 [1][4][21].

North Carolina is **distinctive** in maintaining three **monetary-cap classifications** that determine the maximum project value a contractor may undertake [5][22][24][25]:

| Classification | Project value cap (single project) | Aggregate annual cap |
|---|---|---|
| **Limited** | **$750,000** per project | No statutory aggregate cap |
| **Intermediate** | **$1,500,000** per project | No statutory aggregate cap |
| **Unlimited** | **No cap** — any project value | None |

The cap is per-project and is calculated on the **total cost of the undertaking** (labor + materials + subcontracted scopes), not the GC's net contract margin or fee. A Limited GC who undertakes a $900,000 project — even if the GC's subcontracted scopes absorb most of the cost — is operating outside its classification and is exposed to license revocation and contract unenforceability for the entire project, not merely the over-cap portion [22][24][25].

**Working capital and net worth requirements** are likewise scaled by classification (N.C.G.S. § 87-10(b)(7); 21 NCAC 12 .0202) [5][22][24][25][26]:

| Classification | Minimum working capital OR net worth | Surety bond alternative |
|---|---|---|
| **Limited** | **$17,000** working capital | **$350,000** surety bond + Limited classification |
| **Intermediate** | **$80,000** working capital | **$500,000** surety bond + Intermediate classification |
| **Unlimited** | **$150,000** working capital | **$1,000,000** surety bond + Unlimited classification |

The financial standard is verified through a **financial statement** filed with the license application; the statement must be **prepared and signed by a CPA** (audited, reviewed, or compiled with disclosures) if the applicant claims working capital or net worth to qualify, and must be dated no more than **12 months** prior to the application filing [22][24][25][26]. As an alternative to demonstrating working capital, the applicant may post a continuous surety bond in the amount listed; the bond runs to the State of North Carolina conditioned on the contractor's compliance with Chapter 87 and the contractor's faithful performance of contracts. Bonded qualification is the standard route for newly-formed contractor entities that have not yet accumulated independent working capital.

**Classification scope categories.** Within each monetary-cap classification, the contractor must additionally select **one or more scope classifications** that define the *type* of work the contractor is qualified to undertake [5][22][24][27]:

| Scope classification | Coverage |
|---|---|
| **Building (B)** | Buildings of any type — commercial, industrial, institutional, multifamily residential — including site work and structural elements. Most comprehensive scope. |
| **Residential (RES)** | One- and two-family detached dwellings and townhouses up to three stories; not appropriate for multifamily over four units or commercial structures. |
| **Highway (H)** | Highway, road, bridge, paving, grading, and DOT work. |
| **Public Utilities (PU)** | Water/sewer mains, storm drainage, gas distribution, communication lines, electrical transmission and distribution (not premises wiring — that is electrical contracting under Article 4), and similar utility infrastructure. PU itself is subdivided into specialty subgroups (water lines, sewer lines, fuel distribution, communication lines, electrical lines, etc.) [22][24][27]. |
| **Specialty (S, SP)** | Subordinate scopes including grading and excavation, marine construction, swimming pools, insulation, masonry, concrete, roofing, asbestos, lead abatement, and others. Each specialty is administered as a sub-classification under 21 NCAC 12 [22][24][27]. |

A GC may hold **multiple scope classifications** within a single monetary-cap level, each requiring a separate technical-trade exam pass.

**Examination — two-part structure** (N.C.G.S. § 87-10(a); 21 NCAC 12 .0204) [22][24][25][26][27]:

1. **Trade Examination:** Open-book exam administered by **PSI** (under contract with the NCLBGC), with the question pool selected by the chosen scope classification — Building, Residential, Highway, Public Utilities, or each specialty. The Building exam, for example, covers structural systems, mechanical/electrical/plumbing coordination, building codes (the North Carolina State Building Code, which adopts the IBC with NC Amendments), OSHA construction standards, and project-management content. Approximately 100 questions per scope; minimum passing score **70%** [22][24][25].
2. **Business and Law Examination:** Open-book exam covering North Carolina general contractor licensing law (Chapter 87), lien law (Chapter 44A), retainage and prompt-payment law (Chapter 22C), workers' compensation (Chapter 97), labor and tax basics, contract fundamentals, and ethics. Approximately 50 questions; minimum passing score **70%** [22][24][25].

Both exams are administered by PSI at testing centers across North Carolina; the trade exam fee is approximately $80; the business-and-law exam fee is approximately $80; the NCLBGC application fee is approximately $80 to $100 depending on classification [22][25].

**Experience.** N.C.G.S. § 87-10 does **not** impose a fixed minimum-years-of-experience requirement; rather, the applicant must demonstrate "experience sufficient to qualify for licensing" through the application narrative and supporting documentation. In practice, the NCLBGC reviews applications for evidence of substantive prior construction experience — typically **1 to 3 years** of supervisory or qualifying-party experience, depending on classification — but accepts qualification entirely through the trade examination, which is rigorous enough that an applicant without genuine experience rarely passes [22][24][25][26]. Unlike Georgia (which imposes a hard 4-year/2-year-supervisory rule under O.C.G.A. § 43-41) or Florida (which imposes specific experience under § 489.111), North Carolina's experience requirement is qualitative and exam-tested rather than enumerated.

**Qualifying party.** Every NCLBGC license is held by either an **individual** (sole proprietor) or a **business entity** (LLC, corporation, partnership). An entity-held license must designate a **Qualifier** (also called a **Qualifying Party**), who is the natural person who passed the trade examination and the business-and-law examination on behalf of the entity. The Qualifier must be a full-time employee, officer, member, or partner of the entity; the Qualifier cannot serve simultaneously as the Qualifier for more than one license-holding entity. If the Qualifier separates from the entity, the entity has **90 days** to designate a replacement Qualifier or the license is suspended [22][24][25][28]. The Qualifier requirement is the principal control point preventing license-rental schemes (paying a credentialed individual to "front" for an unqualified entity), and the NCLBGC actively enforces against arrangements that lack genuine employer-employee or ownership ties.

**Renewal.** NCLBGC licenses renew **annually**, expiring **December 31** of each year; renewal applications open in early November and the renewal fee is approximately $75 to $200 depending on classification. Late renewals (January–February) carry restoration fees; licenses lapsed beyond 30 days require re-application and may require re-examination [22][24][25][28]. Continuing education is **not mandatory** for North Carolina general contractors except for limited-scope residential contractors performing certain specialty work; this is a notable contrast to Georgia (which requires 8 hours biennial CE for residential contractors) and Florida (which requires 14 hours biennial CE for all CILB licensees).

**Reciprocity.** The NCLBGC has formal reciprocity agreements with several states, primarily for the trade exam (the business-and-law exam is required of all applicants regardless of out-of-state credentials). North Carolina participates in the **NASCLA Accredited Examination** program — a contractor holding a valid NASCLA Commercial Building Contractor credential from another state may use it to satisfy the NCLBGC Building trade exam, but must still take and pass the North Carolina Business and Law Examination [22][24][27][29]. Reciprocity does not waive the financial-qualification, Qualifier, or bonding requirements.

### 1.4 NC State Board of Examiners of Electrical Contractors (NCBEEC) — N.C.G.S. § 87-39 et seq.

Electrical contracting in North Carolina is licensed at the state level under **Chapter 87 Article 4** (N.C.G.S. § 87-39 through § 87-47), administered by the **North Carolina State Board of Examiners of Electrical Contractors (NCBEEC)** [6][7][30][31].

**Statutory scope** (N.C.G.S. § 87-39): "Electrical contracting" means the business of contracting to install or maintain electrical wiring, devices, appliances, or equipment for use in connection with the generation, transmission, distribution, or utilization of electrical energy [6][30].

**License classifications** (N.C.G.S. § 87-43.3; 21 NCAC 18B) [6][7][30][31][32]:

| Classification | Scope cap |
|---|---|
| **Limited (L)** | Single contract value up to **$50,000** per project (residential or small commercial) |
| **Intermediate (I)** | Single contract value up to **$130,000** per project |
| **Unlimited (U)** | No project value cap; any electrical work |

Within each monetary tier, the licensee selects one or more **specialty classifications** that define the *type* of electrical work:

- **EB** — Electrical Contracting Unlimited (all electrical work within the monetary tier)
- **SP-WP** — Single-Family Detached Residential Dwellings (water pump installations only, on certain limited residential scopes)
- **SP-EE** — Electric Sign and Outline Lighting
- **SP-CLV** — Communications/Limited Voltage (Class 1, 2, 3 circuits, signaling, fire alarm low-voltage, sound, data)
- **SP-LE** — Low-Voltage Landscape Irrigation and Lighting
- **SP-SFD** — Single-Family Detached Dwelling (residential-only)
- **SP-EL** — Elevator
- **SP-SCC** — Swimming Pool
- **SP-RWH** — Residential Water Heater (limited specialty)

Each specialty has a separate exam administered by PSI under NCBEEC contract [6][30][31][32].

**Eligibility requirements** (21 NCAC 18B) [7][30][31][32]:

1. **Age:** At least **18 years** old.
2. **Experience:** Practical experience requirements scale with classification:
   - **Limited:** At least **2 years** of primary working experience in electrical construction, installation, repair, or maintenance, with at least 1 year as the person in charge.
   - **Intermediate:** At least **4 years** of primary experience, with at least 2 years as the person in charge.
   - **Unlimited:** At least **4 years** of primary experience, with at least 2 years as the person in charge of unlimited-scope work.
3. **Examination:** Pass the **NCBEEC Qualifying Examination** for the chosen classification and specialty. Exams are open-book and reference the **North Carolina State Building Code: Electrical** (which adopts the National Electrical Code with NC Amendments — currently the 2020 NEC with NC Amendments effective January 1, 2024) [30][32][33]. Minimum passing score **75%**.
4. **Insurance:** Continuous certificate of liability insurance — minimum **$300,000 per occurrence** general liability for Limited and Intermediate; minimum **$500,000 per occurrence** for Unlimited. Workers' compensation as required by Chapter 97 [30][31][32].
5. **Financial responsibility:** Statement of financial condition demonstrating the applicant can carry out the contracts within its classification. Unlike NCLBGC, the NCBEEC does not impose a numerical working-capital minimum, but the financial statement is reviewed for evidence of capacity.

**Qualifier.** As with the NCLBGC, an entity-held NCBEEC license must designate a **Qualifier** — a natural person who is a full-time employee, officer, member, or partner of the entity and who has passed the qualifying examination. The Qualifier cannot simultaneously qualify another contractor; separation from the entity suspends the license absent designation of a successor within 90 days [30][31][32].

**Renewal.** NCBEEC licenses renew **annually**, expiring **June 30** of each year; renewal fee approximately $100. **Continuing education is required** — **8 hours of approved CE** for Unlimited and Intermediate licensees, **4 hours** for Limited licensees, per annual renewal cycle, covering NEC updates and NC Amendments [31][32][34]. This is a meaningful operational obligation; NCBEEC publishes lists of approved CE providers and tracks completion electronically.

**Penalties.** Unlicensed electrical contracting in North Carolina is a **Class 2 misdemeanor** under N.C.G.S. § 87-46, with potential civil penalties of up to **$2,000 per violation** assessed by NCBEEC under § 87-46.1, and exposure to contract unenforceability and disgorgement [6][30][31].

### 1.5 NC State Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors — N.C.G.S. § 87-16 et seq.

Plumbing, heating, and fire sprinkler contracting in North Carolina are jointly licensed at the state level under **Chapter 87 Article 2** (N.C.G.S. § 87-16 through § 87-27.2), administered by the **North Carolina State Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors (NCSBPHFSC)** [8][9][35][36].

**Statutory scope** (N.C.G.S. § 87-21): "Plumbing contractor" means a person who, for compensation, installs, alters, repairs, or maintains plumbing systems — water supply, drainage, vent, fixtures, and traps. "Heating contractor" means a person who installs, alters, repairs, or maintains heating systems — including HVAC, hydronic, fuel-gas, and oil-fired equipment. "Fire sprinkler contractor" means a person who installs, alters, repairs, or maintains fire-protection sprinkler systems [8][35].

**Plumbing license classifications** (N.C.G.S. § 87-21(b); 21 NCAC 50) [8][9][35][36]:

| Classification | Scope |
|---|---|
| **P-I (Plumbing Group 1)** | All plumbing — water supply, drainage, vent, fixtures, and all related systems, with no specialty restriction. |
| **P-II (Plumbing Group 2)** | Restricted plumbing for water supply and drainage systems only — limited specialty. |

**Heating (HVAC) license classifications** (N.C.G.S. § 87-21(b)) [8][9][35][36]:

| Classification | Scope |
|---|---|
| **H-1** (Heating Group 1) | All heating, ventilation, air-conditioning, and refrigeration systems for any building. Most comprehensive HVAC scope. |
| **H-2** (Heating Group 2) | Limited to warm-air heating and air-conditioning; excludes hydronic and absorption systems. |
| **H-3** (Heating Group 3) | Oil-fired equipment, fuel-oil piping, and related installations. Further split into H-3-I (installation) and H-3-II (service only). |

**Fire sprinkler classification** (N.C.G.S. § 87-21(b)) [8][9][35]:

- **SP-FLS** (Fire Sprinkler / Fire Protection) — Fire-protection sprinkler systems under NFPA 13, 13D, 13R; backflow prevention; standpipe; fire pumps. Limited to fire protection only.

**Eligibility requirements** (N.C.G.S. § 87-21; 21 NCAC 50) [8][9][35][36]:

1. **Age:** At least **18 years** old.
2. **Experience:** At least **2 years** of primary, on-the-job experience in the relevant trade — increased to **4 years** for unrestricted classifications in some specialties. Experience must be documented through employer affidavits.
3. **Examination:** Pass the NCSBPHFSC trade examination for the chosen classification (plumbing, heating, or fire sprinkler), open-book, referencing the North Carolina State Building Code: Plumbing; the North Carolina State Building Code: Mechanical; the North Carolina State Building Code: Fuel Gas; and NFPA 13/13D/13R for fire sprinkler. Minimum passing score **70%** [9][35][36].
4. **Insurance:** Continuous certificate of liability insurance — minimum **$300,000 per occurrence** for unrestricted classifications. Workers' compensation as required by Chapter 97 [9][35].
5. **Qualifier:** Entity-held license must designate a Qualifier as above; 90-day grace on Qualifier separation [9][35][36].

**Renewal.** NCSBPHFSC licenses renew **annually**, expiring **December 31** of each year. Renewal fee approximately $100. **Continuing education is required** — **8 hours of approved CE** annually for unrestricted licensees, **4 hours** for restricted licensees, covering plumbing-code, mechanical-code, fuel-gas, fire-protection, and backflow-prevention updates [9][35][36][37].

**Penalties.** Unlicensed plumbing, heating, or fire sprinkler contracting is a **Class 2 misdemeanor** under N.C.G.S. § 87-26, with civil penalties of up to **$5,000 per violation** under § 87-23, and exposure to contract unenforceability [8][9][35].

### 1.6 NC State Board of Refrigeration Examiners — N.C.G.S. § 87-52 et seq.

Commercial refrigeration is licensed separately under **Chapter 87 Article 5** (N.C.G.S. § 87-52 through § 87-61), administered by the **North Carolina State Board of Refrigeration Examiners** [10][38][39].

**Classifications** [10][38][39]:

| Classification | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Commercial Refrigeration** | Stationary commercial refrigeration systems — supermarkets, cold storage, walk-in coolers, ice rinks, industrial refrigeration. Excludes residential and HVAC systems (which fall under NCSBPHFSC). |
| **Commercial Transport Refrigeration** | Mobile and transport refrigeration units — reefer trucks, refrigerated trailers, marine refrigeration. |

**Eligibility:** Minimum 18 years old; 2 to 4 years of relevant refrigeration experience depending on classification; pass the trade examination; carry GL insurance at $300,000 per occurrence. Renewal annually, December 31; CE not statutorily required for refrigeration but encouraged through industry-association programming [10][38][39].

### 1.7 Structural steel erection — no state license; AISC/AWS by contract

As in Georgia and Illinois, North Carolina does **not** license structural-steel erectors at the state level [11][12][40]. Competency on commercial and DOT projects is established through:

1. **AISC Certified Erector** (AISC 207) and **AISC Certified Fabricator** (AISC 303) — required by most large commercial specifications and by NCDOT bridge specifications.
2. **AWS welder qualification** under **AWS D1.1** (building steel) or **AWS D1.5** (bridge welding); NCDOT requires AWS D1.5 plus NCDOT-specific welder qualification testing for state bridge welds [12][40].
3. **AWS Certified Welding Inspector (CWI)** for commercial and NCDOT inspection.
4. **OSHA 10/30** as contract requirement.
5. **Special inspections** — North Carolina adopts the IBC's Chapter 17 special-inspection regime through the **North Carolina State Building Code** (administered by the NC Department of Insurance, Office of State Fire Marshal). Commercial steel typically requires owner-engaged special inspectors.

A structural-steel contractor with no self-performed plumbing, electrical, or HVAC scope, whose project value exceeds $30,000, must hold the NCLBGC General Contractor license under the **Building (B)** scope classification (or operate as a sub to a properly-licensed prime). The NCLBGC has held that "structural steel erection" is not a recognized standalone specialty under 21 NCAC 12 and that erectors performing $30,000+ work must hold the Building or appropriate specialty classification.

### 1.8 Workers' compensation — N.C.G.S. § 97 (Workers' Compensation Act)

North Carolina workers' compensation is at **N.C.G.S. Chapter 97**, administered by the **North Carolina Industrial Commission (NCIC)** [41][42][43]. The construction industry is the most heavily monitored segment.

**Construction-industry coverage** — **N.C.G.S. § 97-2** [41][42]:

The Act applies to "every person, firm, or private corporation, including any public service corporation, that has in employment **three or more employees**, except those exempted under § 97-13" [41]. North Carolina's three-employee threshold is the **highest** of the major Southeast jurisdictions — Georgia, South Carolina, Tennessee, and Florida all impose lower thresholds for construction. However, the threshold is computed cumulatively:

- **Counting subcontractor employees toward the threshold.** Under N.C.G.S. § 97-19 (the statutory-employer / "principal contractor" rule), a general contractor that engages a subcontractor whose own employees bring the GC's project-side headcount to three or more is treated as the **principal contractor** and is liable for workers' compensation benefits to the sub's employees if the sub does not carry coverage [41][42][44].
- **Sole-proprietor and corporate-officer treatment.** Sole proprietors and partners are not counted as employees and may elect into or out of coverage under their own policy. Corporate officers are presumed to be employees but may file an officer-exclusion election; LLC members may file an analogous election [41][42][43].
- **Independent-contractor / employee distinction.** The NCIC applies a multi-factor "right to control" test rather than the strict ABC test used in Illinois. Misclassification exposure is real but less aggressively enforced than in Illinois or California.

**Statutory employer rule** — **N.C.G.S. § 97-19** [41][42][44][45]:

A general contractor that engages a subcontractor who does not carry the required workers' compensation insurance is liable to pay workers' compensation benefits to that subcontractor's employees as if the GC had directly employed them. The GC's remedy is indemnity against the uninsured sub. **Two practical consequences:**

1. **Always require Certificates of Insurance evidencing WC coverage** from every sub before site access. The COI should name the GC as certificate holder and be currency-verified at minimum quarterly. NCIC's Coverage Verification system (Verify a NCIC Coverage) allows GCs to confirm current coverage at no charge.
2. **Sole-proprietor subs with no employees** may be exempt from carrying WC, but any helper or day laborer triggers the requirement — and the GC inherits liability when the sub fails to carry. Note: even where the sub is exempt, NCIC enforcement against the GC is mitigated by the three-employee threshold, but exposure is not eliminated.

**Penalties for failure to carry WC** (N.C.G.S. § 97-94) [41][42][45]:

- **Civil penalty of up to $100 per day** for each day the employer is uninsured, up to a maximum of **$1,000 per day** for willful violations.
- **Class H felony** for knowing or willful failure to carry coverage; **Class 1 misdemeanor** for non-willful failure under specified circumstances.
- Personal liability for corporate officers who knowingly cause the corporation to fail to carry coverage.
- **Stop-work order** authority — NCIC may issue an emergency cease-and-desist order shutting down job sites until coverage is procured.

The North Carolina Industrial Commission maintains an aggressive **Insurance Compliance Section** that audits employers via cross-referenced payroll, NC Department of Revenue, and unemployment-insurance data; uninsured construction employers are a particular enforcement target.

**Construction-industry classifications.** North Carolina is an NCCI advisory-loss-cost state. Construction classifications (5538 HVAC, 5183 Plumbing, 5190 Electrical, 5057 Iron/Steel Erection, 5645 Carpentry/Single-Family, 5474 Painting, 5022 Masonry) carry the state's highest base rates.

### 1.9 GL insurance — North Carolina statewide minimums

North Carolina does not impose a single statewide GL minimum for general contractors; minimums are set per-license-class:

| Source | Minimum |
|---|---|
| NCLBGC General Contractor — Limited | None statutorily mandated, but contractor must demonstrate insurance capacity in financial responsibility filings |
| NCLBGC General Contractor — Intermediate | Same as above |
| NCLBGC General Contractor — Unlimited | Same as above |
| NC Electrical Contractor — Limited / Intermediate | **$300,000 per occurrence** GL |
| NC Electrical Contractor — Unlimited | **$500,000 per occurrence** GL |
| NC Plumbing / Heating / Fire Sprinkler Contractor | **$300,000 per occurrence** GL (unrestricted classifications) |
| NC Commercial Refrigeration Contractor | **$300,000 per occurrence** GL |
| NC Workers' Compensation (3+ employees) | Statutory under § 97 |

Project owners, lenders, and bonding companies routinely require GL limits well above these statutory minimums — $1M/$2M is the typical commercial floor on private work; $2M/$4M is common on larger commercial and institutional jobs; bonded public-works projects routinely require $5M to $10M aggregate.

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — N.C.G.S. § 44A-7 et seq.

North Carolina mechanic's-lien law is at **N.C.G.S. Chapter 44A Article 2** (Statutory Liens on Real Property — § 44A-7 through § 44A-23), substantially rewritten by **Session Law 2012-158 (HB 1052)** effective April 1, 2013, which created the **Lien Agent system** (§ 44A-11.1) and reorganized the lien-on-funds and lien-on-real-property procedures into separate but coordinated remedies [46][47][48][49][50][51]. The 2013 reform is the single most consequential change in North Carolina lien law in three generations and remains the foundation of every lien claim filed today.

### 2.1 Two remedies — Claim of Lien on Real Property and Claim of Lien Upon Funds

North Carolina is **structurally distinctive** in maintaining **two parallel lien remedies** for unpaid construction labor and materials [46][47][48][49]:

1. **Claim of Lien on Real Property** (N.C.G.S. § 44A-8 through § 44A-12) — A direct lien against the real property improved, filed with the **Clerk of Superior Court** of the county where the property is located. Available to the prime contractor in privity with the owner; available to lower-tier claimants only by **subrogation** to the prime's lien under N.C.G.S. § 44A-23 (which requires the prime to have properly preserved its own lien rights, including timely filing) [46][47][48].
2. **Claim of Lien Upon Funds** (N.C.G.S. § 44A-18 through § 44A-23) — A lien against **funds owed by the owner to the prime contractor**, and against funds owed by the prime to a higher-tier subcontractor. The Lien Upon Funds is a **separate, direct remedy** for subcontractors and suppliers — it does not depend on subrogation to the prime's lien — but it attaches **only** to whatever the owner currently owes the prime (or, for sub-tier liens, what the higher-tier owes the lower-tier). If the owner has fully paid the prime, the Lien Upon Funds is effectively zero [46][47][48][49][52].

This two-remedy structure means that a North Carolina subcontractor pursuing payment must typically file **both** a Lien Upon Funds (to capture whatever the owner still owes the prime) and, by subrogation to the prime's Lien on Real Property, a corresponding Claim of Lien on Real Property (to reach the property itself if the funds are insufficient). Failure to coordinate both filings is a recurring source of underrecovery [49][52][53].

### 2.2 The Lien Agent system — N.C.G.S. § 44A-11.1 (effective April 1, 2013)

The defining feature of post-2013 North Carolina lien law is the **Lien Agent system**, codified at **N.C.G.S. § 44A-11.1** and operationalized through **LiensNC.com**, the state-designated electronic Lien Agent registry administered jointly by title-insurance carriers and the North Carolina Land Title Association [46][50][54][55][56][57].

**Owner registration obligation** — **N.C.G.S. § 44A-11.1(a)** [50][55][56]:

For any improvement to real property where the **total cost is $30,000 or more** — except for improvements to an existing single-family residential dwelling occupied by the owner — the **owner must designate a Lien Agent** before the **earlier of**:

1. **First furnishing of labor or materials** to the project, or
2. Issuance of the **building permit** for the project.

The Lien Agent designation is made through LiensNC.com (the state's exclusive web portal); the owner pays a one-time **registration fee** scaled by project value (typically $25 for small projects, ramping with project value). The designation must include the owner's contact information, project location (street address and parcel ID), brief description of the improvement, and the designated Lien Agent (which is typically the title-insurance company insuring the project's construction loan or owner's policy) [50][55][56].

**Building-permit posting obligation** (§ 44A-11.1(c)): When the building permit issues, the permit-issuing local government **must record the Lien Agent's contact information on the face of the permit** — and the permit must be **posted at the job site** so any subcontractor or supplier visiting the site can identify the Lien Agent. Permit-issuing agencies that fail to comply expose the owner's lien-agent registration to challenge [50][55][56].

**Subcontractor / supplier obligation — Notice to Lien Agent** (N.C.G.S. § 44A-11.2) [54][55][56][57][58]:

A subcontractor or supplier — at any tier below the prime — must **serve a Notice to Lien Agent** within **15 days** after **first furnishing labor or materials** to the project, in order to preserve full lien rights. The Notice to Lien Agent is served **through LiensNC.com**, which automatically forwards the notice to the designated Lien Agent and creates a date-stamped record of service [54][55][56][57].

**Consequence of failing to serve the Notice to Lien Agent timely.** Under § 44A-11.2(l), a subcontractor or supplier who fails to serve the Notice to Lien Agent **within 15 days of first furnishing** loses its ability to claim a lien against the real property for any labor or materials furnished **before** the date the notice is eventually served. The lien rights for labor and materials furnished **after** the service date are preserved, but the pre-service period is unrecoverable [54][55][56][57][58]. The 15-day clock is calendar days, not business days, and is not subject to tolling for weekends, holidays, or supplier-side processing delays.

The practical effect: every North Carolina subcontractor and supplier on a $30,000+ project must develop a **first-furnishing tracking discipline** — recording the date materials were first delivered or labor first performed, and serving the Notice to Lien Agent through LiensNC.com **within 15 days**, no exceptions. The 15-day window is the single most-missed deadline in North Carolina lien practice [55][56][57][58].

**Owner-occupied existing single-family dwelling exception** (§ 44A-11.1(a)): The Lien Agent requirement does **not** apply to improvements to an existing single-family residential dwelling that is the principal residence of the owner. Under this exception, no Lien Agent is required; the subcontractor and supplier preserve lien rights through the traditional Notice of Subcontract procedure under § 44A-19 (see § 2.3 below). The exception is narrowly construed; new construction, multifamily, vacation homes, rentals, and any non-owner-occupied residences fall outside it [50][55][56].

**Penalty for owner non-designation.** If the owner fails to designate a Lien Agent for a project that meets the $30,000 threshold and is not otherwise exempt, the building permit cannot lawfully issue (because the permit-issuing agency cannot complete the § 44A-11.1(c) recordation). In practice, NC building-permit applications now include a "Lien Agent" checkbox; municipalities will not issue the permit until the owner produces LiensNC.com confirmation [50][55][56].

### 2.3 Notice of Subcontract — N.C.G.S. § 44A-19

For projects exempt from the Lien Agent requirement (owner-occupied single-family residences) and as an additional layer on Lien-Agent-covered projects, North Carolina retains the traditional **Notice of Subcontract** procedure under **N.C.G.S. § 44A-19** [46][47][53][59].

The Notice of Subcontract is served on the **owner and on the prime contractor**, by a first-tier subcontractor at the time the subcontract is entered into (or as soon as practicable thereafter), identifying:

- The subcontractor's name and contact information.
- A general description of the labor or materials to be furnished.
- The estimated contract price.

Service of a Notice of Subcontract preserves the subcontractor's ability to file a **Claim of Lien Upon Funds** (§ 44A-18) attaching to amounts the owner owes the prime. **Lower-tier subcontractors** (second-tier and below) follow a parallel procedure under § 44A-19(b), serving the Notice of Subcontract on the next higher-tier obligor [46][47][53][59].

The Notice of Subcontract is **not** a substitute for the Notice to Lien Agent — projects requiring the Lien Agent designation still require Notice to Lien Agent under § 44A-11.2 within 15 days of first furnishing. The Notice of Subcontract is an additional protection that preserves the Lien Upon Funds remedy.

### 2.4 Claim of Lien on Real Property — N.C.G.S. § 44A-12 (filing deadline and contents)

The **Claim of Lien on Real Property** must be filed with the **Clerk of Superior Court** of the county where the property is located, **within 120 days from the last furnishing** of labor or materials by the claimant to the project under the lien-claimant's contract [46][47][48][53][60].

**Required contents** (§ 44A-12(c)) [46][47][53]:

1. Name and address of the owner of the property at the time of filing.
2. Name and address of the claimant.
3. Description of the property sufficient to identify it (legal description preferred; street address acceptable if it permits unambiguous identification).
4. Name and address of the person with whom the claimant contracted (the prime contractor for first-tier subs; the higher-tier sub for lower-tier subs).
5. **Date of first and last furnishing** of labor or materials.
6. General description of the labor performed or materials furnished, and the contract price (or, if no contract price, the value of labor and materials furnished).
7. **Amount claimed** under the lien.
8. **Verification under oath** that the contents are true to the claimant's knowledge.

**The 120-day deadline runs from last furnishing.** "Last furnishing" means the last day on which the claimant performed labor or delivered materials **under the original contract or any timely modification thereof**. North Carolina courts have held that warranty work, punch-list visits, and de minimis repairs do **not** extend the 120-day clock; only new, distinct, contractually-required work restarts it [46][47][48][60][61]. The claimant bears the burden of proving the date of last furnishing.

**Filing fee.** Clerk of Superior Court charges a recording fee (typically $26 for the first page plus additional pages at $4 each, plus indexing fees). The Claim of Lien itself is a one-page filing in most cases [46][47].

**Service.** Within **three days of filing** (excluding weekends and legal holidays), the lien claimant must serve a copy of the filed Claim of Lien on the owner of the real property by personal service, certified mail with return receipt requested, or any method authorized by Rule 4 of the North Carolina Rules of Civil Procedure (§ 44A-11). Failure to serve within three days does not invalidate the lien but may be raised in defense [46][47][48].

### 2.5 Foreclosure / suit to enforce — N.C.G.S. § 44A-13 (180-day deadline)

The Claim of Lien on Real Property must be **enforced by suit** filed in the Superior Court of the county where the property is located, **within 180 days from the last furnishing** of labor or materials by the claimant — i.e., within **60 days after** the 120-day filing deadline [46][47][48][53][60][62].

If suit is **not** filed within 180 days, the lien is **discharged by operation of law** and becomes unenforceable. The 180-day deadline is jurisdictional and not subject to equitable tolling. The filing of suit must be accompanied by a **Notice of Lis Pendens** filed with the Clerk of Superior Court, which preserves the lien as against subsequent purchasers and encumbrancers [46][47][48][62].

**Sub-tier subrogation suits.** A sub or supplier asserting a Claim of Lien on Real Property via subrogation to the prime's lien under § 44A-23 must commence suit within the 180-day window calculated from **the subrogating party's last furnishing** — not the prime's. The subrogation lien is independent in this respect [46][47][48][53].

### 2.6 Claim of Lien Upon Funds — N.C.G.S. § 44A-18 (filing and effect)

The **Claim of Lien Upon Funds** is served on the **obligor** — the party who owes funds to the lien claimant's contracting party. For a first-tier sub, the obligor is the **owner**; for a lower-tier sub or supplier, the obligor is the **higher-tier subcontractor** [46][47][48][49][52].

**Timing:** The Lien Upon Funds is **perfected by service** (not by filing with the Clerk) and attaches to whatever amount the obligor owes the prime (or higher-tier sub) at the time of service. Service must occur **before the obligor pays the prime / higher-tier sub** for the funds to be captured. There is no fixed numeric deadline equivalent to the 120-day deadline for the Lien on Real Property — the practical deadline is "before payment is made" [46][47][48][49][52].

**Effect.** Upon receipt of a properly served Lien Upon Funds, the obligor **must withhold** the amount claimed from the next payment due to the prime (or higher-tier sub) until the lien is resolved by court order, payment to the lien claimant, or release. Disregarding the lien and paying through to the prime exposes the obligor to **direct liability** to the lien claimant for the disregarded amount, up to the value of the disregarded payment [47][48][49][52].

**Sub-tier service.** A lower-tier supplier or sub serves the Lien Upon Funds on each higher-tier obligor in the contracting chain — the immediate higher-tier sub, the prime, and the owner. The owner-served lien attaches to owner-to-prime funds; the prime-served lien attaches to prime-to-sub funds; the chain may be served simultaneously.

**Suit to enforce.** Suit to enforce the Lien Upon Funds must be commenced within **180 days of last furnishing** (the same deadline as the Lien on Real Property), in the Superior Court of the county where the obligor's funds are payable [47][48][49][62].

### 2.7 Lien priority — N.C.G.S. § 44A-10

North Carolina liens enjoy **relation back to the date of first furnishing** of labor or materials on the project as a whole — not the date of the claimant's individual first furnishing. Under N.C.G.S. § 44A-10, all properly perfected liens relate back to **the date the first labor was performed or materials furnished by anyone on the project**, which the recordation of the building permit and the Lien Agent system facilitate documenting [46][47][48][63].

**Practical consequence.** Construction liens routinely take priority over **purchase-money mortgages and deeds of trust** recorded *after* the project's first furnishing, even if the lien claimant began work months later. This is the "relation back" doctrine that distinguishes North Carolina from states (like Georgia and Florida) where lien priority dates from individual claimant furnishing or from notice of commencement.

For construction lenders, the practical consequence is severe: a deed of trust recorded after first furnishing is **junior** to all properly perfected mechanic's liens on the project. Lenders accordingly require the title insurer to issue mechanic's lien coverage and to inspect the site before recording — and the Lien Agent registration on LiensNC.com is the principal record the title insurer uses to assess pre-recording lien risk [46][47][48][50][63].

### 2.8 Lien waivers — N.C.G.S. § 44A-12.1

A lien waiver in North Carolina is governed by **N.C.G.S. § 44A-12.1**, which provides that a written waiver, release, or subordination of lien rights is **enforceable** if the writing is **signed by the lien claimant** and **identifies the specific labor, materials, services, or project** to which the waiver applies [46][47][64]. Conditional progress-payment waivers (effective upon receipt of payment) and unconditional final waivers (effective immediately) are the standard industry forms.

**Anti-waiver constraint.** § 44A-12.1(b) provides that **no advance waiver of lien rights** — i.e., a waiver executed before labor or materials are furnished — is **enforceable to the extent it purports to waive lien rights for labor or materials not yet furnished**. This anti-waiver provision was enacted to prevent contract clauses requiring subs to pre-waive lien rights as a condition of contract award. Owners and primes who attempt to extract pre-furnishing lien waivers through subcontract boilerplate are operating in violation of § 44A-12.1(b), and the pre-waiver is unenforceable [46][47][64].

### 2.9 Bond off — N.C.G.S. § 44A-16

A property owner who wishes to clear a lien from title without paying the lien claimant may **bond off** the lien under **N.C.G.S. § 44A-16** by depositing with the Clerk of Superior Court a **cash deposit or surety bond** in an amount equal to **1.25 times** the lien claim, conditioned on payment of any final judgment in favor of the lien claimant [46][47][48][62]. Upon bonding off, the lien is discharged from the real property and the bond stands as substitute security for the claim. The lien claimant's suit proceeds against the bond rather than against the property, allowing the owner to close on a sale or refinance without lien encumbrance.

### 2.10 Public works — payment bonds under N.C.G.S. § 44A-26

North Carolina is a **Little Miller Act** state. For state and local public-works projects, **N.C.G.S. § 44A-26** requires the prime contractor to furnish a **performance bond** and a **payment bond** for any public construction contract exceeding **$300,000**, both in the full amount of the contract [65][66][67]. For contracts between $50,000 and $300,000, only the payment bond is required.

Subcontractors and suppliers on bonded public projects have **no lien rights against publicly owned real property** (the state cannot be liened); their recovery vehicle is a claim on the payment bond.

**Notice and claim procedure** (N.C.G.S. § 44A-27 and § 44A-28) [65][66][67]:

- **First-tier subcontractor** in privity with the prime: may sue on the payment bond at any time before the **one-year statute of limitations** expires (running from the last day of furnishing or from the completion of the project, whichever is later).
- **Lower-tier subcontractor or supplier** (no direct privity with the prime): must serve **written notice** on the prime contractor — by certified mail, return receipt requested — **within 120 days** of last furnishing, specifying the amount claimed and the party to whom the labor or materials were furnished. Suit on the bond must be commenced within **one year** of the date of last furnishing [65][66][67][68].

The 120-day notice requirement is jurisdictional for lower-tier claimants; failure to serve timely written notice is a complete defense to the bond claim. The one-year suit limitation runs from last furnishing and is not extended by warranty visits.

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage — N.C.G.S. Chapter 22C

North Carolina enacted a comprehensive prompt-payment regime in 1989 (Session Law 1989-457), codified at **N.C.G.S. Chapter 22C — Retainage on Construction Contracts**, expanded by Session Law 1995-485 and amended periodically through the present session [69][70][71][72]. Chapter 22C governs both public and private construction projects, though the operative provisions for public projects also incorporate Chapter 143-134.1 and related public-contract statutes.

### 3.1 Private project prompt payment — N.C.G.S. § 22C-2 and § 22C-3

For private (non-public) construction contracts, the statutory regime provides [69][70][71]:

**Owner to prime contractor.** Owners must pay the prime contractor in accordance with the terms of the contract. If the contract is silent on payment timing, the owner must pay each progress payment **within seven (7) days** of the contractor's properly submitted invoice or the date the invoice is approved, whichever is later. Late payments accrue interest at the contract rate, or, if the contract does not specify, at the **legal rate** of 8% per annum under N.C.G.S. § 24-1 [69][70][71].

**Prime to subcontractor.** Under **N.C.G.S. § 22C-2**, the prime contractor must pay each subcontractor for satisfactorily completed work **within seven (7) days** of receipt of each progress payment from the owner, **less any retainage** authorized under the subcontract [69][70][71]. The seven-day clock is not extended for the prime's own internal payment-processing cycle.

Late payments by the prime to the sub accrue interest at the contract rate or, if silent, **1% per month** (12% annualized) under § 22C-5 [69][70][71].

**Pay-when-paid clauses.** North Carolina enforces **pay-when-paid** clauses (treating them as timing provisions rather than conditions precedent) but generally **does not enforce pay-if-paid** clauses against subcontractor lien and bond claims — the prime's contractual ability to defer payment does not extinguish the sub's statutory lien rights under Chapter 44A or its bond claim under § 44A-26 et seq. [70][71][72][73].

### 3.2 Public project prompt payment — N.C.G.S. § 143-134.1

For public construction contracts (state and local), **N.C.G.S. § 143-134.1** requires [74][75][76]:

**Public owner to prime.** Periodic partial payments are due to the prime within **45 days** of the prime's properly submitted invoice. Late payments accrue interest at the legal rate.

**Prime to subcontractor.** Within **seven (7) days** of receipt of each progress payment from the public owner, the prime must pay each subcontractor for satisfactorily completed work, less authorized retainage. Late payments accrue interest at 1% per month under § 22C-5 [74][75].

### 3.3 Retainage — N.C.G.S. § 143-134.1 (public works) and § 22C-1 (private works definitions)

North Carolina's retainage rules for public construction were substantially reformed by **Session Law 2007-365** and again in **Session Law 2017-130**, capping retainage at progressively more contractor-favorable levels [74][75][76][77]:

**Public projects** (N.C.G.S. § 143-134.1(b)) [74][75][76][77]:

- **Maximum retainage:** **5%** of each periodic payment for projects of $100,000 or more; **no retainage** mandated on smaller projects.
- **Reduction at 50% completion:** When the project reaches **50% completion** and the contractor's performance is satisfactory, **no further retainage may be withheld** from subsequent periodic payments — the cap effectively drops to 0% on new payments after 50% completion.
- **Release of accrued retainage at 50%:** Upon reaching 50% completion with satisfactory performance, the public owner must release **half of the retainage previously withheld** to the prime, who must in turn release the corresponding portion to subs within 7 days under § 22C-2.
- **Final retainage release:** All retainage must be released within **60 days** of substantial completion (or within 60 days of acceptance of the final pay application, whichever is later).
- **Subcontractor retainage early release.** When a subcontractor has fully completed its work but the overall project remains incomplete, the prime must release the sub's retainage upon application, subject to satisfactory completion documentation [74][75][76][77].

**Private projects.** Chapter 22C does not impose a numerical retainage cap on private construction; retainage is a matter of contract. Typical North Carolina commercial private-project retainage runs **10% until 50% completion, then 0% or 5% on subsequent payments**, but the cap is contractually established rather than statutorily mandated.

**Public projects below threshold and exemptions.** § 143-134.1 applies to public construction contracts; certain DOT contracts (N.C.G.S. § 136-28.1 et seq.) operate under parallel but somewhat differentiated retainage provisions, with reductions tied to NCDOT project-management milestones.

## 4. Tax structure

### 4.1 North Carolina state and local sales-and-use tax — the structural framework

North Carolina imposes a **state sales-and-use tax at the rate of 4.75%** under **N.C.G.S. § 105-164.4** [78][79][80]. **Local option sales taxes** under N.C.G.S. § 105-468 and related provisions add **up to 2.75%**, producing combined rates that range from **6.75%** (most counties — state 4.75% + local Article 39 2.00%) to **7.50%** (Mecklenburg County and a small number of others with full local-option layering) [78][79][80][81].

Counties' sales-tax composition reflects layered local-option authority:

- **Article 39 (1-cent local sales tax)** — uniformly 1.00% across all 100 NC counties.
- **Article 40 (½-cent local sales tax)** — 0.50% in all counties.
- **Article 42 (½-cent local sales tax)** — 0.50% in all counties.
- **Article 43 (½-cent transit tax)** — 0.50% in Mecklenburg, Wake, Durham, Orange (transit-funding local-option).
- **Article 46 (¼-cent local sales tax)** — 0.25% in counties that have approved it by referendum (approximately 45+ counties).

The functional combined rates: **6.75%** (the floor), **7.00%** (counties with Article 46), **7.25%** (transit-eligible counties), **7.50%** (Mecklenburg).

### 4.2 The 2017 sales-tax overhaul — the structural distinction every NC contractor must understand

The **single most important sales-tax issue** for North Carolina contractors is the **2017 sales-tax overhaul**, enacted by **Session Law 2016-94 and amended by Session Law 2017-204**, which fundamentally restructured how construction labor and services are taxed [82][83][84][85][86][87]. Effective **March 1, 2016** for initial provisions and **January 1, 2017** for further reforms, North Carolina divided construction work into **two functionally distinct tax categories**:

| Category | Tax treatment | Statutory basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Real Property Contract (RPC)** | Contractor is the **consumer** of materials — pays sales/use tax to the supplier at purchase; does **not** collect tax from the customer; contract price is **not** itself taxable | N.C.G.S. § 105-164.4H |
| **Repair, Maintenance, and Installation (RMI) Services** | Contractor provides a **taxable retail service** — must collect sales tax from the customer on the full charge (labor + materials + service); materials may be purchased tax-exempt for resale via Certificate of Resale Form E-595E | N.C.G.S. § 105-164.4 (definition of "service contract") and § 105-164.4H(b) |

**The distinction is functional, not formal.** What controls is the **nature of the work performed**, not the contract language. The North Carolina Department of Revenue (NCDOR) has issued extensive guidance to help contractors determine which category applies — primarily through **Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 50 (Real Property Contractors)**, **Section 51 (Repair, Maintenance, and Installation Services)**, and supplementary **Directive SD-16-1, SD-16-2, SD-17-1**, and related advisories [82][83][85][88].

### 4.3 Real Property Contract — N.C.G.S. § 105-164.4H

A **Real Property Contract** is a contract to:

- Construct a building, structure, or other improvement that becomes part of the real property,
- Make a permanent improvement, addition, or alteration to existing real property,
- Install certain capital assets that become a fixture to real property (e.g., new HVAC system in new construction, plumbing rough-in for new construction, electrical rough-in).

Under § 105-164.4H, the **contractor is the consumer of all tangible personal property** purchased for incorporation into the real property. The contractor pays **state and local sales/use tax to the supplier at the point of purchase**, just as any end consumer would, at the applicable combined rate (typically 6.75% to 7.50%) [82][83][86][87].

**The contract price itself is NOT subject to sales tax.** The contractor invoices the customer for the lump-sum contract price (or for time and materials, with a markup), and the sales tax already paid on materials is rolled into the contractor's cost; the contractor does not separately charge or collect sales tax on the labor or markup [82][83][86].

**Practical implications:**

- The contractor's material cost is **the supplier invoice price plus sales tax**; contractor markup is computed on the tax-inclusive material cost.
- The contractor **cannot use a Certificate of Resale** (Form E-595E) to purchase RPC materials tax-exempt — doing so is treated as tax evasion and exposes the contractor to use-tax assessment plus penalties.
- If a subcontractor performs an RPC scope under a tax-included subcontract, the subcontractor is treated as the consumer; if the sub bills labor and materials separately under an RMI structure, the analysis flips.

### 4.4 Repair, Maintenance, and Installation Services — N.C.G.S. § 105-164.4(a)(16)

**Repair, Maintenance, and Installation (RMI) Services** are a separately defined taxable category. RMI Services are defined to include:

- Keeping or attempting to keep tangible personal property or real property in working order to avoid breakdown or deterioration.
- Calibrating, refinishing, repainting, restoring, resurfacing, or upgrading tangible personal property or real property.
- Troubleshooting, identifying, or diagnosing service issues.
- **Installing** or applying tangible personal property other than as part of an RPC — i.e., service-call or replacement work performed on existing real property where the work does not constitute a permanent improvement.

Under § 105-164.4(a)(16), the contractor providing RMI Services is a **retailer** and must:

1. **Register as a retailer** with NCDOR and obtain a Certificate of Registration.
2. **Collect sales tax** from the customer on the **gross receipts** of the service — labor + materials + markup + any service charge.
3. **Remit collected sales tax** to NCDOR on monthly or quarterly returns (filing frequency determined by NCDOR based on volume).
4. **Purchase materials for resale under Certificate of Resale (Form E-595E)** — RMI materials are not subject to tax at purchase because the tax will be collected on the resale through the service.

**The RMI categorization captures a huge swath of construction and trade activity that, pre-2017, was treated as nontaxable labor.** The most common RMI work includes:

- Service-call repair of existing plumbing, electrical, HVAC, or refrigeration systems.
- Replacement of fixtures, appliances, or equipment in existing buildings.
- Maintenance contracts and warranty service on installed systems.
- Restoration, cleaning, or refurbishment of existing property.
- Repaint, refinish, recoat work on existing surfaces.

A plumbing contractor that does both new-construction rough-in (RPC) and service-call repair (RMI) on existing residential plumbing **must operate two parallel tax accounting tracks**: pay tax on RPC materials at purchase; collect tax on RMI charges from the customer. The two cannot be commingled.

### 4.5 The mixed-contract problem — N.C.G.S. § 105-164.4H(d)

Many construction projects involve **both** RPC and RMI components. The 2017 reforms addressed mixed contracts through **N.C.G.S. § 105-164.4H(d)**, which provides that:

- If a contract is **primarily** for the performance of an RPC (more than 25% RPC by total cost), the entire contract is treated as an RPC, and the RMI components are absorbed into the RPC tax treatment (contractor pays tax on materials at purchase; does not collect on the contract).
- If a contract is **primarily** for RMI Services (more than 25% RMI by total cost, with RPC components ancillary), the entire contract is treated as RMI; contractor collects tax on gross receipts; materials are purchased exempt for resale.
- For contracts where the categorization is unclear, the contractor may apply for an **NCDOR private letter ruling** prior to performance, or default to the more conservative treatment (RMI).

**The 25% test is not bright-line but reflects NCDOR's pragmatic approach** in the Sales and Use Tax Bulletin and in audit practice. Contracts that hover near the 25% line generate audit risk and should be documented carefully with separately-identified RPC and RMI scopes.

### 4.6 Sales-tax registration and reporting

Every North Carolina contractor must:

- **Register with NCDOR** if performing RMI Services — obtain a Certificate of Registration through NC Department of Revenue's online portal.
- **File monthly or quarterly sales/use tax returns** (Form E-500) — filing frequency assigned by NCDOR based on annual tax liability.
- **Maintain records** of RPC vs. RMI categorization on a contract-by-contract basis, with sufficient documentation to support the categorization on audit.

Even contractors who perform only RPC work are subject to **use tax** on out-of-state material purchases on which no NC sales tax was collected at the source, payable on Form E-500 [78][82][83].

### 4.7 Corporate income and franchise taxes

**Corporate income tax — N.C.G.S. § 105-130.3:**

- **Rate (TY 2025):** **2.5%** flat — North Carolina has the **lowest corporate income tax rate** of any state that imposes one, following a multi-year phased reduction from 6.9% (2013) [89][90][91][92].
- **Phased elimination:** Under Session Law 2021-180, the corporate income tax is scheduled to **phase out entirely by 2030** — declining to 2.25% in 2025, 2.0% in 2026, 0.0% in 2030 [90][91].
- **Applies to:** Domestic and foreign corporations doing business in NC.

**Franchise tax — N.C.G.S. § 105-122:**

- Imposed on corporations doing business in NC at **$1.50 per $1,000 of taxable base**, with minimum $200 / maximum varying by base type [89][90][92].
- The tax base is the greatest of (a) net worth apportioned to NC, (b) investment in NC tangible property, or (c) NC appraised real property value.
- Recent legislation has reduced the franchise tax burden incrementally; further reductions are pending in the 2025–2026 session.

**Personal income tax — N.C.G.S. § 105-153.7:**

- **Rate (TY 2025):** **4.5%** flat for individuals (including pass-through partners, sole proprietors, and S-corp owners), declining to 3.99% by 2027 under SL 2021-180 [89][90][91].

### 4.8 Withholding for nonresident contractors

Under **N.C.G.S. § 105-163.1A**, payments by a North Carolina person to a nonresident contractor for services performed in North Carolina are subject to **4% withholding** if the total compensation paid by the payer to the nonresident contractor in a calendar year exceeds **$1,500**. The withholding is remitted to NCDOR and credited against the nonresident's NC income tax liability [89][93]. The withholding does not apply to W-2 employees of the nonresident contractor — only to compensation paid to the contractor entity itself.

## 5. Workers' compensation and labor (additional items)

### 5.1 Prevailing wage — none at state level

North Carolina is a **right-to-work state** (N.C.G.S. Chapter 95 Article 10) and **does not impose state prevailing-wage requirements** on public construction. Federal Davis-Bacon Act prevailing-wage requirements apply to federally funded projects (HUD, FHWA, federal building construction) operating on NC soil, but no state-level "little Davis-Bacon" overlay exists. This is a significant structural distinction from Illinois (820 ILCS 130), California, and New York, which all impose state prevailing-wage on state-funded construction.

### 5.2 NC Wage and Hour Act — N.C.G.S. § 95-25.1 et seq.

The **NC Wage and Hour Act** governs wage payment timing and minimum wage. Key construction-relevant features:

- Wages must be paid on the **regular payday** at intervals not less frequent than monthly (most NC employers pay bi-weekly or semi-monthly).
- Final wages must be paid on or before the **next regular payday** after termination.
- NC minimum wage tracks the federal minimum wage of $7.25/hour; no NC-specific construction minimum wage.
- The North Carolina Department of Labor (NCDOL) Wage and Hour Bureau enforces the Act.

### 5.3 Worker classification — N.C.G.S. § 143-762 (NC Employee Fair Classification Act)

North Carolina enacted the **Employee Fair Classification Act** in 2017 (Session Law 2017-203), codified at **N.C.G.S. § 143-762 et seq.**, creating an **Employee Classification Section** within the NC Industrial Commission to investigate misclassification of employees as independent contractors [94][95].

Unlike Illinois's strict ABC test, North Carolina applies the traditional **multi-factor right-to-control test** for worker classification, drawing from the IRS's 20-factor analysis and common-law agency principles. The Employee Classification Section investigates complaints, refers cases to the Industrial Commission for workers' compensation enforcement and to the Department of Revenue for tax compliance, and may levy civil penalties.

**Penalties** (§ 143-769) [94][95]:

- Civil penalty of up to **$1,000 per misclassified employee** for first offenses.
- Up to **$2,000 per employee** for subsequent or willful offenses.
- Cross-referral to NCIC for unpaid WC premiums and to NCDOR for unpaid withholding.

### 5.4 E-Verify — N.C.G.S. § 64-26

North Carolina mandates **E-Verify** use under **N.C.G.S. § 64-26** for:

- **Public employers** of all sizes.
- **Private employers** with **25 or more employees** in the state.

Construction employers meeting the 25-employee threshold must register with the federal E-Verify system within 3 business days of hire and verify the employment eligibility of every new hire. The NC Department of Labor enforces E-Verify compliance; violations carry civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation for repeat offenders [94][96].

## 6. Recent changes (2024–2026)

### 6.1 Session Law 2024-49 — Lien Agent fee adjustments (effective July 1, 2024)

**Session Law 2024-49**, signed by Governor Roy Cooper on June 28, 2024, amended N.C.G.S. § 44A-11.1 to adjust the Lien Agent registration fee structure on LiensNC.com, scaling the fee with project value and easing the lien-agent search fee for sub-tier claimants [50][55][97][98]. The amendment preserves the substantive Lien Agent framework (designation before first furnishing or building permit; 15-day Notice to Lien Agent obligation for subs and suppliers) but adjusts the fee economics to reflect inflation and the actual administrative cost of the LiensNC platform.

### 6.2 Session Law 2023-134 — NC Industrial Commission rule updates (effective January 1, 2024)

**Session Law 2023-134**, the 2023 Appropriations Act, included provisions amending Chapter 97 procedures, including updates to NCIC's electronic filing systems and adjustments to the maximum weekly benefit (which is annually indexed to NC average weekly wage). The maximum weekly indemnity benefit for accidents occurring in 2026 is approximately $1,326, indexed under § 97-29(c) [41][42].

### 6.3 Session Law 2023-136 — Building code update (effective January 1, 2024)

**Session Law 2023-136** directed the North Carolina Building Code Council to update the NC State Building Code to incorporate the **2018 International Building Code** with NC Amendments, effective January 1, 2024 [33][99]. The 2024 NC State Building Code remains in force for projects permitted on or after January 1, 2024, and applies to commercial new construction, renovations, and many remodel projects.

The NC State Building Code: Electrical was simultaneously updated to adopt the **2020 National Electrical Code** with NC Amendments — meaning that NCBEEC qualifying examinations administered after January 1, 2024 reference the 2020 NEC [30][32][33].

### 6.4 Session Law 2023-108 — Sales tax adjustments on installation services (effective October 1, 2023)

**Session Law 2023-108**, the 2023 NC Budget Bill, included sales-tax clarifications on RMI Services and the boundary with RPC contracts. The amendments tightened the definition of "service contract" and clarified that delivery and installation of standalone equipment that does **not** become a fixture to real property — e.g., portable equipment, plug-in appliances, certain pre-fabricated modular units — is taxable as a retail sale of tangible personal property, not as an RPC [82][83][88]. The amendment closed a small but contested category of mixed equipment-and-installation transactions.

### 6.5 Bills pending in the 2025–2026 session

The NC General Assembly's 2025–2026 session (in progress as of this audit) has introduced several construction-relevant bills under review:

- **HB 220 (2025)** — Proposed amendments to N.C.G.S. § 44A regarding electronic filing of Claims of Lien on Real Property with the Clerks of Superior Court, aimed at modernizing the lien-recording infrastructure. Pending in committee.
- **SB 145 (2025)** — Proposed amendments to N.C.G.S. § 87-10 regarding NCLBGC financial-responsibility documentation, exploring alternative qualification routes for small contractors. Pending.
- **HB 312 (2025)** — Proposed amendments to N.C.G.S. § 22C regarding prompt payment, expanding the prime's pay-down obligation to include faster turnaround on retainage release for substantially completed subcontract scopes. Pending.

None of these bills had been enacted as of the audit date.

### 6.6 Discrepancy resolutions

Several persistent secondary-source errors should be flagged:

- Some third-party "NC contractor licensing guides" continue to describe the NCLBGC license as **classification by experience years**. The NCLBGC classification is by **monetary cap** (Limited / Intermediate / Unlimited) plus **scope** (Building / Residential / Highway / Public Utilities / Specialty) — not by experience years per se [22][24][25].
- Some commentary continues to report a **90-day lien filing deadline**. The deadline is **120 days** under N.C.G.S. § 44A-12; the 90-day timing in older commentary likely confuses the lien-filing deadline with the pre-2013 90-day Notice of Subcontract deadline (which was replaced by the Lien Agent / 15-day Notice to Lien Agent system in 2013) [46][47][50][55].
- Some references continue to describe NC as a **"trust fund state"** for construction payments. **North Carolina has no construction trust fund statute** analogous to Texas Chapter 162 or New York Lien Law Article 3-A. Civil recovery for misapplied payments proceeds under common-law fraud, the lien statute, the bond statute, and contract theories.
- Some plumbing-licensing summaries cite a "Master Plumber" NC state license. North Carolina does **not** issue a "Master Plumber" — the NCSBPHFSC issues classification-based licenses (P-I, P-II) under § 87-21; the "Master Plumber" terminology is industry shorthand and is not a separate state credential.
- Some commentary treats RMI Services and RPC contracts as the **same** for sales-tax purposes. **They are not.** The 2017 reforms (SL 2016-94, SL 2017-204) created a structural distinction that controls every NC contractor's sales-tax accounting [82][83][86][87].

## 7. Practical compliance checklist for a North Carolina contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**

- **Confirm the project's value triggers the NCLBGC license requirement** ($30,000 threshold under § 87-1(a)). For any project at or above $30,000, the GC must hold the appropriate classification (Limited / Intermediate / Unlimited) and scope (Building / Residential / Highway / Public Utilities / Specialty).
- **Confirm the GC's classification cap accommodates the project value.** A Limited GC ($750,000 cap) cannot lawfully undertake a $900,000 project; an Intermediate GC ($1,500,000 cap) cannot lawfully undertake a $1.8M project. Operating outside classification voids the contract for the entire project, not the over-cap portion.
- **Confirm the state trade licenses** for any self-performed or subcontracted plumbing, electrical, HVAC, fire sprinkler, or refrigeration scope:
  - Electrical — NCBEEC license (Limited / Intermediate / Unlimited with EB or specialty subclass); $300,000–$500,000 GL.
  - Plumbing — NCSBPHFSC license (P-I or P-II); $300,000 GL.
  - HVAC — NCSBPHFSC license (H-1, H-2, H-3); $300,000 GL.
  - Fire Sprinkler — NCSBPHFSC license (SP-FLS); $300,000 GL.
  - Refrigeration — NC Board of Refrigeration Examiners license; $300,000 GL.
- **Verify Qualifier status.** Entity-held licenses must have a current designated Qualifier; if the Qualifier separated within the last 90 days and no successor has been designated, the license is at risk of suspension.
- **Confirm workers' compensation coverage** if the GC has 3 or more employees (statutorily required under § 97-2). Even GCs with fewer than 3 employees should obtain Certificates of Insurance from every sub before site access (NCIC statutory-employer exposure under § 97-19).
- **Verify E-Verify enrollment** if the contractor has 25 or more employees in NC (§ 64-26).
- **Determine the sales-tax categorization of the project — RPC or RMI?** New construction and permanent improvements are RPC (contractor pays tax on materials at purchase). Service-call repair, maintenance, and replacement on existing real property are RMI (contractor collects tax on gross receipts). Mixed contracts default to the dominant category under the 25% test.

**At project commencement — Lien Agent designation:**

- **For projects of $30,000 or more (with the owner-occupied-existing-single-family exemption)**, the **owner must designate a Lien Agent** on LiensNC.com **before the earlier of** (a) first furnishing or (b) building permit issuance.
- **Lien Agent information must be posted on the building permit at the job site** under § 44A-11.1(c).
- **The owner pays a registration fee** on LiensNC.com scaled by project value.

**Within 15 days of first furnishing (every sub and supplier, every project):**

- **Serve the Notice to Lien Agent through LiensNC.com.** The 15-day clock runs from the date the subcontractor or supplier **first** delivered materials to the project or first performed labor on site — **not** from the subcontract execution date and **not** from the first invoice date.
- **The Notice to Lien Agent is served electronically** through the LiensNC.com platform, which automatically forwards the notice to the designated Lien Agent and creates a date-stamped record.
- **Missing the 15-day deadline forfeits lien rights for all labor and materials furnished before the eventual service date.** Lien rights for post-service labor and materials are preserved, but the pre-service window is unrecoverable. This is the single most-missed deadline in NC lien practice.

**During the work — payment cycle:**

- **Owner pays prime within 7 days** of properly submitted invoice on private projects under § 22C-2 default (or contract terms if specified).
- **Public owner pays prime within 45 days** under § 143-134.1.
- **Prime pays subs within 7 days** of receipt of owner payment, less retainage, under § 22C-2.
- **Retainage on public projects capped at 5%** with mandatory release of half at 50% completion under § 143-134.1(b); private retainage is contractual.
- **Late-payment interest at 1%/month** on prime-to-sub late payments under § 22C-5.

**Lien filing (against real property):**

- **Within 120 days** of last day of furnishing labor or materials, file the **Claim of Lien on Real Property** with the **Clerk of Superior Court** of the county where the property is located (§ 44A-12). This is the substantive deadline.
- **Serve a copy on the owner within 3 days of filing** (§ 44A-11).
- **No tolling.** Warranty work, punch-list visits, and de minimis repairs do **not** extend the 120-day clock; only new, distinct work under the original contract restarts it.

**Claim of Lien Upon Funds (sub-tier remedy):**

- **Serve the Claim of Lien Upon Funds** on the obligor (the owner for first-tier subs; the next higher-tier sub for lower-tier claimants) **before the obligor pays the prime / higher tier**.
- The Lien Upon Funds is **perfected by service**, not by filing with the Clerk.
- Sub-tier claimants typically serve **both** a Lien Upon Funds (on the obligor) and a Claim of Lien on Real Property (via subrogation under § 44A-23).

**Suit to enforce:**

- **Within 180 days** of last day of furnishing, commence suit to enforce the lien (§ 44A-13). The 180-day deadline is jurisdictional and not subject to equitable tolling.
- **File a Notice of Lis Pendens** with the Clerk of Superior Court to preserve the lien as against subsequent purchasers and encumbrancers.

**Public works:**

- **Bonding threshold:** Performance and payment bonds required at $300,000 contract value; payment bond only at $50,000–$300,000 (§ 44A-26).
- **Lower-tier 120-day notice:** A lower-tier subcontractor must serve written notice on the prime within 120 days of last furnishing to preserve bond rights (§ 44A-27).
- **Suit on bond within 1 year** of last furnishing (§ 44A-28).

**Sales-and-use tax compliance (ongoing):**

- **Real Property Contract (RPC) work:** Pay sales tax to suppliers at purchase. Materials cost is tax-inclusive; cannot use Certificate of Resale.
- **Repair, Maintenance, and Installation (RMI) Services:** Register with NCDOR; collect sales tax from customer on gross receipts (labor + materials + markup); purchase materials for resale exempt under Form E-595E.
- **Mixed contracts:** Apply the 25% test or seek NCDOR private letter ruling.
- **File NCDOR Form E-500 monthly or quarterly** depending on volume.
- **Use tax** owed on out-of-state materials brought into NC on which no NC sales tax was collected at source.

**Annual / biennial compliance:**

- **NCLBGC license renewal** — annually, expiring December 31; renewal opens November.
- **NCBEEC electrical license renewal** — annually, expiring June 30; **8 hours CE** for Unlimited/Intermediate, 4 hours for Limited.
- **NCSBPHFSC plumbing/HVAC/fire sprinkler license renewal** — annually, expiring December 31; **8 hours CE** for unrestricted classifications.
- **NCDOR sales-and-use tax returns** — monthly or quarterly per assigned filing frequency.
- **NCDOR corporate income and franchise tax returns** — annually.
- **NCIC workers' compensation policy renewal** — annually.
- **E-Verify monitoring** — ongoing for any new hires (employers with 25+ employees).

**Worker classification audit (every project, every sub):**

- **Verify each on-site worker** is either a W-2 employee of a properly-registered subcontractor entity carrying current workers' compensation, or qualifies under the multi-factor right-to-control test for independent-contractor status.
- **Obtain Certificate of Insurance** from every sub before site access, naming the GC as certificate holder; verify currency quarterly through NCIC's online Coverage Verification system.
- **Document sole-proprietor subs** explicitly: if a sub has zero employees and operates as a sole proprietor without WC, document the sub's exempt status; if the sub adds any helper or day laborer, require immediate WC coverage.

## 8. Key gotchas — what a casual North Carolina contractor most often misses

1. **The 15-day Notice to Lien Agent is the single most-missed deadline in NC lien practice.** Every sub and supplier on a $30,000+ project must serve through LiensNC.com within 15 calendar days of **first furnishing** — not the contract date, not the first invoice. Miss this and lien rights for all labor and materials furnished before the eventual notice are gone.

2. **The $30,000 GC license threshold is computed on the total cost of the undertaking, not the prime contract amount.** A GC who treats a $40,000 job as two $20,000 contracts to avoid licensure is operating in violation of § 87-1(a) and exposes the entire transaction to contract unenforceability under *Brady v. Fulghum*.

3. **NCLBGC monetary caps are project-by-project, not annual.** A Limited GC ($750,000 cap) cannot lawfully undertake a $900,000 project even if its annual volume is below $1M.

4. **The 2017 RPC vs. RMI distinction controls sales-tax compliance.** Service-call work, maintenance, and replacement on existing buildings are RMI (collect tax from customer on full gross receipts); new construction and permanent improvements are RPC (pay tax on materials at purchase, do not collect from customer). A contractor that does both must maintain parallel tax tracks.

5. **Trade licenses are separate boards and separate renewals.** A GC who self-performs electrical, plumbing, HVAC, fire sprinkler, or refrigeration work must hold the appropriate trade license **in addition to** the NCLBGC license. There is no general-contractor exemption from trade licensing.

6. **The Qualifier rule has a 90-day grace period.** If the qualifying employee leaves and no successor is designated within 90 days, the license is suspended — and any contracts entered into during the gap are exposed to unenforceability.

7. **Pre-furnishing lien waivers are unenforceable under § 44A-12.1(b).** Subcontract boilerplate purporting to waive lien rights for unperformed work is invalid; subs should not be deterred from preserving lien rights by aggressive prime-contractor pay-app waiver demands.

8. **Lien priority relates back to project first furnishing, not individual claimant first furnishing.** A lender's deed of trust recorded after first labor or materials are on site is junior to all properly perfected mechanic's liens, even those filed by subs who started work months later.

9. **NC's 3-employee WC threshold doesn't exempt the GC from § 97-19 statutory-employer liability.** A GC with fewer than 3 employees is still liable for the sub's employees' WC claims if the sub is uninsured and the cumulative project-side headcount reaches 3.

10. **120 days for lien filing, 180 days for suit, 7-day prime-to-sub payment under § 22C-2.** Three different deadlines, three different consequences. Calendar each independently from the date of last furnishing or payment receipt.
