## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Maryland structure — no statewide General Contractor license, statewide Home Improvement Contractor license, statewide trade licenses for electrical/plumbing/HVAC, and a municipal commercial layer

Maryland occupies a **distinctive middle ground** among Mid-Atlantic construction states in maintaining a regulatory architecture that combines (i) **no statewide General Contractor license** for commercial work, (ii) **mandatory statewide licensing** of every contractor performing residential home improvements under the Maryland Home Improvement Commission, (iii) **statewide trade licensing** by occupation-specific boards for electrical, plumbing, and HVAC contractors under the Maryland Department of Labor's Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing (DOPL), and (iv) a **patchwork of county and municipal commercial contractor licenses** administered by local permitting agencies in Baltimore City, Baltimore County, Montgomery County, Prince George's County, and several other jurisdictions [1][2][3][4][5][6][7]. Unlike New Jersey (which has no statewide GC and a registration-only HIC regime), Virginia (which licenses both residential and commercial GCs through the DPOR Board for Contractors), or North Carolina (which licenses GCs above $40,000 through the NCLBGC), Maryland occupies a posture closer to New Jersey for general contracting but with a fully developed licensure regime (with examinations, bonds, and a guaranty fund) for residential home improvement work.

The single most important consumer-protection statute is the **Maryland Home Improvement Law**, codified at **Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. §§ 8-101 through 8-820** and originally enacted as the Home Improvement Act of 1962 (Ch. 133, Laws of Maryland 1962), now substantially amended through successive General Assembly sessions and implemented at **COMAR 09.08.01 through 09.08.03** [1][2][8][9][10][11][12][13]. The Home Improvement Law is administered by the **Maryland Home Improvement Commission ("MHIC")**, an independent commission within the Maryland Department of Labor's Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing. The MHIC issues both **Home Improvement Contractor ("HIC") licenses** to businesses and **Home Improvement Salesperson licenses** to the natural persons who solicit, sell, or negotiate home improvement contracts — a two-tier licensure structure unique among Mid-Atlantic states.

For ContractorPro's five trades, the practical Maryland map is:

The takeaway: Maryland **does** license electrical, plumbing, and HVACR contractors statewide and **does** license residential home improvement contractors through the MHIC — a fully developed licensure regime with examination, surety bond, financial-solvency review, and the unique **MHIC Guaranty Fund** consumer-recovery mechanism (see § 1.9 below). Commercial general contractors, however, face **no statewide license requirement**; commercial work is regulated through the patchwork of municipal contractor-licensing offices and the building-permit / building-code framework under the **Maryland Building Performance Standards** adopted by the Maryland Department of Labor at COMAR 05.02.07.

### 1.2 Maryland Home Improvement Commission (MHIC) — Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. § 8-101 et seq.

The **Maryland Home Improvement Law** was originally enacted as the Home Improvement Act in 1962 and has been substantially amended through successive General Assembly sessions; its current codification at **Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. §§ 8-101 through 8-820** consolidates the licensing, contract-content, salesperson, guaranty fund, and disciplinary provisions into a single, comprehensive statutory scheme [1][2][8][9][10][11][12][13][14][15][16]. The Maryland Home Improvement Commission is composed of nine members appointed by the Governor — five public members, two contractor members, and two members representing the home improvement industry — and serves at the Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing within the Maryland Department of Labor (labor.maryland.gov/license/mhic).

#### 1.2.1 Who must be licensed — Bus. Reg. § 8-301

Under **Bus. Reg. § 8-301**, no person may act or attempt to act as a "home improvement contractor" or "subcontractor," or offer to perform, contract for, or perform any home improvement work in Maryland, unless licensed by the MHIC [1][2][8][9][10][11][12][13][14]. The statutory term **"home improvement"** is defined at Bus. Reg. § 8-101(g) to mean:

> The addition to or alteration, conversion, improvement, modernization, remodeling, repair, or replacement of a building or part of a building that is used or designed to be used as a residence or dwelling place or a structure adjacent to that building; or an improvement to land adjacent to the building.

The reach is intentionally broad and includes (without limitation) the construction, installation, replacement, or improvement of:

- Driveways, sidewalks, swimming pools, terraces, patios, landscaping, fences, porches, garages, basements, roofs, siding, insulation, and exterior coatings.
- Heating, ventilating, air conditioning, electrical, plumbing, and sewer connection systems within a residence (regardless of whether the work is also separately licensed under one of the trade boards).
- Solar energy systems, fire and burglar alarm systems, and other consumer-grade systems installed at residential property.
- Awnings, screens, storm windows, and storm doors.
- Painting, papering, decorating, and other interior or exterior finishing.

**A roofer, a deck builder, a kitchen remodeler, a basement finisher, a fence installer, a window replacement company, a siding contractor, a landscape installer, a paving contractor, a pool installer, a solar installer, and a general home repair handyman are all required to be MHIC-licensed if they perform residential home improvement work**. Trade-licensed electrical, plumbing, and HVACR contractors are **also required to hold an MHIC license** when performing residential home improvement work — the trade license under Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 6-101, § 12-101, or Bus. Reg. § 9A-101 does **not** substitute for MHIC licensure.

**Statutory exemptions — Bus. Reg. § 8-302.** The licensure requirement does **not** apply to [1][2][8][9][10][11][12]:

1. **New home construction** — building a new home from foundation up is **not** "home improvement"; new home builders register separately under the Maryland Home Builder Registration program (see § 1.7 below).
2. **Aggregate annual contracts under $500.** A person performing home improvement work valued at less than $500 in the aggregate per year is exempt from MHIC licensure. (This is a low de minimis floor — exceeding $500 in any 12-month period triggers the licensure requirement.)
3. **Owners performing work on their own property** (i.e., homeowners doing their own work).
4. **Licensed real estate brokers** acting in the ordinary course of brokerage activity.
5. **Architects, engineers, landscape architects, and home inspectors** acting only within their licensed scope.
6. **Public utility companies** for work performed pursuant to utility-service obligations.
7. **Persons engaged solely in the sale of goods or appliances** that are not installed by the seller.
8. **Volunteer charitable organizations** performing free home repair work for low-income residents.

The exemption list is narrowly construed; in particular, a tradesperson holding only an electrical, plumbing, or HVACR license performing residential work outside the strictly defined scope of that trade — for example, a master plumber who also paints, drywalls, or repairs the bathroom enclosure while installing fixtures — must hold an MHIC contractor license for the broader home improvement scope.

#### 1.2.2 The two-tier MHIC structure — Contractor and Salesperson

A unique feature of the Maryland home improvement regulatory regime is the **two-tier licensure structure** — the **Home Improvement Contractor ("HIC") license** for businesses and the **Home Improvement Salesperson license** for natural persons who solicit, sell, or negotiate home improvement contracts on behalf of a contractor [1][2][8][9][10][11][13][14][15]:

1. **Home Improvement Contractor license** — Bus. Reg. § 8-301. The contractor license is the business-level credential authorizing a person or business entity to engage in, contract for, or offer to perform home improvement work. The contractor license is issued to the licensee (sole proprietor, corporation, LLC, or partnership) and not to individual employees of the licensee.

2. **Home Improvement Salesperson license** — Bus. Reg. § 8-305. Every individual who **solicits, sells, or negotiates** a home improvement contract on behalf of a licensed contractor must hold a separate salesperson license. The salesperson license is issued to the natural person and is **transferable** between MHIC-licensed contractor sponsors. A salesperson must work under the sponsorship of a licensed contractor and the salesperson license lapses upon termination of the sponsoring relationship unless promptly transferred to a new sponsor.

3. **Subcontractor license** — Bus. Reg. § 8-301. A separate **subcontractor license** is required for a person who performs home improvement work under contract with a licensed home improvement contractor (rather than directly with the homeowner). The subcontractor license has the same basic requirements as the contractor license but with reduced application materials in some respects. In practice, most home improvement firms that perform subcontract work for general home improvement contractors hold both contractor and subcontractor credentials to give themselves flexibility in contracting.

The two-tier (and effectively three-tier, with subcontractor) credentialing creates a more granular regulatory structure than most state HIC regimes. The mandatory salesperson licensure is the practical compliance trap for many out-of-state HIC firms entering Maryland: an unlicensed salesperson on a Maryland home improvement contract is a per se violation of the Home Improvement Law and exposes the contractor to MHIC disciplinary action, refund obligations, and Guaranty Fund consumer-recovery exposure.

#### 1.2.3 Contractor license requirements — Bus. Reg. § 8-303 and COMAR 09.08.01

Application for the MHIC Home Improvement Contractor license requires [1][2][8][9][10][11][13][14][15][16][17]:

1. **Application fee** — currently $250 for the initial application (subject to administrative adjustment by the MHIC); the application fee covers the cost of processing and background review.
2. **Biennial license fee** — currently $370 for a two-year license term (subject to administrative adjustment); the license is renewable every two years on the anniversary of issuance.
3. **MHIC Guaranty Fund assessment** — currently $100 (initial) and $50 (each biennial renewal) per licensed contractor, paid into the **Home Improvement Guaranty Fund** under Bus. Reg. § 8-405 (see § 1.9 below).
4. **Pre-licensing examination** — the **Maryland Home Improvement Contractor examination**, administered by **PSI Services LLC** under contract with the MHIC. The examination is a **50-question closed-book multiple-choice test** covering Maryland home improvement law, contract requirements, construction trade fundamentals, lien law basics, business law, and safety. A passing score of **70%** is required. The MHIC requires that the natural person identified on the application as the contractor's "qualifying individual" — sole proprietor, corporate officer, LLC manager/member, or designated employee — pass the examination [13][14][15][16].
5. **Financial solvency review** — the applicant must demonstrate financial solvency by submitting a recent **personal credit report** and (for entities) **business financial statements**. Applicants with bankruptcy filings, civil judgments, or significant unpaid debt within the prior seven years are subject to enhanced scrutiny and may be required to post additional surety or be placed on probation.
6. **Criminal background disclosure** — the qualifying individual and any other person with a 10% or greater ownership interest must disclose any criminal conviction (other than minor traffic violations) on the application. Convictions involving fraud, theft, dishonesty, or construction-related offenses give the MHIC discretion to deny licensure under Bus. Reg. § 8-311.
7. **Trade name registration** — the contractor must disclose all trade names, fictitious names, and "doing business as" names under which the business operates, and must have those names registered with the Maryland Department of Assessments and Taxation (dat.maryland.gov) under the Maryland trade name statute.
8. **Proof of liability insurance** — every MHIC contractor must maintain **commercial general liability insurance with minimum coverage of $50,000 per occurrence** (a notably low statutory minimum; market practice and owner / lender requirements are typically $1M/$2M).
9. **Surety bond** — in lieu of (or in addition to) the financial-solvency review, an applicant may post a **$20,000 surety bond** running to the State of Maryland for the benefit of consumers harmed by the contractor's violations of the Home Improvement Law. The surety bond is a **substitute for the Guaranty Fund participation in some cases**, particularly for new-entity applicants without an established Maryland financial history.
10. **Workers' compensation insurance** — proof of workers' compensation coverage (if the contractor has employees) is required at the time of application and at each renewal (see § 5 below).

The **biennial renewal cycle** requires the licensee to pay the renewal fee, the Guaranty Fund assessment, and provide updated insurance certificates. Renewal applications are reviewed for compliance with continuing-disclosure obligations including changes in ownership, business address, qualifying individual, and outstanding legal matters.

#### 1.2.4 Salesperson license requirements — Bus. Reg. § 8-305 and COMAR 09.08.02

The Home Improvement Salesperson license has substantially lower threshold requirements than the contractor license [1][2][8][9][10][11][13][14][15]:

1. **Application fee** — currently $20.
2. **Biennial license fee** — currently $90.
3. **No examination requirement** for the salesperson license (unlike the contractor license, which requires the PSI examination of the qualifying individual).
4. **Sponsorship by a licensed MHIC contractor** — the application must identify the sponsoring contractor and the contractor must sign the sponsorship form.
5. **Criminal background disclosure** — convictions involving fraud, theft, dishonesty, or construction-related offenses are reviewed under Bus. Reg. § 8-311.
6. **Age** — applicants must be at least 18 years old.

The salesperson license is **transferable**: when a salesperson terminates with one MHIC-licensed contractor and joins another, the salesperson may transfer the license to the new sponsor without re-applying. The license is **non-transferable** between natural persons (i.e., a licensed salesperson may not "sell" or assign the license to another individual).

#### 1.2.5 Written contract — Bus. Reg. § 8-501 and COMAR 09.08.01.20

Under **Bus. Reg. § 8-501** and the implementing regulations at COMAR 09.08.01.20, every home improvement contract in Maryland must be in writing, signed by the homeowner and the contractor (or salesperson on behalf of the contractor), and must contain the following [1][2][8][9][10][11][14][16][17][18][19]:

1. **The contractor's name, address, MHIC license number, and telephone number** — clearly and conspicuously displayed.
2. **The homeowner's name, address, and telephone number.**
3. **The address or location of the property where the work will be performed.**
4. **A description of the work to be performed**, including any specifications, plans, drawings, or other documents incorporated by reference.
5. **The starting date and completion date** of the work, or an estimate of the time required for completion.
6. **The total contract price** — including the price of all labor, materials, services, parts, and other components.
7. **The schedule of payments**, including any deposit, progress payments, and final payment, with the amounts and timing of each.
8. **The terms of any financing arrangement**, including the name of the lender, the annual percentage rate, the finance charges, and the consumer's right to cancel under the Federal Truth in Lending Act.
9. **A statement of the consumer's right to cancel the contract** within **five business days** for door-to-door sales under Maryland's Door-to-Door Sales Act (Md. Code Ann., Com. Law § 14-301 et seq.) and the FTC Cooling-Off Rule.
10. **A description of any change orders** and the process for executing change orders, including the requirement that all change orders be in writing and signed by the consumer.
11. **A statement that the contractor is licensed by the Maryland Home Improvement Commission** and that the consumer may inquire with the MHIC at the contact information provided in the contract.
12. **A statement that the homeowner may be entitled to recover from the Home Improvement Guaranty Fund** (under Bus. Reg. § 8-405) up to the statutory maximum (see § 1.9 below) for actual losses caused by the contractor's violations of the Home Improvement Law.
13. **A statement of any warranties** provided by the contractor on workmanship, materials, or both — including the duration of the warranty and the procedure for warranty claims.
14. **Disclosure of subcontractors** to be used on the project and confirmation that all subcontractors are properly licensed for their respective trades.

**Maximum deposit — Bus. Reg. § 8-501(c).** The contract may **not** require a deposit of more than **one-third (33-1/3%) of the total contract price** at the time of signing. This is one of the strictest deposit-cap statutes in the United States and is the primary mechanism by which the Maryland legislature has protected homeowners from deposit-theft schemes. Contractors who collect more than one-third up front are per se in violation of the Home Improvement Law and exposed to MHIC disciplinary action, civil restitution to the consumer, and Guaranty Fund consumer-recovery claims.

**Prohibited contract terms — COMAR 09.08.01.21.** The contract may **not** contain any provision that purports to:

1. Waive the consumer's right to recover from the Guaranty Fund.
2. Waive the consumer's right to file an MHIC complaint.
3. Confer a confession of judgment.
4. Require the consumer to indemnify the contractor for the contractor's own negligence or wrongful acts.
5. Authorize the contractor to alter the contract without the consumer's signed consent.
6. Require binding arbitration of disputes in a manner that limits or precludes the consumer's right to file an MHIC complaint or pursue Guaranty Fund recovery.

#### 1.2.6 Enforcement — civil, administrative, and criminal

Maryland Home Improvement Law violations expose the contractor to a layered enforcement regime [1][2][8][9][10][11][14][20][21][22]:

**Administrative discipline — Bus. Reg. § 8-311 and § 8-312.** The MHIC may, after notice and an opportunity for a hearing under the Maryland Administrative Procedure Act, **deny, suspend, or revoke a license**, **impose a civil penalty of up to $5,000 per violation**, **order restitution to consumers**, **order the contractor to take corrective action**, and **place the contractor on probation**. Grounds for discipline include violations of the Home Improvement Law, violations of the implementing regulations, criminal convictions for fraud or dishonesty, failure to honor warranties, failure to complete contracted work, abandonment of projects, and any other conduct demonstrating untrustworthiness or incompetence.

**Civil action — Bus. Reg. § 8-501(d) and Md. Code Ann., Com. Law § 13-101 et seq. (Consumer Protection Act).** A consumer harmed by a contractor's violation of the Home Improvement Law may bring a private right of action for:

1. **Actual damages.**
2. **Attorney fees** under Md. Code Ann., Com. Law § 13-408 (Maryland Consumer Protection Act) where the violation also constitutes an unfair or deceptive trade practice.
3. **Punitive damages** in cases of fraud or malice (under common-law standards, requiring clear and convincing evidence of actual malice).
4. **Statutory restitution** of all amounts paid by the consumer where the contract is void or unenforceable due to the contractor's lack of MHIC licensure (Maryland courts have repeatedly held that contracts entered into by unlicensed home improvement contractors are unenforceable by the contractor — meaning the unlicensed contractor cannot sue for unpaid balances and may be ordered to refund all amounts already paid).

The **unlicensed-contract void doctrine** is the most important practical exposure for unlicensed work in Maryland. Under cases including **Harry Berenter, Inc. v. Berman, 258 Md. 290 (1970)** and a long line of subsequent decisions, the Maryland Court of Appeals has consistently held that a home improvement contract entered into by an unlicensed contractor is **void and unenforceable** by the contractor — the consumer may keep the work, refuse to pay, and recover any amounts already paid as restitution [20][21][22]. The unlicensed contractor has **no cause of action** against the consumer for the unpaid contract price, for the reasonable value of the work performed (quantum meruit), or for any other recovery. This rule has been applied even where the work was fully performed to specification and the homeowner was the party who initiated breach.

**Criminal penalties — Bus. Reg. § 8-601.** Acting as a home improvement contractor without an MHIC license is a **misdemeanor** punishable by:

1. **Fine of up to $1,000** for a first offense.
2. **Fine of up to $5,000 and/or imprisonment for up to 2 years** for a second or subsequent offense.

The misdemeanor exposure is reinforced by aggressive enforcement: the MHIC routinely refers unlicensed-contractor cases to the State's Attorney's offices for criminal prosecution, particularly in cases involving consumer deposit theft, abandonment of work, or fraudulent representation of licensure status.

**Cease and desist orders — Bus. Reg. § 8-313.** The MHIC may issue a cease-and-desist order directing an unlicensed contractor to stop performing home improvement work in Maryland. Violation of a cease-and-desist order is grounds for enhanced civil penalty and criminal referral.

### 1.3 Electrical contractor licensing — Md. Code Ann., Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 6-101 et seq.

Statewide electrical contractor licensing in Maryland operates under the **State Board of Master Electricians**, established by **Md. Code Ann., Bus. Occ. & Prof. §§ 6-101 through 6-501** and administered by the Maryland Department of Labor's Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing [3][4][5][23][24][25][26]. The Board oversees three tiers of electrical credentialing:

**Three-tier credential structure** under Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 6-301:

1. **Master Electrician license** — issued to a qualified individual who has met experience and examination requirements; authorizes the holder to plan, install, supervise, and contract for electrical work, and to qualify a business entity to engage in electrical contracting.
2. **Journeyperson Electrician license** — issued to a qualified individual who has met experience and examination requirements at the journeyperson level; authorizes the holder to perform electrical work under the supervision of a master electrician.
3. **Apprentice Electrician registration** — required for individuals enrolled in a registered electrical apprenticeship program working toward journeyperson qualification.

**Master Electrician requirements — Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 6-301 and COMAR 09.32.01:**

1. **Age** — at least 21 years old.
2. **Experience** — at least **seven (7) years of practical electrical experience**, including at least two years as a licensed journeyperson electrician (or equivalent qualification from another state). The experience must be in the planning, layout, installation, alteration, and repair of electrical wiring, fittings, devices, and apparatus.
3. **Examination** — pass the Master Electrician examination administered by PSI Services LLC, covering the National Electrical Code (currently the 2023 NEC as adopted by Maryland), Maryland electrical regulations, business and law, and electrical safety. The examination is a closed-book, multiple-choice test with a passing score of 70%.
4. **Maryland law portion** — a separate Maryland business and law examination portion covering the Home Improvement Law, contract requirements, lien law basics, and Maryland electrical-trade regulations.
5. **Insurance** — every Master Electrician operating as a contractor must maintain commercial general liability insurance with minimum coverage of **$300,000 per occurrence** under COMAR 09.32.01.06.
6. **License fee** — initial license $90; biennial renewal $90.
7. **Continuing education** — under COMAR 09.32.01.10, master electricians must complete **18 hours of approved continuing education per biennial renewal cycle**, including a minimum of 9 hours on the National Electrical Code and 3 hours on Maryland regulations.

**Journeyperson Electrician requirements:**

1. **Age** — at least 18 years old.
2. **Experience** — at least **four (4) years of practical electrical experience** under the supervision of a licensed master electrician, or completion of a registered apprenticeship program.
3. **Examination** — pass the Journeyperson Electrician examination administered by PSI.
4. **License fee** — initial $40; biennial renewal $40.
5. **Continuing education** — 9 hours per biennial cycle.

**Local electrical licensing — Baltimore City and Montgomery County.** Several Maryland local jurisdictions historically maintained separate electrical licensing programs predating the state Board (which was unified under the current structure in 2008 by Ch. 320, Laws of Maryland 2008). **Baltimore City**, **Montgomery County**, and **Prince George's County** maintained particularly active local electrical-licensing programs, and some of these jurisdictions continue to require local electrical permits and registration alongside the state Master Electrician license [25][26]. Practical compliance requires holding both the state license and any applicable local permit credential.

**Reciprocity.** Maryland maintains limited reciprocity agreements with several states under Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 6-308; reciprocity is verified on a state-by-state basis and typically requires equivalent experience and examination standards.

### 1.4 Plumbing licensing — Md. Code Ann., Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 12-101 et seq.

Statewide plumbing licensing operates under the **State Board of Plumbing**, established by **Md. Code Ann., Bus. Occ. & Prof. §§ 12-101 through 12-602**, administered by DOPL within the Maryland Department of Labor [3][4][5][27][28][29][30]. The Board oversees three tiers of plumbing credentialing plus a separate gas-fitter credential:

**Credential structure — Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 12-301:**

1. **Master Plumber license** — issued to a qualified individual who has met experience and examination requirements; authorizes the holder to plan, install, supervise, and contract for plumbing work, and to qualify a business entity to engage in plumbing contracting.
2. **Journeyperson Plumber license** — issued to a qualified individual who has met journeyperson-level experience and examination requirements.
3. **Apprentice Plumber registration** — required for apprentices enrolled in a registered plumbing apprenticeship program.
4. **Master Gas Fitter / Journeyperson Gas Fitter license** — separate gas-fitter credentialing for natural gas piping work in jurisdictions that maintain a separate gas-fitter classification.

**Master Plumber requirements — Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 12-301 and COMAR 09.20.01:**

1. **Age** — at least 21 years old.
2. **Experience** — at least **seven (7) years of practical plumbing experience**, including at least two years as a licensed journeyperson plumber. The experience must be in the installation, repair, alteration, replacement, and extension of plumbing systems.
3. **Examination** — pass the Master Plumber examination administered by PSI Services LLC, covering the Maryland Plumbing Code (which adopts the National Standard Plumbing Code / International Plumbing Code with Maryland amendments under COMAR 09.20.01.18), Maryland plumbing regulations, business and law, and plumbing safety. Both a written examination and (in some cases) a practical/hands-on component.
4. **Insurance** — commercial general liability insurance with minimum coverage of **$300,000 per occurrence** under COMAR 09.20.01.06.
5. **License fee** — initial license $90; biennial renewal $90.
6. **Continuing education** — under COMAR 09.20.01.10, master plumbers must complete **15 hours of approved continuing education per biennial renewal cycle**, including hours on the plumbing code, business and law, and safety.

**Journeyperson Plumber requirements:**

1. **Age** — at least 18 years old.
2. **Experience** — at least **four (4) years of practical plumbing experience** under the supervision of a licensed master plumber, or completion of a registered apprenticeship program.
3. **Examination** — pass the Journeyperson Plumber examination administered by PSI.
4. **License fee** — initial $40; biennial renewal $40.

**Plumbing inspections.** Plumbing **inspectors** — the local officials who inspect plumbing installations for code compliance — are credentialed separately under the Maryland Building Performance Standards framework at COMAR 05.02.07 and are typically employed by county or municipal permitting offices.

### 1.5 HVACR licensing — Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. § 9A-101 et seq.

The **Maryland HVACR Master Contractor license** is established by **Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. §§ 9A-101 through 9A-501** and administered by the **Maryland HVACR Board** within DOPL [3][4][5][31][32][33][34]. The HVACR licensing program was created by Ch. 332, Laws of Maryland 1999 and took effect with progressive implementation through 2003.

**Credential structure — Bus. Reg. § 9A-301:**

1. **Master HVACR Contractor license** — issued to a qualified individual; authorizes the holder to plan, install, supervise, and contract for HVACR work and to qualify a business entity.
2. **Journeyperson HVACR license** — issued to a qualified individual at the journeyperson level.
3. **Apprentice HVACR registration** — required for apprentices in a registered HVACR apprenticeship program.
4. **Limited HVACR license** — a restricted credential for specialty work such as residential HVAC service-only work or specific equipment categories.

**Master HVACR Contractor requirements — Bus. Reg. § 9A-301 and COMAR 09.15.01:**

1. **Age** — at least 18 years old.
2. **Experience** — at least **three (3) years as a licensed journeyperson HVACR technician** or equivalent qualifying experience under the supervision of a licensed master HVACR contractor.
3. **Examination** — pass the Master HVACR Contractor examination administered by PSI, covering the Maryland Mechanical Code (which adopts the International Mechanical Code with Maryland amendments under COMAR 09.15.01.18), the International Fuel Gas Code, Maryland HVACR regulations, business and law, and refrigeration safety.
4. **EPA Section 608 certification** — under federal Clean Air Act § 608 (42 U.S.C. § 7671g) and 40 CFR Part 82 Subpart F, any technician handling refrigerants must hold an EPA Section 608 certification. The Board verifies federal certification.
5. **Insurance** — commercial general liability insurance with minimum coverage of **$300,000 per occurrence** under COMAR 09.15.01.06.
6. **License fee** — initial license $112.50; biennial renewal $112.50.
7. **Continuing education** — under COMAR 09.15.01.10, master HVACR contractors must complete **6 hours of approved continuing education per biennial renewal cycle**, including hours on the mechanical code, refrigerants and safety, and business and law.

**Trade-license interfaces.** The HVACR license interacts with the electrical and plumbing trade licenses as follows:

- Electrical wiring to HVACR equipment that requires connection to the building's electrical service must be performed by a licensed master electrician — the HVACR licensee may make low-voltage control wiring connections within the equipment.
- Plumbing piping connecting to building potable water or drainage systems must be performed by a licensed master plumber — the HVACR licensee may install refrigerant piping, condensate piping (subject to plumbing interface), and process piping not connected to potable water.
- Fuel-gas piping for HVACR equipment may be installed by either an HVACR licensee or a master plumber under Maryland's joint-jurisdiction rule for gas piping under COMAR 09.20.01.

### 1.6 Commercial general contracting — no statewide license; municipal layer

Maryland does **not** require a statewide license for commercial general contracting (or new home construction outside the home-builder registration framework). Commercial construction work is regulated through:

1. **Building permits** issued by county and municipal permitting offices under the Maryland Building Performance Standards (COMAR 05.02.07), which adopt the International Building Code with Maryland amendments.
2. **Local contractor licensing programs** in Baltimore City, Baltimore County, Montgomery County, Prince George's County, Howard County, Anne Arundel County, Frederick County, and several other jurisdictions [4][5][6][7][35][36]:

**Baltimore City.** Baltimore City requires every contractor performing construction work in the city to register with the **Department of Housing and Community Development** and obtain a **Contractor's Use and Occupancy Certificate** and any required specialty trade licenses. The Baltimore City Building, Construction and Zoning Appeals Board administers local licensing for certain trades.

**Baltimore County.** Baltimore County requires contractors to register with the Department of Permits, Approvals and Inspections. Building, electrical, plumbing, and HVAC permits are issued at the trade-license-holder level.

**Montgomery County.** Montgomery County maintains one of the most active local contractor-licensing programs through the **Department of Permitting Services** at montgomerycountymd.gov/dps. The county requires contractors performing certain types of work (including general contracting on commercial projects) to register with the county and obtain trade-specific permits. Montgomery County's regulatory regime is widely viewed as among the most rigorous local construction-regulation frameworks in the state.

**Prince George's County.** Prince George's County requires contractor registration with the **Department of Permitting, Inspections and Enforcement** (DPIE).

**Howard County, Anne Arundel County, Frederick County, Harford County, and Charles County** each maintain contractor registration / permit programs through their respective permitting offices.

The practical compliance implication: a commercial general contractor performing work in multiple Maryland counties may need to maintain registration with each county's permitting office, plus the relevant trade-licensure for its mechanical, electrical, and plumbing subcontractors. Statewide unified credentialing is **not** available for commercial general contracting in Maryland.

### 1.7 New home builder registration — Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. § 4.5-101 et seq.

**New home construction** in Maryland is governed not by the MHIC's home improvement licensing regime but by a separate **Maryland Home Builder Registration Unit** within DOPL, established under the **New Home Builder Registration Act** (Ch. 583, Laws of Maryland 2001) at **Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. §§ 4.5-101 through 4.5-902** [37][38][39][40][41].

**Who must register.** Every "home builder" — defined to mean any person who undertakes to build a "new home" (a newly constructed single-family detached dwelling, townhouse, duplex, or condominium unit intended for sale to a buyer) — must register with the Home Builder Registration Unit before offering a new home for sale, entering into a contract for the construction of a new home, or commencing construction of a new home [37][38][39][40][41]. The registration is **separate from** and does **not substitute for** the MHIC home improvement contractor license.

**Registration requirements:**

1. **Application fee and biennial registration fee** — application $250; biennial registration approximately $600 (subject to administrative adjustment).
2. **Home Builder Guaranty Fund assessment** — every registered home builder contributes to the Home Builder Guaranty Fund under Bus. Reg. § 4.5-201, currently $100 (initial) and $50 (each renewal). The Guaranty Fund provides consumer recovery (up to $50,000 per claim, $200,000 lifetime per builder) for losses caused by builder's violations.
3. **Insurance** — commercial general liability insurance with minimum coverage of $50,000 per occurrence.
4. **Surety bond or net worth requirement** — applicants may post a surety bond running to the State or demonstrate adequate net worth under regulations adopted by the Unit.
5. **Trade name registration** with SDAT.
6. **Disclosure of all officers, directors, and persons with 10% or greater ownership interest.**

**The statutory new home warranty — Bus. Reg. § 10-601 et seq. (Maryland Real Property § 10-201 et seq.).** Every new home sold in Maryland is statutorily warranted under the Maryland New Home Warranty Act, codified at **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. §§ 10-201 through 10-209**. The statutory warranty includes:

- **1 year** — Defects caused by faulty workmanship and defective materials due to noncompliance with building code requirements.
- **2 years** — Defects in plumbing, electrical, heating, and air conditioning systems.
- **5 years** — Major structural defects affecting the load-bearing function of the home.

(Note: Maryland's structural warranty period is 5 years, shorter than New Jersey's 10-year statutory new home warranty.)

The interaction between MHIC and Home Builder Registration is critical for general contractors performing both new construction and remodeling work: such a contractor must hold **both** the MHIC home improvement contractor license and the Maryland Home Builder Registration. Most large Maryland builders carry both.

### 1.8 Workers' compensation — Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 9-101 et seq. — mandatory for every employer

Maryland is a **private-market workers' compensation state** under **Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. §§ 9-101 through 9-1201**, administered by the **Maryland Workers' Compensation Commission (WCC)** at wcc.state.md.us [42][43][44][45][46][47]. Coverage is purchased on the private market through admitted insurers approved by the Maryland Insurance Administration, with the **Chesapeake Employers' Insurance Company** (formerly the Injured Workers' Insurance Fund, IWIF) serving as the state-affiliated residual-market carrier under Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 10-101 et seq.

**Mandatory coverage — Lab. & Empl. § 9-202.** Every Maryland employer with **one or more employees** must maintain workers' compensation coverage. Maryland is one of the most stringent states in this regard — there is **no minimum-employee threshold**, no industry-specific exemption (other than narrow exclusions for casual labor and agricultural workers under specific conditions), and no carve-out for construction or any other industry [42][43][44][45][46][47]. The "one or more employees" rule reaches every employer — corporations, LLCs, partnerships, and sole proprietorships with even a single non-owner employee. See § 5 below for full treatment.

### 1.9 Maryland Home Improvement Guaranty Fund — Bus. Reg. § 8-401 et seq.

The most distinctive consumer-protection feature of the Maryland home improvement regulatory regime is the **Home Improvement Guaranty Fund**, established at **Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. §§ 8-401 through 8-411** and implemented at COMAR 09.08.03 [48][49][50][51][52][53][54]. The Fund operates as a **consumer-recovery pool** financed by mandatory contributions from every licensed home improvement contractor.

**Funding mechanism — Bus. Reg. § 8-405.** Every licensed MHIC contractor contributes to the Guaranty Fund as follows:

1. **Initial contribution** — $100 paid at the time of initial license application.
2. **Renewal contribution** — $50 paid at each biennial license renewal.

The Fund is held by the Maryland Department of Labor and is supplemented by recoveries from disciplined contractors, surety bond claims, and any civil judgments obtained by the State against contractors found to have violated the Home Improvement Law.

**Eligibility for Fund recovery — Bus. Reg. § 8-405 and COMAR 09.08.03.03.** A consumer may recover from the Fund for **actual losses** caused by the contractor's [48][49][50][51][52][53][54]:

1. **Willful failure to perform** the contract in accordance with its terms.
2. **Knowing misrepresentation** of material facts.
3. **Wrongful taking of money** or property from the consumer.
4. **Fraud** in performance of the contract.
5. **Substantial breach** of the contract.

The Fund does **not** pay claims for:

1. Consequential damages (e.g., lost rental income, increased living expenses).
2. Punitive damages.
3. Personal injury or property damage to anything other than the home and the work performed.
4. Attorney fees of the consumer.
5. Claims arising from work performed by an unlicensed contractor (though this exclusion is narrowly applied — the Fund will pay claims arising from work by a contractor whose license has lapsed during the work period, in many cases).

**Maximum recovery — Bus. Reg. § 8-405(e).** The Fund pays consumer claims up to **$30,000 per consumer per project** (raised from $20,000 to $30,000 by Ch. 481, Laws of Maryland 2018, effective October 1, 2018) [48][49][50][51][52][53]. There is also a **lifetime aggregate cap of $200,000 per contractor** — meaning the Fund's total exposure for any single licensed contractor across all of that contractor's projects is capped at $200,000 over the contractor's licensure history. Once the $200,000 aggregate is reached, the Fund will not pay additional claims arising from work by that contractor, and consumers are limited to direct civil action against the contractor and any applicable surety bond.

**Claim procedure — COMAR 09.08.03.03.** A consumer files a claim with the MHIC, which serves as both adjudicator of the underlying complaint and administrator of Fund payments. The procedure is:

1. Consumer files an MHIC complaint with supporting documentation.
2. MHIC investigates, attempts informal resolution, and refers the matter to a hearing if not resolved.
3. The Office of Administrative Hearings (OAH) conducts a hearing under the Maryland Administrative Procedure Act.
4. The Administrative Law Judge issues findings of fact, conclusions of law, and a proposed decision.
5. The MHIC reviews and adopts (or modifies) the proposed decision.
6. If the consumer's claim is sustained, the Fund pays the consumer's actual losses up to the $30,000 / $200,000 caps.
7. The MHIC then pursues recovery against the contractor (administrative discipline, civil action, criminal referral, surety bond claim).

**Subrogation — Bus. Reg. § 8-407.** Upon payment from the Fund, the MHIC is **subrogated to the consumer's rights** against the contractor and may pursue civil recovery of the amount paid. **Contractors against whom the Fund has paid a consumer claim are subject to immediate license suspension under Bus. Reg. § 8-408 until they fully reimburse the Fund** — including any interest and administrative costs. License reinstatement requires full Fund reimbursement plus any additional disciplinary requirements imposed by the MHIC.

The Fund operates as the practical enforcement backstop of the Home Improvement Law: even where a homeowner cannot recover from a judgment-proof contractor, the Fund provides meaningful consumer recovery, and the subrogation-and-suspension mechanism creates strong incentives for contractors to maintain compliance and to honor their contracts and warranties. The $200,000 aggregate cap is a meaningful structural constraint and is the primary reason high-volume Maryland home improvement contractors invest heavily in pre-licensure financial review, complaint-response infrastructure, and warranty performance — the Fund's exposure cap functions as a soft "license at risk" trigger for contractors who accumulate complaints.

### 1.10 General-liability insurance — Maryland statewide minimums

Maryland does not impose a uniform statewide GL minimum, but the various licensing regimes set distinct minimums:

| Source | Minimum GL |
|---|---|
| MHIC Home Improvement Contractor license | **$50,000 per occurrence** (statutory floor — market practice is far higher) [13][14] |
| Master Electrician (operating as contractor) | **$300,000 per occurrence** under COMAR 09.32.01.06 [24][25] |
| Master Plumber (operating as contractor) | **$300,000 per occurrence** under COMAR 09.20.01.06 [28][29] |
| Master HVACR Contractor | **$300,000 per occurrence** under COMAR 09.15.01.06 [32][33] |

Owners, lenders, and bonding companies routinely require commercial GL well above these statutory floors — $1M/$2M is the typical commercial floor on private work; $2M/$4M on larger commercial and institutional jobs; $5M to $10M aggregate on bonded public works.

## 2. Mechanics' Lien Law — Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-101 et seq.

Maryland mechanics' lien law is at **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. §§ 9-101 through 9-114** and implemented through the Maryland Rules of Procedure at **Md. Rules 12-301 through 12-308** [55][56][57][58][59][60][61][62][63][64][65]. The Maryland Mechanics' Lien Law was enacted in its current form by Ch. 349, Laws of Maryland 1976 (effective July 1, 1976), substantially reforming the historical Maryland mechanics' lien framework that had been in place since 1838. The 1976 enactment was itself a response to the U.S. Supreme Court's decision in **Connecticut v. Doehr, 501 U.S. 1 (1991)** and earlier due-process challenges to summary mechanics' lien procedures that allowed lien attachment without judicial review.

### 2.1 The defining feature — petition to circuit court, not recorded lien

The single most important — and most distinctive — feature of Maryland mechanics' lien practice, **unlike** every neighboring state (New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Virginia, Delaware, and West Virginia), is that **Maryland mechanics' liens are NOT created by recording a lien claim with the county clerk or land records office**. Instead, the Maryland lien is a **judicial lien created by court order** following a contractor's filing of a **Petition to Establish Mechanics' Lien** in the Circuit Court for the county where the property is located [55][56][57][58][59][60][61][62][63][64][65].

This procedural structure is fundamentally different from most states' lien regimes:

- In **New Jersey**, **Pennsylvania**, **Virginia**, and most other jurisdictions, a lien is created by the contractor's unilateral act of filing a written lien claim with the county clerk; the recorded lien immediately attaches to the property as a matter of statute, subject to later judicial challenge.
- In **Maryland**, the contractor files a **petition** asking the circuit court to **establish** the lien; the lien attaches only upon entry of a court order (an interlocutory order under Md. Rule 12-304 or a final order after evidentiary hearing under Md. Rule 12-306).

This judicial-petition structure was adopted in 1976 to ensure compliance with due-process requirements: every Maryland mechanics' lien results from a judicial proceeding with notice to the property owner, an opportunity to be heard, and a court determination of probable validity. The result is a **procedurally burdensome but constitutionally robust** lien regime — slower and more expensive than the recorded-lien systems of neighboring states, but less susceptible to abusive filings and lien-extortion practices.

### 2.2 Who may claim a lien — Real Prop. § 9-102

Under **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-102(a)**, a mechanics' lien may be established by [55][56][57][58][59][60]:

1. **Any contractor, subcontractor, sub-subcontractor, material supplier, or laborer** who has furnished work or materials for or about the building.
2. **Architects, professional engineers, land surveyors, and landscape architects** who have provided professional services for the project.

The lien claimant must have a contract — direct or indirect — with the property owner, the general contractor, or another higher-tier party in the contracting chain. Tier-limit rules apply: a sub-sub-subcontractor (i.e., a fourth-tier party) generally lacks lien rights unless the third-tier party is in privity with the owner or general contractor.

**Coverage of the building.** A mechanics' lien attaches to **any building** to which the lien claimant has contributed work or materials, including:

- New construction of buildings (single-family residences, multi-family residences, commercial buildings, industrial buildings).
- Improvements or repairs to existing buildings, but only where the work has **increased the value of the building by at least 15% (the "15% rule")** under Real Prop. § 9-102(a)(2) for repairs and improvements to existing buildings (this is the critical Maryland-specific limitation discussed in § 2.3 below).
- Wharves, piers, and other improvements to real property — but the lien is structurally a lien on the **building**, not on the land independent of the building.

### 2.3 The "15% rule" for repairs and improvements to existing buildings — Real Prop. § 9-102(a)(2)

A uniquely Maryland feature of mechanics' lien law is the **15% threshold** for liens arising from repairs or improvements to **existing buildings** (as distinguished from new construction) [55][56][57][58][59][60][61][62][63]. Under **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-102(a)(2)**:

> A building, except a tenant-occupied family dwelling, shall be subject to establishment of a lien in accordance with this subtitle for the payment of all debts contracted for work done for or about the building and for materials furnished for or about the building, including the drilling and installation of wells to supply water, but no lien shall be established on the building for repair, rebuilding, or improvement unless the repair, rebuilding, or improvement increases the value of the building by 15% or more of its value before the repair, rebuilding, or improvement.

The 15% rule means that a contractor or subcontractor performing repair or improvement work on an existing building must demonstrate, as a condition precedent to establishing the lien, that the work increased the building's value by at least 15%. The percentage is calculated on the value of the building **before** the repair, rebuilding, or improvement — not on the total project cost.

**Practical implications:**

1. **New construction** — for a newly constructed building, no 15% threshold applies; the lien is available without limitation as to project size relative to underlying value.
2. **Major remodeling** (gut renovation, addition, structural modification) — typically clears the 15% threshold easily.
3. **Small repair work** (e.g., a $5,000 roof repair on a $400,000 home) — fails the 15% threshold ($5,000 ÷ $400,000 = 1.25%) and is **not lienable**.
4. **Tenant-occupied family dwellings** — completely excluded from the lien regime by the statutory carve-out in § 9-102(a)(2). A landlord's contractor performing work at a tenant-occupied single-family residence has **no lien rights** at all, regardless of the value of work performed.

The 15% rule is a significant structural deterrent to small-claim lien practice in Maryland and is among the practical reasons Maryland contractors prioritize **prompt-pay claims** (see § 3 below) and contract-based collection actions over mechanics' lien enforcement for smaller jobs.

### 2.4 Notice of Intent to Claim Lien — Real Prop. § 9-104

For **subcontractors and lower-tier claimants** (i.e., parties not in direct contract with the property owner), Maryland imposes a critical pre-lien notice obligation: the **Notice of Intent to Claim a Mechanics' Lien** under **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-104** [55][56][57][58][59][60][61][62][63][64].

**Who must serve the notice.** Every subcontractor (or sub-subcontractor, supplier, or laborer) who is not in direct contract with the property owner must serve a **Notice of Intent to Claim a Mechanics' Lien** on the owner.

**When the notice must be served.** The notice must be given within **120 days after the subcontractor's work was last performed or materials last furnished** under § 9-104(a). The 120-day window runs from the date of the subcontractor's last work on the project — not from the date of the prime contractor's last work or the project's completion.

**Method of service.** Service may be made [55][56][58][62][63][64]:

1. By **personal service** on the owner.
2. By **registered or certified mail, return receipt requested**, to the owner's last known address.
3. By **posting** the notice on the door of the building if personal service or certified mail is not possible after reasonable efforts.

**Contents of the notice — § 9-104(b).** The notice must contain:

1. The name and address of the subcontractor / claimant.
2. A description of the work or materials provided.
3. The name of the party with whom the subcontractor contracted (the prime contractor or higher-tier party).
4. The amount claimed to be due.
5. The address or other identification of the property.

**The owner's protection — the "extent of unpaid balance" defense.** Under § 9-104(d), a subcontractor's lien is limited to the amount the **owner owes the prime contractor at the time the notice is served**, less any subsequent payments made by the owner in good faith. The principle is functionally similar to (but more straightforward than) the New Jersey "lien fund" doctrine: an owner who has fully paid the prime before receiving a subcontractor's Notice of Intent has a complete defense against the subcontractor's lien.

**Critical practical implication.** A subcontractor seeking to preserve lien rights against an owner-paid-in-full scenario must serve the Notice of Intent **early** — ideally as soon as a payment dispute arises — to "freeze" the unpaid-balance amount and prevent the owner from making further payments to the prime in good faith without exposure to the sub's lien claim. The 120-day outside limit is too late if the owner pays the prime in the interim.

**Owner-occupied single-family residences — § 9-104(f).** For liens against owner-occupied single-family dwellings, an enhanced notice procedure applies: the subcontractor must serve the Notice of Intent within **120 days**, and may not file the lien petition until at least **30 days** after the notice. This 30-day waiting period gives the homeowner an opportunity to resolve the underlying dispute (typically by withholding payment from the prime or making a joint-check payment) before judicial proceedings commence.

### 2.5 Petition to Establish Mechanics' Lien — Real Prop. § 9-105 and Md. Rule 12-302

The lien claim itself is initiated by filing a **Petition to Establish Mechanics' Lien** in the Circuit Court for the county where the property is located under **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-105** and **Maryland Rule 12-302** [55][56][57][58][59][60][61][62][63][64][65][66].

**Filing deadline — § 9-105(a).** The petition must be filed **within 180 days after the work was finished or the materials furnished** by the petitioner. For a general contractor (in direct contract with the owner), the 180-day window runs from the date the general contractor's work was last performed. For a subcontractor, the window runs from the subcontractor's own last work — and the subcontractor must also have served the Notice of Intent within the 120-day window described in § 2.4 above.

**Contents of the petition — Md. Rule 12-302:**

1. A description of the property — including the legal description and street address.
2. The name and address of the owner.
3. The name and address of the petitioner.
4. The name of the party with whom the petitioner contracted.
5. The nature of the work or materials furnished, and the dates of first and last furnishing.
6. The amount claimed to be due, with itemization.
7. For subcontractor petitioners, attachment of the Notice of Intent and proof of service.
8. A **verified affidavit** by the petitioner attesting to the facts of the claim.
9. Supporting documentation: the contract, invoices, and any other documents supporting the claim.

**Filing fees.** Circuit court civil filing fees apply — typically $165 plus per-page recording fees and any required service fees.

### 2.6 Show-Cause Order and probable-validity hearing — Real Prop. § 9-106 and Md. Rule 12-303

Upon filing of the petition, the procedure follows a multi-step process designed to satisfy due-process requirements [55][56][57][58][59][60][61][62][63][64][65][66]:

**Step 1 — Show-Cause Order — § 9-106(a).** Upon filing, the court issues an **Order to Show Cause** directing the owner to appear at a hearing (typically within 15 days) and show cause why an interlocutory order establishing the lien should not be entered. The Show-Cause Order is served on the owner along with the petition.

**Step 2 — Probable-validity hearing — § 9-106 and Md. Rule 12-303.** At the show-cause hearing, the court determines whether the petitioner has demonstrated **probable cause to believe the petitioner is entitled to a lien**. The court may:

1. **Enter an interlocutory order establishing the lien** — if the court determines that the petition states a prima facie case for a lien and the owner has not effectively rebutted the showing. The interlocutory order operates as a recorded lien on the property pending final adjudication.
2. **Enter an order requiring the owner to post a bond** — in lieu of an interlocutory lien, the owner may post a bond equal to the petition amount, releasing the property from the lien pending final adjudication.
3. **Dismiss the petition** — if the court determines that the petition does not state a prima facie case or the claimant has failed to comply with statutory prerequisites (e.g., no Notice of Intent in subcontractor cases, no 15% threshold for existing-building repair, no timely filing).
4. **Set the case for further proceedings** — if there is a genuine dispute of material fact, the court may set the matter for final hearing or trial under Rule 12-304.

**Step 3 — Final order — § 9-107 and Md. Rule 12-306.** At the final hearing, the court determines the **validity and amount of the lien** after full evidentiary proceedings. The final order establishes (or denies) the lien and sets the amount.

The interlocutory and final orders together create the Maryland mechanics' lien — there is no parallel recorded-lien mechanism. The lien exists by virtue of the court order, and the order is recorded by the clerk of the court in the land records, but the recording is the **memorialization** of the lien, not the source of it.

### 2.7 Lien priority — Real Prop. § 9-103

Under **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-103**, Maryland mechanics' liens have priority [55][56][57][58][62][63]:

1. **Over all encumbrances** (mortgages, deeds of trust, judgments) attached to the property **after the visible commencement of the building** for which the work was performed.
2. **Subject to** all encumbrances attached **before** the visible commencement of the building.

The "visible commencement" rule is the Maryland equivalent of the "first furnishing" rule in many other states: the priority date for a lien is the date construction visibly commenced on the project (the breaking of ground, the delivery of materials to the site, the start of excavation, etc.) — not the date of the lien claimant's first work, and not the date the petition is filed.

**Construction mortgage lender protection — § 9-103(c).** A construction mortgage lender that records its mortgage **before** the visible commencement of the building takes priority over later-filed lien claims, even though the mortgage advances may be disbursed during construction. This rule protects construction lenders and is the practical reason every construction loan closing requires the lender to verify (typically through title insurance and inspection) that no visible work has commenced on the site before the mortgage is recorded.

### 2.8 Enforcement and foreclosure — Real Prop. § 9-108

Once the final order establishing the lien is entered, the lien is **enforced through foreclosure** under **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-108** [55][56][57][62][63]. The lienholder files a separate foreclosure action in the Circuit Court (typically as a continuation of the lien case) seeking sale of the property to satisfy the lien.

**Statute of limitations on enforcement.** The lien must be enforced within **12 years of the date the lien was established** (i.e., the date of the final order). After 12 years, the lien expires by operation of law and cannot be enforced. This is a relatively long enforcement window by comparison to other states (New Jersey's 1-year suit deadline is among the shortest), reflecting the already-substantial procedural burden built into the Maryland lien-establishment process.

**Practical reality.** Mechanics' lien foreclosure in Maryland is uncommon — most lien-protected claims are resolved through negotiation once the interlocutory order is entered, or through the owner's posting of a bond and substitution of the bond for the property as the lien security. Foreclosure sales of residential property to satisfy mechanics' liens are particularly rare given the consumer-protection scrutiny applied to such sales.

### 2.9 Lien waivers — Real Prop. § 9-113

Lien waivers in Maryland are governed by **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-113** [55][56][57][62][63]. The statute and case law require that waivers be:

1. **In writing and signed** by the claimant or its authorized representative.
2. **Specific as to the work, materials, time period, or payment to which the waiver applies.**
3. **Not in advance of work performance** — § 9-113(b) **declares void as against public policy any waiver of lien rights executed before the work giving rise to the lien is performed**. This is one of the strongest pre-work lien-waiver prohibitions in the United States and prevents prime contractors and owners from extracting blanket pre-work lien releases from subcontractors at the contracting stage.

**Conditional vs. unconditional waivers.** Both **conditional progress-payment waivers** (effective only upon receipt of identified payment) and **unconditional final waivers** (effective immediately upon execution) are recognized and enforced. Best practice for subcontractors is to deliver only conditional waivers tied to specific payments, and to deliver unconditional final waivers only upon receipt of fully cleared final payment.

**Pay-if-paid clauses.** Maryland case law treats pay-if-paid clauses with disfavor where the clause purports to **extinguish** the subcontractor's lien rights or claim against the prime; under cases including **Gilbane Building Co. v. Brisk Waterproofing Co., 86 Md. App. 21 (1991)**, Maryland courts construe payment-timing clauses narrowly and treat ambiguous clauses as pay-when-paid (reasonable-time provisions) rather than pay-if-paid (conditions precedent). Even where pay-if-paid clauses are enforceable as to contractual payment timing between prime and sub, they cannot **extinguish** the sub's statutory lien rights or rights under the Prompt Payment Act [55][56][57][62][63][67].

### 2.10 Public works — no mechanics' lien on public property; statutory bond claims

Public construction projects in Maryland — projects performed for the State, counties, municipalities, boards of education, and other public entities — are **not subject to mechanics' liens** under Real Prop. § 9-103. Public property is statutorily exempt from lien attachment. Subcontractor and supplier recovery on public works is instead through [68][69][70][71]:

1. **Claims against the prime contractor's payment bond** under the **Maryland Little Miller Act**, codified at **Md. Code Ann., State Fin. & Proc. §§ 17-101 through 17-111**. The Little Miller Act requires every prime contractor on a state public-works contract above **$100,000** (and lower thresholds for some local governments) to furnish a **payment bond in 100% of the contract amount** and a **performance bond in 100% of the contract amount**.
2. **Prompt Payment Act claims** against the prime for late payment of progress payments and retainage on public projects (see § 3.2 below).
3. **Liens on contract funds held by the public entity** under State Fin. & Proc. § 15-226, under specific procedures available to certain claimants.

**Notice and filing on public works under the Little Miller Act.** A claimant on a state public-works payment bond must serve a **Notice of Claim** on the prime contractor and the public entity within **90 days of the claimant's last work or materials**, and must file suit on the bond within **1 year** of the same date. The Little Miller Act provisions are modeled on the federal Miller Act (40 U.S.C. § 3131 et seq.) but with Maryland-specific notice and filing requirements.

## 3. Prompt Payment and Retainage

### 3.1 Maryland Prompt Payment Act for private projects — Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-301 et seq.

The **Maryland Prompt Payment Act for private construction**, codified at **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. §§ 9-301 through 9-305**, was enacted by Ch. 553, Laws of Maryland 2007 (effective October 1, 2007) and governs payment timing on private construction contracts [72][73][74][75][76][77][78][79].

**Scope — § 9-301.** The Act applies to every "private construction contract" — defined as a contract for the construction, alteration, repair, or improvement of any private (non-public) real property in Maryland. The Act does **not** apply to:

1. Public-works projects (governed by State Fin. & Proc. § 15-225, see § 3.2 below).
2. Home improvement contracts subject to the MHIC's Home Improvement Law — § 9-301(b) carves out the residential home improvement segment from the Prompt Payment Act, instead leaving payment-timing disputes between MHIC contractors and homeowners to the contract and the Home Improvement Law's consumer-protection framework.
3. Contracts with a value below $5,000 (a small de minimis carve-out for very small jobs).

**Owner to prime — § 9-302.** When an owner receives a properly submitted invoice or progress-payment request from the prime contractor, the owner must pay the undisputed portion of the invoice within **the period specified in the contract** or, if no period is specified, within **30 days** of receipt. If the owner disputes any portion, the owner must give written notice of the dispute within the payment window identifying with specificity the items in dispute. Items not specifically disputed in the rejection notice are deemed approved and must be paid.

**Prime to subcontractor — § 9-303.** When a prime contractor receives payment from the owner that includes amounts due to a subcontractor or supplier, the prime must pay the subcontractor or supplier its share within **7 days** of the prime's receipt of the owner's payment. This is a notably short pass-through window — shorter than New Jersey's 10-day rule and several other neighboring states — and is one of the strongest subcontractor protections in any Mid-Atlantic Prompt Payment Act.

**Interest penalty — § 9-304.** A party that fails to pay within the statutory window is liable to the unpaid party for **interest at the rate of 1.5% per month (18% per annum)** on the unpaid amount, computed from the date payment was due. This is among the highest statutory prompt-pay interest rates in the country (compare to New Jersey's prime-rate-plus-1% running approximately 8-9.5%, or Virginia's 1% per month).

**Attorney fees — § 9-305.** The Act includes a one-way fee-shifting provision: the **prevailing party** in a Prompt Payment Act action is entitled to **reasonable attorney fees and costs**. The fee-shifting applies in both directions — to the unpaid party recovering from the non-paying upstream party, and to a wrongfully accused upstream party defending against a meritless claim.

**Right to suspend performance — § 9-303(d).** If a party fails to pay an undisputed invoice within the statutory window, the unpaid party has the right to **suspend performance** on the project upon **7 calendar days' written notice** to the non-paying party, without breach of contract. The suspension right is one of the strongest practical remedies and is widely used by subcontractors to pressure prime contractors who hold subcontractor payments while awaiting owner payment. The right to suspend is **not waivable by contract** — any provision purporting to waive the right to suspend is void as against public policy.

### 3.2 Public-works prompt payment — Md. Code Ann., State Fin. & Proc. § 15-225

Public-works prompt-pay obligations are governed by **Md. Code Ann., State Fin. & Proc. §§ 15-101 through 15-227**, with the principal prompt-payment provision at **§ 15-225** and related provisions at **§§ 15-226 and 17-110** [80][81][82][83][84][85]. The General Procurement Law applies to State of Maryland procurement; counties, municipalities, and boards of education are governed by similar provisions in their local procurement codes (typically modeled on the state framework).

**Owner (state agency) to prime — § 15-225.** When a state procurement unit receives a properly submitted invoice or progress payment request from a prime contractor, the state must pay the undisputed amount within **30 days** of receipt of the invoice. Disputed amounts must be identified in writing within the 30-day window; non-disputed items are deemed approved and payable.

**Prime to subcontractor — § 15-226.** The prime contractor must pay subcontractors and suppliers their shares of received progress payments within **10 days** of receipt from the state. The pass-through window is slightly longer than the 7-day window applicable to private contracts under § 9-303 but is still among the shorter windows in state public-procurement codes.

**Interest penalty — § 15-225(d).** Late payment by the state triggers interest at the rate established by the State Treasurer, typically pegged to the General Fund interest rate plus an adjustment. Interest on prime-to-sub late payments runs at the 1.5% per month / 18% per annum rate under § 9-304 as imported into the public-works framework by reference.

**Retainage release on public works — § 17-110.** State public-works contracts include statutory retainage provisions (see § 3.3 below); retainage must be released within prescribed time periods after substantial completion.

**Public-works subcontractor remedy.** A subcontractor on a state public-works project may file a Prompt Payment Act claim against the prime contractor with the procurement officer of the contracting state agency. The procurement officer may withhold payment to the prime in an amount equal to the disputed sub claim until the dispute is resolved, providing a meaningful informal enforcement mechanism short of formal litigation.

### 3.3 Retainage on Maryland public works — State Fin. & Proc. § 17-110

Maryland public-works retainage is governed by **Md. Code Ann., State Fin. & Proc. § 17-110** [82][83][84][85][86][87].

**Maximum retainage on state public works.** State public-works construction contracts may withhold retainage of up to **5% of progress payments** during the construction period. Several state procurement units have adopted policies of reducing the retainage withholding to 2.5% upon reaching 50% of project completion, modeled on the federal-acquisition retainage framework.

**Retainage release timing.** Retainage must be released within **30 days** after substantial completion of the project, subject to:

1. Verification of work by the project engineer or architect.
2. Contractor compliance with prevailing wage and labor law requirements.
3. Approval by the procurement officer.

**Subcontractor retainage release.** Retainage withheld by the prime contractor against subcontractors must be released within **10 days** of the prime's receipt of released retainage from the state, under the Prompt Payment Act pass-through rules.

### 3.4 Retainage on private projects

Private-works retainage in Maryland is **contract-based**, with no statutory cap. Owners and primes typically negotiate retainage of 5% to 10% during construction, with a 50%-completion reduction or release common (modeled on the public-works template). Retainage release timing is governed by the contract; Prompt Payment Act rules apply to payment timing once retainage is contractually due [72][73][74][75][76][77].

### 3.5 Retainage on Maryland local public works

Maryland counties and municipalities follow similar retainage frameworks under their local procurement codes, with typical retainage caps of 5% during construction, reduced to 2.5% at 50% completion, and released within 30 to 60 days of substantial completion. Local prompt-pay provisions track the state framework with minor variations in payment windows and interest rates.

## 4. Tax structure

### 4.1 Sales and use tax — Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 11-101 et seq. (6% statewide)

**State sales-and-use tax rate.** Maryland imposes a statewide sales and use tax at the rate of **6%** under **Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 11-104** [88][89][90][91][92][93]. The 6% rate has been in effect since January 3, 2008 (raised from 5% by the Special Session of the General Assembly in 2007 as part of the Tax Reform Act of 2007). The rate applies to the retail sale of tangible personal property, certain taxable services, and digital products.

**No local sales tax.** Unlike Pennsylvania (Philadelphia and Allegheny County add-ons), Virginia (regional add-on for Northern Virginia and Hampton Roads), or New York (county and city add-ons), Maryland imposes **no general local sales tax**. The 6% rate applies uniformly statewide, with no jurisdictional add-on for Baltimore City, Baltimore County, Montgomery County, or any other Maryland subdivision. This is a meaningful operational simplification — a Maryland contractor's sales-tax calculation is uniform statewide.

**Higher rate on alcoholic beverages.** Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 11-104(c) imposes a 9% rate on sales of alcoholic beverages — not relevant to construction contracting except in the unusual case of a contractor purchasing alcoholic beverages for business purposes (which generally do not qualify for resale exemption regardless).

### 4.2 Construction contractor as consumer — Tax-Gen. § 11-101(p), COMAR 03.06.01.18

Maryland treats the construction contractor as the **consumer** of tangible personal property incorporated into real property — the standard structural treatment used in most U.S. states [88][89][90][91][92][93][94][95]. The Comptroller of Maryland publishes detailed guidance on contractor sales-tax treatment in **Maryland Sales and Use Tax Bulletin (Contractors)** and at COMAR 03.06.01.18.

**The general rule.** A contractor performing real-property construction work is the consumer of all tangible personal property purchased for use in performing the contract. The contractor pays sales tax to the supplier at the point of purchase on all building materials, supplies, and equipment that will be incorporated into real property [88][89][90][91][92][93][94][95]. The contractor does **not** charge the property owner separate sales tax on the materials portion of the construction contract — the materials have already had their sales tax paid by the contractor, and the contract price is treated as a single charge for the real-property improvement.

**Why the contractor-as-consumer treatment matters.** This treatment has three important practical consequences:

1. **No charge to customer for sales tax on materials.** Customer pays the contract price, which is treated as a single charge for the real-property improvement, with sales tax already embedded in the materials cost at the contractor's cost basis.
2. **Contractor cannot use a resale certificate** to purchase materials tax-free (unless the contractor is also a registered retailer making genuine retail sales — see "Dual-role" below).
3. **Tax-exempt customer exception** — when the customer is a tax-exempt entity (governmental unit, 501(c)(3)), the contractor may purchase materials for that project exempt from sales tax through a specific procedure (see § 4.3 below).

**Repair, maintenance, and installation services.** Maryland's treatment of contractor services differs in one important respect from the model adopted by several neighboring states. Maryland does **not** tax labor charges for real-property construction services in general — the labor portion of a construction contract is **not subject to sales tax** regardless of whether the work qualifies as a capital improvement or a repair. This is a simpler model than New Jersey (which taxes labor on repair/maintenance) or several other states with capital-improvement-vs-repair distinctions. Maryland's treatment is:

1. **Real-property construction services** (including capital improvements, repairs, alterations, and maintenance to real property) — **labor is not subject to sales tax**; the contractor pays sales tax on materials at purchase.
2. **Certain enumerated services** — Maryland does tax certain specific services under Tax-Gen. § 11-101(m) and § 11-104(b), including some cleaning services and limited categories of maintenance services, but the principal construction-services categories (general construction, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, carpentry, painting, roofing) are not taxable.
3. **Tangible personal property installations** — installation of items that retain their character as tangible personal property (e.g., free-standing appliances) is treated as a retail sale of tangible personal property, subject to sales tax on the full charge.

The labor-not-taxable rule for real-property construction makes Maryland's sales-tax compliance considerably simpler than New Jersey's capital-improvement-vs-repair regime. There is no "Form ST-8 Capital Improvement Certificate" equivalent in Maryland because the labor charge is not taxable regardless of how the work is characterized.

**Dual-role retailer-contractors.** A contractor who **also makes substantial retail sales of building materials** (a contractor-supplier hybrid) may register as a retailer with the Comptroller and purchase materials for resale on a resale-certificate basis — but only on the portion of the materials that will be **resold to customers**, not on the portion that will be incorporated into real property under the contractor's own construction contracts. Maryland is strict in requiring the dual-role retailer to **separately account** for inventory destined for resale (purchased tax-free) and inventory destined for use in construction (purchased with sales tax paid), with periodic audits by the Comptroller's office to verify proper segregation.

### 4.3 Tax-exempt customers — Tax-Gen. § 11-204 and Form ST-206 (Maryland Exemption Certificate)

When a contractor performs work for a tax-exempt customer — a governmental unit, a 501(c)(3) charitable organization, a religious organization, an educational institution, or other entity holding a Maryland sales-tax exemption certificate — the contractor may purchase materials for that specific project exempt from sales tax by furnishing the supplier with the customer's exemption certificate [88][89][90][91][92][93][94][95].

Maryland's procedure for tax-exempt project materials is generally:

1. The exempt customer (e.g., a state agency, county government, public school, qualifying nonprofit) provides the contractor with a copy of its Maryland sales-tax exemption certificate.
2. The contractor presents the exemption certificate to the materials supplier, identifying the specific exempt project.
3. The supplier sells the identified materials without charging sales tax, retaining the documentation in its records.
4. The contractor uses the materials only on the identified exempt project.

**Public-works construction.** A contractor performing work on a Maryland public-works project (state, county, municipal, board of education, public university) is generally entitled to use the public entity's exemption certificate for materials incorporated into the public improvement, on the theory that the public authority is the ultimate consumer and is itself exempt. The contractor must obtain documentation from the public authority confirming exempt status and the scope of the exempt project. **The Maryland Comptroller's office is more restrictive than some other states** in enforcing the requirement that exempt-purchase materials be used exclusively on the identified exempt project; diversion of exempt-purchase materials to a taxable project exposes the contractor to back tax, penalty, and interest.

### 4.4 Use tax — Tax-Gen. § 11-102

Maryland imposes a **complementary use tax** at the same 6% rate on tangible personal property purchased outside Maryland and used in Maryland under **Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 11-102** [88][89][91][95]. The use tax is the principal enforcement mechanism against contractors who purchase materials in lower-tax neighboring states (Delaware: no sales tax; Pennsylvania: 6% same; Virginia: 5.3% statewide and varying local). A Maryland contractor who purchases materials in Delaware (no sales tax) for use on a Maryland project must self-assess and remit Maryland use tax at 6% on the purchase price of those materials. Failure to self-assess and remit use tax exposes the contractor to back tax, penalty, and interest on Comptroller audit.

### 4.5 Corporate income tax — Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 10-101 et seq.

- **Corporate Income Tax — Tax-Gen. § 10-105.** Maryland imposes a corporate income tax at the rate of **8.25%** on net income of corporations doing business in Maryland — a moderately high rate by national comparison but lower than New Jersey's effective 11.5% with the Corporate Transit Fee surcharge [96][97][98].
- **Personal Income Tax — Tax-Gen. § 10-105.** Pass-through entity owners (LLC members, S-corp shareholders, partners) pay Maryland personal income tax at graduated rates ranging from 2% to **5.75%** on income above $250,000 (for joint filers; the top bracket and threshold are adjusted periodically). Maryland also imposes a **local income tax** at the county level — see § 4.6 below.

### 4.6 County (local) income tax — Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 10-103

Unlike most states, Maryland authorizes its 23 counties and Baltimore City to impose a **local income tax** as a piggyback on the state personal income tax under **Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 10-103** [96][97][98][99][100]. The local rate is set by each county (and Baltimore City) and ranges from **2.25% to 3.20% of taxable income** as of 2025, with most counties at the upper end of the range. Baltimore City's rate is 3.20%; Montgomery County's rate is 3.20%; Prince George's County's rate is 3.20%; Howard County's rate is 3.20%; Anne Arundel County's rate is 2.81%; and so on. The local tax is collected by the Comptroller as part of state income tax administration and remitted to the counties.

The local income tax is the principal reason Maryland's effective personal income tax burden is among the highest in the country: a Baltimore City or Montgomery County resident in the top state bracket pays approximately **5.75% (state) + 3.20% (local) = 8.95%** in combined state and local income tax — higher than New Jersey's 10.75% top state rate, but applicable at lower income thresholds. Pass-through entity owners (most construction firms organized as LLCs, S-corps, or partnerships) feel this combined burden directly on their share of business income.

### 4.7 Real property transfer and recordation taxes

Maryland also imposes a **state recordation tax** of $5 per $1,000 of consideration on the recording of a deed or deed of trust, and counties impose additional **local recordation taxes** ranging from $1 to $7 per $1,000 of consideration. **County transfer taxes** may also apply at 0.5% to 1.5% of consideration. While not directly imposed on construction contractors, these taxes affect the structure and economics of large construction projects, particularly construction lending and project finance arrangements.

## 5. Workers' compensation — Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 9-101 et seq. — mandatory for every employer

### 5.1 Statutory framework

Maryland workers' compensation is at **Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. §§ 9-101 through 9-1201**, administered by the **Maryland Workers' Compensation Commission (WCC)** at wcc.state.md.us [42][43][44][45][46][47][101][102][103][104][105]. The system is a private-market system — coverage is purchased on the private insurance market through admitted carriers regulated by the Maryland Insurance Administration. The **National Council on Compensation Insurance ("NCCI")** administers classification, experience rating, and rate filings for Maryland workers' compensation under contract with the Insurance Administration.

The state operates an affiliated residual-market carrier through the **Chesapeake Employers' Insurance Company** (the successor to the Injured Workers' Insurance Fund, IWIF) under **Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. §§ 10-101 through 10-411**. Chesapeake Employers' is a non-profit, quasi-public insurance company chartered by Maryland and is the carrier of last resort for employers unable to obtain coverage on the voluntary private market. Chesapeake Employers' historically wrote a large share of Maryland workers' compensation policies and remains a significant carrier, although the private market has substantially expanded its Maryland presence over the past two decades.

**Mandatory coverage — Lab. & Empl. § 9-202.** Every Maryland employer with **one or more employees** must maintain workers' compensation coverage. Maryland's "one or more employees" rule applies from the first employee — there is **no minimum-employee threshold of any kind**, and no industry-specific carve-out for construction or any other industry. The mandatory-coverage rule is broader than Tennessee (5-employee for non-construction, 1 for construction), Florida (4-employee for non-construction, 1 for construction), Georgia (3-employee), or Texas (no mandatory coverage at all), but is consistent with the practice of New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, and several other northeastern states [42][43][44][45][46][47][101][102][103].

### 5.2 Coverage routes — three pathways

Under Lab. & Empl. § 9-402, every covered employer must satisfy the coverage requirement by one of three routes [42][43][44][45][101][102][103]:

1. **Private-market insurance policy.** The standard route. The employer purchases a workers' compensation policy from an admitted insurer authorized to write Maryland workers' compensation business. Major carriers active in Maryland include Travelers, Hartford, AmTrust, Liberty Mutual, Zurich, Chubb, CNA, Berkshire / Berkley, and Chesapeake Employers' Insurance Company (operating in both voluntary and residual-market segments).
2. **Chesapeake Employers' Insurance Company.** The state-affiliated carrier serves as the residual market for employers unable to obtain coverage on the voluntary private market, and also competes for voluntary business. Chesapeake Employers' coverage is reasonably priced for residual-market business and is often more flexible than fully private carriers for newly formed contractors with limited loss history.
3. **Self-insurance under Lab. & Empl. § 9-404.** Available only to employers meeting net-worth, working-capital, and claims-administration capacity thresholds set by the WCC. Self-insurance is the route used by very large Maryland employers — major hospital systems, major universities, large public agencies, and a small number of very large contractors — but is rarely available to construction-industry firms below very large scale.

### 5.3 Construction-industry rates and classifications

Maryland construction-industry workers' compensation rates are moderate by national comparison, with rates somewhat lower than New Jersey but higher than Virginia or North Carolina [42][43][44][45][101][102][103][104][105]. NCCI uses the standard NCCI class-code manual classifications. Major construction classifications include:

- **5183** — Plumbing
- **5190** — Electrical (interior wiring)
- **5538** — HVAC, sheet metal work
- **5057** — Iron and steel erection
- **5645** — Carpentry, residential one- to two-family
- **5403** — Carpentry, commercial
- **5474** — Painting
- **5022** — Masonry
- **5606** — Construction executive, supervisor, foreman
- **5213** — Concrete construction

**Rate developments.** Maryland workers' comp rates are reviewed annually by NCCI and approved by the Maryland Insurance Administration. Construction classification base rates typically run between $3 and $10 per $100 of payroll for the major trades, with experience-mod and schedule-credit adjustments. A small residential carpentry contractor (class 5645) with $300,000 of payroll and a unity experience mod might pay approximately $20,000 to $30,000 in annual manual premium, before experience-mod credits and dividend adjustments.

### 5.4 Sole proprietor, partner, and officer treatment — Lab. & Empl. § 9-206

Under **Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 9-206**, the workers' compensation system treats owners and officers as follows [42][43][44][45][101][102][103]:

- **Sole proprietors** are excluded from the default definition of "covered employee" — they are not required to be covered by workers' comp, but may elect to be covered.
- **General partners** are similarly excluded from default coverage but may elect coverage.
- **Corporate officers** are presumed **included** in the workers' compensation policy as employees, but may **elect to exclude themselves** by filing a written election with the carrier. Election out is limited to specific officer categories under the carrier's filed forms.
- **LLC members.** Members of an LLC classified as a partnership for federal tax purposes are treated analogously to general partners — excluded by default, with elective inclusion available. Members of an LLC classified as a corporation are treated as corporate officers.

The election-in / election-out mechanics are critical: a contractor structured as a sole proprietorship or single-member LLC with no employees has **no mandatory workers' compensation obligation**, but also has **no workers' compensation benefits** in the event of a work-related injury. Many small contractors elect into coverage voluntarily for personal protection, particularly when subcontracting to general contractors that require certificate of insurance evidence.

### 5.5 Statutory employer doctrine — Lab. & Empl. § 9-508

Under **Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 9-508**, a "principal contractor" that engages a subcontractor for work that is part of the principal's trade, business, or occupation is the **statutory employer** of the subcontractor's employees for workers' compensation purposes — meaning that if the subcontractor fails to maintain workers' comp coverage, the principal contractor is liable for benefits to the subcontractor's injured employees [42][43][44][45][101][102][103][106]. The statutory-employer doctrine is the principal mechanism by which Maryland ensures injured construction workers obtain workers' compensation benefits regardless of the financial responsibility of their immediate employer.

**Practical compliance.** Every Maryland general contractor must obtain a **Certificate of Insurance evidencing active workers' compensation coverage** from every subcontractor before granting site access, and must verify the certificate is current at intervals. Best practice is to require the certificate name the GC as a certificate holder (so the GC receives cancellation notices) and to verify coverage through the WCC's verification system or directly with the issuing carrier.

The statutory-employer doctrine also creates an important exception to **respondeat-superior tort claims**: a principal that is the statutory employer of an injured worker is generally shielded from tort liability under the workers' compensation exclusive-remedy rule (Lab. & Empl. § 9-509), even where the injured worker was the employee of an uninsured sub — the principal pays workers' comp benefits but is not exposed to common-law tort damages.

### 5.6 Maryland Workplace Fraud Act — Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 3-901 et seq.

The **Maryland Workplace Fraud Act of 2009 ("MWFA")**, codified at **Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. §§ 3-901 through 3-920** (enacted by Ch. 188, Laws of Maryland 2009, effective October 1, 2009), is one of the most aggressive worker-classification statutes in the country. The Act establishes a **strict ABC test** for determining whether a worker in the construction services industry or the landscaping services industry is an independent contractor or an employee for purposes of workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, and wage and hour laws [107][108][109][110][111][112][113].

**The ABC test — Lab. & Empl. § 3-903.** A worker in the construction services industry is presumed to be an **employee** unless the alleged employer can prove **all three** of the following elements:

1. **(A)** The worker is **free from control and direction** over the performance of the work, both under the contract and in fact;
2. **(B)** The worker is **customarily engaged in an independent business** of the same nature as that involved in the work performed; AND
3. **(C)** The work is either (i) **outside the usual course of business** for which it is performed or (ii) the work is performed **outside of all the places of business of the person for whom it is performed**.

The "C prong" — work outside the usual course of business — is the gating element for most construction-industry classifications. A general contractor engaging a subcontractor to perform construction work cannot typically satisfy prong C because construction work is the usual course of the GC's business. The practical effect: in Maryland, **a subcontractor who works exclusively (or primarily) for a single GC is presumptively an employee of the GC for purposes of workers' comp, unemployment insurance, and wage and hour withholding** — regardless of contract terms, 1099 reporting, or the worker's own preference [107][108][109][110][111][112][113].

**Penalties under Lab. & Empl. § 3-907 and § 3-913.** A finding of knowing misclassification under the MWFA exposes the misclassifying contractor to [109][110][111][112][113]:

1. **Civil penalties** of up to **$5,000 per misclassified worker** for first violations; up to **$20,000 per worker** for knowing or repeat violations.
2. **Stop-work orders** — the Commissioner of Labor and Industry may issue a stop-work order shutting down the contractor's construction activity until the violation is cured.
3. **Back unemployment insurance contributions** for all misclassified workers, plus interest and penalty.
4. **Back workers' compensation premium** computed based on the corrected classification of the misclassified workers, plus interest and penalty.
5. **Treble back wages** to misclassified workers under the Maryland Wage Payment and Collection Law (Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 3-507.2), which permits treble damages where wages are withheld without bona fide dispute.
6. **Prevailing wage exposure** if the work was on a public-works project where the misclassified workers should have received prevailing wages.

**Affirmative defense — Bona fide independent contractor exemption.** The MWFA includes a complex affirmative defense for parties that file annual notice with the Department of Labor identifying the independent contractor relationship and documenting the basis for independent-contractor classification. Most parties find the documentation burden of the affirmative defense substantial and prefer to structure their workforce relationships as W-2 employment where the ABC test is difficult to satisfy.

### 5.7 Penalties for failure to maintain workers' compensation coverage

Failure of a Maryland employer to maintain mandatory workers' compensation coverage exposes the employer to [42][43][44][45][101][102][103][106]:

- **Civil penalties.** The WCC may impose civil penalties of up to **$10,000 per employee not covered**, plus retroactive premium for the uncovered period and the cost of any benefits paid by the Uninsured Employers' Fund (see below).
- **Criminal penalties** under Lab. & Empl. § 9-1004 — failure to insure is a **misdemeanor** punishable by fine and/or imprisonment; willful or repeat violations are subject to enhanced penalties.
- **Personal liability of corporate officers.** Corporate officers may be held personally liable for the corporation's failure to insure under principles similar to those applied in unemployment insurance and wage and hour enforcement.
- **Direct tort exposure.** An uninsured employer loses the workers' compensation exclusive-remedy defense and faces direct tort exposure under common-law negligence to the injured worker.
- **Uninsured Employers' Fund** — Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 10-301 et seq. The Uninsured Employers' Fund (UEF) may pay benefits to an injured worker whose employer was uninsured and may seek recoupment from the uninsured employer, including penalty assessments and reimbursement of all benefits paid.
- **Disqualification from public-works contracting** under prevailing wage and procurement regulations.
- **Stop-work orders** under Lab. & Empl. § 3-907 (as the workers' comp violation typically coincides with misclassification under the MWFA).

## 6. Recent legislative changes (2024–2026)

### 6.1 MHIC Guaranty Fund cap — historical context and recent administrative refinement

The MHIC Guaranty Fund cap was raised from $20,000 to $30,000 per claim by Ch. 481, Laws of Maryland 2018 (effective October 1, 2018), with corresponding amendments to the contractor contribution rates. The $30,000 cap and $200,000 lifetime contractor aggregate cap have remained in place since 2018. Several bills introduced in the 2024 and 2025 Regular Sessions of the General Assembly have proposed increasing the per-claim cap to $50,000, but none has been enacted as of May 2026. ContractorPro should monitor mgaleg.maryland.gov for HB / SB updates affecting Bus. Reg. § 8-405 [48][49][50][51][52][53].

### 6.2 Workplace Fraud Act enforcement — ongoing

The Maryland Workplace Fraud Act of 2009 (Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 3-901 et seq.) and the related stop-work order infrastructure have continued to generate substantial enforcement activity through 2024 and 2025. The Maryland Department of Labor's Division of Labor and Industry publishes periodic reports on construction-industry misclassification findings; multiple investigations have resulted in stop-work orders, six-figure penalties, and referrals to the Comptroller and the WCC for back tax / back premium recovery. ContractorPro should treat the MWFA ABC test (Lab. & Empl. § 3-903) as a continuing high-risk compliance area, especially for any contractor that engages 1099 labor [107][108][109][110][111][112][113].

### 6.3 Mechanics' Lien Law — stable through 2024–2025

The **Maryland Mechanics' Lien Law** at Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-101 et seq. has remained substantively stable through the 2024 and 2025 General Assembly sessions. Several bills have been introduced in recent sessions to address specific procedural issues (including a 2024 bill (HB 638) to clarify Notice of Intent service requirements for owner-occupied dwellings, and a 2025 bill (SB 412) to address the 15% threshold calculation for major renovations), but neither has advanced to enactment as of May 2026.

### 6.4 Prompt Payment Act — Real Prop. § 9-301 et seq. and State Fin. & Proc. § 15-225

The Maryland Prompt Payment Act for private projects and the public-works prompt-payment provisions have remained stable through 2024 and 2025 with no major statutory changes. The 1.5% per month / 18% per annum interest rate under Real Prop. § 9-304 remains the highest statutory prompt-pay interest rate in the Mid-Atlantic [72][73][74][75][76][77][78][79][80][81][82].

### 6.5 Sales tax — Tax-Gen. § 11-101 et seq.

The 6% sales-and-use tax rate has remained stable since 2008. No general expansion of taxable services to include construction labor has been enacted, and proposals in the 2024 General Assembly session to expand the sales tax base to selected services did not advance. Maryland continues to treat construction labor on real property as **non-taxable**, with the contractor remaining the **consumer** of materials [88][89][90][91][92][93][94][95].

### 6.6 Corporate income tax — Tax-Gen. § 10-105

The Maryland corporate income tax rate has remained at **8.25%** through 2024 and 2025. Proposals in recent General Assembly sessions to raise the rate have not advanced. Maryland's combined corporate income tax burden (8.25% state corporate income tax, plus the personal income tax effectively imposed on pass-through entity owners' share of business income at a 5.75% top state rate plus 2.25%-3.20% local rate) remains among the higher state tax burdens in the country but is meaningfully lower than New Jersey's effective 11.5% on large C-corps.

### 6.7 MHIC examination — administrative updates

PSI Services LLC continues to administer the MHIC contractor examination on behalf of the Maryland Department of Labor's Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing. Periodic updates to examination content reflect changes in Maryland home improvement law, building codes, and consumer protection regulations. No major changes to the examination structure are pending as of May 2026.

### 6.8 Workers' compensation — annual NCCI rate filings

NCCI files annual workers' compensation rate adjustments with the Maryland Insurance Administration. The most recent approved rate filing (effective January 1, 2025) included moderate adjustments for several construction-industry classifications. Construction classifications routinely run between $3 and $10 per $100 of payroll for the major trades [42][43][44][101][102][103].

## 7. Practical compliance checklist — the gotchas a casual contractor would miss in Maryland

### 7.1 MHIC licensure is mandatory for residential home improvement — and unlicensed contracts are VOID

- **Every residential home improvement contractor** must hold an MHIC license under Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. § 8-301 — there is a $500 annual de minimis floor (§ 1.2.1), but otherwise the licensure requirement applies broadly.
- **Both the contractor and any salesperson** must be separately licensed — Bus. Reg. § 8-305 requires every individual who solicits, sells, or negotiates a home improvement contract to hold a salesperson license (§ 1.2.2). Unlicensed sales activity is a per se violation.
- **Unlicensed home improvement contracts are VOID and UNENFORCEABLE** by the contractor under the Harry Berenter, Inc. v. Berman doctrine — the consumer may keep the work, refuse to pay, and recover any amounts already paid as restitution; the unlicensed contractor has no cause of action for the contract price or quantum meruit (§ 1.2.6).
- **Unlicensed work is a misdemeanor** — up to $1,000 fine (first offense), up to $5,000 fine and 2 years' imprisonment (subsequent offenses) (§ 1.2.6).
- **Maximum deposit on any home improvement contract is one-third (33.3%)** of the total contract price under Bus. Reg. § 8-501(c) — one of the strictest deposit caps in the United States; collecting more than one-third up front is a per se violation (§ 1.2.5).

### 7.2 MHIC Guaranty Fund is the consumer-protection backstop

- Every MHIC licensee pays **$100 initial + $50 biennial renewal** into the Home Improvement Guaranty Fund under Bus. Reg. § 8-405 (§ 1.9).
- Consumers may recover up to **$30,000 per claim** (raised from $20,000 by Ch. 481, Laws of Maryland 2018) for actual losses caused by contractor's willful failure to perform, knowing misrepresentation, fraud, or substantial breach (§ 1.9).
- **Lifetime aggregate cap of $200,000 per contractor** — once the Fund pays $200,000 in claims arising from a single contractor's work, no further claims are payable from the Fund (§ 1.9).
- **Subrogation triggers immediate license suspension** under Bus. Reg. § 8-408 until full Fund reimbursement (§ 1.9).
- The Fund is the principal enforcement backstop and is why MHIC compliance is a real operational priority, not just a paperwork exercise.

### 7.3 Mechanics' liens are JUDICIAL — petition to circuit court, not recorded lien

- Maryland mechanics' liens are **NOT created by recording** with the county clerk; they are created by **court order** following a Petition to Establish Mechanics' Lien filed in the Circuit Court for the county where the property is located under Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-105 and Md. Rule 12-302 (§ 2.1, § 2.5). This is fundamentally different from New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Virginia, and most other states.
- **Petition must be filed within 180 days** of the petitioner's last work or materials (§ 2.5).
- **Show-cause hearing** typically within 15 days of filing; court enters an interlocutory order establishing the lien only if probable validity is shown (§ 2.6).
- **Final order** after evidentiary hearing under Md. Rule 12-306 establishes the lien for collection / foreclosure (§ 2.6).
- Foreclosure must be brought within **12 years** of the date the lien was established (§ 2.8).

### 7.4 Subcontractors must serve Notice of Intent within 120 days

- Every subcontractor (not in direct contract with the owner) must serve a **Notice of Intent to Claim a Mechanics' Lien** on the property owner **within 120 days** of the subcontractor's last work or materials under Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-104 (§ 2.4).
- The 120-day window runs from the subcontractor's own last work — not from the prime contractor's last work or project completion.
- Service may be by personal service, certified mail, or (if those fail) posting on the property.
- **Owner's "amount unpaid to prime" defense** — the subcontractor's lien is limited to the amount the owner owes the prime at the time the Notice of Intent is served; an owner who has fully paid the prime in good faith before notice has a complete defense (§ 2.4).
- **Owner-occupied single-family dwellings** require a **30-day waiting period** after Notice of Intent before the petition may be filed (§ 2.4).

### 7.5 The 15% threshold for existing-building repair / improvement

- Mechanics' liens on **existing buildings** (as distinguished from new construction) require that the work have **increased the building's value by at least 15%** under Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-102(a)(2) (§ 2.3).
- **Tenant-occupied family dwellings** are completely excluded from the mechanics' lien regime — no lien rights regardless of work value (§ 2.3).
- The 15% threshold is a significant deterrent to small-claim residential lien practice; contractors performing small repair or maintenance work should focus on contract-based collection and Prompt Payment Act claims rather than mechanics' lien enforcement.

### 7.6 Pre-work lien waivers are void

- Under Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-113(b), any waiver of lien rights executed **before the work giving rise to the lien is performed** is **void as against public policy** (§ 2.9).
- This is one of the strongest pre-work waiver prohibitions in the United States and prevents primes and owners from extracting blanket pre-work waivers at the contracting stage.
- Contractors should refuse to execute pre-work lien waivers and should only execute progress-payment waivers tied to specific work performed and specific payment received.

### 7.7 Trade licensing — statewide for electrical, plumbing, HVAC

- **Electrical contractors** must hold a Master Electrician license under Md. Code Ann., Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 6-301; requires 7 years' experience (including 2 as journeyperson), PSI exam, $300K GL minimum, 18 hours of CE per biennial cycle (§ 1.3).
- **Plumbers** must hold a Master Plumber license under Md. Code Ann., Bus. Occ. & Prof. § 12-301; requires 7 years' experience (including 2 as journeyperson), PSI exam, $300K GL minimum, 15 hours of CE per biennial cycle (§ 1.4).
- **HVACR contractors** must hold a Master HVACR Contractor license under Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. § 9A-301; requires 3 years' journeyperson experience, PSI exam + EPA Section 608, $300K GL minimum, 6 hours of CE per biennial cycle (§ 1.5).
- Trade licensure does **not** substitute for MHIC licensure when performing residential work — trade-licensed contractors performing residential remodeling must also hold the MHIC contractor license (§ 1.2.1).
- **Local trade licensing** persists in Baltimore City, Montgomery County, Prince George's County, and several other jurisdictions alongside state credentialing (§ 1.3, § 1.6).

### 7.8 Commercial general contracting — no statewide license; municipal patchwork

- Maryland has **no statewide general contractor license** for commercial work (§ 1.6).
- Commercial GCs operating in multiple Maryland jurisdictions must register with each county's permitting office: **Baltimore City** (Department of Housing and Community Development), **Baltimore County** (Department of Permits, Approvals and Inspections), **Montgomery County** (Department of Permitting Services), **Prince George's County** (DPIE), **Howard County**, **Anne Arundel County**, **Frederick County**, **Harford County**, and **Charles County** all maintain separate registration and permit programs (§ 1.6).
- **New home construction** requires separate Maryland Home Builder Registration under Md. Code Ann., Bus. Reg. § 4.5-101 et seq. — not the MHIC home improvement license (§ 1.7).

### 7.9 Workers' compensation — mandatory from the first employee; statutory employer doctrine

- Maryland is a **private-market workers' compensation state** with **no minimum employee threshold** — every employer with one or more employees must carry workers' comp (Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 9-202). Coverage is purchased through admitted carriers or Chesapeake Employers' Insurance Company as the state-affiliated residual-market carrier (§ 5.1, § 5.2).
- The **statutory employer doctrine** under Lab. & Empl. § 9-508 makes a principal contractor liable for workers' comp benefits to an uninsured sub's injured employees — the principal compliance discipline is obtaining and verifying COI from every sub before site access (§ 5.5).
- Failure to insure is a **misdemeanor** with civil penalties up to **$10,000 per uncovered employee**, retroactive premium liability, and Uninsured Employers' Fund recoupment (§ 5.7).

### 7.10 Worker misclassification — Workplace Fraud Act ABC test

- The **Maryland Workplace Fraud Act of 2009** (Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 3-901 et seq.) imposes a strict three-part ABC test for independent-contractor classification in the construction services industry — most subcontractors performing construction services for a GC will **fail prong C** (the work is within the usual course of the GC's business) and be presumptively classified as employees (§ 5.6).
- Misclassification exposure includes **stop-work orders**, **civil penalties up to $5,000-$20,000 per worker**, **back unemployment insurance contributions**, **back workers' comp premium**, and **treble back wages under the Maryland Wage Payment and Collection Law** (§ 5.6).
- ContractorPro should treat 1099 labor in the construction services industry as a high-risk compliance area requiring proactive bona-fide-independent-contractor documentation or restructuring to W-2 employment.

### 7.11 Prompt Payment Act — 30/7 framework with 18% annual interest

- Under **Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 9-302**, owners must pay properly submitted invoices within **30 days** (or contract period, if specified); primes must pay subs within **7 days** of receipt of corresponding owner payment under § 9-303 (§ 3.1).
- Late payment triggers **interest at 1.5% per month / 18% per annum** under § 9-304 — among the highest statutory prompt-pay interest rates in the country (§ 3.1).
- **Attorney fees to the prevailing party** under § 9-305 (§ 3.1).
- **Right to suspend performance on 7 days' written notice** for undisputed payment defaults under § 9-303(d) — non-waivable by contract (§ 3.1).
- Public-works prompt-pay under **State Fin. & Proc. § 15-225** (owner-to-prime) and **§ 15-226** (prime-to-sub 10-day pass-through) (§ 3.2).
- **Home improvement contracts** subject to MHIC licensing are **carved out** from the Prompt Payment Act under § 9-301(b) — payment-timing disputes between MHIC contractors and homeowners are governed by the contract and the Home Improvement Law's consumer-protection framework, not the Prompt Payment Act (§ 3.1).

### 7.12 Sales tax — contractor is consumer; labor on real property NOT taxable

- Statewide sales-and-use tax rate is **6%** with **no local sales tax add-ons** (uniform since 2008); contractor pays sales tax on materials at point of purchase and does not charge separate sales tax to the owner on materials (§ 4.1, § 4.2).
- **Labor on real-property construction is NOT subject to sales tax** in Maryland — a simpler model than New Jersey's capital-improvement-vs-repair distinction. There is no Maryland equivalent of New Jersey's Form ST-8 Capital Improvement Certificate (§ 4.2).
- Tax-exempt customers (governmental units, 501(c)(3)s, schools, hospitals): contractor uses the customer's exemption certificate to purchase materials tax-free for the specific project (§ 4.3).
- **Use tax** (also 6%) applies to materials purchased outside Maryland (e.g., Delaware, no sales tax) and used in Maryland projects — Maryland contractors must self-assess and remit (§ 4.4).

### 7.13 County local income tax — additional 2.25% to 3.20%

- Maryland is one of the few states with a **county-level local income tax** piggybacking on the state personal income tax under Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 10-103 (§ 4.6).
- Local rates range from **2.25% to 3.20%** of taxable income; **Baltimore City, Montgomery County, Prince George's County, and Howard County** are at the **3.20% top rate** (§ 4.6).
- Combined state and local effective top rate is **5.75% + 3.20% = 8.95%** for residents of the high-rate counties — significant for pass-through entity owners (LLC members, S-corp shareholders) of construction firms.
- The local tax is administered by the Comptroller as part of state income tax collection.

### 7.14 Public-works payment bond on state contracts above $100,000

- Public-works prime contractors must furnish a **100% payment bond and 100% performance bond** on state contracts above **$100,000** under the Maryland Little Miller Act, Md. Code Ann., State Fin. & Proc. § 17-101 et seq. (§ 2.10).
- Sub / supplier claims on the public-works payment bond require **Notice of Claim** served within **90 days** of last furnishing, and **suit filed within 1 year** of last furnishing (§ 2.10).
- **No mechanics' lien rights against publicly owned property** under Real Prop. § 9-103 — bond claim is the exclusive recovery vehicle for unpaid subs and suppliers on public projects (§ 2.10).
