## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Georgia structure — a state-licensed general contractor with separately-boarded trades

Georgia, unlike Texas, **does require a statewide general contractor license** for work above a statutory threshold [1][2][3]. The licensing authority is the **State Licensing Board for Residential and General Contractors (SLBRGC)** within the Georgia Secretary of State's Professional Licensing Boards Division, established by the Residential and General Contractors Act, **O.C.G.A. § 43-41-1 et seq.** [1][4][5]. The five mechanical and electrical trades that ContrPro routinely encounters — electrical, plumbing, HVAC (conditioned air), low-voltage, and utility — are licensed under a **separate statute**, **O.C.G.A. § 43-14-1 et seq.**, and a **separate umbrella board**, the **State Construction Industry Licensing Board (SCILB)** (also referred to in agency publications as the "Construction Industry Licensing Board" or "CILB"), which contains divisions for each trade [6][7][8]. Structural-steel erection has **no Georgia state license** at all; competency is established through AISC, AWS, and project-specification routes [9][10].

For ContrPro's five trades, the practical map is:

| Trade | Statewide license? | Board | Statutory authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| General contracting (commercial) | **Yes** — Commercial General Contractor (Unlimited) and Limited Tier | SLBRGC (Secretary of State) | O.C.G.A. § 43-41-1 et seq. [4][5] |
| Residential building | **Yes** — Residential-Basic and Residential-Light Commercial | SLBRGC | O.C.G.A. § 43-41-1 et seq. [4][5] |
| Electrical contracting | **Yes** — Class I (Restricted) and Class II (Unrestricted) | Division of Electrical Contractors (SCILB) | O.C.G.A. § 43-14-1 et seq. [7][11] |
| Plumbing | **Yes** — Master Plumber Class I (Restricted) and Class II (Unrestricted), plus Journeyman | Division of Master and Journeyman Plumbers (SCILB) | O.C.G.A. § 43-14-1 et seq. [7][12] |
| HVAC | **Yes** — Conditioned Air Contractor Class I (Restricted) and Class II (Unrestricted) | Division of Conditioned Air Contractors (SCILB) | O.C.G.A. § 43-14-1 et seq. [7][13] |
| Structural steel erection | **No state license** | Local building department; AISC/AWS by contract | Industry standard, building code |

### 1.2 The $2,500 / $10,000 / $100,000 thresholds — the gateway

The Georgia licensing statute is **threshold-driven**, and missing the threshold reading is the single most common reason an unlicensed Georgia contractor finds itself with an unenforceable contract under **O.C.G.A. § 43-41-17(b)** [14][15][16]:

- **Residential-Basic Contractor:** Required for construction or renovation of **detached one- and two-family residential dwellings** and **multifamily residential structures of up to four units**, where the total cost is **$2,500 or more**, except as exempted under § 43-41-17 [14][15][16][17].
- **Residential-Light Commercial Contractor:** Required for the above scope **plus** light-commercial structures up to **three stories above grade**, with limitations on size and complexity. Pre-engineered structural steel buildings up to **50,000 square feet of interior floor space** fall within this class [15][16][18].
- **General Contractor (Commercial — Unlimited):** Required for commercial work where the total cost equals or exceeds **$100,000**, unlimited as to type or size of project; permits the holder to perform any work for which a Title 43 license is required, including (by statute) electrical, plumbing, conditioned-air, low-voltage, and utility contracting [5][15][16][18][19].
- **General Contractor — Limited Tier:** Required for commercial work above the $100,000 threshold but **capped at $500,000 per project** and **$1,500,000 aggregate** annual contract volume; intended for smaller commercial GCs that do not yet meet the financial qualifications for the unlimited license [16][18].
- **Below-threshold work:** A person performing residential work of total value **less than $2,500** is not required to hold a state contractor license under § 43-41-17(b). The exemption does **not** cover any work that requires a separate trade license under Title 43 Chapter 14 — touching electrical, plumbing, HVAC, or low-voltage systems requires the appropriate trade license **regardless of project value** [15][16][20].

**Unenforceable-contract penalty.** Under O.C.G.A. § 43-41-17(b), a contract entered into by a person who is required to be licensed under Chapter 41 but is **not** licensed is **unenforceable in law or in equity** by the unlicensed contractor [14][15][16]. The contractor cannot sue for the contract price, cannot file a mechanic's lien, and cannot recover quantum meruit. The owner, by contrast, may recover. The defense is mandatory and cannot be waived by contract. Georgia courts have repeatedly enforced § 43-41-17(b) to bar recovery by contractors that lacked the right class of license at the time the contract was executed — including cases in which the contractor obtained the license later [14][15].

### 1.3 SLBRGC — Commercial General Contractor (Unlimited)

**Statutory authority:** O.C.G.A. § 43-41-1 through § 43-41-18 [4][5]. **Administrative rules:** **Georgia Compiled Rules and Regulations Chapter 553** (specifically Chapters 553-1 through 553-12) [5][18][19]. **Licensing agency:** State Licensing Board for Residential and General Contractors, 237 Coliseum Drive, Macon, GA 31217-3858; mailing address Professional Licensing Boards Division, 237 Coliseum Drive, Macon, GA 31217-3858; (478) 207-2440 [1][5].

**Eligibility requirements** (Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. r. 553-4) [18][19][21]:

1. **Age:** At least **21 years** old.
2. **Experience:** At least **four (4) years of construction experience** in commercial general contracting work, of which at least **two (2) years** must have been in a **supervisory** capacity (project manager, project superintendent, owner-officer in charge of construction, or equivalent) [18][19][21]. Experience is documented through a Project List attached to the application.
3. **References:** **Three (3) notarized reference forms** from persons attesting to the applicant's character and construction experience. References must demonstrate familiarity with the applicant's qualifying experience and may be from owners, developers, GCs, design professionals, or other industry parties; references from immediate family members are not accepted [18][19][21].
4. **Financial responsibility:** A **CPA-prepared financial statement** demonstrating **minimum net worth of $150,000** [18][19][21][22]. The financial statement must be either an audit, a review, or a compilation with disclosures, and must be signed by a Georgia-licensed CPA. For first-time applications, the statement must be dated no more than 12 months prior to filing.
5. **Insurance:** Certificate of commercial general liability insurance with **minimum coverage of $500,000** per occurrence, on file with SLBRGC at the time of application and continuously thereafter [21][22][23].
6. **Examination:** Pass the **NASCLA Accredited Commercial Building Contractor Examination** (a 115-question, open-book trade exam covering structural, mechanical, code, OSHA, and project-management content) plus the **Georgia Business and Law Examination** (50-question, open-book, covering Georgia construction law, licensing rules, liens, and prompt payment). Both administered by PSI; minimum passing score **70%** [22][24].
7. **Fees:** Application fee **$200**; exam fees vary by provider (NASCLA approximately $106; Business and Law approximately $60–$100). Initial license fee assessed at issuance [22][23].

**Renewal cycle.** **Biennial** — every two years, due by **June 30** of even-numbered years (O.C.G.A. § 43-41-7). The 2026 renewal cycle opened **April 6, 2026**; licenses expire **June 30, 2026** [3][22][23].

**Continuing education.** Effective with the 2024 renewal cycle, Commercial General Contractors must complete **eight (8) hours** of approved continuing education per two-year renewal cycle [3][25]. **Beginning January 1, 2026**, all SLBRGC licensees must register their CE credits in **CE Broker** [3][25]. Failure to record CE in CE Broker, even if hours have been completed, is treated as a renewal deficiency.

**Reciprocity.** SLBRGC has **no formal reciprocity agreements** with other states' contractor boards. However, an applicant who holds a current general contractor license in another state of comparable scope may receive **waiver of the NASCLA trade exam** if that state recognizes the NASCLA exam; the Georgia Business and Law exam must still be passed [3][22].

### 1.4 SLBRGC — General Contractor Limited Tier

A second commercial tier — **General Contractor — Limited Tier** — was created by SLBRGC to allow contractors with experience but smaller balance sheets to access commercial work [16][18][26].

**Scope** (Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. r. 553-4-.05) [18][26]: The Limited Tier license permits commercial general contracting work with the following caps:

- **Maximum single project value:** **$500,000** (including any change orders).
- **Maximum aggregate volume:** **$1,500,000** in any 12-month period.

**Eligibility** [18][26]:

- Age 21+, three (3) notarized references, four (4) years of experience (two supervisory) — identical to the unlimited license.
- **Minimum net worth $25,000** (CPA-prepared, dated within 12 months).
- **GL insurance minimum:** $300,000 per occurrence (vs. $500,000 for the unlimited tier).
- Pass the same NASCLA and Georgia Business and Law exams.

**Application fee** $200. Biennial renewal; same CE structure as unlimited tier [18][22][26].

### 1.5 SLBRGC — Residential-Basic and Residential-Light Commercial

**Residential-Basic Contractor** (Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. r. 553-3) [18][27][28]:

- **Scope:** Detached one- and two-family residential dwellings and multifamily residential structures of up to four units (with separate utility services). Total cost $2,500 or more.
- **Experience:** At least **two (2) years** of construction experience in residential work, with at least one year having been in a supervisory capacity.
- **Net worth (affirmed, not CPA-attested):** **$25,000** [18][22][28].
- **GL insurance:** **$300,000** per occurrence on residential GL [22][23][28].
- **Examination:** Georgia Residential-Basic Contractor Trade Exam and Georgia Business and Law Exam; minimum 70% [22][24].
- **Application fee:** $200.

**Residential-Light Commercial Contractor** (Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. r. 553-3-.02) [18][29]:

- **Scope:** All residential-basic scope, plus light-commercial structures up to three stories above grade; pre-engineered metal buildings up to **50,000 square feet** interior floor space; tenant build-outs and renovations within scope.
- **Experience:** **Three (3) years** in residential or light-commercial work, with at least one year supervisory.
- **Net worth (affirmed):** **$25,000** [18][28][29].
- **GL insurance:** **$500,000** per occurrence [22][29].
- **Examination:** Georgia Residential-Light Commercial Contractor Trade Exam and Georgia Business and Law Exam; minimum 70%.
- **Application fee:** $200.

**Continuing education for Residential-Basic and Residential-Light Commercial.** Effective with the 2024 renewal cycle, residential licensees must complete **eight (8) hours of approved CE per two-year cycle**, of which at least three hours must be in topics specific to residential construction (energy codes, the Georgia Amendments to the IRC, lead-paint, asbestos, or moisture control). **Beginning January 1, 2026**, all CE must be recorded in **CE Broker** [3][25].

**The handyman trap.** A person performing repairs, maintenance, or improvements on a residential dwelling for less than $2,500 in total project value is exempt from SLBRGC licensing under § 43-41-17(b)(1). The exemption **does not** allow the handyman to (a) perform any work for which a Title 43 Chapter 14 license is required (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, low-voltage), or (b) advertise as a "contractor" — the term is statutorily restricted to license holders [16][17]. Crossing either trip-wire converts the handyman into an unlicensed contractor under § 43-41-17(a) and renders the contract unenforceable [15][16][17].

### 1.6 Conditioned Air Contractor (HVAC) — O.C.G.A. § 43-14

**Statutory authority:** O.C.G.A. § 43-14-1 through § 43-14-18 [7][30]. **Administrative rules:** Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. Chapter 123 [13]. **Licensing agency:** Division of Conditioned Air Contractors within the State Construction Industry Licensing Board (Secretary of State Professional Licensing Boards Division), 237 Coliseum Drive, Macon, GA 31217 [1][13][30].

**Statutory mandate** (O.C.G.A. § 43-14-8(c)): "No person shall engage in the business of conditioned air contracting as a conditioned air contractor unless such person has a valid license from the Division of Conditioned Air Contractors" [7][30][31]. Georgia has required HVAC contractor licensure continuously **since 1949** [30][32].

**License classes** [13][30][31][32]:

| Class | Scope | Experience |
|---|---|---|
| **Class I (Restricted)** | Heating systems **175,000 BTU/hour or less** and cooling systems **5 tons or less** | **At least 4 years** of practical installation experience |
| **Class II (Unrestricted)** | Heating and cooling of **any size** | **At least 5 years** of practical installation experience, including experience on systems exceeding 175,000 BTU/hour heating or 5 tons cooling |

**Qualifying agent — businesses.** Under **O.C.G.A. § 43-14-8(h)–(j)**, no partnership, limited liability company, or corporation may engage in conditioned air contracting unless a person who actually holds a Conditioned Air license, on a **full-time basis**, is "regularly connected with" the entity as a **qualifying agent** [30][33]. The qualifying agent's license must be tied to the entity in SCILB records; if the qualifying agent leaves, the entity has limited time to designate a replacement before its right to contract is suspended.

**Examination:** PSI-administered closed-book exam covering the Georgia Mechanical Code, Georgia Fuel Gas Code, the International Residential Code mechanical chapters, the State Minimum Standard Codes, and the Georgia laws and rules governing conditioned air contracting. Minimum passing score 70%. Three (3) references required, including at least one (1) reference from a licensed conditioned-air contractor [13][32][34].

**Fees** [13][32][35]:

- Application/exam fee: approximately **$80** application + PSI exam fee.
- Initial license fee: **$75–$100** (depends on the license cycle).
- **Biennial renewal: $75–$100**, due by **November 30 of odd-numbered years** [32][35][36].

**Insurance and bond — state level.** **No state-level GL minimum and no state surety bond** are imposed by the Division of Conditioned Air Contractors for the license itself; municipal permit requirements may add bonds and insurance certificates [32][35][37].

**Continuing education.** Effective beginning the renewal cycle following adoption, Conditioned Air Contractors must complete **four (4) hours of CE per year** (8 hours per renewal cycle) [13][38]. As with the SLBRGC trades, CE must be recorded in **CE Broker beginning January 1, 2026** [25][38].

**Penalty for unlicensed practice.** **O.C.G.A. § 43-14-12** — engaging in conditioned-air contracting without a license is a **misdemeanor** for each violation, plus civil administrative penalties up to **$500 per day per violation** under § 43-14-12.1 [30][39][40]. **Senate Bill 125 (2025) substantially strengthened the SCILB's cease-and-desist authority and financial-penalty caps**, effective July 1, 2025 — see §5.3 below [41][42].

### 1.7 Electrical Contractor — O.C.G.A. § 43-14 (Title 43 Chapter 14)

**Statutory authority:** O.C.G.A. § 43-14-1 through § 43-14-18 [7][30]. **Administrative rules:** Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. Chapter 121 (governing SCILB qualifications generally) and Chapter 122 historically (and 121-3 since reorganization) [11][43]. **Licensing agency:** Division of Electrical Contractors within SCILB, Secretary of State Professional Licensing Boards Division [1][11].

**Important structural feature — Georgia licenses contracting, not individuals.** Unlike Texas (Master/Journeyman/Apprentice individual tiers) or New York (Master Electrician individual licensure), **Georgia issues only Electrical Contractor licenses to the person who holds out to engage in the business of electrical contracting**. There is **no statewide Journeyman Electrician license** [11][44][45]. Individual electricians work under the supervision of an Electrical Contractor licensee. A few Georgia municipalities (notably Savannah, Augusta-Richmond, and Columbus) do issue local journeyman cards for permit-pulling purposes, but these are local, not state, credentials.

**License classes** (Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. r. 121-3-.01) [11][44][45]:

| Class | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Class I (Restricted)** | Electrical contracting limited to **single-family dwellings of up to three levels and multifamily structures of not more than two levels**, with **single-phase electrical installations not exceeding 400 amperes** at the service drop or service lateral |
| **Class II (Unrestricted)** | All electrical contracting, no size or service-amperage limit; required for any commercial work and for any multifamily building exceeding two stories |

**Experience requirements** under Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. r. 121-3-.01 [11][44][45]:

- **Class I:** **Four (4) years of Primary Experience** in at least six (6) of the Primary Experience categories defined in the rule. Primary Experience means hands-on installation work covered by the National Electrical Code or Georgia Amendments thereto, under a licensed Class I or II contractor.
- **Class II:** **Four (4) years of Primary Experience in all Primary Experience categories**, plus experience with single-phase installations exceeding 400 amperes or three-phase work, while working under a Class II Unrestricted contractor.

**References:** Three (3) on the prescribed form. At least one (1) must be from a licensed electrical contractor and must include the contractor's license number [11][44].

**Qualifying agent.** Same § 43-14-8(h)–(j) rule as conditioned air — a partnership, LLC, or corporation must have a full-time qualifying agent who holds the Electrical Contractor license [7][30][33].

**Examination:** PSI-administered exam covering the National Electrical Code (NEC) (current edition adopted by the State Construction Industry Licensing Board), the Georgia Amendments, electrical theory, electrical calculations, and Georgia laws and rules. Open-book for the NEC portion; minimum passing score 70%. Application fee $80; exam fee approximately $89 [11][44][46].

**Fees** [44][46][47]:

- Application fee: **$80**.
- Initial license fee: **$75–$100**.
- **Biennial renewal: $75–$100**, due by **June 30 of even-numbered years** [44][46][47].

**Insurance and bond — state level.** **None imposed at the state level** by the Division of Electrical Contractors; municipal jurisdictions may add their own bond and GL requirements [44][46][47].

**Continuing education.** Effective with current renewal cycles, electrical contractors must complete **four (4) hours of approved CE per year (8 hours per two-year renewal)** [38][47]. CE must include topics on the NEC, Georgia electrical laws and rules, and electrical safety. **Effective January 1, 2026, all CE credits must be recorded in CE Broker** [25][38][47].

**Penalty for unlicensed practice.** O.C.G.A. § 43-14-12 — misdemeanor for unlicensed practice; civil administrative penalties under § 43-14-12.1 up to $500 per day per violation. Cease-and-desist authority strengthened by **SB 125 (2025)**, effective July 1, 2025 [39][40][41][42].

### 1.8 Plumbing — Master Plumber and Journeyman Plumber, O.C.G.A. § 43-14

**Statutory authority:** O.C.G.A. § 43-14-1 through § 43-14-18 [7][30]. **Administrative rules:** Ga. Comp. R. & Regs. Chapter 121 (qualifications) and Chapter 122 (plumber-specific rules) [12][48][49]. **Licensing agency:** Division of Master and Journeyman Plumbers within SCILB [1][12].

**License types** [12][48][49][50]:

| License | Scope | Experience |
|---|---|---|
| **Journeyman Plumber** | Plumbing work under the supervision of a Master Plumber | Documented experience in plumbing covered by the Georgia State Plumbing Code; exam required |
| **Master Plumber Class I (Restricted)** | Plumbing on **single-family dwellings, two-family dwellings (one level)**, and **commercial structures up to 10,000 square feet** | **Five (5) years** of plumbing experience, of which at least **two (2) years** as a licensed Georgia Journeyman Plumber |
| **Master Plumber Class II (Unrestricted)** | All plumbing — no size or use limit | Five (5) years of plumbing experience, of which at least two (2) years as a Georgia Journeyman Plumber, **plus documented experience in commercial or industrial plumbing** |

**References:** Three (3) notarized references on the prescribed form. **Class I:** at least one (1) reference from a licensed Master Plumber. **Class II:** at least two (2) references from licensed Master Plumbers [48][50].

**Qualifying agent.** Same § 43-14-8(h)–(j) rule — a partnership, LLC, or corporation must have a full-time qualifying agent who holds the Master Plumber license. The Journeyman license does not qualify the entity to engage in the business of plumbing contracting; only the Master Plumber license does [7][30][48].

**Examination:** PSI-administered exam covering the Georgia State Plumbing Code (which adopts the International Plumbing Code with Georgia Amendments), the Georgia Fuel Gas Code (for plumbing-related fuel-gas piping), and Georgia laws and rules. Open-book; minimum passing score 70%. Three references required as above [12][48][50].

**Fees** [48][50]:

- Application fee: **$80**.
- Initial license fee: **$75–$100**.
- **Biennial renewal: $75–$100**, due by **June 30 of even-numbered years** [48][50].

**Insurance and bond — state level.** **None imposed at the state level**; municipal jurisdictions may impose [48][50].

**Continuing education.** Master Plumbers and Journeyman Plumbers must complete **four (4) hours of approved CE per year (8 hours per two-year renewal)** [38][50]. CE must include the Georgia State Plumbing Code, plumbing laws and rules, and water-protection topics. **Effective January 1, 2026, all CE must be recorded in CE Broker** [25][38].

**Backflow prevention.** Georgia also recognizes a **Backflow Prevention Tester certification** under the Georgia Safe Drinking Water Act administered by the Department of Natural Resources, Environmental Protection Division. This is a separate credential from the Master Plumber license but is required for backflow-prevention assembly testing on public water systems.

### 1.9 Low-Voltage Contractor and Utility Contractor

Title 43 Chapter 14 also covers two additional contractor classes that ContrPro contractors occasionally encounter [7][30]:

- **Low-Voltage Contractor** — categorized as LV-A (alarm), LV-T (telecommunications), LV-G (general), LV-U (unrestricted). Required for systems carrying less than 50 volts (fire alarms, security, voice/data cabling, structured wiring). Administered by the Division of Low-Voltage Contractors. **Senate Bill 125 (2025) restructured the low-voltage license classifications**, effective July 1, 2025 [41][42][51].
- **Utility Contractor** — Required for installation of underground utility infrastructure (water, sewer, stormwater, gas, telecom) outside the building envelope. Two utility-manager classifications, with safety-training requirements amended by SB 125 [41][42].

ContrPro's five-trade focus does not normally trigger these licenses, but a contractor doing tenant build-outs that include fire-alarm wiring or low-voltage controls must hold an LV license or sub the work to a licensed LV contractor.

### 1.10 Structural steel — no Georgia state license

Georgia does **not** issue a contractor license specifically for structural-steel erection [9][10][52]. Competency is established through:

1. **AISC Certified Erector / AISC Certified Fabricator** — the American Institute of Steel Construction's quality-management certifications under AISC 207/AISC 303-22 [10][53]. Required by most large commercial specifications and by GDOT bridge-steel specifications.
2. **AWS welder qualification** — individual welder qualifications under **AWS D1.1** (structural steel) or **AWS D1.5** (bridge welding) [52][54]. GDOT (Georgia Department of Transportation) Standard Specifications, Section 855, require AWS D1.5 qualifications for state bridge welding.
3. **AWS Certified Welding Inspector (CWI)** — typically required for inspection on commercial and DOT projects.
4. **OSHA 10/30** — typically a contract requirement, not a state mandate.
5. **Special inspections** — Georgia adopts the IBC's Chapter 17 special-inspection regime through the **State Minimum Standard Codes** (110-11-1) [55]. Structural-steel erection on most commercial buildings requires an independent special inspector engaged by the owner.

A structural-steel erector working below the SLBRGC threshold (and not self-performing electrical, plumbing, HVAC, or low-voltage work) may operate without any Georgia state license, provided the steel work is performed under permit through the municipal building department and AISC/AWS certifications are held as required by the contract documents.

### 1.11 Municipal layer — Atlanta, Savannah, Augusta-Richmond, Columbus

Because state SLBRGC licensure is required for work above the $2,500 / $100,000 thresholds, Georgia's **municipal contractor-registration layer is much lighter than Texas's** — most Georgia cities recognize the state license rather than imposing a separate registration. But several jurisdictions add their own requirements:

- **City of Atlanta** [56][57]: A state SLBRGC license is required to pull permits. Atlanta separately maintains a **business tax certificate / business license** for any business operating within the city, paid annually based on gross receipts (a municipal occupation tax, not a contractor-specific registration). Atlanta requires permit registration through the Office of Buildings.
- **Savannah** [58]: Recognizes state SLBRGC licenses but maintains a separate **trade license for electrical and plumbing journeymen** — Savannah journeyman cards are required to pull permits on local work even where the state issues only contractor (not journeyman) licenses.
- **Augusta-Richmond County** [59]: Maintains a local mechanical contractor registration and adds a $50,000 / $100,000 GL minimum for mechanical permit-pulling.
- **Other counties.** Cobb, Gwinnett, Fulton (outside Atlanta), Cherokee, DeKalb, Forsyth, and most other large counties accept state SLBRGC and SCILB licenses without additional registration. Confirm at each county building department before bidding.

### 1.12 Workers' compensation — required at 3+ employees; statutory employer exposure

Georgia workers' compensation is at **O.C.G.A. § 34-9-1 et seq.**, administered by the **State Board of Workers' Compensation (SBWC)** [60][61][62]. Coverage is required of any employer that **regularly employs three (3) or more employees**, full-time, part-time, or seasonal, except for certain exempt classes [60][61][63].

**Construction-specific statutory employer rule — O.C.G.A. § 34-9-8** [60][62][64]:

When a **general contractor** engages a subcontractor who does **not** carry the required workers' compensation insurance, the GC's policy becomes the **backstop** for injuries to that uninsured sub's employees. The GC becomes the "statutory employer" of those workers and bears liability for workers' compensation benefits to the same extent as if the GC had directly employed them. The doctrine is intentionally designed to force GCs to demand and police WC coverage from every sub.

Two practical consequences for ContrPro:

1. **Always require Certificates of Insurance evidencing WC coverage** from every sub, regardless of project size. Track expiration dates and require updated COIs.
2. **A sole-proprietor sub with no employees** is not required to carry WC, but the GC is still exposed: if that sole proprietor brings a helper or hires day labor on the job, the GC has no defense.

**Sole-proprietor / corporate-officer election.** Sole proprietors and corporate officers may elect to **be covered** under the GC's policy or **exclude themselves** from coverage. The exclusion election is filed on SBWC Form WC-10 [60][62].

**Construction-industry classification rates.** Georgia is a NCCI advisory-loss-cost state. Construction-industry classifications (5538 Heating/Air Conditioning, 5183 Plumbing, 5190 Electrical, 5057 Iron/Steel Erection, 5645 Carpentry/Single-Family) have the state's highest experience-modification volatility and typically high rates.

**Penalty for failure to carry.** Under O.C.G.A. § 34-9-126, an employer that fails to carry required WC coverage is subject to a civil penalty of not less than **$500 nor more than $5,000 per violation**, plus a 10% penalty added to any benefits paid to an injured worker. Repeat or willful violations may result in **misdemeanor charges** [60][62].

### 1.13 General liability insurance — statewide statutory minimums

Georgia imposes GL minimums **only through the SLBRGC** (not through SCILB) [21][22][23]:

| License class | GL minimum (per occurrence) |
|---|---|
| Commercial General Contractor (Unlimited) | **$500,000** |
| Commercial General Contractor (Limited Tier) | **$300,000** |
| Residential-Light Commercial Contractor | **$500,000** |
| Residential-Basic Contractor | **$300,000** |
| Conditioned Air Contractor (Class I or II) | **None at state level** |
| Electrical Contractor (Class I or II) | **None at state level** |
| Master Plumber (Class I or II) | **None at state level** |

The certificate of insurance must list SLBRGC as the certificate holder. Project owners, lenders, and municipalities commonly require substantially higher limits — $1M/$2M is the typical commercial floor; $2M/$4M is common on large commercial work; bonded public-works projects routinely require $5M+.

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-360 through § 44-14-369

Georgia mechanic's lien law is at **O.C.G.A. § 44-14-360 through § 44-14-369**, within Title 44 Chapter 14 Article 8 Part 3 ("Mechanics and Materialmen") [65][66][67]. The substantive law has been substantially reworked over the past two decades, most notably by **Senate Bill 143 (2021)**, which amended the lien-waiver and Affidavit of Nonpayment regime [68][69][70], and by the **2022 revisions** to the public-works retainage statute (§5.2 below) [71][72][73].

### 2.1 Who can claim a lien — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361

Under **O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361(a)** [66][67][74], Georgia lien rights attach to real property for the following classes of claimants:

- **General contractors, subcontractors, materialmen** — for labor, services, or materials furnished for the improvement of real property under a contract with the owner, the owner's agent, or the contractor.
- **Architects, engineers, registered foresters, landscape architects, registered surveyors** — for services rendered in connection with improvement of real property.
- **Persons furnishing rental tools, appliances, machinery, or equipment** to be used in the improvement.
- **Persons furnishing labor or materials to subcontractors of any tier**.

**Aggregate cap — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.1(e).** "In no event shall the aggregate amount of liens set up by Code Section 44-14-361 exceed the contract price of the improvements made or services performed" [66][67][75]. The cap is tied to the contract price, not the property value. If the GC's contract is $1.0M and the sum of all sub liens exceeds $1.0M, the subs share pro rata in the contract-price pool. This protects the owner from double liability when the GC's contract was firm-price.

### 2.2 Notice of Commencement — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.5

A **Notice of Commencement (NOC)** is filed by the **owner, owner's agent, or the contractor** with the **clerk of the superior court of the county where the project is located**, **not later than 15 days after the contractor physically commences work** [76][77][78]. A copy of the NOC must be **posted on the project site**.

**Required contents of the NOC** (O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.5(b)) [76][77]:

1. Name, address, and telephone number of the contractor.
2. Name and location of the project being constructed, plus the legal description of the property upon which the improvements are being made.
3. Name and address of the true owner of the property.
4. Name and address of the person other than the owner at whose instance the improvements are being made (if different from the owner — e.g., a tenant for tenant-finish work).
5. Name and address of the surety for the performance and payment bonds, if any.
6. Name and address of the construction lender, if any.

**Why an NOC matters.** When an NOC is filed, **any subcontractor or supplier who does not have direct privity of contract with the prime contractor must give a Notice to Contractor (NTC) within 30 days** of the later of (a) the NOC's filing date or (b) the claimant's first delivery of labor or materials [77][78][79][80]. Failure to give the NTC **forfeits lien rights** as to that claimant. The NOC, in other words, **opens a 30-day fuse on the supplier's right to a lien**.

**Public works.** Under **O.C.G.A. § 36-82-104(f)** and § 13-10-62 [81][82], for any public-works contract subject to the Little Miller Act, the **contractor** (rather than the public owner) is required to file a Notice of Commencement; the rules on indexing, contents, and effect of NTC parallel the private-works rules but apply for purposes of payment-bond claims rather than lien rights (you cannot lien public property).

### 2.3 Notice to Contractor — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.5(c)

A subcontractor, supplier, or other party **not in direct privity of contract with the prime contractor**, working on a project where an NOC has been filed, must give a **Notice to Contractor (NTC)** to both the owner and the contractor at the addresses listed on the NOC, by **registered or certified mail, statutory overnight delivery, or hand delivery** [77][78][79][80].

**Deadline:** **Within 30 days** of the later of (a) the date the NOC was filed, or (b) the date the claimant first delivered labor, services, or materials to the project [77][78][79].

**Contents of the NTC** [77][78][79]:

- Name, address, and telephone number of the person providing labor, services, or materials.
- Name and address of each person at whose instance (i.e., the upstream party) the labor, services, or materials are being furnished.
- Name and location of the project (matching the NOC).
- Description of the labor, services, or materials being provided, and the contract price or anticipated value.

**Effect of failure.** If an NOC was filed and the claimant does **not** send the NTC within 30 days, the claimant **forfeits lien rights** under § 44-14-361.5(d). The lien is unenforceable even if the work is fully performed and unpaid. This is one of Georgia's two perennial supplier-side traps.

**Exception — privity of contract.** A party who is in **direct privity with the prime contractor** (i.e., a first-tier subcontractor) is **not required** to send an NTC, because the prime is on notice of its own subs by virtue of the contractual relationship [77][79][80]. The 30-day NTC requirement applies only to **second-tier-and-below** claimants — sub-subs and suppliers to subs.

**Trap.** A sub-sub that mistakenly believes it is in privity with the prime, or that fails to track the NOC's filing date, can blow the 30-day fuse and lose all lien rights on a project where it is owed substantial money. The defensive move is to **send an NTC reflexively** on every project where the supplier is not the prime — even when in doubt about privity.

### 2.4 Preliminary Notice of Lien Rights — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.3 (optional)

In addition to the NTC, Georgia provides for an **optional Preliminary Notice of Lien Rights** under **O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.3** [83][84]. Unlike the NTC (which is sent only to the owner and contractor), the Preliminary Notice is **filed with the clerk of superior court** in the project county, with copies to the prime contractor.

**Deadline:** Within **30 days** after the claimant first furnishes labor, services, or materials [83][84].

**Purpose.** The Preliminary Notice **preserves a claimant's lien priority date as of the date of first furnishing**, which can be important when (a) competing security deeds are recorded mid-project, or (b) the claimant wants to assert priority over post-commencement encumbrances. The Preliminary Notice is **not a substitute for the NTC** — it has different statutory effects and a different audience [83][84].

Many practitioners file both an NTC and a Preliminary Notice as belt-and-suspenders practice. The Preliminary Notice has lapsed in popularity since the SB 143 (2021) revisions to § 44-14-366 made interim lien waivers more enforceable [68][69], but it remains a valid option.

### 2.5 Lien priority — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.1; relation back to commencement

Georgia lien priority is governed by the **doctrine of relation back** [65][66][85]:

- A properly perfected mechanic's lien **relates back to the date the lienholder first commenced work or first delivered materials**.
- A lien for general contracting attaches to the property from the **commencement of work on the contract**, and takes priority **over** title acquired after commencement by a grantee under a deed to secure debt — **provided that grantee had actual notice** of the contractor's claim [65][85].
- All liens for repairs, building, or furnishing materials or services on the same property **rank as of the same date** when declared and filed for record within 90 days after the work is done.

**Practical lender protection — the construction-loan deed.** Under prevailing Georgia practice, lenders file the construction-loan security deed **before any work commences** on the project, including before grading or staking. This fixes the lender's priority date ahead of the contractor's commencement date. Lenders that allow a Notice of Commencement to be filed before their deed is recorded create a priority risk; lenders that allow a clearing-and-grubbing contractor to start work before the deed records create the same risk.

### 2.6 Filing the Claim of Lien — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.1

**Deadline:** A Claim of Lien must be filed with the **clerk of the superior court of the county where the property is located** within **90 days after the last day on which labor, services, or materials were furnished** [65][66][67][86].

The 90-day clock is **strict and is not subject to discovery rules**. If the contractor furnished the last day of materials on, e.g., March 1, the Claim of Lien must be filed by **May 30**. The clock does **not** restart if the contractor returns to perform warranty work or punch-list items — Georgia courts have repeatedly held that warranty visits and de minimis return trips do **not** extend the 90-day window if the original work was substantially complete. The window restarts only if the additional work is **new, distinct work performed under the original contract** [66][67][86].

**Service requirement — § 44-14-361.1(a)(4).** **No later than 2 business days after** filing the Claim of Lien, the claimant must send a true and accurate copy by **registered or certified mail, statutory overnight delivery, or hand delivery** to:

- The **owner of the property**, at the address listed on the deed records or the NOC; if the owner's address cannot be located, then to the **contractor** [65][66][67][87].
- **If an NOC has been filed**, the claimant must **also** send a copy to the **contractor at the address shown on the NOC** [65][66][67][87].

**Failure to serve within 2 business days** voids the lien as a matter of law [66][86][87]. This is the second of Georgia's two perennial supplier-side traps — many claimants record the lien but fail to serve it within 48 hours of filing.

**Form of the Claim of Lien.** The Claim of Lien must be sworn (notarized), and must contain the amount claimed, the names of the owner and contractor, the description of the property sufficient to identify it, and the name and address of the claimant. Statutory form is provided at O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.1(a)(2) [65][66][67].

### 2.7 Action on the underlying debt — 365 days from filing

Once the Claim of Lien is filed, the claimant must **commence a lien action for the recovery of the amount of the claim within 365 days** from the date of filing the Claim of Lien [65][86][88][89]. Failure to do so renders the Claim of Lien **unenforceable** and authorizes the property owner to file a notice to remove the lien from the records.

**This is the action on the underlying debt, not the lien foreclosure.** The 365-day deadline applies to a suit **against the party with whom the lien claimant contracted** — typically the prime contractor (for a sub) or the owner (for a prime) [88][89]. The lien-foreclosure action against the property follows a different (and longer) statute-of-limitations track.

**Notice of filing the lien action.** Within **30 days after filing** the lien action, the claimant must **file a notice of the lien action** with the **clerk of superior court of the county where the lien was originally filed**, cross-referencing the lien-record book and page [65][86][88]. Failure to file the notice does not extinguish the lien but is a common ground for owner-side motions to remove.

### 2.8 Lien Waivers — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-366 (interim and unconditional)

Georgia's lien-waiver regime is one of the strictest in the country [68][69][70][90]. **Pre-paid (advance) waivers are void** under § 44-14-366(a): "A right to claim a lien or to claim upon a bond may not be waived in advance of furnishing labor, services, or materials" [70][90][91]. Every lien waiver must be tied to actual receipt of payment.

**Two statutory forms** (O.C.G.A. § 44-14-366(c)) [68][70][90][91]:

1. **Interim Waiver and Release Upon Payment** — used to release lien rights with respect to a **progress payment**.
2. **Unconditional Waiver and Release Upon Final Payment** — used to release lien rights with respect to **final payment**.

**Strict form requirement.** A lien waiver is **not valid and enforceable** unless it **"substantially follows" the statutory form** set forth in O.C.G.A. § 44-14-366(c) (interim) or § 44-14-366(d) (final) **in boldface capital letters in at least 12-point font** [68][70][91][92]. The statutory form contains a prominent notice to the claimant ("NOTICE: WHEN YOU EXECUTE AND SUBMIT THIS DOCUMENT, YOU SHALL BE CONCLUSIVELY DEEMED TO HAVE BEEN PAID IN FULL THE AMOUNT STATED ABOVE...") that must appear verbatim. **Non-statutory waiver forms — e.g., AIA G706/G706A, ConsensusDocs, or owner-drafted forms — are unenforceable in Georgia** [68][90][91][92].

**The 90-day conversion — the SB 143 (2021) amendment.** Under the version of § 44-14-366 in effect before 2021, an interim waiver became **automatically conclusive evidence of payment** sixty (60) days after its execution, unless the claimant filed an Affidavit of Nonpayment within that window. **SB 143, signed into law in 2021, extended the conversion window from 60 days to 90 days** and eliminated the prior rule that a Claim of Lien filing could itself prevent the conversion [69][70][91]. The current rule:

- An Interim Waiver and Release Upon Payment becomes **conclusive evidence of full payment** of the amount stated in the waiver after **90 days** from the date of the waiver, **unless** the claimant files an **Affidavit of Nonpayment** within that 90-day window in the records of the superior court of the county where the property is located, with a copy to the owner and contractor [69][70][91][92].
- Filing a Claim of Lien within the 90-day window is **no longer** a substitute for filing the Affidavit of Nonpayment — the claimant must file the Affidavit of Nonpayment as a separate document [69][91].

**Trap.** A subcontractor that executes an interim lien waiver in exchange for a check that bounces, or for a check that is short-paid, must file the **Affidavit of Nonpayment within 90 days** of the waiver to preserve the lien claim. After 90 days, the interim waiver becomes conclusive evidence of payment **regardless of whether the money actually changed hands** [69][70][91]. This is the third perennial supplier-side trap.

**Affidavit of Nonpayment form** (O.C.G.A. § 44-14-366(f)) [69][70][91]: Must contain claim amount, project identification, owner and contractor names, and a statement that the claimant has not been paid the amount claimed. Must be filed in the superior court of the project county and served on owner and contractor by registered or certified mail or statutory overnight delivery within seven (7) days.

### 2.9 Bonding off a lien — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-364

The owner of real property or the contractor employed to improve the property may **discharge a lien by filing a bond** with the clerk of superior court under **O.C.G.A. § 44-14-364** [93][94][95]. Once approved by the clerk, the bond replaces the lien as the source of payment to the claimant, and the lien is removed from the property records.

**Bond amount** [93][94][95]:

- **Standard rule:** **Double the amount claimed** under the lien.
- **Exception:** For a lien against the **owner's domicile**, the bond is in the **amount claimed** (not doubled).

**Bond form:** Either a **surety bond with good security** approved by the clerk of superior court, or a **cash bond** [93][94][95].

**Service:** Within **7 days** of filing the bond, the party filing must send a notice of the filing and a copy of the bond by **registered or certified mail or statutory overnight delivery** to the lien claimant [93][94][95].

**Surety defenses.** A surety on a lien-discharge bond is entitled to assert any defenses that would have existed in an action on the lien itself — including statute of limitations, defective service of the Claim of Lien, failure to file an Affidavit of Nonpayment, and underlying contract defenses [93][94][95].

**Clerk's protection.** The clerk of superior court is held harmless under § 44-14-364(d) for "good faith discretionary acts" in connection with bond approval, so long as the clerk acts within reasonable judgment as to security and form [94][95].

### 2.10 Cancellation of lien — O.C.G.A. § 44-14-368

A lien claimant who is paid in full must file a **cancellation** of the lien on the records of the superior court within a reasonable time. **O.C.G.A. § 44-14-368** also provides for a **Notice of Contest of Lien** procedure, by which the owner or contractor may shorten the 365-day action-on-the-debt deadline to **60 days** by filing and serving a Notice of Contest [86][96]. After service, the claimant has only 60 days to commence the underlying suit. The Notice of Contest is the principal tool by which owners with pending refinances or closings clear stale or speculative liens off the property records quickly.

### 2.11 Georgia construction-fund "trust fund" exposure — O.C.G.A. § 16-8-15

Georgia does **not** have a dedicated construction-trust-fund statute like Texas Chapter 162. Instead, Georgia imposes **criminal liability for conversion of payments for real property improvements** under **O.C.G.A. § 16-8-15** [97][98][99].

**The offense:** Any "architect, landscape architect, engineer, contractor, subcontractor, or other person who **with intent to defraud** shall use the proceeds of any payment made to him on account of improving certain real property for any other purpose than to pay for labor or service performed on or materials furnished by his order for this specific improvement while any amount for which he may be or become liable for such labor, services, or materials remains unpaid" commits a felony [97][98].

**Penalty:** Punishable by imprisonment for **not less than 1 year nor more than 5 years**, OR — upon the recommendation of the jury or in the discretion of the trial judge — punished as a **misdemeanor** [97][98]. § 16-8-15 thus mirrors many elements of Texas Chapter 162 but with two important differences:

1. **Intent to defraud** is an express element. Georgia prosecutors must prove fraudulent intent beyond a reasonable doubt; Texas's § 162.031 by contrast requires only intentional, knowing, or fraudulent misapplication.
2. **No private civil cause of action arising directly from the statute.** Georgia courts have held that § 16-8-15, standing alone, does **not** authorize a sub or supplier to sue a contractor civilly for misapplication of funds [99]. The criminal statute is a basis for prosecution by the District Attorney, not a civil claim by an unpaid sub. Civil recovery proceeds under the lien, the bond, the contract, or fraud common-law theories — not under § 16-8-15 directly.

**Constructive-trust doctrine.** Georgia case law nonetheless recognizes a **constructive-trust** theory imposed on contractor receipts to the extent of unpaid subs and suppliers, which can support a separate civil claim for breach of constructive trust (along with associated personal liability for the corporate officer who controlled the disbursement) [99]. The constructive-trust theory is not codified in a dedicated statute and is doctrinally distinct from § 16-8-15. Practically, an unpaid sub asserts (a) a lien under Chapter 14; (b) a bond claim if applicable; (c) a breach-of-contract action; (d) potentially a fraud or constructive-trust claim alleging that the contractor diverted progress payments. The criminal exposure under § 16-8-15 hangs over the contractor's officer as a separate matter that the District Attorney may or may not pursue.

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

Georgia's prompt-payment regime is unusually compact: a **single statute, O.C.G.A. § 13-11-1 et seq., the Georgia Prompt Payment Act**, applies to **both private and public construction projects** [100][101][102]. This is in contrast to Texas (which has separate private and public regimes) and California (which has multiple overlapping statutes).

### 3.1 Owner-to-prime payment deadline — O.C.G.A. § 13-11-4

**Statutory authority:** O.C.G.A. § 13-11-4 [101][102][103].

**Rule:** If the contractor has performed in accordance with the terms of the contract (including any conditions precedent to payment), the owner shall pay the contractor **within 15 days** after the date the request for payment is received [100][101][102].

The 15-day deadline runs from the owner's **receipt** of a properly submitted pay application. Pay applications that lack required backup, lien waivers, or other contract-mandated items do not start the clock until cured.

### 3.2 Prime-to-subcontractor payment deadline — O.C.G.A. § 13-11-4(b)

**Rule:** The contractor shall pay each subcontractor and supplier the proportionate share of the owner's payment, less any retainage withheld, **within 10 days** after the contractor receives payment from the owner [100][101][102]. The 10-day deadline cascades down: each subcontractor must pay its sub-subs within 10 days of receipt.

### 3.3 Interest on overdue payment — O.C.G.A. § 13-11-7

If a periodic or final payment to a contractor is **delayed more than 15 days**, or if a periodic or final payment to a subcontractor is **delayed more than 10 days** after the upstream payment is received, **interest accrues at 1% per month** on the unpaid balance (12% per annum simple) [104][105][106].

**Notice requirement — § 13-11-7(b).** Interest is **not** automatically due. The party charging interest must **notify the upstream party at the time the payment request is made** that interest will be charged under § 13-11-7 if payment is late [105][106]. Many Georgia contractors include a stock paragraph on every pay application:

> "Pursuant to O.C.G.A. § 13-11-7, interest at 1% per month will accrue on any unpaid amount delayed more than 15 days (10 days for subcontractor payment) following receipt of this pay application."

Without that notice, the interest claim is unavailable even if the payment is months overdue [105][106].

**Contractual override — § 13-11-7(c).** Parties may agree by contract to **different interest rates, payment periods, and terms** that override § 13-11-7 — except that in case of a **willful breach** of contract payment terms, the statutory 1%/month rate applies regardless of contract terms [104][105][106]. The willful-breach exception preserves a statutory floor.

### 3.4 Permitted withholding

Under O.C.G.A. § 13-11-5, an owner or contractor may withhold payment for **good-faith disputes**, defective work, or as authorized by contract [100][101][107]. Common grounds for permitted withholding include:

- Non-conforming work pending correction.
- Failure to make payments to subs or suppliers (under back-charge / set-off provisions).
- Filing of a lien against the property or filing of a payment-bond claim (or credible threat thereof).
- Statutory retainage (see §3.5–3.6).
- Failure to provide required lien waivers or releases.

The withholding must be **bona fide** — pretextual withholding to delay payment is itself a Prompt Payment Act violation and triggers interest.

### 3.5 Retainage on private projects

Private retainage in Georgia is **contract-based**. There is **no statutory cap or floor on private retainage** [108][109]. Contracts may set retainage at 0%, 5%, 10%, or higher. The most common Georgia private-works retainage is **10%** of each progress payment, with **reduction to 5%** at 50% completion if the contract so provides — but this is a contract convention, not a statutory requirement.

**Pass-through retainage rule — O.C.G.A. § 13-11-4(c).** Regardless of the absence of a statutory cap, the **contractor must, within 10 days of receipt of retainage from the owner, pass through retainage payments to subcontractors and reduce each subcontractor's retainage in the same manner and proportion as the contractor's retainage is reduced by the owner** [100][101][102]. The pass-through rule prevents a prime from hoarding retainage that the owner has released.

**Pass-through reduction at 50% complete.** If a subcontractor's work is **complete and in place at 50% of the subcontract value**, the prime is required (per the same § 13-11-4(c)) to reduce that subcontractor's retainage in the same percentage as the prime's retainage has been reduced by the owner [100][102][108]. This protects the sub from being held to a fuller retainage than the prime is held to.

**Retainage release at substantial completion.** Upon substantial completion of the project (typically marked by a certificate of substantial completion from the design professional or owner), the owner must pay retainage to the prime **within 30 days** after invoice and appropriate documentation [108][109]. The prime then has 10 days to pass through retainage to subs under § 13-11-4(c).

### 3.6 Retainage on public works — O.C.G.A. § 13-10-80 (state public works) and § 36-91 (local public works)

Public-works retainage was **substantially restructured by Senate Bill 438 (Act 781) of the 2022 legislative session**, effective for contracts entered into on or after **July 1, 2022** [71][72][73].

**Current rule (contracts entered on or after July 1, 2022)** [71][72][73][110]:

- **Maximum retainage on each progress payment: 5%** (reduced from 10% under prior law).
- **No 50%-completion threshold** that eliminated retainage entirely (prior law cut off further retainage at 50% complete).
- **Substantial completion release: 30 days** from substantial-completion invoice to release retainage to the prime [73][110].
- **Punch-list withholding:** If at substantial completion there are incomplete items, the owner may withhold an amount equal to **200% of the value of each incomplete item** until the item is completed [71][110]. The 200% withholding is item-specific and applies in lieu of retaining the full 5% pool.
- **Pass-through:** The prime must pass through retainage to subs within **10 days** of receipt, in the same percentage reduction as the prime's retainage was reduced by the owner [71][102][110].

**Coverage** [71][73][110][111]:

- **State public works (O.C.G.A. § 13-10-80):** Applies to all public-works contracts let by the State of Georgia, state authorities, and state agencies.
- **Local public works (O.C.G.A. § 36-91-80 et seq.):** Parallel rules apply to local public works (city, county, school board, and local authority projects) under Title 36 Chapter 91.

### 3.7 Coverage and exemptions — O.C.G.A. § 13-11-3

The Prompt Payment Act applies to **owner-prime and prime-sub-and-supplier payment relationships** on Georgia construction projects [102][103]. Exemptions and edge cases:

- **Residential one-family or two-family dwellings**: Some Georgia courts have construed § 13-11-3 to exempt single-family residential prime-owner relationships. The 10-day pass-through rule generally still applies between the prime and its subs even on small residential work.
- **Federal projects**: Pre-empted by the federal Prompt Payment Act for federal-property contracts.
- **DOT highway construction**: Limited modification by GDOT-specific procurement rules; the basic 15/10/1% framework applies but with specific carve-outs.

### 3.8 Attorneys' fees and remedies

Georgia's Prompt Payment Act does **not** expressly authorize attorneys' fees for prevailing contractors or subs in a Prompt Payment Act action — a notable contrast with Texas (which allows full fee-shifting) and Florida (which allows fee-shifting for the substantially prevailing party). A claimant under O.C.G.A. § 13-11-7 may recover statutory interest plus contract damages but generally bears its own fees unless the underlying contract has a fee-shifting clause or unless attorney fees are awardable under **O.C.G.A. § 13-6-11** (bad-faith conduct, stubborn litigiousness, or unnecessary trouble and expense) — a higher and discretionary standard [112].

## 4. Tax structure for contractors

### 4.1 State and local sales tax rates

- **State sales tax rate:** **4%** under **O.C.G.A. § 48-8-30(a)** [113][114][115].
- **Local sales tax:** Counties, cities, transit authorities, and special-purpose districts may impose combined local sales taxes. The principal local levies are **LOST** (Local Option Sales Tax), **SPLOST** (Special-Purpose Local Option Sales Tax), **ESPLOST / E-SPLOST** (Education SPLOST), **HOST/MOST** (Homestead Option / Municipal Option), **TSPLOST** (Transportation SPLOST), and **MARTA** (the Atlanta-area transit tax) [114][115][116].
- **Combined rate range:** **7% to 9%** across Georgia's 159 counties. **Atlanta (Fulton County)** runs at **8.9%** as of 2026, comprising the 4% state rate plus 3% Fulton County local sales taxes plus 1.5% City of Atlanta (including the 1% MOST water-infrastructure tax) plus 0.4% TSPLOST [114][116][117][118].
- **Administered by:** **Georgia Department of Revenue (DOR)** [113][119].

The applicable combined rate depends on the **location of delivery/installation**, not the location of the contractor's office. Multi-jurisdiction Georgia contractors must track combined rates by county and submit accordingly.

### 4.2 Contractor sales-tax treatment — contractor as consumer

Georgia follows the **"contractor as consumer of materials"** model for real-property improvement [120][121][122][123]. The rule is articulated in **O.C.G.A. § 48-8-30** (general sales-tax imposition) and refined in **Georgia DOR Reg. 560-12-2-.26** (Construction Contractors) and DOR Letter Ruling LR SUT-2019-05 [120][121][122][123][124].

**Core rule.** When a contractor purchases tangible personal property (lumber, steel, copper, concrete, electrical wire, HVAC equipment, plumbing fixtures, etc.) and **incorporates it into real property** in the performance of a construction contract, the contractor is the **consumer of those materials**. The contractor **pays sales tax at the time of purchase from the supplier**. The contractor **does not collect sales tax from the customer** on the contract price [120][122][123][124].

**Materials become real estate.** "Materials become real estate when incorporated into or affixed to real estate (land, buildings and improvements). Thus, contractors'/subcontractors' purchases of such materials are taxable as retail sales or uses; contractors/subcontractors do not collect tax from the developers/owners/customers" [122][123].

**Labor:** **Labor charges that are separately stated on an invoice are not subject to sales tax** under § 48-8-3 and DOR Reg. 560-12-2-.26. If labor is **not separately stated** (i.e., bundled into a lump-sum charge), the DOR may attempt to tax the bundled amount as a sale of tangible personal property. Best practice — separately state labor and materials on all invoices [120][122][123][124].

**No re-collection trap.** Unlike Texas (where commercial repair-and-remodel labor is taxable as a service), **Georgia does not tax construction services as services**. The state-level sales-tax obligation is entirely on the front end (purchase of materials). This means Georgia contractors can typically operate under a **lump-sum** contract structure without needing to re-characterize as a "separated" contract — but should still separately state labor on invoices to avoid bundled-amount disputes [122][123].

### 4.3 Use tax on out-of-state materials

Materials purchased outside Georgia for use in a Georgia construction project are subject to **Georgia use tax** at the combined state-plus-local rate of the delivery location [113][119][120]. The contractor must file a Georgia Sales and Use Tax Return reporting the use-tax liability. A credit is available for like state and local sales taxes paid in the state of purchase (state-credited-against-state, local-credited-against-local — "like tax against like tax") under § 48-8-42 [113][120].

This is a routine DOR audit target for multi-state contractors — steel fabricators, modular suppliers, and out-of-state specialty equipment installers. The DOR can assess unpaid use tax going back **3 years** (or 6 years if fraud or substantial under-reporting is alleged) under O.C.G.A. § 48-2-49.

### 4.4 Registration — all contractors must obtain a Georgia Sales and Use Tax Number

Per DOR's **Contractor FAQ** and DOR Reg. 560-12-1 [120][122]: "ALL contractors, resident, nonresident, general and subs, must register for a sales and use tax number." Registration is free; failure to register and remit can result in administrative penalties under O.C.G.A. § 48-2-44.

### 4.5 Corporate income and net-worth taxes

**Corporate income tax — O.C.G.A. § 48-7-21** [125][126][127][128]:

- **Rate (TY 2025 forward):** **5.19%** flat rate. The rate was reduced from 5.39% to 5.19% by legislation effective for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2025 [126][127][128].
- **Rate history:** 6.00% (through 2018) → 5.75% (2019–2023) → 5.39% (2024) → 5.19% (2025 forward).
- **Applies to:** C-corporations and entities taxed as C-corps. S-corporations, partnerships, and LLCs taxed as pass-throughs are not subject to the corporate income tax; their income flows to the owners' individual returns at the Georgia personal income-tax rate (also 5.19% flat for 2025 forward) [126][128].

**Net-worth tax (also called "corporation franchise tax") — O.C.G.A. § 48-13-70 et seq.** [129][130]:

- **Applies to:** Domestic and foreign corporations doing business in Georgia.
- **Schedule:** No tax due on net worth **$100,000 or less**; sliding scale upward; **maximum $5,000** on net worth exceeding **$22,000,000** [129][130].
- **Filing requirement:** Returns must be filed annually with the corporate income tax return even if no tax is due.

### 4.6 Personal income tax

Georgia personal income tax (relevant to sole proprietors, pass-through partners, and S-corp owners) is at **O.C.G.A. § 48-7-20** [126][128]:

- **Rate (TY 2025 forward):** **5.19%** flat (reduced from 5.39% in 2024 and from 5.75% prior). Georgia moved from a graduated bracket system to a flat tax effective TY 2024 [126][128].
- Personal exemptions and Georgia standard deduction apply. Itemized deductions allowable.

### 4.7 Withholding tax for nonresident contractors

Georgia imposes a **nonresident contractor withholding** requirement under **O.C.G.A. § 48-7-129** for nonresident subcontractors performing work on Georgia projects exceeding $250,000. The prime contractor must withhold **2% of payments** to a nonresident sub, unless the sub registers with the Georgia DOR and posts a bond. This is a routine compliance item for ContrPro contractors that engage subs based outside Georgia.

## 5. Recent changes (2024–2026)

Georgia's General Assembly meets every year for a 40-legislative-day regular session beginning the second Monday in January (typically running into late March or early April). The 2025 regular session ran January 13, 2025 to April 4, 2025 and produced four bills materially affecting Georgia construction practice [42][51][131][132].

### 5.1 House Bill 137 — Little Miller Act payment-bond threshold raised to $250,000, effective July 1, 2025

**HB 137 (Ga. Acts 2025)**, sponsored by Rep. Matt Reeves, amended **O.C.G.A. § 13-10-40, § 13-10-60, § 36-91-50, and § 36-91-90** to raise the threshold for required performance and payment bonds on Georgia public-works projects from **$100,000 to $250,000** [133][134][135]. The bill was signed by Governor Brian Kemp and is **effective July 1, 2025** for contracts entered on or after that date [133][134][136].

**Practical impact for ContrPro:**

- State and local public-works projects with an estimated contract value **at or below $250,000** are no longer statutorily required to include a Performance Bond or Payment Bond; the procuring agency retains discretion to require bonds at any threshold.
- Public-works projects **above $250,000** still require Performance Bond and Payment Bond in the amount of the contract price.
- Subcontractors and suppliers on smaller public projects (between $100,000 and $250,000) **lose statutory bond-claim rights** unless the agency exercises its discretionary authority to demand bonds. ContrPro subs on smaller public-works jobs should require the agency to confirm whether bonds were procured before relying on payment-bond claims as a backstop.

### 5.2 Senate Bill 125 — SCILB licensing reform, effective July 1, 2025

**SB 125 (Ga. Acts 2025)** amended O.C.G.A. Title 43 Chapter 14 in multiple respects [41][42][51][131]:

1. **Continuing-education tracking — § 43-14-6 / § 43-14-5.** All SCILB-administered licensees (electrical, plumbing, conditioned air, low-voltage, utility) must register and report CE through the **CE Broker** system. The Board may treat failure to record CE in CE Broker as a renewal deficiency separate from failure to complete CE [25][41][42].
2. **Specialty-engineer experience-and-exam decoupling — § 43-15-8.** Specialty engineers may sit for certain licensure exams earlier in their experience track than under prior law (technical change affecting the engineering-board pipeline, of limited direct impact on ContrPro's five trades).
3. **Low-voltage contractor classifications.** Restructured low-voltage classes (LV-A alarm, LV-T telecommunications, LV-G general, LV-U unrestricted) with class-specific exams [41][42][51].
4. **Utility manager licensure — § 43-14-10 / § 43-14-11.** New certified safety-training requirement for utility managers; periodic safety-training renewal mandated.
5. **Enhanced enforcement — § 43-14-12 / § 43-14-12.1.** SCILB granted explicit **cease-and-desist** authority over unlicensed practice, plus financial penalty caps raised. Misleading advertising claiming licensure when none is held is a separately enumerated violation [41][42][51].

Effective date: **July 1, 2025**, with CE Broker reporting requirements layering in beginning **January 1, 2026** for all SCILB renewals [25][41].

### 5.3 House Bill 635 — SLBRGC clarification, effective July 1, 2025

**HB 635 (Ga. Acts 2025)** amended O.C.G.A. Title 43 Chapter 41 to clarify renewal, eligibility, and enforcement procedures under the SLBRGC's authority [42][131]. Specific provisions include:

- Clarification of license-class eligibility paths between Residential-Basic, Residential-Light Commercial, and General Contractor tiers.
- Renewal procedure clarifications and grace-period rules.
- Enforcement-procedure language paralleling the SCILB changes in SB 125.

Effective **July 1, 2025**. The amendments do not substantively change ContrPro contractors' day-to-day licensing path but tighten several gray areas — particularly the rules for upgrading from Residential-Basic to Residential-Light Commercial or from Limited Tier to Unlimited General Contractor.

### 5.4 Senate Bill 553 — Title 43 Chapter 14 reorganization (2026 session)

**SB 553**, introduced in the 2025–2026 legislative session, **repeals and reenacts Title 43 Chapter 14** to consolidate SCILB's structure [137][138]. The bill creates a unified "State Construction Industry Licensing Board" of 27 governor-appointed members with subsidiary divisions for each trade (electrical, plumbing, conditioned air, low-voltage, utility), formalizes reciprocity authority, and consolidates licensure and certification rules. **SB 553 passed the Georgia General Assembly on March 27, 2026** [137]. As of this audit's verification date (2026-05-13), the bill awaits the Governor's signature; the effective date will be **July 1, 2026 or January 1, 2027** depending on signing.

ContrPro contractors should treat SB 553 as a structural-administrative change rather than a substantive licensing-criteria change — the underlying experience, exam, and CE requirements roll forward into the reenacted chapter without material modification. Watch for revised renewal forms and a possible re-numbering of administrative rules in Chapters 121, 122, and 123 after the effective date.

### 5.5 SB 143 (2021) lien-waiver amendments — established baseline, in force throughout 2025–2026

**SB 143 (Ga. Acts 2021)** amended O.C.G.A. § 44-14-366 to:

1. Extend the **conversion period for interim lien waivers from 60 days to 90 days** [69][70][91].
2. **Eliminate filing a Claim of Lien** as a method to prevent the conversion to unconditional. Filing an **Affidavit of Nonpayment** is now the sole prescribed method [69][70][91][92].
3. Strengthen the statutory-form requirements (12-point boldface, capital letters) [69][91].

The SB 143 changes have been settled law since 2021 and remain the governing regime in 2026. The most common errors in current practice are (a) using out-of-date pre-SB 143 waiver forms (with 60-day language) that fail the "substantially follows the statutory form" test, and (b) filing a Claim of Lien but not an Affidavit of Nonpayment within 90 days, on the mistaken belief that the lien filing prevents the waiver's conversion to unconditional.

### 5.6 Public-works retainage reform — SB 438 (2022) and subsequent guidance

**SB 438 (Ga. Acts 2022, Act 781)** amended O.C.G.A. § 13-10-80 and § 36-91-80 et seq. to reduce public-works retainage from 10% to **5%** and to eliminate the prior-law 50%-completion threshold above which retainage was prohibited [71][72][73][110]. The amendments apply to contracts entered on or after **July 1, 2022** and remain in force in 2026. Pre-July-2022 contracts continue under the 10% / 50%-cutoff regime.

### 5.7 Bills proposed but not enacted (verification cross-reference)

A review of the 2025 General Assembly bill tracker via legiscan.com and the Office of the Governor signed-legislation index [131][132] identifies several construction-relevant bills that were filed but did not become law:

- Proposed legislation to impose a statutory cap on **private-works retainage at 5%** mirroring the public-works rules **did not pass**. Georgia private retainage remains contract-based with no statutory ceiling.
- Proposed legislation to require **fee-shifting in Prompt Payment Act cases** **did not pass**. § 13-11-7 still does not award attorneys' fees independent of contract.
- Proposed legislation to **establish a statewide journeyman electrician and journeyman plumber licensure tier** **did not pass**. Georgia still licenses only Electrical Contractors (Class I/II) and Master Plumbers / Journeyman Plumbers; there is no statewide journeyman electrician license.

### 5.8 Discrepancy resolutions

Several persistent secondary-source errors should be flagged:

- Some third-party "Georgia lien law guides" still report a **60-day** Affidavit of Nonpayment window. **SB 143 (2021) extended this to 90 days** [69][70][91]. Forms and calendars referencing 60 days are dangerously stale.
- Some commentary continues to report a **10%** retainage limit on Georgia public works. **SB 438 (2022) reduced this to 5%** effective for contracts on or after July 1, 2022 [71][72][73].
- Some published Georgia HVAC licensing summaries imply a state-level GL insurance requirement for Conditioned Air Contractors. **There is no state-level GL minimum** for the Title 43 Chapter 14 trades (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, low-voltage, utility); GL minimums apply only to SLBRGC-licensed general and residential contractors [21][22][23][32][35].
- Some Georgia contractor-licensing portals continue to describe Class II Electrical and Class II Master Plumber as "Unrestricted **Master**" — the term "Master" is correct for plumbers (Master Plumber Class II Unrestricted) but **not** for electricians. Georgia does not issue a "Master Electrician" license; the unrestricted electrical license is simply "Electrical Contractor Class II (Unrestricted)" [11][44][45].
- Some commentary describes O.C.G.A. § 16-8-15 as a "construction trust fund act." This overstates the statute's reach. § 16-8-15 is a **criminal** statute requiring **intent to defraud**; it does not create a private civil right of action; and it is not a Texas-Chapter-162 analog [97][98][99]. Civil recovery for misapplied payments proceeds under lien, bond, contract, or common-law fraud theories.

## 6. Practical compliance checklist for a Georgia contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**

- Confirm the **state license** in hand for each scope of work to be self-performed:
  - General contracting (commercial $100,000+) — SLBRGC Commercial General Contractor (Unlimited) or Limited Tier; $500,000 or $300,000 GL respectively.
  - Residential ($2,500+) — SLBRGC Residential-Basic or Residential-Light Commercial; $300,000 or $500,000 GL.
  - HVAC — SCILB Conditioned Air Contractor Class I or II; qualifying agent on file with SCILB.
  - Electrical — SCILB Electrical Contractor Class I or II; qualifying agent on file.
  - Plumbing — SCILB Master Plumber Class I or II; qualifying agent on file.
  - Structural steel — no state license; verify AISC certification per contract; verify AWS D1.1 (or D1.5 for DOT bridge) welder qualifications.
- Confirm **municipal-layer requirements** — Atlanta business license, Savannah journeyman cards if pulling permits there, Augusta-Richmond mechanical registration if applicable.
- Confirm **workers' compensation strategy**: any sub that brings workers must carry WC if it has 3+ employees; the GC's policy will backstop any uncovered sub under O.C.G.A. § 34-9-8.
- Confirm **Georgia DOR sales and use tax number** is current.
- Confirm contract is **written and signed before commencement** for residential work — many Georgia counties require a written contract attached to the building permit application for residential.

**At project commencement:**

- **Owner/agent/contractor files Notice of Commencement** with superior court of the project county **within 15 days** of physical commencement; posts copy on site (O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.5). For public works, the contractor files the NOC; private works, typically the owner or the GC.

**For sub-tier and supplier claimants — within 30 days of NOC filing or first delivery:**

- **Send the Notice to Contractor (NTC)** to both the owner and the contractor at the addresses on the NOC, by registered or certified mail, statutory overnight delivery, or hand delivery (O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.5(c)). **Without this NTC, lien rights are forfeited.** First-tier subs in privity with the prime are exempt — but supply a notice anyway if any doubt about privity.

**During the work — payment cycle:**

- **Prompt Payment Act (O.C.G.A. § 13-11-4):** Owner pays prime within **15 days** of properly submitted pay app; prime pays subs within **10 days** of receipt. Interest at **1% per month (12% APR)** on late payments under § 13-11-7 — **but only if the payment request contains the statutory notice**. Include the notice on every pay app.
- **Statutory pass-through retainage reduction at 50% complete** (§ 13-11-4(c)): When a sub's work reaches 50% complete and the prime's retainage has been reduced by the owner, the prime must reduce the sub's retainage in the same proportion within 10 days.

**For interim lien waivers:**

- Use the **statutory § 44-14-366(c) Interim Waiver and Release Upon Payment form**, in **12-point boldface capital letters**. Forms that don't substantially follow the statutory form are unenforceable.
- **Watch the 90-day clock** from waiver execution. If payment isn't received in full, **file an Affidavit of Nonpayment** in the superior court of the project county within 90 days. Service on owner and contractor within 7 days. **A Claim of Lien filing is NOT a substitute** for the Affidavit of Nonpayment.

**Lien filing:**

- **Within 90 days of last day of furnishing labor, services, or materials**, file the **Claim of Lien** in the superior court of the project county (O.C.G.A. § 44-14-361.1).
- **Within 2 business days** of filing, send copy to owner by registered/certified mail or statutory overnight delivery; if NOC filed, also send copy to contractor at NOC address (§ 44-14-361.1(a)(4)). **Failure of this 2-business-day service voids the lien.**

**Underlying-debt action:**

- **Within 365 days** of filing the Claim of Lien, **commence the lien action for the underlying debt** in court against the party with whom you contracted (sub sues prime; prime sues owner). § 44-14-361.1.
- **Within 30 days** of filing the action, **file a notice of the action** with the clerk of superior court of the original lien-filing county, cross-referencing the lien's record book and page.
- If owner/contractor serves a **Notice of Contest of Lien** under § 44-14-368, the 365-day deadline collapses to **60 days** from service.

**Public-works projects — Little Miller Act (post-July-1-2025):**

- **Payment Bond and Performance Bond** required on contracts above **$250,000** (raised from $100,000 by HB 137 effective July 1, 2025). For contracts $100,001–$250,000, bonds at agency discretion — confirm before relying.
- **Payment-bond claim deadline:** Generally 90 days from last day of furnishing labor or materials (parallel to the lien deadline). Public works don't allow liens on public property, so the payment bond is the principal recovery vehicle for unpaid subs and suppliers.
- **Retainage:** **5% max** on each progress payment (per § 13-10-80 / § 36-91-80; SB 438 (2022)). **30-day release** after substantial-completion invoice. **Punch-list withholding 200% of value of each incomplete item** in lieu of retaining the full 5% pool.

**Conversion of payments — O.C.G.A. § 16-8-15:**

- Construction payments received by a contractor are subject to a **criminal felony provision** if used to pay anything other than labor/services/materials for the specific project while obligations to sub/supplier/laborer remain unpaid, **and** intent to defraud is present. Penalty: **1–5 years prison** or misdemeanor.
- **No private civil cause of action** directly under § 16-8-15. Civil recovery proceeds via lien, bond, contract, or fraud/constructive-trust common-law theories. Officer/director liability for misappropriation typically requires a constructive-trust or fraud-based pleading.

**Annual / biennial compliance:**

- File and pay **Georgia sales and use tax monthly** (or quarterly if low-volume); tax on materials at the combined state-plus-local rate of the delivery location. **Separately state labor on invoices** to avoid bundled-amount disputes.
- File **Georgia corporate income tax** at **5.19% flat** (or pass-through at 5.19% personal); annual.
- File **Georgia net-worth tax** annually (sliding scale; max $5,000).
- File **federal Form 1099-NEC** for all subs paid $600+; nonresident-sub 2% withholding applies above $250,000.
- **Renew SLBRGC license biennially** ($75–$100; due June 30 of even-numbered years). **CE: 8 hours per cycle; recorded in CE Broker beginning January 1, 2026.**
- **Renew SCILB licenses biennially** (Conditioned Air: due Nov. 30 odd years; Electrical and Plumbing: due June 30 even years; $75–$100 fees; 8 hours CE per cycle; **CE Broker reporting from January 1, 2026**).
- Maintain GL insurance certificates on file with SLBRGC; SCILB does not require state-level GL.
- Maintain workers' compensation certificates of all subs continuously updated; deny site access to subs without current COI showing WC.
