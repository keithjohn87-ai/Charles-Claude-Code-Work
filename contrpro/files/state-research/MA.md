## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Massachusetts structure — a unique dual-license residential regime, statewide trade licensing, and no general commercial contractor license

Massachusetts is **structurally unique** among the fifty states in maintaining a **dual-license residential regulatory regime** that requires most contractors performing work on owner-occupied homes of one-to-four units to hold **two separate, independently issued, non-overlapping credentials**: (i) the **Home Improvement Contractor ("HIC") registration** issued by the **Office of Consumer Affairs and Business Regulation ("OCABR")** under **M.G.L. c. 142A**, and (ii) the **Construction Supervisor License ("CSL")** issued by the **Department of Public Safety ("DPS")** under **M.G.L. c. 143 § 95** [1][2][3][4][5][6][7][8]. The two regimes are administered by different agencies, governed by different statutes, enforced through different mechanisms, and serve different policy objectives. No other state requires this particular two-credential combination for residential general contracting, and the consequence — discussed at length in § 1.5 below — is that a Massachusetts residential general contractor who holds only an HIC, or only a CSL, is **out of compliance** and exposed to administrative penalties under one statute and criminal penalties under the other.

The single most important consumer-protection statute is **Chapter 93A** (the Massachusetts Consumer Protection Act, M.G.L. c. 93A § 1 et seq.), which makes "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce" unlawful and authorizes **treble damages, attorney fees, and costs** to prevailing plaintiffs in private actions under M.G.L. c. 93A § 9 (consumer) and § 11 (business-to-business). HIC regulations at **201 CMR 14.00** define numerous contractor practices as **per se violations of Chapter 93A**, including failure to register, failure to use a compliant written contract, and unauthorized demands for advance payment in excess of one-third of the contract price. The combination of 142A registration + 93A treble-damages exposure is functionally equivalent to New Jersey's CRA/CFA framework and creates the same risk profile: a non-trivial procedural mistake on a residential contract is an automatic statutory violation regardless of harm [1][2][9][10][11][12].

The takeaway: Massachusetts **licenses every mechanical trade** at the state level (electrical, plumbing, gas, sheet metal, fire sprinkler, hoisting, asbestos, lead) **and** requires the **CSL** for anyone supervising structural work on buildings regulated by 780 CMR. It does **not** issue a general-contractor competency license for either residential or commercial work — the CSL functions as the rough analog of a GC license but is technical-supervision-of-the-building-code only, not financial responsibility or business competency. Layered on top, for residential work, is the **HIC consumer-protection registration**. The dual-license requirement is the dominant compliance gotcha and is enforced aggressively in litigation.

### 1.2 Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration — M.G.L. c. 142A and 201 CMR 14.00

The **Home Improvement Contractor Act**, codified at **M.G.L. c. 142A**, was enacted as **St. 1991, c. 453** ("An Act Relative to the Regulation of Home Improvement Contractors"), signed into law by Governor Weld on January 14, 1992, and became effective in 1992 with full registration enforcement commencing thereafter [1][2][3][13][14][15]. It is administered by the **Office of Consumer Affairs and Business Regulation ("OCABR")** pursuant to regulations at **201 CMR 14.00** and is the practical centerpiece of Massachusetts residential-contractor regulation, alongside the CSL.

#### 1.2.1 Who must register — M.G.L. c. 142A § 9 and 201 CMR 14.02

Under **M.G.L. c. 142A § 9**, "no contractor or subcontractor shall perform or offer to perform residential contracting services" unless registered with the Director of the Office of Consumer Affairs and Business Regulation [1][2][3][13]. The statutory terms are defined at **M.G.L. c. 142A § 1**:

- **"Residential contracting"** means the reconstruction, alteration, renovation, repair, modernization, conversion, improvement, removal, or demolition, or the construction of an addition to, any pre-existing owner-occupied building containing at least one but not more than four dwelling units, which building or portion thereof is used or designed to be used as a residence or dwelling unit, or to structures which are adjacent to such residence or building.
- **"Owner"** means any homeowner, tenant or other person who orders, contracts for or purchases residential contracting services or services of a contractor or subcontractor, or the person entitled to the performance of the work of a contractor or subcontractor pursuant to a residential contracting agreement.
- **"Contractor"** means any person who owns and operates a residential contracting business or who undertakes, offers to undertake, purports to have the capacity to undertake or submits a bid for residential contracting services.

The **scope of "residential contracting"** is intentionally broad and covers any work on existing owner-occupied homes of one-to-four units. It does **not** cover (and HIC registration is **not** required for):

1. **New home construction** — explicitly excluded under M.G.L. c. 142A § 1 ("the construction of a new home, residence, or building" is outside the definition). New construction is instead regulated under the State Building Code by the CSL holder of record [1][2][13].
2. **Owner-occupied buildings of five or more units** (multifamily 5+ falls outside "residential contracting" — even though the building may be residential in use, it is not within HIC scope; commercial-property-style regulation applies).
3. **Non-owner-occupied buildings** (rental property is *not* covered by HIC unless the owner resides on-site). This is heavily litigated and the safer course is to register and use a compliant contract regardless.
4. **Commercial property of any kind**.
5. **Work where the total contract price is less than $500** (per 201 CMR 14.02 — below this threshold the written-contract content rules do not attach, though good practice is to issue a written contract regardless).
6. **Persons performing residential contracting on their own property** for their own occupancy.
7. **Architects, engineers, and similar licensed design professionals** acting only within their licensed scope.
8. **Licensed plumbers, electricians, gas fitters, and sheet metal workers** acting *strictly* within their respective trade-license scope (this exemption is narrow: a master plumber doing fixture work as part of a kitchen renovation that also includes tile and cabinets must register).

**Penalty for unregistered residential contracting — M.G.L. c. 142A § 17.** Performing residential contracting work without an HIC registration is a **violation of Chapter 93A as a matter of law** under 201 CMR 14.05(4), exposing the unregistered contractor to **treble damages plus attorney fees** in any subsequent consumer claim, and to an administrative civil penalty of up to **$2,000 per violation** payable to OCABR. The Massachusetts Appeals Court has further held in *D'Annolfo v. Stoneham Hous. Auth.*, 19 Mass. App. Ct. 1011 (1985), and progeny, that an unregistered contractor's claim for compensation may be barred entirely in certain circumstances, although Massachusetts has resisted the harsher "complete bar to recovery" rule applied in some jurisdictions [9][10][11][12].

#### 1.2.2 Registration requirements — 201 CMR 14.03 and M.G.L. c. 142A § 3

Registration with OCABR requires [1][2][3][13][14][15][16]:

1. **Registration fee — $150 for two-year registration** (set by 201 CMR 14.03; previously $100 + $100 Guaranty Fund assessment; current schedule combines into the registration fee).
2. **Guaranty Fund assessment** — every contractor must pay into the **Residential Contractor's Guaranty Fund** established by **M.G.L. c. 142A § 5**. The Fund is the consumer's last-resort recovery mechanism for unsatisfied judgments against HIC-registered contractors, with maximum recovery currently **$10,000 per claim** under § 5(f). The assessment is paid as part of the registration fee; additional assessments may be required if Fund balance drops below statutory thresholds.
3. **Disclosure of the contractor's full legal name, dba names, business address, telephone number, federal employer identification number (FEIN), and date of incorporation or formation.**
4. **Disclosure of all owners, officers, directors, and persons with a 10% or greater ownership interest**, including full names and residential addresses.
5. **Criminal background disclosure** — the contractor and each disclosed principal must disclose all criminal convictions (other than minor motor-vehicle violations) within the previous ten years. Director may deny registration under M.G.L. c. 142A § 9 for convictions involving fraud, deceit, or financial dishonesty.
6. **Disclosure of any prior registrations** in Massachusetts or other states, including suspensions, revocations, and disciplinary actions, and any pending complaints with OCABR.
7. **Workers' Compensation and Liability Insurance disclosure** — the registration application requires disclosure of WC insurance carrier and policy number (mandatory under M.G.L. c. 152 — see § 5) and recommends but does not require commercial general liability insurance. OCABR's compliant-contract form provides a section for the contractor to state whether it carries CGL insurance, and the consumer must initial the disclosure if the contractor does *not* carry insurance.
8. **The Registration Number issued by OCABR must be prominently displayed on:**
   - All contracts, advertisements, and proposals (201 CMR 14.04(2));
   - All written estimates, invoices, and correspondence;
   - The contractor's place of business (if any);
   - All commercial vehicles used in the residential contracting business (though Massachusetts is less prescriptive than New Jersey on vehicle-lettering requirements);
   - All website and digital marketing materials.

#### 1.2.3 Written contract — 201 CMR 14.04 — required for any home improvement over $1,000

Massachusetts is more demanding than most states on the **content** of the written contract for residential work, and the **rules apply to any project with a contract price exceeding $1,000** [13][14][15][16][17][18]. (The $500 *registration*-trigger and the $1,000 *contract-form*-trigger are commonly confused; the contract-form rules attach above $1,000.)

Under **201 CMR 14.04(1)**, every home improvement contract for a contract price in excess of **$1,000** must be in writing, must be signed by all parties **before any work commences**, and must include:

1. **The complete agreement of the contract**, including all terms relating to warranties, representations, and statements.
2. **The complete name, registration number, address, and telephone number** of the contractor.
3. **The owner's name, address, and date of the contract.**
4. **A detailed description of the work to be done** and the materials to be used, including specifications, plans, drawings, or other documents incorporated by reference.
5. **The total contract price** agreed upon, including all finance charges.
6. **A time schedule of payments** to be made under the contract and the amount of each payment stated in dollars, including all finance charges. **Initial deposits may not exceed one-third of the total contract price**, except where a special order of materials is required (see § 1.2.5).
7. **The dates** on which the work is to begin and to be substantially completed.
8. **A description of any change orders**, including the requirement that change orders be in writing, signed by both parties, and incorporated into the contract by reference.
9. **An express, written warranty** statement, or a statement that no express warranty is given (in which case the statutory implied warranty of merchantability still applies).
10. **A "Notice of Cancellation"** giving the owner the right to cancel within **three business days** of signing — this incorporates by reference the Massachusetts Door-to-Door Sales Act (M.G.L. c. 93 § 48) and the FTC Cooling-Off Rule (16 CFR 429), and applies to any contract signed at a place other than the contractor's place of business.
11. **A statement of the contractor's registration number and a statement that all home improvement contractors and subcontractors are required by law to be registered**, and that any inquiry by the owner concerning a contractor or subcontractor may be made to the Director of the Office of Consumer Affairs and Business Regulation.
12. **A statement that the homeowner may pursue claims against the Guaranty Fund** established under M.G.L. c. 142A § 5 if the contractor fails to satisfy a judgment.
13. **The signatures of the contractor (or its authorized representative) and the owner**.
14. **All permits required for the work**, with a statement of whether the contractor or the owner will obtain them, and the warning that owners who secure their own permits are **excluded from the protection of the Guaranty Fund** under M.G.L. c. 142A § 5(c) (a critical consumer-side gotcha — a homeowner who pulls his or her own permit *forfeits* recourse against the Fund).

**Prohibited contract terms — 201 CMR 14.04(2).** The regulations expressly prohibit any contract provision that purports to:

1. Require the owner to waive any rights afforded by 201 CMR 14.00 or M.G.L. c. 142A.
2. Authorize the contractor to apply for permits as the owner's agent if the contractor is not properly registered or licensed.
3. Require the owner to pay any sum in excess of one-third of the contract price as an initial deposit (subject to the materials-special-order exception).
4. Provide for final payment before the work is substantially completed and the owner has had a reasonable opportunity to inspect.
5. Disclaim any implied warranty of merchantability or fitness for ordinary purpose.

#### 1.2.4 Initial deposit cap — one-third — and the special-order exception

The **one-third deposit cap** under 201 CMR 14.04(1) is one of the most strictly enforced Massachusetts residential-contracting rules. A contract that requires a deposit in excess of one-third of the total contract price is **void as to the excess**, and the contractor is liable for return of the excess plus Chapter 93A treble damages and attorney fees [13][14][15][16][19].

**Exception — special-order materials.** Where the contract calls for the purchase of materials that are specifically designed or made on a custom basis (cabinetry, custom windows, custom millwork, special-order tile, etc.), the contractor may require a deposit equal to **one-third of the contract price plus the cost of any special-order materials**, with the special-order amount itemized on the face of the contract. The exception is narrowly construed — generic in-stock materials, even if they require a substantial up-front purchase by the contractor, do not qualify [13][14][15][16].

#### 1.2.5 The Guaranty Fund — M.G.L. c. 142A § 5

The **Residential Contractor's Guaranty Fund** under **M.G.L. c. 142A § 5** is a unique Massachusetts mechanism: it is funded by registration assessments and provides **a last-resort consumer recovery for unsatisfied judgments** against HIC-registered contractors. Key features [1][2][3][13][14]:

1. **Maximum recovery — $10,000 per claim** under § 5(f). (Some secondary sources reference higher caps; the statutory maximum in current form is $10,000.)
2. **Eligibility — limited to claimants who** (i) hired an HIC-registered contractor at the time of contract, (ii) obtained a final judgment, arbitration award, or agency order against the contractor, (iii) made reasonable efforts to collect on the judgment and were unable to do so, (iv) **pulled the permit through the contractor** (or no permit was required), and (v) filed the Guaranty Fund claim within **one year of the unsatisfied judgment**.
3. **The "owner-pulled-permit" exclusion** under § 5(c): a homeowner who pulled the building permit as the "homeowner-as-builder" is **completely excluded** from recovery against the Fund. Contractors sometimes propose this to homeowners as a fee-saving measure; doing so eliminates the homeowner's Guaranty Fund safety net and creates significant downstream exposure for the contractor under Chapter 93A.
4. **Subrogation — after the Fund pays a claim**, the contractor's HIC registration is **automatically suspended** under § 5(h) until the contractor reimburses the Fund. This is an enormous practical penalty, as a contractor whose registration is suspended cannot lawfully perform residential contracting in Massachusetts.

#### 1.2.6 Mandatory arbitration — M.G.L. c. 142A § 4

Under **M.G.L. c. 142A § 4**, **mandatory binding arbitration is available** at the **homeowner's election** for disputes arising under residential contracting agreements. The arbitration is administered by an OCABR-approved private arbitration provider. Key features:

1. **Binding on the contractor** if the homeowner elects arbitration; the contractor cannot avoid the proceeding.
2. **Filing fee** paid by the homeowner; cost-shifting available.
3. **Decisions are appealable** only on narrow Massachusetts Arbitration Act grounds (M.G.L. c. 251).
4. The arbitration mechanism does **not** preclude the homeowner from pursuing a Chapter 93A claim in court; the homeowner has the choice. In practice, sophisticated consumers' counsel will pursue the 93A court action because of the treble-damages exposure unavailable in arbitration awards.

### 1.3 Construction Supervisor License (CSL) — M.G.L. c. 143 § 95 and 780 CMR 110.R5

The **Construction Supervisor License ("CSL")** regime is the **technical-competency** half of the Massachusetts dual-license residential structure (and the only license required for commercial work). It is administered by the **Department of Public Safety ("DPS")**, Board of Building Regulations and Standards ("BBRS"), under **M.G.L. c. 143 § 95** and is implemented through the **Massachusetts State Building Code** at **780 CMR 110.R5** (residential portion) and **780 CMR 110** (commercial supervision) [4][5][6][7][20].

#### 1.3.1 Statutory authority — M.G.L. c. 143 § 95

**M.G.L. c. 143 § 95** provides that "no person shall, by himself or by his employees, agents or otherwise, engage in the business of supervising the construction, reconstruction, alteration, repair, removal or demolition" of any building or structure regulated by the State Building Code without holding a Construction Supervisor License. The CSL is **mandatory** for any person who:

1. Supervises the construction, reconstruction, alteration, repair, removal, or demolition of any building containing **fewer than 35,000 cubic feet** of enclosed space (the "unrestricted residential" CSL); or
2. Supervises the construction, reconstruction, alteration, repair, removal, or demolition of any building containing **35,000 cubic feet or more** of enclosed space (the "unrestricted full" CSL); or
3. Pulls a building permit on behalf of an owner under 780 CMR 110.R5 (the "permit-pulling" function — see § 1.3.4 below).

The CSL applies to **both residential and commercial** construction. There is no commercial general-contractor license per se in Massachusetts; the CSL substitutes for it as the technical-supervision credential. Whoever signs the building-permit application as "construction supervisor" is the CSL holder of record and is personally responsible for code compliance for the entire project.

#### 1.3.2 License classes — 780 CMR 110.R5 and DPS regulations

The CSL is issued in multiple classes, each with a distinct scope. The principal classes (as of 2025) are [4][5][6][7][20]:

1. **Unrestricted Construction Supervisor License** — permits supervision of any building, of any size, of any use group, regulated by the State Building Code.
2. **Restricted 1-2 Family Construction Supervisor License** — restricted to one- and two-family dwellings (use Group R-3) and accessory structures.
3. **Specialty CSLs**:
   - **Demolition** — restricted to demolition work.
   - **Roofing Coverings** — restricted to roof covering replacement.
   - **Window and Siding** — restricted to window replacement and exterior siding.
   - **Solid Fuel Burning Appliances** — restricted to wood stoves, pellet stoves, etc.
   - **Masonry Limited to Chimneys** — restricted to chimney work.
   - **Insulation Limited to 1-2 Family Dwellings** — restricted to residential insulation.

#### 1.3.3 Exam, experience, and renewal requirements

To obtain an **Unrestricted CSL** under DPS regulations [4][5][6][7][20]:

1. **Three years of experience** in building construction (verifiable employer attestations, with W-2 documentation preferred);
2. **Passing score** on the DPS-administered Massachusetts Construction Supervisor Examination, which covers the State Building Code, applicable codes (energy, plumbing, electrical, mechanical), and construction practice;
3. **CSL renewal every two years**, contingent on completion of **12 hours of continuing education** in topics approved by DPS (energy code updates, accessibility, structural changes to the Building Code, etc.). The continuing-education requirement is enforced — a CSL holder who renews without the required CE hours has the license suspended pending compliance.

A CSL is held **individually**, not by a business entity. A construction company **does not** have a "CSL" — its individual employees do. This creates a critical compliance gotcha (see § 1.5).

#### 1.3.4 Permit-pulling rights and the homeowner-builder rule

Under **780 CMR 110.R5**, the building permit for any work regulated by the State Building Code must be applied for by a **licensed construction supervisor** who is also (typically) an HIC-registered contractor for residential work. There is **one statutory exception** — the **"homeowner-as-builder"** rule, codified at 780 CMR 110.R5.1.3.1 and 780 CMR 110.R5.1.3.2, which permits an owner of a one- or two-family dwelling to act as his or her own construction supervisor on his or her own dwelling, **provided that** the building official is given written notice that the owner is acting as the construction supervisor, the work will be performed by the owner or under his or her direct supervision, and the building official accepts the owner's assumption of responsibility.

Critically, **a contractor cannot ask a homeowner to pull the permit "as a homeowner"** in order to circumvent the CSL/HIC requirements — doing so (i) exposes the contractor to unlicensed-construction-supervision penalties under M.G.L. c. 143 § 94, (ii) **eliminates the homeowner's Guaranty Fund eligibility** under M.G.L. c. 142A § 5(c), and (iii) is a **per se Chapter 93A violation** under 201 CMR 14.05. This is one of the most aggressively enforced compliance violations in Massachusetts residential construction [13][14][15][16][20].

#### 1.3.5 Penalties for working without a CSL — M.G.L. c. 143 § 94

Under **M.G.L. c. 143 § 94**, supervising regulated construction without a CSL is **a criminal offense** punishable by a fine of up to **$500 per day** of violation and/or **imprisonment for up to six months**. Each day of work is a separate offense. The criminal liability under c. 143 is distinct from, and in addition to, the administrative penalty and Chapter 93A exposure under c. 142A. A contractor who performs residential work without **both** the HIC and the CSL faces criminal exposure under c. 143 § 94 **and** Chapter 93A treble-damages exposure under c. 142A — the dual-statute, dual-penalty regime is what makes Massachusetts uniquely punishing.

### 1.4 Trade licensing — electrical, plumbing, gas, sheet metal

Massachusetts licenses every mechanical trade at the state level, administered through trade-specific boards within the **Division of Occupational Licensure ("DOL")** (formerly the Division of Professional Licensure) [21][22][23][24].

#### 1.4.1 Electrical — M.G.L. c. 141 and 237 CMR

Under **M.G.L. c. 141 § 1 et seq.** and **237 CMR**, the **Board of State Examiners of Electricians** licenses three principal categories [21][22]:

1. **Master Electrician (Class A)** — required for any person engaged in the business of installing, altering, or repairing wires, conduit, or apparatus for carrying or using electricity for light, heat, or power.
2. **Journeyman Electrician** — works under the direction of a master.
3. **Apprentice Electrician** — required to be registered under the apprenticeship program in 237 CMR.

Corporate electrical contractors must register the business entity and identify a **Master Electrician of record** under M.G.L. c. 141 § 1A. Performing electrical work without a license is a violation of M.G.L. c. 141 § 5 and is punishable by fines and license revocation. Electrical work is exempt from HIC if performed strictly within the trade-license scope; however, "package" residential contracting that incorporates electrical work alongside other trades requires HIC registration **as well as** the trade license.

#### 1.4.2 Plumbing and gas fitting — M.G.L. c. 142 and 248 CMR

Under **M.G.L. c. 142 § 1 et seq.** and **248 CMR**, the **Board of State Examiners of Plumbers and Gas Fitters** licenses [23]:

1. **Master Plumber** — owns, operates, and supervises a plumbing business.
2. **Journeyman Plumber** — performs plumbing work under master supervision.
3. **Apprentice Plumber** — registered apprentice.
4. **Master Gas Fitter, Journeyman Gas Fitter, Apprentice Gas Fitter** — parallel structure for gas-fitting work.

Massachusetts has the **strictest plumbing code in the country**, the Uniform State Plumbing Code at **248 CMR 10.00**, which contains numerous Massachusetts-specific deviations from the IPC (e.g., requirements for back-flow prevention, materials standards more restrictive than national standards, and the long-standing prohibition on PEX in certain potable-water applications, since relaxed). Code compliance is enforced through plumbing inspectors at the municipal level, with state Board oversight.

#### 1.4.3 Sheet metal — M.G.L. c. 112 § 13½ÊC

Under **M.G.L. c. 112 § 13½ÊC** (added by St. 2007, c. 320 effective 2008), the **Board of Examiners of Sheet Metal Workers** licenses [24]:

1. **Master Sheet Metal Worker**;
2. **Journeyman Sheet Metal Worker**;
3. **Apprentice Sheet Metal Worker**.

Sheet metal licensure covers ductwork, gutters, roof flashings, and similar metal-fabrication work; it does **not** cover HVAC unit installation per se, which falls under refrigeration / EPA Section 608.

#### 1.4.4 HVAC — Massachusetts has no general HVAC license

Massachusetts is unusual in **not** having a single, unified HVAC contractor license. The functions are split across:

- **Sheet metal** (ductwork) — Sheet Metal license;
- **Gas piping** — Gas Fitter license;
- **Refrigeration / chiller work** — EPA Section 608 federal certification;
- **Electrical connection** — Electrician license;
- **Boiler installation** — DPS Boiler Inspector certification for installation of larger commercial boilers;

A residential HVAC change-out company typically holds a Sheet Metal license and a Gas Fitter license (or employs licensees in each), and registers as an HIC for residential work. Failure to recognize the **trade-by-trade fragmentation** is a common compliance error for out-of-state HVAC contractors entering Massachusetts [24].

#### 1.4.5 Fire protection sprinkler — M.G.L. c. 146 and 527 CMR

Fire sprinkler work is licensed at two levels:

1. **Fire Protection Sprinkler System Contractor** — business-level license issued by DPS;
2. **Sprinkler Fitter** — individual installer license.

The State Fire Code at 527 CMR governs installation requirements and is enforced jointly by the Office of the State Fire Marshal and municipal fire departments.

#### 1.4.6 Hoisting Engineer — M.G.L. c. 146 § 53 and 520 CMR 6.00

Anyone operating a hoisting machine (crane, derrick, hoist, telehandler above certain capacity ratings) requires a **Hoisting Engineer license** issued by DPS. The license is issued in multiple classes (1A, 1B, 1C, 1D, 2A, 2B, 2C, 2D, 3A, 4G, etc.) corresponding to equipment categories. This is in addition to federal OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1427 crane-operator certification, which Massachusetts treats as cumulative rather than substituted [25].

### 1.5 The dual-license trap — HIC + CSL — and how it most often fails

The single most expensive Massachusetts compliance failure is the **dual-license trap**: a residential general contractor who holds an HIC registration **without** a CSL, or a CSL **without** HIC registration, is **out of compliance** and exposed under both regimes. Common failure patterns observed in litigation [13][14][15][16][20][26][27]:

1. **The "HIC-only" contractor.** A small remodeler obtains HIC registration through OCABR (which is administratively easy — no exam, no experience requirement beyond background disclosure) and begins performing residential work that includes structural alterations (load-bearing wall removal, deck construction, foundation work, framing for additions). Each of those is regulated work under 780 CMR and **requires a CSL** to supervise. The contractor is exposed under c. 143 § 94 (criminal) and faces voiding of permits and stop-work orders from the building inspector.

2. **The "CSL-only" contractor.** A skilled carpenter holds a CSL (he passed the exam and has three years' experience) but never registers with OCABR. He performs renovation work on owner-occupied homes. Every contract is a per se Chapter 93A violation under 201 CMR 14.05(4); his contracts may be voidable; he is exposed to treble damages plus attorney fees on every job.

3. **The "borrowed CSL" scheme.** A company employs a CSL holder and uses his license number on permit applications, but the CSL holder does not actually supervise the work. This violates M.G.L. c. 143 § 95 (the CSL holder must actually supervise the work he permits) and exposes both the holder and the company to administrative discipline. The CSL holder's license is at risk of revocation.

4. **The "permit-by-homeowner" scheme.** The contractor talks the homeowner into pulling the permit as a homeowner-builder, ostensibly to save fees. This (i) is a per se 93A violation if used to evade the CSL requirement, (ii) eliminates Guaranty Fund eligibility, and (iii) shifts code-compliance liability to the homeowner — but the contractor is still on the hook for poor workmanship and remains a contractor for HIC purposes.

5. **The "subcontractor relied on the GC's HIC" scheme.** Under M.G.L. c. 142A § 9, **both the contractor and subcontractor** performing residential contracting services must be registered. A subcontractor who relies on the prime's registration is **independently in violation** and exposed to Chapter 93A. The Appeals Court has confirmed this in cases including *Mass. Bldg. Inspectors Ass'n* progeny.

6. **The "renewal-lapse" failure.** HIC registration is for two years; CSL is for two years; the renewal calendars do **not** sync, and the continuing-education requirement for CSL renewal is independent. A contractor whose CSL has lapsed for one project becomes an "unregistered" supervisor for that project — every day of permit issuance is potentially a c. 143 § 94 criminal violation.

ContractorPro's Massachusetts compliance flow must therefore verify **both** credentials at job intake, must track **renewal expirations for both** under independent calendars, and must surface a hard block on any residential job if either credential is missing or in lapse status. The credential-verification logic for Massachusetts is fundamentally **AND**-logic across two independent agency databases, not the **OR**-logic that suffices in single-license states.

### 1.6 No commercial general-contractor license; DCAMM prequalification for public work

For **commercial private** construction, Massachusetts requires **only** the CSL (for technical supervision of regulated work) and trade licenses (for regulated trades). There is **no commercial general-contractor business license**, no commercial registration, and no business-experience prequalification at the state level for private commercial work.

For **public construction**, however, Massachusetts has the most elaborate prequalification regime in the country. Under **M.G.L. c. 149 § 44D**, every general contractor and filed sub-bidder seeking to perform public building construction must be **prequalified** annually by the **Division of Capital Asset Management and Maintenance ("DCAMM")** [28]. DCAMM prequalification requires:

1. **Annual financial statements** (CPA-reviewed at minimum, CPA-audited above certain project thresholds);
2. **Project references and performance history**;
3. **Single-Project Limit (SPL)** and **Aggregate Project Limit (APL)** — DCAMM issues each contractor a maximum project size and aggregate workload it may bid;
4. **Category certifications** — separate prequalification certificates for general building construction, roofing, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, demolition, painting, masonry, etc.;
5. **Compliance with c. 149 § 44A through § 44H** — the "Filed Sub-Bid Statute," which is unique to Massachusetts and requires sub-bidders for certain trade categories on public projects above $150,000 (raised from prior thresholds) to bid directly to the awarding authority rather than to the general contractor.

The DCAMM prequalification process is rigorous and effectively functions as a Massachusetts-specific "public-works general contractor license," even though it is technically a procurement-prequalification regime rather than a licensing regime.

## 2. Mechanics' Liens — M.G.L. c. 254

The Massachusetts mechanics' lien statute, codified at **M.G.L. c. 254 § 1 through § 33**, is one of the **most procedurally complex** mechanics' lien regimes in the United States and contains several uniquely Massachusetts requirements that have no analog in any other state. The statute is highly mechanical — strict compliance with each procedural step is required, and a single missed deadline or improperly filed document will defeat the lien entirely. The Massachusetts Appeals Court has repeatedly held that c. 254 is "in derogation of the common law" and is **strictly construed** against the claimant [29][30][31][32].

### 2.1 The 2010 amendments — modern lien framework

Massachusetts comprehensively revised c. 254 in **St. 1996, c. 364**, which created the modern "Notice of Contract" framework, and refined it in **St. 2010, c. 419**, which made several technical improvements including clarifying the treatment of design professionals, retainage interactions, and the discharge mechanism. The current statute reflects approximately three decades of accumulated technical adjustments and judicial interpretation.

### 2.2 Who is entitled to a lien — M.G.L. c. 254 § 2 and § 4

Three categories of claimants are entitled to a c. 254 lien [29][30][31][32]:

1. **Prime contractor** — a person who has a **written contract** with the **owner** of the property to perform labor or to furnish labor and materials, or to perform labor and furnish materials, in the erection, alteration, repair, or removal of a building or structure on the land. M.G.L. c. 254 § 2.
2. **Subcontractor / supplier** (any tier) — a person who has a contract with the prime contractor (or with a subcontractor) and who has provided labor or labor and materials. M.G.L. c. 254 § 4. The lien is extended to all tiers of subcontractors and suppliers, including those who supply solely materials.
3. **Design professional** (architect, engineer, surveyor) — a registered design professional who has a written contract with the owner. M.G.L. c. 254 § 2A (added by St. 1996, c. 364 and refined in 2010).

**Two critical Massachusetts peculiarities at the threshold:**

(a) **The prime contractor's lien requires a *written* contract with the owner.** This is uncommon — most states allow oral contracts to support a prime contractor lien. In Massachusetts, an oral prime contract is **not** lien-supportable; the prime is left to its common-law breach-of-contract remedy only. The "writing" need not be elaborate (a signed proposal accepted in writing suffices) but it must be a genuine memorialization of the contract terms. M.G.L. c. 254 § 2; *NES Rentals v. Maine Drilling & Blasting*, 465 Mass. 856 (2013).

(b) **The subcontractor's lien does *not* require a written sub-subcontract**, but does require a chain of writings connecting the work to the prime contract — the prime contract must be written (so it can be recorded — see § 2.3), and the sub's right derives from the lien attaching to the property under the prime contract.

### 2.3 The Notice of Contract — M.G.L. c. 254 § 2 (prime) and § 4 (sub)

Massachusetts's **unique** lien-creation mechanism is the **Notice of Contract** (for prime contractors) or **Notice of Subcontract** (for subcontractors), which **must be recorded in the Registry of Deeds for the county where the property is located** in order to establish the lien [29][30][31][32][33][34]. Unlike most states, where the lien attaches automatically by operation of law upon the furnishing of work and is later perfected by recording a lien claim, Massachusetts requires **affirmative recording at the front end** to establish the lien at all.

#### 2.3.1 Notice of Contract — prime contractor

Under **M.G.L. c. 254 § 2**, the prime contractor records a **Notice of Contract** at the Registry of Deeds. The Notice must:

1. Be recorded **no later than the earliest of**:
   - **Sixty (60) days after filing or recording of the Notice of Substantial Completion** (M.G.L. c. 254 § 2A, recorded by the owner upon substantial completion); or
   - **Ninety (90) days after filing or recording of the Notice of Termination** (M.G.L. c. 254 § 2B, recorded by the owner upon termination of the contract); or
   - **Ninety (90) days after the prime contractor's last day of work or last delivery of materials**.
2. Include the **legal description of the property**;
3. Include the **name of the owner**;
4. Include the **name of the prime contractor**;
5. Include the **date and amount of the prime contract** (the contract price);
6. Be **verified by oath** of the prime contractor;
7. Be filed in the **Registry of Deeds for the county where the land is located**.

The Notice of Contract must be filed in **substantially the form prescribed by M.G.L. c. 254 § 2** — Massachusetts case law requires near-literal compliance, and immaterial defects in form may be excused but material defects (missing legal description, missing oath, missing contract date) defeat the lien.

#### 2.3.2 Notice of Subcontract — subcontractor / supplier

Under **M.G.L. c. 254 § 4**, the subcontractor or material supplier records a **Notice of Subcontract**, which differs from a prime's Notice of Contract in several respects [29][30][31][32][34]:

1. Must be recorded **before** the subcontractor's lien attaches. (The subcontractor lien does not attach to the property until the Notice of Subcontract is recorded — this is the moment of attachment for sub liens.)
2. Must include:
   - The **legal description of the property**;
   - The **name of the owner**;
   - The **name of the prime contractor**;
   - The **name of the subcontractor or supplier**;
   - The **name of the party with whom the subcontractor / supplier has contracted** (which may be the prime or an intermediate sub);
   - The **amount of the subcontract or supply contract**, including the unpaid balance if known;
3. Must be **verified by oath**;
4. Must be **served on the owner** by mailing or delivery to the owner's address as set forth in the Notice of Contract or as appearing in the Registry of Deeds chain of title. Service on the owner is a **separate**, distinct requirement from recording at the Registry of Deeds — both are required.
5. Must be filed no later than the **earliest of**:
   - **Sixty (60) days after filing of the Notice of Substantial Completion** by the owner; or
   - **Ninety (90) days after filing of the Notice of Termination** by the owner; or
   - **Ninety (90) days after the subcontractor's last day of work or last delivery of materials**.

The **subcontractor's lien amount is capped at the amount owed by the owner to the prime contractor** at the time the Notice of Subcontract is filed — i.e., the sub cannot recover more from the lien than the amount that the owner still owes the prime, regardless of how much the prime owes the sub. This is the **"pay-when-paid" cap** built into c. 254 § 4. The sub is encouraged to file the Notice of Subcontract as **early as possible**, because filing freezes the owner's payment obligation: any payment the owner makes to the prime **after** the Notice is filed does **not** reduce the sub's lien rights. This is among the most unusual lien-statute features in the country and is essential to understand for subcontractor protection planning.

### 2.4 Statement of Account — M.G.L. c. 254 § 8

After recording the Notice of Contract or Notice of Subcontract, the lien claimant must record a **Statement of Account** that itemizes the unpaid balance [29][30][31][32]. The Statement of Account is the document that converts the recorded notice into an enforceable lien for a specific dollar amount. Under **M.G.L. c. 254 § 8**, the Statement of Account must:

1. Be recorded in the Registry of Deeds **no later than the earliest of**:
   - **Ninety (90) days after filing of the Notice of Substantial Completion**; or
   - **120 days after filing of the Notice of Termination**; or
   - **120 days after the claimant's last day of work or last delivery of materials**.
2. Include the **legal description of the property**;
3. Include the **name of the owner**;
4. Include a **just and true account of the amount due**, including labor, materials, equipment rentals, and reasonable interest;
5. Identify the contract date and parties;
6. Be **verified by oath** of the claimant.

A defective Statement of Account that materially misstates the amount due may defeat the lien if the misstatement is willful or reckless; mere mathematical error or de minimis discrepancy will not. *Tremont Tower Condo. v. George B.H. Macomber Co.*, 436 Mass. 677 (2002).

### 2.5 Filing the lien enforcement action — M.G.L. c. 254 § 5

After recording the Statement of Account, the lien claimant must **file a civil action to enforce the lien within 90 days of recording the Statement of Account** [29][30][31][32]. Specifically, under **M.G.L. c. 254 § 5**:

1. The enforcement action must be filed in the **Superior Court** for the county where the property is located, or in the District Court if jurisdictional limits are met (typically Superior Court for any substantial lien);
2. The complaint must seek a **judgment establishing the validity and amount of the lien** and ordering **sale of the property to satisfy the lien**;
3. A **memorandum of lis pendens** must be recorded in the Registry of Deeds reflecting the filing of the action;
4. **Failure to file the enforcement action within 90 days of the Statement of Account dissolves the lien automatically by operation of law** under M.G.L. c. 254 § 5. This is the deadline that most commonly defeats Massachusetts liens in practice.

The 90-day enforcement deadline is **strict** — there is no statutory mechanism for extension, no equitable tolling, and no relief for excusable neglect. *Ng Bros. Constr., Inc. v. Cranney*, 436 Mass. 638 (2002).

### 2.6 The Massachusetts deadline puzzle — summary

The Massachusetts lien-deadline structure is therefore a multi-step, multi-deadline mechanism that has no analog in any other state. Summarizing for a hypothetical project where the owner has *not* filed a Notice of Substantial Completion or Notice of Termination:

| Step | Deadline (counted from last work / last delivery) |
|---|---|
| Notice of Contract (prime) | **90 days** |
| Notice of Subcontract (sub / supplier) | **90 days** |
| Statement of Account | **120 days** (i.e., 30 days after the Notice deadline) |
| Civil action to enforce | **90 days after recording Statement of Account** |
| **Total worst-case window** | **210 days** from last work to enforcement filing |

If the owner accelerates by filing a **Notice of Substantial Completion** under § 2A, the Notice-of-Contract deadline shortens to 60 days, the Statement-of-Account deadline shortens to 90 days, and the enforcement deadline (90 days after Statement of Account) compresses accordingly. If the owner files a **Notice of Termination** under § 2B, the deadlines shift to the termination-based clocks.

A Massachusetts general contractor relying on lien rights must therefore track at minimum:

1. **Last day of work** (continuously updated through final punch-list completion);
2. **Whether the owner has filed a Notice of Substantial Completion** (which the owner is *not* required to file but may choose to do strategically to accelerate the lien clock);
3. **Whether the owner has filed a Notice of Termination**;
4. **The 90-day cut-off for the Notice of Contract**;
5. **The 30-day window between Notice and Statement of Account**;
6. **The 90-day window between Statement and enforcement filing**.

This is fundamentally more complex than the single-deadline lien regimes of most states (Texas, Florida) and is among the most important Massachusetts-specific compliance behaviors that ContractorPro must build out.

### 2.7 Bond discharge — M.G.L. c. 254 § 14

The owner may **discharge a c. 254 lien by recording a bond** in the Registry of Deeds in an amount equal to **the amount of the lien**, with a surety approved by the court (or specified directly by statute) [29][30][31][32]. Once the bond is recorded:

1. The lien is **discharged from the real property**;
2. The lien attaches to the **bond proceeds** in lieu of the property;
3. The claimant must proceed against the bond.

In practice, this is the dominant resolution for owner-friendly disputes — the owner posts a bond, the property is freed for refinancing or sale, and the claimant proceeds against the surety.

### 2.8 Lien on lessee's interest — M.G.L. c. 254 § 25

A tenant-improvement contractor may obtain a lien on the **lessee's leasehold interest** in the property (not the fee) when the work is performed under a contract with the lessee and not with the fee owner. The lien on the leasehold is junior to any encumbrances on the fee. M.G.L. c. 254 § 25.

### 2.9 Liens against public funds — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29

For **public construction projects**, Massachusetts substitutes a **statutory payment bond regime under M.G.L. c. 149 § 29** for mechanics' liens — public property cannot be subject to a c. 254 lien because Massachusetts has not waived sovereign immunity for that purpose. Subcontractors and suppliers on public projects instead make claim against the **§ 29 payment bond** required of every public-construction general contractor. Notice of claim must be served within 65 days of the last day of work, and suit must be filed within one year [35][36].

### 2.10 No-lien clauses — M.G.L. c. 254 § 32

**Massachusetts prohibits enforceable no-lien clauses in private contracts.** Under **M.G.L. c. 254 § 32**, "any agreement in writing or otherwise to the effect that no lien under this chapter shall attach to a particular property or the labor or materials furnished" is **void and unenforceable** as to any claimant who was not a party to that agreement. This is a critical Massachusetts protection for subcontractors: a contractual no-lien clause between owner and prime does **not** bind subcontractors and suppliers, who retain c. 254 lien rights regardless of upstream contractual language. The provision is functionally analogous to the lien-waiver-restriction statutes in New York, Texas, and other lien-protective jurisdictions.

### 2.11 Lien waivers — M.G.L. c. 254 § 32 partial- and conditional-waiver framework

While Massachusetts prohibits prospective no-lien clauses (§ 2.10), it does permit **partial and conditional lien waivers** in the standard pay-app context. The Massachusetts AIA-style partial waiver — provided for payment received — is enforceable and is the dominant industry practice. Final lien waivers are likewise enforceable for amounts actually paid. Massachusetts has *no* statutory form for lien waivers (unlike California, which mandates a statutory form for conditional and unconditional waivers under Civ. Code § 8132 et seq.); contractors are free to use AIA G706/G706A or equivalent industry forms, and most do. The waiver itself must be a writing supported by consideration (payment) [37][38].

## 3. Prompt Pay and Retainage — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E and § 29F

Massachusetts enacted comprehensive **prompt-payment legislation** in 2010 through **St. 2010, c. 424** ("An Act Relative to Fair Practices in Construction"), which added §§ 29E and 29F to chapter 149 and provides one of the most robust statutory frameworks in the country [37][38][39][40].

### 3.1 Scope — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(a)

The Massachusetts Prompt Payment Act applies to **private construction contracts** of $3,000,000 or more (the threshold reaches large commercial and multifamily projects but excludes most small commercial and residential work). Under **M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(a)**, the statute applies to contracts:

1. **Between an owner and a general contractor** for construction, reconstruction, alteration, remodeling, or repair of real property;
2. **Between a general contractor and a subcontractor**, and between any subcontractor and a lower-tier subcontractor or supplier;
3. With **a value of $3,000,000 or more** (aggregate over the term);
4. For work performed in **Massachusetts**;
5. **Other than residential** projects of fewer than four units (which are excluded from § 29E).

Public construction has its own prompt-pay framework under c. 30 § 39K and c. 30 § 39F (retainage on public projects).

### 3.2 Payment application and approval — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(c) and (d)

The statute prescribes a **detailed pay-app processing schedule** [37][38][39][40]:

1. **Payment applications** must be submitted at intervals not less frequent than monthly;
2. The **owner has 15 days** to approve or reject a payment application after submission (M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(c));
3. **Rejection must be in writing**, identify the specific reason for rejection (with reference to specific items in the application), and be **certified by an officer or authorized agent of the owner**. Mere "unspecified rejection" or "boilerplate rejection" is invalid and renders the application **deemed approved** under § 29E(c)(2);
4. **Approved payment applications must be paid within 45 days** of submission (i.e., 30 days after the 15-day approval window), and partial approvals must be paid in the approved amount within the same 45 days under § 29E(d).

### 3.3 Pass-through to subcontractors — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(e)

For payments received by the general contractor from the owner, the general contractor must pay each subcontractor for **its approved portion** within **45 days of submission** of the subcontractor's pay app, or **7 days** after receipt of the corresponding owner payment, **whichever is later**. The pass-through obligation continues down through each subcontract tier under § 29E(e)(2) and (3). This is **stronger** than the typical state prompt-pay regime — Massachusetts allows the GC to wait for owner payment only up to 7 days after receipt, not the unlimited "pay-when-paid" deferral common elsewhere.

### 3.4 Interest on late payment — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(f)

Any payment that is wrongfully withheld or delayed accrues **interest at the rate of 1% per month** (12% annualized), running from the date payment was due [37][38][39][40].

### 3.5 Right to suspend performance — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(g)

A general contractor or subcontractor who has not received undisputed payment within **45 days of the date the payment was due** may, after giving written notice and a 7-day opportunity to cure, **suspend performance** of the contract without breach. This is a powerful remedy unavailable in most states' prompt-pay statutes and provides meaningful leverage in payment disputes. The suspension right survives any contractual "no-suspension" clause under M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(g)(3), which expressly voids such clauses [40].

### 3.6 Anti-waiver — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(h)

The prompt-pay statute is **non-waivable**: any contract provision purporting to waive the rights granted by § 29E is **void and unenforceable**. M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E(h). This includes "pay-when-paid" and "pay-if-paid" clauses to the extent they purport to extend payment beyond the statutory deadlines.

### 3.7 Retainage on private work — M.G.L. c. 149 § 29F

The companion **retainage** statute, **M.G.L. c. 149 § 29F**, governs retainage on private construction contracts of $3,000,000 or more [38][39][40][41]:

1. **Maximum retainage — 5%** of the value of each pay app for the entire term of the contract under M.G.L. c. 149 § 29F(b). (Historically, Massachusetts allowed 10% retainage; the 2010 statute reduced the cap to 5% across the board on covered projects.)
2. **Application to subs — same 5% cap** under § 29F(b)(2), with mandatory pass-through;
3. **Release upon substantial completion** — within **60 days of substantial completion**, the owner must release retainage minus three times the value of any "incomplete or defective work" identified on a punch list, under § 29F(c);
4. **Punch list must be issued within 14 days of substantial completion** under § 29F(c)(2); failure to issue the punch list forfeits the right to retain anything from retainage;
5. **Final release** must occur within **30 days** of completion of all punch-list items;
6. **Anti-waiver** — any contractual provision purporting to extend retainage beyond the statutory cap or to delay release is void under § 29F(d).

### 3.8 Public-construction retainage — M.G.L. c. 30 § 39F and § 39K

For **public construction projects**, retainage and prompt payment are governed by separate statutes [42]:

1. **M.G.L. c. 30 § 39F** governs retainage on public projects — historically 5% retainage, with release tied to substantial completion and punch-list completion;
2. **M.G.L. c. 30 § 39K** governs prompt payment of subcontractors on public projects;
3. **M.G.L. c. 149 § 29** governs the public-construction payment bond regime as the substitute for lien rights.

### 3.9 Contractor compliance behaviors

For ContractorPro, the prompt-pay framework dictates several systematic behaviors on Massachusetts projects above $3M:

1. **Pay-app submission tracking** with a 15-day "owner approval deadline" alarm;
2. **Pay-app rejection-letter review** to confirm specific-and-certified rejection (defective rejections become deemed approvals);
3. **45-day "owner payment due" alarm** from pay-app submission;
4. **7-day "GC pay-down to subs" alarm** from owner payment receipt;
5. **1%/month interest accrual** auto-calculation on overdue invoices;
6. **45-day "right to suspend" trigger** with 7-day cure-notice template;
7. **5% retainage cap** validation at contract setup (and automatic flag if contract attempts 10% or more);
8. **Substantial-completion + 14-day punch-list issuance** alarm; **60-day retainage release** alarm.

These behaviors are *qualitatively* different from a "30 days net" billing reminder and require Massachusetts-specific logic that ContractorPro must build out separately from the generic AR/AP module.

## 4. Tax — M.G.L. c. 64H and Department of Revenue Guidance

### 4.1 Sales tax framework — M.G.L. c. 64H

Massachusetts imposes a **6.25% state sales tax** on retail sales of tangible personal property and certain services under **M.G.L. c. 64H § 2**, with a parallel use tax under M.G.L. c. 64I [43][44][45]. Several Massachusetts-specific points are critical for contractors:

1. **No local sales tax** — Massachusetts is one of a small number of states with no county or municipal sales tax. The 6.25% statewide rate is the entire sales tax burden, with no local layering. This simplifies tax calculation across job sites significantly compared to states like Illinois, Texas, or California, where local rates vary by zip code.
2. **No sales tax on services** — Massachusetts taxes only **tangible personal property** and a narrow list of enumerated services (telecommunications, certain repairs in c. 64H § 1(13)). Construction services as such are **not** subject to sales tax, but the materials incorporated into construction are.

### 4.2 The contractor-as-consumer rule — DOR Directive 88-13

Massachusetts follows the **contractor-as-consumer** model under **DOR Directive 88-13** and Letter Ruling 84-89: a contractor performing construction services is treated as **the consumer** of materials incorporated into real property, and pays sales tax at the time of purchase from suppliers [43][44][45]. The contractor does **not** charge sales tax to the customer on either materials or labor; the materials are taxed at the supplier level (sales tax paid by the contractor), and labor is not taxed at all.

Key implications:

1. **Contractor purchases materials at retail and pays the 6.25% sales tax** at the supplier level. The contractor does not provide a resale certificate (it is not reselling — it is consuming).
2. **Customer invoice should not separately state sales tax** on materials, because the sale to the customer is a sale of real-property improvements (not taxable), not a sale of tangible personal property.
3. **If the contractor separately states sales tax on the customer invoice**, that constitutes "tax representations" potentially exposing the contractor to DOR audit and to liability under M.G.L. c. 62C for the represented amount.
4. **Sub-fabrication of items off-site** — if the contractor fabricates items off-site (custom cabinets, custom millwork, custom steel) and then installs them, the off-site fabrication value may be reanalyzed by DOR as a sale of tangible personal property installed by the seller — this is the "fabricator-installer" gray zone, and the contractor's treatment turns on the specific fact pattern under DOR's "incidental services" analysis.

### 4.3 Exempt-entity work — DOR TIR 18-1 and Form ST-5

Sales of materials to a contractor that are **incorporated** into a project for an **exempt entity** (M.G.L. c. 64H § 6(d) — 501(c)(3) charities, governmental entities, religious organizations) may be **purchased exempt from sales tax** under the **Form ST-5** exemption certificate framework [44][45][46]. The exemption is **derivative** — the contractor passes through the exempt status of the customer by:

1. **Obtaining a Form ST-5** from the exempt entity (verifying its tax-exempt status with DOR);
2. **Providing a Form ST-5C** (Contractor's Sales Tax Exempt Purchase Certificate) to the supplier, attesting that the purchase is for a specific exempt-entity project;
3. **Retaining records** linking each exempt purchase to the specific project for at least three years for audit purposes.

The ST-5/ST-5C framework is the **only** mechanism for contractor exempt purchases — there is no general contractor's sales-tax-exemption license in Massachusetts. Misuse of ST-5C (using it on a taxable project) is a serious DOR enforcement priority and can expose the contractor to assessment of the unpaid tax plus penalty and interest, and to potential criminal exposure under M.G.L. c. 62C § 73 for willful evasion.

### 4.4 Use tax — M.G.L. c. 64I

The Massachusetts **use tax** at 6.25% applies to any tangible personal property **purchased out-of-state and brought into Massachusetts for use** in a Massachusetts project [44][45]. Common contractor scenarios:

1. **Out-of-state material purchase** — contractor buys equipment or materials in New Hampshire (no sales tax) and brings them to a Massachusetts job → 6.25% use tax owed to Massachusetts;
2. **Internet purchase from out-of-state vendor that does not collect Massachusetts sales tax** — use tax owed (though post-*Wayfair* most vendors now collect);
3. **Donated or transferred property** — generally not subject to use tax if no purchase consideration paid.

Use tax compliance is the **single largest unrecognized exposure** for contractors that source materials in New Hampshire. The New Hampshire / Massachusetts state line is the most-traveled tax-arbitrage boundary in New England and is a known DOR enforcement priority.

### 4.5 Corporate excise tax — M.G.L. c. 63

Massachusetts imposes a **corporate excise tax** under M.G.L. c. 63, with two components:

1. **Net income measure** — 8.0% of Massachusetts taxable net income;
2. **Property measure** — $2.60 per $1,000 of net worth or tangible property in Massachusetts, with a minimum tax of **$456**.

S-corporations are subject to a "sting tax" measured on Massachusetts net income above certain thresholds. LLCs are pass-through entities (no separate corporate excise) unless they elect corporate treatment. Sole proprietors and partnerships are subject only to personal income tax at **5.0%**.

### 4.6 Personal income tax — M.G.L. c. 62 and the "millionaire surtax"

Massachusetts has a **flat 5.0% personal income tax** under M.G.L. c. 62 § 4, with a **4% surtax on income above $1,000,000** (the "Fair Share Amendment" / "millionaire surtax") effective January 1, 2023, under Article 44 of the Massachusetts Constitution as amended by Question 1 on the 2022 ballot. The effective top rate on income above $1M is therefore **9.0%**. The surtax has significant implications for contractor-owners drawing large pass-through K-1 income from S-corps and partnerships and is a planning consideration for any owner approaching the $1M threshold.

### 4.7 Withholding — M.G.L. c. 62B

Employers must withhold Massachusetts income tax under M.G.L. c. 62B at the 5.0% flat rate (with no graduated bracket structure to manage), simplifying contractor payroll significantly relative to states with graduated income tax brackets.

## 5. Workers' Compensation — M.G.L. c. 152

### 5.1 Coverage requirement — M.G.L. c. 152 § 25A

Under **M.G.L. c. 152 § 25A**, every employer in the Commonwealth that "has one or more employees" must provide **workers' compensation insurance coverage**. Unlike many states, Massachusetts has **no minimum employee count exemption** — even a single part-time employee triggers the coverage requirement [47][48][49][50][51]. The Massachusetts rule is more strict than:

- **New Jersey** (which similarly has no minimum but allows certain exemptions for sole proprietors);
- **Pennsylvania** (which has a 1-employee threshold but allows owner-officer exemptions);
- **Ohio** (which is a monopolistic state with the Bureau of Workers' Compensation as the exclusive carrier).

Massachusetts is a **private-carrier state**: WC coverage is purchased from private insurance carriers, with rates filed through the **Workers' Compensation Rating and Inspection Bureau of Massachusetts ("WCRIBMA")** under M.G.L. c. 152 § 52A. The **assigned-risk pool** is the residual market for employers unable to obtain voluntary coverage.

### 5.2 Construction-industry application — M.G.L. c. 152 § 1(4) and § 26

The Workers' Compensation Act applies broadly in construction. Key Massachusetts construction-specific rules [47][48][49][50][51]:

1. **Sole proprietors and partners** are **not** automatically covered as "employees" and must affirmatively elect coverage under M.G.L. c. 152 § 1(4)(b) to be covered.
2. **Corporate officers** are presumptively covered as employees but may file a **Form 153 election** to exclude themselves from coverage if they own at least 25% of the corporation.
3. **LLC members** are treated similarly to corporate officers with an election option.
4. **Subcontractor coverage cascade — M.G.L. c. 152 § 18** — a general contractor is **liable for workers' compensation benefits to the employees of an uninsured subcontractor** ("statutory employer" doctrine). This makes verification of subcontractor WC coverage at job intake **mandatory** as a risk-management matter, not optional.

### 5.3 Stop-Work Orders — M.G.L. c. 152 § 25C

The Massachusetts Department of Industrial Accidents has aggressive enforcement powers under **M.G.L. c. 152 § 25C** [47][48][49][50]:

1. **Stop-Work Order ("SWO")** — DIA may issue a SWO at any work site where an employer is operating without WC coverage. The SWO requires **immediate cessation** of all work at the site.
2. **$100 per day civil penalty** for each day the employer operates without coverage, payable to the Workers' Compensation Trust Fund.
3. **Criminal penalty** — up to **$1,500 per day** in fines and/or **imprisonment for up to one year** under M.G.L. c. 152 § 25C(11) for willful failure to provide coverage.
4. **Treble-damages exposure** — an injured employee of an uncovered employer may sue the employer directly in tort and recover **without** the exclusive-remedy bar of M.G.L. c. 152 § 24, including pain and suffering and punitive components. *Bisazza v. Bisazza*, 425 Mass. 412 (1997).
5. **Public-bidding disqualification** — an employer subject to an active SWO is **automatically debarred** from bidding on public construction under c. 152 § 25C(5).
6. **Personal liability of officers** — under M.G.L. c. 152 § 25A(2)(b), corporate officers may be **personally liable** for unpaid WC premium and Trust Fund assessments where the corporation has failed to provide coverage. This is rare among states and a significant individual-exposure issue for contractor-owners.

### 5.4 Independent contractor / ABC test — M.G.L. c. 149 § 148B

Massachusetts has the **strictest worker-classification statute in the country**, the **Independent Contractor Statute** at **M.G.L. c. 149 § 148B**, originally enacted as part of the wage-act reform in 1990 and amended to its current form by **St. 2004, c. 193**, effective July 19, 2004 [52][53][54][55].

A worker is presumed an **employee** unless the hiring entity establishes **all three** of the following:

**(A)** The worker is **free from control and direction** in the performance of the service, both under the contract and in fact;
**(B)** The service is performed **outside the usual course of the business** of the employer; AND
**(C)** The worker is **customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business** of the same nature as the service performed.

The **(B) prong** is the most aggressive in the country and is virtually impossible for a construction contractor to satisfy with respect to workers performing core construction trades. A roofing contractor cannot classify roofers as 1099 independent contractors because roofing is "in the usual course of business" — Prong B fails as a matter of law. *Somers v. Converged Access, Inc.*, 454 Mass. 582 (2009); *Schwann v. FedEx Ground Package System, Inc.*, 813 F.3d 429 (1st Cir. 2016) (interpreting Massachusetts law).

Misclassification consequences under M.G.L. c. 149 § 148B and the Wage Act (c. 149 § 148) include:

1. **Treble damages on unpaid wages** under M.G.L. c. 149 § 150;
2. **Attorney fees** to the prevailing employee;
3. **Civil penalties** payable to the Attorney General;
4. **WC premium recapture** by the carrier under M.G.L. c. 152, plus assessment by the Trust Fund;
5. **Unemployment-insurance assessment** by the Department of Unemployment Assistance;
6. **Withholding-tax assessment** by DOR for unpaid Massachusetts income tax withholding;
7. **Personal liability of corporate officers** under c. 149 § 148, *Wiedmann v. The Bradford Group*, 444 Mass. 698 (2005) (corporate president personally liable for unpaid wages);
8. **Criminal exposure** for willful violations under M.G.L. c. 149 § 27C.

The combined exposure for a roofer who classifies a 10-person crew as 1099s for two years can easily reach **seven figures** when the Wage Act, WC, UI, withholding, and treble-damages multipliers are stacked.

### 5.5 The "in the usual course of business" prong — practical guidance

Several pragmatic patterns survive Prong B in Massachusetts construction [54][55]:

1. **Truly independent subcontracting firms** — a roofing contractor subcontracts to **another corporate roofing contractor** (separate entity, separate WC, separate FEIN, separate insurance, separate trucks). The subcontractor's *employees* are its employees, not the prime's. This is the standard approach.
2. **Specialty trades outside core scope** — a general contractor engaging an electrical subcontractor satisfies Prong B because electrical work is not in the GC's usual course of business (the GC does general contracting, not electrical work).
3. **Professional services** — engaging an architect, engineer, or surveyor as a 1099 is permissible because design services are not within the contractor's usual course of business.

The pattern that **does not** survive is 1099 individual labor — a roofer engaging 1099 roofers, a framing contractor engaging 1099 framers, a painter engaging 1099 painters. These all fail Prong B and convert the workers to employees as a matter of law, with the cascade of consequences set forth above.

### 5.6 Wage Act — M.G.L. c. 149 § 148

The Massachusetts **Wage Act** at **M.G.L. c. 149 § 148** is among the most employer-hostile wage-payment statutes in the country [54][55][56]:

1. **Wages must be paid weekly or bi-weekly** within six days of the end of the pay period for hourly employees;
2. **Final wages on termination** must be paid **on the day of termination** (involuntary) or by the next regular payday (voluntary resignation);
3. **Vacation pay is treated as wages** and must be paid at termination if accrued;
4. **Treble damages, attorney fees, and costs** are **automatic** under M.G.L. c. 149 § 150 — not discretionary. Any violation, even a one-day late payment, exposes the employer to 3x the unpaid amount plus the employee's attorney fees, regardless of intent or good faith.
5. **Personal liability of officers** under § 148 — corporate president, treasurer, and any "officer or agent having the management of such corporation" can be **personally liable** for unpaid wages. *Wiedmann v. The Bradford Group*, 444 Mass. 698 (2005); *Cook v. Patient Edu, LLC*, 465 Mass. 548 (2013).

### 5.7 Prevailing Wage — M.G.L. c. 149 §§ 26-27H

For **public construction projects**, the Massachusetts Prevailing Wage Act at **M.G.L. c. 149 §§ 26-27H** requires payment of the prevailing wage rate determined by the **Department of Labor Standards ("DLS")** for the locality and trade [56][57]. Key features:

1. **Locality-based wage rates** — DLS issues a wage rate schedule for each project, valid for the duration of the contract;
2. **Certified payroll** — each contractor and subcontractor must submit weekly **certified payroll** records (DLS Form WH-2 or equivalent);
3. **Apprentice ratios** — prevailing-wage projects require registered apprentice utilization at the trade-specific ratios published by the Division of Apprentice Standards;
4. **Penalties** for failure to pay prevailing wages include treble damages under c. 149 § 150 and **debarment** from public contracts for up to three years.

### 5.8 Earned Sick Time Law — M.G.L. c. 149 § 148C

Under the **Massachusetts Earned Sick Time Law** at **M.G.L. c. 149 § 148C**, all employers in the Commonwealth must provide employees with up to **40 hours of earned sick time per year**, **paid** if the employer has 11 or more employees and **unpaid** if fewer than 11 employees [56]. Sick time accrues at one hour per 30 hours worked and is usable for the employee's own illness, family member illness, or routine medical appointments. Compliance includes accrual tracking, year-end carryover, and notice requirements.

### 5.9 Paid Family and Medical Leave — M.G.L. c. 175M

The **Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave Act ("PFML")** at **M.G.L. c. 175M** (enacted as St. 2018, c. 121 and effective for benefits January 1, 2021) is a statewide, employer- and employee-funded paid family and medical leave program covering up to **20 weeks of medical leave** and **12 weeks of family leave** annually, with combined leave capped at **26 weeks** [56][58]. Employees and (for employers of 25+) employers pay a payroll-tax contribution to the **Department of Family and Medical Leave** Trust Fund. The current contribution rate is approximately **0.88% of wages** (split between employee and employer for employers of 25+, employee-only for smaller employers), updated annually.

## 6. Chapter 93A — Consumer Protection Act

### 6.1 Statutory framework — M.G.L. c. 93A § 1 et seq.

Massachusetts's **Chapter 93A** is among the most powerful consumer-protection statutes in the country, enacted as St. 1967, c. 813 ("An Act Regulating Business Practices for Consumer's Protection") and substantially amended thereafter. The core provisions [9][10][11][12]:

1. **§ 2(a)** — declares unlawful "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce";
2. **§ 2(c)** — authorizes the Attorney General to promulgate regulations defining unfair and deceptive practices, codified at **940 CMR 3.00 et seq.**;
3. **§ 9** — provides a private cause of action for any **consumer** injured by a violation, with mandatory **double-to-treble damages** for willful or knowing violations, plus **attorney fees and costs**, plus injunctive relief;
4. **§ 11** — provides a parallel private cause of action for **businesses** injured by another business's violation in trade or commerce, with similar damages structure.

### 6.2 Demand letter — M.G.L. c. 93A § 9(3)

Before filing a consumer 93A claim, the consumer must serve a **demand letter** on the defendant **at least 30 days prior to filing suit**, identifying:

1. The claimant and claimant's counsel;
2. The unfair or deceptive act or practice;
3. The injury suffered;
4. The damages sought.

The defendant has 30 days to make a **reasonable written offer of settlement**. If the offer is rejected and the claimant later recovers more than the offer in litigation, **double damages** are automatic; if the defendant's rejection was willful or knowing, **treble damages**. Conversely, if the defendant's reasonable offer is unreasonably rejected, the claimant's recovery may be capped at the rejected offer amount under § 9(3). *Slaney v. Westwood Auto, Inc.*, 366 Mass. 688 (1975).

The demand letter is therefore **a strategic inflection point**: a contractor receiving a 93A demand letter should treat it as a 30-day window to make a reasonable settlement offer and avoid the double/treble multiplier on any subsequent litigation recovery.

### 6.3 Construction-industry 93A landscape

Several Massachusetts regulations make specific construction-industry practices **per se 93A violations** [9][10][11][12]:

1. **Failure to register as HIC** — 201 CMR 14.05;
2. **Failure to use a compliant written contract above $1,000** — 201 CMR 14.04;
3. **Demanding deposit above one-third** — 201 CMR 14.04(2);
4. **Failure to display HIC registration number** on contracts and advertising — 201 CMR 14.04;
5. **Failure to obtain required permits** or causing the homeowner to pull permits as "homeowner-builder" — 201 CMR 14.05;
6. **Failure to honor the three-day cooling-off right** under M.G.L. c. 93 § 48 (door-to-door) — 940 CMR 3.05;
7. **Misrepresentation of credentials**, work to be performed, or warranties — 940 CMR 3.05.

Every one of these practices that goes wrong on a residential project converts directly into a 93A claim with double/treble damages and attorney fees. The cumulative effect is that residential contractors in Massachusetts operate under a regulatory regime with extraordinary downside asymmetry — small procedural errors produce large financial exposures.

### 6.4 Comparison to New Jersey CFA

The Massachusetts 93A/HIC regime is **functionally equivalent** to the New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act / Contractors' Registration Act regime in punitive structure (treble damages, attorney fees, per se violations) but differs procedurally:

- Massachusetts requires a 30-day demand letter (NJ does not);
- Massachusetts has a separate Guaranty Fund (NJ does not at the state level);
- Massachusetts has a separate CSL technical-supervision license (NJ has no analog);
- New Jersey's CRA insurance requirement is $500,000 GL (Massachusetts has no statutory GL requirement);
- New Jersey applies the three-day cooling-off right to door-to-door sales similarly.

The treble-damages exposure is comparable in magnitude. Both states demand significant contractor compliance investment to manage.

## 7. Recent Legislative Changes — 2024–2026

### 7.1 193rd General Court (2023–2024) and 194th General Court (2025–2026)

Massachusetts operates on two-year legislative sessions. The 193rd General Court sat 2023–2024, and the 194th General Court convenes January 2025 through December 2026. Several construction-relevant bills have advanced or are pending [59][60][61][62][63]:

#### 7.1.1 HIC Modernization — 2024

An Act Relative to the Modernization of the Home Improvement Contractor Program was filed in the 193rd session as part of OCABR's regulatory-update package. Among the proposals:

1. **Increased Guaranty Fund cap** from $10,000 to $25,000 per claim;
2. **Continuing-education requirement** for HIC registration (currently no CE requirement, unlike CSL);
3. **Updated insurance requirement** — proposed minimum $300,000 CGL (currently not statutorily required for HIC, though disclosure is required);
4. **Mandatory digital recordkeeping** of contracts and pay applications, with retention period harmonized at seven years;
5. **Mandatory pre-construction disclosure** of subcontractors to be used, with HIC verification of subs' registrations.

Status as of May 2026: the bill remains in committee; portions have been incorporated into OCABR's pending 2026 regulatory update at 201 CMR 14.00. Contractors should monitor the 194th session for movement.

#### 7.1.2 Lien Statute Amendments — 2025

H. 1745 (193rd) / S. 2208 (194th) — proposed clarifications to M.G.L. c. 254, including:

1. **Codification of "substantial completion" trigger** for the Notice of Termination clock;
2. **Service-on-owner requirements for Notice of Subcontract** clarified to include certified mail with return receipt requested;
3. **Lien dissolution mechanism** simplified for projects where claim is paid post-recording but pre-enforcement.

Status: pending in Joint Committee on the Judiciary.

#### 7.1.3 Prompt Pay Threshold — 2024

S. 1024 (193rd) proposed reducing the M.G.L. c. 149 § 29E threshold from $3,000,000 to **$1,000,000** to extend prompt-pay protections to smaller commercial projects and to address the impact of inflation on the $3M threshold since 2010 enactment. The bill drew significant support from the subcontractor community (Associated Subcontractors of Massachusetts) and opposition from larger commercial developers. Status: passed Senate in 2024, pending in House Joint Committee on Labor and Workforce Development in the 194th session.

#### 7.1.4 Worker Misclassification Reform — Ongoing

In response to enforcement actions by the Attorney General's Fair Labor Division and to multiple federal-court rulings interpreting M.G.L. c. 149 § 148B, several bills filed in the 194th session would:

1. **Increase the civil-penalty range** for misclassification violations;
2. **Authorize the Attorney General to impose stop-work orders** for misclassification (currently limited to WC stop-work);
3. **Create a public registry of contractors with misclassification judgments** to inform consumer choice and public-bidding decisions.

Status: in committee. The misclassification regime in Massachusetts is already among the strictest in the country, and enforcement intensity is increasing rather than statutory change driving the trend.

#### 7.1.5 PFML Updates — 2025

The Department of Family and Medical Leave updates the M.G.L. c. 175M contribution rate annually. For calendar year 2025, the total contribution rate is approximately **0.88% of covered wages**; for 2026, expected to remain in the 0.85%-0.90% range subject to actuarial determination. Employers must update payroll systems annually.

#### 7.1.6 Building Code transition — 780 CMR Tenth Edition

The Massachusetts State Building Code is transitioning from the **Ninth Edition** (based on 2015 ICC codes with Massachusetts amendments) to the **Tenth Edition** (based on 2021 ICC codes), with the Board of Building Regulations and Standards (BBRS) issuing the Tenth Edition in stages through 2025–2026. The Tenth Edition incorporates:

1. **2021 International Residential Code (IRC)** with Massachusetts-specific amendments at 780 CMR 51.00;
2. **2021 International Building Code (IBC)** with amendments at 780 CMR 1-10;
3. **Updated Massachusetts Energy Code** at 780 CMR 13 (with stretch-code and opt-in net-zero overlay provisions);
4. **Updated accessibility provisions** at 521 CMR (Architectural Access Board), retained alongside the Tenth Edition.

ContractorPro should treat any code-cross-reference logic for Massachusetts as **versioned**, with the active code edition determined by permit issuance date.

#### 7.1.7 Clean Energy / Decarbonization Mandates — 2024–2026

Massachusetts has aggressive clean-energy mandates affecting construction. Key statutes:

1. **An Act Driving Clean Energy and Offshore Wind** — St. 2022, c. 179 — establishes targets for emissions reductions and electrification of new construction;
2. **Net-Zero Stretch Code** under 225 CMR 22 and 23 — opt-in by municipalities; as of 2026, **approximately 38 municipalities** have adopted the Net-Zero Stretch Code, requiring all-electric or net-zero-equivalent design for new construction;
3. **Fossil-fuel-free demonstration program** — St. 2022, c. 179 § 84 — pilot allowing up to 10 municipalities to **prohibit fossil-fuel hookups in new construction**; participating municipalities include Brookline, Cambridge, Newton, Lexington, Concord, Acton, Aquinnah, Lincoln, West Tisbury, and Arlington. Contractors building new construction in these municipalities **cannot install natural gas or propane lines** for space or water heating.

The municipality-by-municipality variation in code requirements is a significant compliance complexity that contractors must track at the project-jurisdiction level.

## 8. ContractorPro Implementation Notes — Massachusetts-Specific Behaviors

Drawing the threads above together, ContractorPro's Massachusetts behaviors must include:

### 8.1 Credential management

1. **Dual-credential verification at contractor onboarding** — both **HIC** (via OCABR public registry) and **CSL** (via DPS public registry) for any residential general contractor. Hard block on residential job creation if either is missing or in non-active status.
2. **Separate renewal calendars** for HIC (2-year cycle) and CSL (2-year cycle with 12 hours of continuing education).
3. **Trade-license verification** — electrical, plumbing, gas, sheet metal each with separate licensee lookup and renewal tracking.
4. **DCAMM prequalification tracking** for any public-bid work, with single-project-limit (SPL) and aggregate-project-limit (APL) enforcement at bid creation.

### 8.2 Contract intake

1. **Property-type classification** — owner-occupied 1-4 unit, multifamily 5+, commercial, or new construction. Determines HIC applicability and Guaranty Fund eligibility.
2. **Permit responsibility flag** — contractor pulls or owner pulls. If owner pulls, surface warning: "Owner-pulled permit forfeits Guaranty Fund eligibility under M.G.L. c. 142A § 5(c) and may create Chapter 93A exposure for contractor under 201 CMR 14.05."
3. **Contract-template generation** — Massachusetts-specific template for any contract above $1,000 incorporating all 201 CMR 14.04(1) required elements, the three-day cancellation notice for door-to-door, HIC number disclosure, and Guaranty Fund notice.
4. **Initial deposit validation** — block contract creation if deposit exceeds one-third of contract price unless special-order-materials exception is invoked with itemized special-order disclosure.

### 8.3 Lien-rights tracking

1. **Last-day-of-work continuous tracking** through final punch-list.
2. **Owner-filed Notice of Substantial Completion / Notice of Termination monitoring** — alarm when filed.
3. **Notice-of-Contract deadline alarm** at 90 days from last work (60 from NSC, 90 from NoT).
4. **Statement-of-Account deadline alarm** at 120 days from last work (90 from NSC, 120 from NoT).
5. **Enforcement-action deadline alarm** at 90 days post-Statement.
6. **Subcontractor-of-record Notice of Subcontract tracking** with owner-service confirmation.

### 8.4 Prompt-pay tracking ($3M+ private projects)

1. **15-day owner-approval alarm** from pay-app submission.
2. **45-day owner-payment-due alarm**.
3. **7-day GC-pay-down-to-sub alarm** from owner payment receipt.
4. **1%/month interest auto-accrual** on overdue amounts.
5. **5% retainage cap enforcement** at contract setup; flag retainage above 5%.
6. **14-day punch-list-issuance alarm** at substantial completion; **60-day retainage-release alarm**.

### 8.5 Worker classification

1. **No 1099 within core trade scope** — hard block on 1099 designation for workers performing the contractor's core trade (e.g., a roofing contractor cannot 1099 roofers). Prong B of M.G.L. c. 149 § 148B fails as a matter of law.
2. **Subcontractor WC verification** at every subcontract — required by M.G.L. c. 152 § 18 statutory-employer doctrine. Lapsed WC at subcontractor surfaces immediate alarm.
3. **Certified payroll generation** for any prevailing-wage project under M.G.L. c. 149 §§ 26-27H.

### 8.6 Tax handling

1. **No sales tax on customer invoices** for Massachusetts residential and commercial construction (contractor-as-consumer model).
2. **ST-5C generation** for exempt-entity projects with linked ST-5 from the customer.
3. **Use-tax accrual** for any New Hampshire or other no-tax-jurisdiction material purchases brought into Massachusetts.
4. **Withholding-tax calculation** at flat 5% + 4% surtax for K-1 income above $1M (owner-level concern, but flow-through visibility helpful for owner planning).

### 8.7 Chapter 93A handling

1. **Demand-letter intake** — when a 30-day 93A demand letter arrives, flag immediately for legal review with a 30-day countdown to settlement-offer deadline.
2. **Reasonable settlement offer worksheet** — record offer made and rejection/acceptance for damages-cap-on-rejected-offer purposes under § 9(3).
3. **HIC violation surveillance** — track each potential per se 93A trigger (deposit > 1/3, contract missing required elements, advertising without HIC number, etc.) and alarm before exposure crystallizes.

Massachusetts is the **most regulatorily intensive** ContractorPro market alongside New Jersey and California: dual-license residential, strict ABC misclassification test, robust prompt-pay/retainage, complex mechanics' liens, and aggressive consumer-protection enforcement. The implementation surface is roughly comparable in volume to New Jersey but with several uniquely Massachusetts mechanics (Notice of Contract, CSL/HIC dual-license, Guaranty Fund, sales-tax contractor-as-consumer with no local rate) that require state-specific code paths.
