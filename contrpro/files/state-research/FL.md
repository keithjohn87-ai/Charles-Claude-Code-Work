## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 Two boards, one department — Florida's split licensing structure

Florida regulates construction contractors through **two separate licensing boards housed within a single state agency**, the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR), 2601 Blair Stone Road, Tallahassee, FL 32399-0791 [1][2]:

- The **Construction Industry Licensing Board (CILB)**, established under **Fla. Stat. § 489.107**, licenses general, building, residential, and most specialty trades (HVAC, plumbing, mechanical, roofing, structural steel as specialty) under **Florida Statutes Chapter 489, Part I** (§§ 489.101–489.147) [1][3].
- The **Electrical Contractors' Licensing Board (ECLB)**, established under **Fla. Stat. § 489.507**, licenses electrical and alarm-system contractors under **Florida Statutes Chapter 489, Part II** (§§ 489.501–489.538) [4][5].

The two boards share staff, fee structures, the same biennial renewal cycle, and the same continuing-education framework, but each board has its own application, examination, financial-responsibility, and disciplinary regime. **A contractor that self-performs electrical work must qualify under Part II in addition to whatever Part I credential supports its general construction operations.** This split distinguishes Florida from California (single CSLB for all trades) and from Tennessee (TBLC for all trades with mechanical subcategories).

### 1.2 Certified vs. Registered — the most-misunderstood Florida distinction

Florida is one of the few states that operates a **two-track contractor licensing system** in which both tracks are state-administered but only one is state-wide. Under **Fla. Stat. § 489.115** and **§ 489.117** [6][7][8]:

- A **Certified contractor** has passed the state-administered competency examination and may contract **anywhere in the state of Florida** without further local examination. Certification is issued at the state level by CILB (Part I) or ECLB (Part II) and carries the prefix "C" on the license number (e.g., CGC1234567 for Certified General Contractor; CAC1234567 for Certified Air Conditioning Contractor Class A; CFC for Certified Plumbing Contractor; CEC for Certified Electrical Contractor).
- A **Registered contractor** has met **local-government competency requirements only** — typically a county-level board examination — and is "register[ed]" with DBPR for purposes of state oversight (insurance verification, disciplinary jurisdiction, and access to the Construction Industry Recovery Fund). Under **Fla. Stat. § 489.117(2)**, "registration allows the registrant to engage in contracting only in the counties, municipalities, or development districts where he or she has complied with all local licensing requirements" [7][8]. Registered contractors carry an "R" prefix (RG, RA, RR, RM, RP, RE, etc.).

**Practical effect for ContrPro:** A Florida-licensed general contractor operating from Jacksonville under a registered license (RG) **cannot legally contract for work in Orlando** without first qualifying under Orlando's local competency board. The same contractor holding a **certified** license (CGC) may bid and contract in Jacksonville, Orlando, Tampa, Miami, or any other Florida jurisdiction without local re-examination, subject only to local permit/registration fees.

Under **§ 489.117(4)**, a registered contractor that performs work outside its registered jurisdiction is subject to a Class A misdemeanor charge for unlicensed practice under **§ 489.127** [7][9]. There is a **24-month emergency-declaration exception** that allows out-of-jurisdiction work during and following a declared state of emergency (a hurricane-driven carve-out used heavily in Florida) [7].

### 1.3 Project value threshold and the unlicensed-contracting hammer (§§ 489.127, 489.128, 489.132)

Florida imposes **no dollar threshold** below which contracting is unregulated. Any person who "engages in the business or acts in the capacity of a contractor" in any category enumerated in Fla. Stat. § 489.105(3) must hold an active license, regardless of contract size [3][10]. Unlike Tennessee (where contractor licensing applies above $25,000 in T.C.A. § 62-6-103) or California (where CSLB jurisdiction triggers at $1,000 in Bus. & Prof. Code § 7048), Florida regulates from the first dollar.

**Penalties for unlicensed practice** under **§ 489.127** and **§ 489.132** [9][10]:

- **First offense:** First-degree misdemeanor under § 489.127(2) — up to 1 year in jail and a $1,000 fine.
- **Second or subsequent offense:** Third-degree felony — up to 5 years in prison and a $5,000 fine.
- **Engaging in unlicensed contracting during a declared state of emergency:** Automatic third-degree felony under § 489.127(2)(c), even on a first offense. This provision is enforced aggressively after hurricanes — see § 5.6 below.
- **Civil penalty:** DBPR may impose an administrative fine up to **$10,000** per violation under § 489.13 [9].
- **Contract unenforceable.** Under **Fla. Stat. § 489.128** [11], a contract entered by an unlicensed contractor is **unenforceable in law or in equity** by that contractor. The unlicensed contractor has no right to compensation, no right to a lien, no quantum-meruit recovery, and no defense based on the customer's bad faith. Florida courts apply § 489.128 mechanically — the homeowner who hires an unlicensed contractor, accepts the work, and refuses to pay is fully protected from suit. This is the single most important consequence of licensure status in Florida.

### 1.4 License classifications relevant to ContrPro's five trades

Under **Fla. Stat. § 489.105(3)** [3][10], Florida divides contractors into **Division I** (general construction) and **Division II** (specialty). The categories relevant to ContrPro's five trades:

**Division I — general construction:**

| Classification | Statutory citation | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **General Contractor (CGC / RG)** | § 489.105(3)(a) | Unlimited scope; may contract any construction work the contractor is qualified to supervise, with limited subcontracting restrictions on electrical, mechanical, plumbing, roofing, and sheet metal (must subcontract to a licensee unless the GC also holds that specialty) |
| **Building Contractor (CBC / RB)** | § 489.105(3)(b) | Commercial buildings and residential structures **not exceeding three stories in height**, and non-structural remodeling of buildings of any size |
| **Residential Contractor (CRC / RR)** | § 489.105(3)(c) | One-, two-, or three-family dwellings **not exceeding two habitable stories above no more than one uninhabitable story** |

The three-tier general-construction structure (general / building / residential) is a Florida-specific scheme; California uses a single B-General Building Contractor classification, and Tennessee uses a graded BC-A / BC-B / BC-C system tied to project type.

**Division II — specialty trades (selected for ContrPro):**

| Classification | Statutory citation | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Air-Conditioning Contractor — Class A (CAC / RA)** | § 489.105(3)(d) | Unlimited capacity HVAC; refrigeration; ventilation; ductwork |
| **Air-Conditioning Contractor — Class B (CAC / RA)** | § 489.105(3)(e) | Capped at **25 tons cooling and 500,000 BTU heating** in any one system |
| **Air-Conditioning Contractor — Class C** | § 489.105(3)(f) | Service-only; **closed classification** — only contractors registered or certified before October 1, 1988 may hold this credential [3] |
| **Mechanical Contractor (CMC / RM)** | § 489.105(3)(g) | Unlimited HVAC, boilers, pressure vessels, process piping (broader than Class A air-conditioning by including non-comfort systems) |
| **Plumbing Contractor (CFC / RF)** | § 489.105(3)(m) | Unlimited plumbing — water supply, drain/waste/vent, medical gas, fire-sprinkler/domestic-water combination, propane/natural gas piping inside building |
| **Specialty Contractor (RX, RZ — registered local only)** | § 489.105(3)(o) and (q); § 489.117 | Includes structural steel erection where local boards register the category. **No state Certified classification for stand-alone structural steel** — see § 1.5 below |

**Electrical Contractor (Part II):**

| Classification | Statutory citation | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Certified Electrical Contractor (CEC)** | § 489.505(12); § 489.511 | Unlimited electrical work statewide |
| **Registered Electrical Contractor (ER)** | § 489.505(20); § 489.513 | Electrical work within the registered local jurisdiction only |
| **Certified Alarm System Contractor I/II (CAS)** | § 489.505(2); § 489.511 | Alarm and low-voltage systems |
| **Specialty Electrical Contractor** | § 489.505(22) | Limited electrical scope (signs, residential utility hookups, etc.) |

### 1.5 Structural steel — no dedicated state classification

Unlike HVAC, electrical, plumbing, mechanical, or roofing, structural steel erection is **not separately enumerated as a CILB classification** in Fla. Stat. § 489.105(3) [3]. Structural steel falls into one of three paths:

1. **As part of a CGC scope.** A Certified General Contractor's "unlimited" scope includes structural steel erection within the GC's overall project responsibility, provided the GC subcontracts welding work to qualified personnel and the project complies with the Florida Building Code's structural-steel provisions.
2. **As a local specialty registration** under § 489.117(2). A county or municipality may register a "Specialty Structural" or "Structural Erection" classification with locally administered competency; the registered contractor's scope is then limited to that jurisdiction.
3. **As an AISC-Certified Fabricator/Erector with no state license** — for steel that is fabricated in another state and erected on a Florida project under a licensed GC's overall scope. AISC Certified Fabricator/Erector certifications and AWS D1.1 welder qualifications are routine contract requirements on commercial Florida projects regardless of state-licensure status.

For ContrPro's purposes: a structural-steel-only operator in Florida should expect to either (a) operate as a subcontractor under a CGC's umbrella with AISC and AWS credentials in lieu of state licensure, or (b) maintain a local specialty registration in each county or municipality where it bids work. There is no Florida statewide structural-steel certification analog to Tennessee's "S — Erection and Fabrication of Structural Steel" classification.

### 1.6 Experience requirements (Fla. Stat. § 489.111)

Under **Fla. Stat. § 489.111** [12][13], an applicant for any Division I or Division II Certified license must:

- Be at least **18 years old** and of "good moral character" [12].
- Demonstrate **4 years of construction experience**, of which **at least 1 year must have been as a foreman** (i.e., supervisory experience) [12][13]. The 4 years must include experience in the trade category for which licensure is sought; structural-only or laborer-only time does not qualify for a GC application.
- Alternative routes: A **baccalaureate degree** in engineering, architecture, or building construction substitutes for **3 years** of the 4-year experience requirement (i.e., a degree plus 1 year of practical experience qualifies). Specific trade college coursework counts on a credit-hour-for-experience basis under DBPR rule [12].
- For specialty-to-general-contractor transitions, applicants typically need additional experience in the higher classification.

ECLB experience requirement for **Certified Electrical Contractor** (Part II, § 489.511): **3 years of management experience** within the 6 years immediately preceding the application, in addition to a minimum of 6 years total experience in the trade. **At least 40% of the work experience must involve three-phase service installation** [4][14]. This is materially stricter than the Part I CILB four-year requirement.

### 1.7 Examination (Fla. Stat. § 489.111(2); Fla. Admin. Code Ch. 61G4)

Florida administers contractor competency examinations through **Pearson VUE** (formerly through Professional Testing Inc.) [12][14]. The exams cover:

1. **Trade-specific competency exam** — covers the relevant chapter of the Florida Building Code, applicable construction technical reference standards, and trade practice.
2. **Business and Finance exam** — covers Florida construction statutes (Chapter 489, Chapter 713, Chapter 440), construction accounting, project management, contracts, and contract law. Recommended reference text: NASCLA *Contractors' Guide to Business, Law and Project Management* (Florida edition).

**Passing score: 70%** on each section [12][13]. Examinations are administered through the year at Pearson VUE testing centers; reciprocity is recognized with several states for the business-and-finance portion only (not the trade portion).

**Fees** (as of 2026 per DBPR fee schedule under Fla. Admin. Code Ch. 61G4-12) [13][14]:

- Application/exam fee for Certified Electrical Contractor: **$316.25** to DBPR plus **$78.75** to Pearson VUE on test day [14].
- CILB Division I/II examination fees vary by classification (approximately $135 application + $80 per test section).
- Initial license fee (after passing): approximately **$249** for Certified, **$249** for Registered.
- Biennial renewal fee: approximately **$209** (CILB) or **$249** (ECLB) plus continuing-education compliance.

### 1.8 Financial responsibility and credit (Fla. Admin. Code Ch. 61G4-15.005; § 489.115)

Florida requires every contractor applicant to demonstrate **financial responsibility** [6][12][15]:

- A **credit report** from a national credit-reporting agency must accompany the application.
- Under **Fla. Admin. Code Rule 61G4-15.006**, the applicant or qualifying agent must have a **minimum FICO score of 660** [14][15]. An applicant with a FICO score below 660 must complete a **14-hour DBPR-approved financial-responsibility course** *and* may be required to post a surety bond.
- For ECLB Certified Electrical Contractor applicants with FICO below 660: a **$20,000 surety bond** is required in lieu of demonstrated financial responsibility [14].
- For CILB applicants with FICO below 660: a surety bond of **$20,000 (Division I)** or **$10,000 (Division II)** is typically required, pending the Board's discretionary review [12][15].

This Florida-specific FICO-and-bond test is **the most prescriptive financial-responsibility regime among large states**. Texas does not impose a state-level financial-responsibility test (only municipal in some cities); California's CSLB imposes a $25,000 contractor bond ([Bus. & Prof. Code § 7071.6) but no FICO test; Tennessee uses CPA-reviewed or audited financial statements scaled to monetary limit (T.C.A. § 62-6-108).

### 1.9 Insurance — public liability, property damage, workers' compensation (§ 489.115; Fla. Admin. Code Ch. 61G4-15.003)

Under **§ 489.115(5)** and Fla. Admin. Code Rule 61G4-15.003 [6][12][15], every certified or registered Division I and Division II contractor must maintain:

- **Public liability insurance:** minimum **$300,000** per occurrence for Division I (General, Building, Residential) and **$100,000** per occurrence for Division II (HVAC, plumbing, mechanical, etc.).
- **Property damage insurance:** minimum **$50,000** per occurrence for Division I and **$25,000** per occurrence for Division II.
- **Combined single-limit policies** that cover both public liability and property damage at or above the aggregate of the two minimums are accepted.
- **Workers' compensation insurance** under Chapter 440 — see § 1.10 below. Construction-industry employers must carry WC for every employee, including the first employee. Sole proprietors and qualifying corporate officers may elect exemption.

Certificate-of-insurance evidence must be filed with DBPR before the license is issued and at each renewal. DBPR maintains certificate-of-insurance verification through its electronic licensee portal.

For ECLB Certified Electrical Contractors under § 489.515, the public-liability minimum is also **$300,000** per occurrence with **$50,000** property damage [4][14].

**No statewide surety-bond requirement** for the license itself (compare California's $25,000 bond and Tennessee's $500,000/$1,000,000 bond). Surety bonds in Florida are project-specific (payment and performance bonds on public works under Fla. Stat. § 255.05) or financial-responsibility substitutes for FICO-below-660 applicants (above).

### 1.10 Workers' compensation — Florida's 1-employee construction threshold (Fla. Stat. Ch. 440)

Florida's construction-industry workers' compensation rule is among the strictest in the nation. Under **Fla. Stat. § 440.02(17)(b)2** [16][17]:

- **Construction industry: 1 or more employees.** Every employer engaged in the construction industry with **one or more employees** must secure workers' compensation insurance coverage. This includes part-time and casual labor.
- **Non-construction industry: 4 or more employees.** Non-construction employers are required to carry WC only when employing four or more.

**Officer/owner exemption (§ 440.05).** Corporate officers in the construction industry may elect an exemption from WC by filing with the Florida Department of Financial Services' Division of Workers' Compensation. **Construction-industry exemption is capped at three (3) officers per corporation or affiliated group** under § 440.02(15)(b) [16][17]. A sole proprietor or partner in a construction-industry business may similarly elect exemption.

**Statutory employer doctrine (§ 440.10).** Under § 440.10(1)(b), a contractor "shall be liable for, and shall secure, the payment of compensation to all such employees" of its subcontractors who have **not** secured their own WC coverage [16]. The general contractor effectively guarantees that every tier below it carries WC; verifying every sub's certificate of insurance — and updating verification at every policy expiration — is a non-negotiable Florida compliance task.

**Penalties for failure to secure WC (§ 440.107).** The Florida Department of Financial Services may issue a **Stop-Work Order** that halts all business operations until compliance is restored. The administrative penalty is **2 times the amount the employer would have paid in premium during the periods of non-compliance**, with a minimum penalty of $1,000 [17]. Failure to secure WC is a separate **second-degree felony** under § 440.10(1)(g) — up to 15 years in prison [16][17].

### 1.11 Continuing education (§ 489.115(4); Fla. Admin. Code Ch. 61G4-18)

Under **§ 489.115(4)** [6][12], every certified and registered Division I or Division II contractor must complete **14 hours of continuing education per biennium** (i.e., per 2-year renewal cycle):

- **1 hour** in workplace safety
- **1 hour** in workers' compensation
- **1 hour** in business practices
- **1 hour** in laws and rules (Chapter 489 and CILB rules)
- **1 hour** in the Florida Building Code (Florida-specific; not a generic IBC hour)
- **1 hour** in advanced Florida Building Code (added by Fla. Admin. Code Rule 61G4-18.001 revisions)
- **8 elective hours** in trade-specific or related construction topics

The same 14-hour biennial structure applies to ECLB licensees under § 489.517, with topic-area substitutions appropriate to the electrical trade (NEC instead of FBC structural, plus 1 hour electrical-specific safety).

### 1.12 Renewal cycle (§ 489.115(3))

All Certified and Registered Division I/II licenses under CILB renew on a **2-year cycle** [6][12]:

- **Even-numbered license numbers** renew on **August 31 of even-numbered years** (2026, 2028).
- **Odd-numbered license numbers** renew on **August 31 of odd-numbered years** (2025, 2027).

ECLB licenses follow the same biennial schedule, also on August 31. Continuing-education evidence must be entered into DBPR's online portal before renewal; an expired license can be reinstated within 12 months on payment of a late fee, but lapses beyond 12 months require re-application and (typically) re-examination.

### 1.13 Construction Industry Recovery Fund (§§ 489.140–489.145)

Florida operates a **Construction Industry Recovery Fund** under §§ 489.140–489.145 [18]. Funded by a per-license assessment on Division I contractors, the Fund pays consumer claims up to **$50,000 per contract** and **$500,000 per licensee** for documented losses caused by contractor misconduct (financial mismanagement, abandonment of project, false statements, violation of § 489.129 disciplinary grounds). For ContrPro contractors, the Recovery Fund is primarily a marketing and consumer-confidence backstop, but the assessment is a renewal cost line-item.

The **Construction Industries Recovery Fund** assessment for ECLB-licensed electrical contractors is administered separately under § 489.143 [4][18].

## 2. Construction lien law — Florida Statutes Chapter 713, Part I

Florida's construction lien law is at **Fla. Stat. Chapter 713, Part I (§§ 713.001–713.37)** [19][20]. It is among the most complex and pro-claimant lien regimes in the United States, with a unique architecture of pre-construction recordation by the owner (Notice of Commencement) and pre-lien notice by the subcontractor or supplier (Notice to Owner), backed by criminal sanctions for misapplication of construction funds (§ 713.345) and a final-payment affidavit gating contractor lien rights (§ 713.06(3)(d)).

### 2.1 The structural architecture: Notice of Commencement → Notice to Owner → Claim of Lien

Unlike Texas (which relies on monthly pre-lien notices) or California (which relies on a single 20-day Preliminary Notice), Florida sequences three separate notice events:

1. **Notice of Commencement (§ 713.13)** — recorded by the **owner** before work begins. Identifies the project, the owner, the contractor, the surety, the lender, and the designated agent for service of process. This document is the recorded anchor against which lien priority and Notice to Owner deadlines are measured.
2. **Notice to Owner (§ 713.06(2))** — served by **each subcontractor, sub-subcontractor, or materialman not in privity with the owner**, within **45 days** of first furnishing labor or materials. Failure to serve a timely Notice to Owner is a **complete defense to any lien** by the non-serving party. This is Florida's most-litigated trap.
3. **Claim of Lien (§ 713.08)** — recorded by the lienor within **90 days** of last furnishing labor or materials, and served on the owner.

If all three notices are properly executed, the lien attaches and remains enforceable for **1 year** under § 713.22 unless the owner shortens the period via a Notice of Contest of Lien (to 60 days) or the lien is transferred to a bond under § 713.24.

### 2.2 Notice of Commencement — § 713.13

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 713.13 [21][22].

**Who records:** The property owner (or owner's authorized agent) records the Notice of Commencement in the official records of the county where the property is located **before commencing improvement of real property**. Recording is mandatory for any direct contract greater than **$2,500** under § 713.135 (with a separate threshold for residential roofing repairs of $7,500). A copy of the recorded Notice must also be **posted at the jobsite** before commencement of work [21][22].

**Required content:**

- Legal description, street address, and tax folio number of the property
- General description of the improvement
- Owner's name and address; the nature of owner's interest (fee simple, lessee, etc.); and fee-simple titleholder identity if owner is not the fee-simple owner
- Contractor's name and address
- Surety information for any payment bond (name, address, bond amount)
- Lender's name and address (if applicable)
- **Designated agent for service of notices** (must be a Florida resident or a Florida-registered agent)
- Statutory **expiration date** (otherwise effective for 1 year)

**Effective period and posting trap:** The Notice of Commencement is effective for **1 year** from the date of recording unless a different expiration is specified (which may not exceed the actual completion of the project under § 713.13(1)(d)) [22]. If construction has not commenced within **90 days** of recording the Notice of Commencement, the Notice is **void** [21].

**Consequence of failure to record (§ 713.13(7)):** The recorded Notice gates the owner's protection. Florida law requires the owner to demand, and the contractor to deliver, a **Contractor's Final Payment Affidavit (§ 713.06(3)(d))** before final payment. The Notice of Commencement is the recorded baseline against which the Final Affidavit's enumeration of lienors who served Notice to Owner is verified. The statutory warning printed on the form reads in part: **"ANY PAYMENTS MADE BY THE OWNER AFTER THE EXPIRATION OF THE NOTICE OF COMMENCEMENT ARE CONSIDERED IMPROPER PAYMENTS... AND CAN RESULT IN YOUR PAYING TWICE FOR IMPROVEMENTS"** [21][22]. An owner that closes a construction loan, makes the final draw, and pays the prime *without* a valid (unexpired) Notice of Commencement is exposed to **double payment** to subs and suppliers who properly served Notice to Owner and recorded a Claim of Lien — even if the owner already paid the prime in full.

### 2.3 Notice to Owner — § 713.06(2)(a) — the 45-day trap

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 713.06(2)(a) [23][24].

**Who must serve:** Every lienor not in privity with the owner — i.e., every subcontractor, sub-subcontractor, materialman, equipment lessor, and design-professional sub. **Direct contractors in privity with the owner are exempt** (they perfect their lien rights through the contract itself and the Final Affidavit; see § 2.6 below). **Laborers** are also exempt from the Notice-to-Owner requirement (they perfect lien rights through the Claim of Lien alone) [23][24].

**Deadline:** The Notice to Owner must be served "**before commencing, or not later than 45 days after commencing, to furnish his or her labor, services, or materials**" [23][24]. The 45 days runs from the date of **first furnishing on the project**, not from the date of contract signing.

**Consequence of failure:** Under § 713.06(2)(a), "**the failure to serve the notice, or to timely serve it, is a complete defense to enforcement of a lien by any person**" [23][24]. This is the single most-litigated clause in Florida construction collections. There is **no grace period, no doctrine of substantial compliance**, no "no harm, no foul" exception. A subcontractor that begins furnishing materials on Day 1 and serves Notice to Owner on Day 46 has forfeited all lien rights on the project, even if the owner has actual notice of the subcontractor's presence and even if the owner has not yet paid the prime contractor a dime.

**Service methods (§ 713.18):** The Notice may be served by (a) personal delivery; (b) U.S. Postal Service certified or registered mail with return receipt; (c) actual delivery to and signed receipt by the addressee. Recent practice has accepted **traceable private courier delivery** (FedEx, UPS) provided proof of delivery is preserved [22][23]. Service must be on **both the owner and the designated agent** identified in the Notice of Commencement, and copies must also be served on the contractor and the contractor's surety if a payment bond exists.

**Service to wrong address.** Service to the address listed in the recorded Notice of Commencement is **statutorily sufficient**, even if that address is wrong or outdated. The owner bears the risk of an outdated Notice of Commencement; the lienor that serves the address on the recorded Notice is protected. This shifts the address-accuracy burden from the subcontractor (who likely never sees the owner's address until after the work starts) to the owner (who controls the recorded Notice).

**Required content:** Under § 713.06(2)(c) [23], the Notice to Owner must contain:

- Lienor's name and address
- Property description sufficient for identification
- Name of the person with whom the lienor is contracting (i.e., the prime contractor or upper-tier sub)
- Nature of services or materials being furnished
- **Mandatory boldface warning language** beginning with "**WARNING! FLORIDA'S CONSTRUCTION LIEN LAW ALLOWS SOME UNPAID CONTRACTORS, SUBCONTRACTORS, AND MATERIAL SUPPLIERS TO FILE LIENS AGAINST YOUR PROPERTY EVEN IF YOU HAVE MADE PAYMENT IN FULL**" [22][23] — the statute prescribes the exact language and font requirement.

### 2.4 Claim of Lien — § 713.08 — the 90-day filing deadline

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 713.08 [25][26].

**Deadline:** The Claim of Lien must be recorded with the clerk of the circuit court in the county where the property is located **not later than 90 days after the final furnishing of the labor or services or materials by the lienor**, OR within 90 days of termination of the original contract, whichever occurs first [25][26]. The 90 days runs from the date of *actual last furnishing* — punch-list items, warranty work, and de minimis return trips after substantial completion do **not** extend the 90 days under settled Florida case law (a rule analogous to other states' anti-tacking doctrines, intended to prevent lienors from sandbagging the deadline by performing trivial later work).

**Required form (§ 713.08(3))** [25]:

- Lienor's name and service address
- Name of person with whom lienor contracted
- Description of labor, services, or materials furnished, and contract price
- Real property description sufficient for identification
- Owner's name
- Dates of first and last furnishing
- Unpaid balance and finance charges
- For lienors not in privity, dates and method of Notice to Owner service

**Service after recording.** Under § 713.08(4)(c), the lienor must serve a copy of the recorded Claim of Lien on the owner **before recording or within 15 days after recording**. Failure of this service is not automatically fatal — the lien becomes voidable only "if such failure has prejudiced any person entitled to rely on service" [25]. This is one of the few areas of Florida lien law that offers a substantial-compliance defense.

**Mandatory warning on the lien.** The recorded Claim of Lien must include a prominent warning that the lien expires automatically one year after recording unless the lienor commences a foreclosure action [25].

### 2.5 Notice of Contest of Lien — § 713.22(2) — the 60-day accelerator

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 713.22 [27][28].

A Florida property owner (or owner's attorney) may file and record a **Notice of Contest of Lien**, which **shortens the lienor's foreclosure deadline from 1 year to 60 days from the date of service of the Notice of Contest** [27][28]. If the lienor does not commence a foreclosure action within the 60-day window, the lien is **extinguished automatically** by operation of law and may be removed from the property records.

The Notice of Contest is the single most powerful defensive tool available to a Florida property owner. An owner with a recorded lien that the owner believes is defective, exaggerated, or extortive can short-circuit the contractor's 1-year planning window and force a litigation decision within 60 days. Sophisticated owners file Notices of Contest within days of receiving a Claim of Lien to compress the dispute timeline.

**Practical implication for the contractor:** A Florida contractor or subcontractor that records a Claim of Lien should be prepared to file the foreclosure action immediately if the owner files a Notice of Contest. Failure to file within 60 days is fatal — the lien is gone, and the underlying breach-of-contract claim (which may or may not survive the limitations period) is the only remaining remedy.

### 2.6 Final Contractor's Affidavit — § 713.06(3)(d) — the prerequisite to suit

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 713.06(3)(d) [23][24].

**Who delivers:** Only the **direct contractor in privity with the owner** (the prime contractor). The Final Contractor's Affidavit does not apply to subcontractors, who perfect lien rights through Notice to Owner.

**Content:** The sworn affidavit must state that all lienors under the prime's direct contract who timely served Notice to Owner on the owner and contractor have been paid in full, **OR** must identify each unpaid lienor and the amount owed [23][24].

**Timing — the 5-day rule:** "**The contractor shall execute the affidavit and deliver it to the owner at least 5 days before instituting an action as a prerequisite to the institution of any action to enforce his or her lien**" [23][24]. A prime contractor that records a Claim of Lien and immediately files a foreclosure suit without first delivering the Final Affidavit at least 5 days in advance is barred from prosecuting the lien.

**Penalty for non-delivery:** "**The contractor shall have no lien or right of action against the owner for labor, services, or materials furnished under the direct contract while in default for not giving the owner the affidavit**" [23][24]. The default cures upon delivery, but the contractor cannot recover under any theory while in default.

**Substantial-compliance shield.** "[T]he negligent inclusion or omission of any information in the affidavit which has not prejudiced the owner does not constitute a default that operates to defeat an otherwise valid lien" [23]. Minor errors in the affidavit (typos, immaterial omissions) do not defeat the lien — only outright non-delivery, fraud, or material omission does.

### 2.7 Transfer of lien to security — § 713.24

A lien may be removed from the property and "transferred" to a cash deposit or surety bond under **Fla. Stat. § 713.24** [29]. The amount required is:

- **The lien amount, PLUS**
- **Interest at the legal rate for 3 years, PLUS**
- **The greater of $5,000 OR 25% of the lien amount** (for the claimant's projected attorney's fees and court costs)

The transfer mechanism is heavily used in Florida by owners with title-closing or refinancing pressure. Once the transfer is recorded, the property is released and the lien attaches only to the deposit or bond. The claimant's foreclosure suit then proceeds against the bond rather than the real property.

**Comparison to other states:** Texas requires a bond of **150% of the lien amount** under Tex. Prop. Code § 53.171. California allows release-bond procedures under Cal. Civ. Code § 8424 at **125% of the claim**. Florida's formula (100% + 3 years' interest + greater of $5,000/25%) is among the most prescriptive and tends to be the most expensive when interest rates are high.

### 2.8 Misapplication of construction funds — § 713.345 — Florida's criminal hammer

Florida imposes **personal criminal liability** on contractors and corporate officers who divert construction funds, under **Fla. Stat. § 713.345** [30][31]. This is Florida's analog to Texas's Construction Trust Fund Act (Tex. Prop. Code Ch. 162) and Tennessee's prompt-pay misapplication provisions.

**Offense:** A person who, having received money for improvement of real property as a fund for the express purpose of paying for labor, services, or materials, and who **knowingly and intentionally fails to apply that money** as required, commits a criminal offense [30][31].

**Personal liability for officers and agents:** Officers, agents, and employees of a corporation or business entity who participate in the misapplication face the **same personal criminal liability** as the entity. The corporate form does not shield individual decision-makers. For licensed contractors under Chapter 489, conviction is also a **separate disciplinary ground** for license revocation [30][31].

**Penalty tiers (§ 713.345(1)(b))** [30]:

| Misapplied amount | Offense grade | Maximum penalty |
|---|---|---|
| **Less than $1,000** | **Third-degree felony** | Up to **5 years** in prison and a $5,000 fine |
| **$1,000 to $99,999** | **Second-degree felony** | Up to **15 years** in prison and a $10,000 fine |
| **$100,000 or more** | **First-degree felony** | Up to **30 years** in prison and a $10,000 fine |

Florida's § 713.345 is **more severe than Texas's Construction Trust Fund Act**, which tops out at a third-degree felony (2–10 years). A Florida contractor who diverts $100,000 of construction payments away from subs and suppliers — for instance, by using a draw to pay personal expenses or another job's overhead — faces a first-degree felony (life-altering exposure equivalent to drug trafficking or armed robbery).

### 2.9 Liens on homestead property and special threshold rules

Florida's lien law applies to homestead property without the special spousal-signature requirements seen in Texas. However, **Fla. Stat. § 713.015** requires every direct construction contract greater than **$2,500** for improvement of an owner-occupied dwelling to include the statutory **Notice of Lien Rights** in **at least 12-point boldface type** at the top of the contract [32]. The notice warns the homeowner of lien exposure for unpaid sub-tier work. Failure to include the notice **does not bar enforcement of the contract**, but it does subject the contractor to a CILB disciplinary action and an administrative fine.

For residential roofing repairs, the threshold is **$7,500** under § 713.135 — a separate rule that allows minor roofing repairs to proceed without recorded Notice of Commencement.

### 2.10 Priority among lienors — § 713.06(4)

When the owner's available funds are insufficient to pay all properly perfected lienors, **§ 713.06(4)(a)** establishes a three-tier priority [23]:

1. **All laborers' liens** (no Notice to Owner required for laborers).
2. **All liens of persons other than the contractor** (subs, materialmen, design professionals, equipment lessors who timely served Notice to Owner).
3. **The contractor's lien.**

Within each tier, if funds are insufficient, claims are paid **pro rata**. The contractor stands last — a structural feature that protects sub-tier claimants at the contractor's expense and that aligns with Florida's policy of protecting the workers and material suppliers most distant from the funding source.

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

Florida's prompt-payment regime sits in three statutes: **Fla. Stat. § 218.735** for local government works (Local Government Prompt Payment Act, Part VII of Chapter 218), **Fla. Stat. §§ 255.073 / 255.077 / 255.078** for state public works, and **Fla. Stat. § 715.12** for private construction projects [33][34][35][36]. **Senate Bill 346 of the 2023 Regular Session (Ch. 2023-134, Laws of Florida)** comprehensively rewrote the public-works portions effective **July 1, 2023** — see § 5.1 below for the legislative history; the substantive content is integrated throughout this section.

### 3.1 Private works — Fla. Stat. § 715.12 (Construction Contract Prompt Payment Law)

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 715.12 [36].

**Owner-to-contractor payment.** Under § 715.12(2), an owner that contracts directly with a contractor for improvement of real property must make payment within **14 days** after (a) the contractor's submission of a written request for payment, (b) the contractor's furnishing of the affidavits or waivers required by § 713.06, and (c) any contractor-cured deficiencies in the payment request [36]. A request that is materially incomplete restarts the 14-day clock once corrected.

**Contractor-to-subcontractor cascade.** Under § 715.12(3), the contractor must pay the subcontractor's portion **upon (a) the contractor's receipt of payment from the obligor (owner), and (b) the subcontractor's submission of a written request and required releases**. The statute does not impose a fixed number of days after receipt — payment must occur as soon as the receipt-and-release conditions are met. In practice, sophisticated owner/lender requirements (and § 218.735(6)–(7) by analogy on public works) drive a de facto 7-to-10-day cascade.

**Interest on overdue.** Under § 715.12(7), overdue amounts bear interest at **the statutory legal rate in § 55.03 plus an additional 12% per annum**. The Florida statutory legal rate is published quarterly by the Department of Financial Services; for 2026 the rate is approximately 7%, yielding a total of approximately **19% per annum** on overdue private-construction payments [36][37]. Florida's private-works interest rate is therefore comparable to Texas's 18% per annum (1.5%/month under Tex. Prop. Code § 28.004).

**Dispute mechanics (§ 715.12(8)).** A bona-fide dispute permits withholding **only of the disputed portion**. Undisputed amounts must be paid on the statutory schedule. The obligor may not withhold undisputed payments on Project A to leverage a dispute on Project B — disputes are project-specific.

**No private-works retainage cap.** Florida law does **not** cap retainage on private construction contracts. Retainage on private projects is purely contract-driven; 10% is common in residential and small commercial, 5% on larger commercial, with progressive release at substantial completion the prevailing practice. The contractor's only protection against excessive private retainage is contract negotiation and lien rights under Chapter 713 (a retainage Claim of Lien is recordable under the 90-day rule of § 713.08 once final payment is due, including retainage).

### 3.2 Local-government works — Fla. Stat. § 218.735 (Local Government Prompt Payment Act)

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 218.735 (Part VII, Local Government Prompt Payment Act), within Chapter 218 [33].

**Local-government-to-contractor payment.** Under § 218.735(1), payment to the prime contractor is due within **25 business days** after receipt of the payment request, when the project requires approval by an agent (architect or engineer); the deadline is **20 business days** when no agent approval is required [33]. The 25-business-day requirement is roughly equivalent to **35 calendar days**, comparable to Texas's 35-day private-works rule.

**Contractor-to-subcontractor payment.** Under § 218.735(6), the contractor must pay the subcontractor **within 10 days** after receipt of payment from the local government. The 10-day window cascades down the chain — sub-subs must be paid within 7 days under § 218.735(7) [33]. Failure to cascade is itself a separate prompt-pay violation and exposes the upper-tier contractor to the same interest-and-attorney's-fees structure.

**Interest on overdue.** Under § 218.735(8), overdue amounts bear interest at **the rate of 2% per month** OR the contract rate, whichever is greater [33]. The 2% per month rate (24% APR) is among the highest in the United States and is a strong contractor incentive to enforce prompt-pay claims aggressively against slow-paying local governments.

**Punch list and retainage (§ 218.735(7)–(8)).** Within **30 days of substantial completion** for projects ≤ $10 million, or **45 days** for projects > $10 million, the local government and contractor must develop a written punch list of items to be completed before final payment. Within **20 business days** after the punch list is finalized and the contractor submits a payment request, the local government must pay the contractor the remaining contract balance — **including all retainage previously withheld** — minus **150% of the estimated cost to complete** the punch-list items. The 150% retention is the only legitimate post-substantial-completion withholding [33][38][39].

**Retainage cap — local-government works.** Under § 218.735(8)(b), retainage may not exceed **5% of each progress payment** on local-government works [33]. The 5% cap was set by SB 346 (2023, effective July 1, 2023), reducing what had been a project-specific contractual ceiling. The 5% cap does **not** apply to contracts of **$200,000 or less** [33][39].

### 3.3 State public works — Fla. Stat. §§ 255.073, 255.077, 255.078

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 255.073 (timely payment for construction services), § 255.077 (project closeout and retainage), § 255.078 (public construction retainage), all amended by SB 346 (Ch. 2023-134) effective July 1, 2023 [34][35][38][39].

**State-agency-to-contractor payment.** Under § 255.073, state-agency payments to the prime are due on schedules tied to the construction-services-procurement statutes (Fla. Stat. §§ 255.0525, 287.057), with the same general 25-business-day window seen at the local level. Specific deadlines vary by project type (continuing services contract vs. project-by-project contract).

**Retainage cap — state public works.** Under § 255.078, public entities may withhold a maximum of **5% retainage** of each progress payment [35][38][39]. The 5% applies throughout the project — Florida abolished the historic "10% to 50% complete, then 5%" graduated structure in **SB 346 (2023)**, replacing it with a flat 5% from the first progress payment forward [35][39]. The 5% cap does **not** apply to projects valued at **$200,000 or less** under § 255.078(1) [35].

**Retainage release — Florida-specific punch-list interaction.** Under § 255.077, the state public-entity must pay retainage in proportion to work completed and may not retain on any element of work after that element is complete. After substantial completion, retainage release follows the punch-list mechanic described in § 3.2 above (150% of the estimated cost to complete punch-list items, with the remainder released within 20 business days of punch-list finalization).

**Federal-funds exception.** Under § 255.078(2)(b), the 5% cap does **not** apply to federally funded portions of a project where federal procurement requirements impose a different retainage rule [35]. Federally funded transportation, housing, and infrastructure projects in Florida often carry FHWA, HUD, or other federal retainage rules that displace § 255.078.

### 3.4 Dispute resolution under SB 346 (2023)

SB 346 also tightened the **dispute-resolution timeline** on local-government contracts. Under § 218.76 as amended, a local government must commence resolution of a vendor dispute within **30 days** (reduced from 45 days) and must issue a final decision within **45 days** (reduced from 60 days) [33][38][39]. Failure to meet these deadlines exposes the local government to per-day penalties under § 218.74.

### 3.5 Payment bonds on public works — § 255.05

For state and local public works in excess of **$100,000**, the prime contractor must furnish a **payment and performance bond** under **Fla. Stat. § 255.05** [40]. The bond runs to the public entity for the benefit of subcontractors and suppliers, who may sue on the bond if unpaid. Statutorily, sub-tier claims on a § 255.05 bond require **a 45-day Notice to Contractor before suit** (the public-works analog to the Notice to Owner in private-works lien practice). The notice requirements on payment bonds in Florida are strict and analogous to the Miller Act on federal works.

### 3.6 Pay-if-paid and pay-when-paid clauses

Florida courts **enforce pay-if-paid clauses** as risk-shifting provisions when the contract clearly so provides. The leading authority is the Florida Supreme Court decision in *Peacock Construction Co. v. Modern Air Conditioning, Inc.*, 353 So. 2d 840 (Fla. 1977), which held that ambiguous "pay-when-paid" language is enforced as a reasonable-time obligation but does not transfer the risk of owner non-payment onto the subcontractor; an *unambiguous* pay-if-paid clause is enforceable [41]. Florida has **not** statutorily limited pay-if-paid clauses in the way Texas has under Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code Ch. 56 — the matter remains common-law and clause-drafting-dependent. ContrPro subcontractors should expect pay-if-paid clauses in Florida prime-sub agreements and should negotiate explicit ceiling or waiver provisions where possible.

## 4. Tax structure for contractors

### 4.1 No state personal income tax

Florida imposes **no state personal income tax**. Sole proprietors, partners in pass-through partnerships, members of pass-through LLCs, and S-corporation shareholders pay no Florida income tax on construction-business earnings. Compare California's 13.3% top marginal personal income-tax rate or Tennessee's (now repealed) Hall tax on investment income. This single feature is a structural cost advantage for Florida-domiciled contractors over almost every other major state except Texas and Tennessee.

### 4.2 Corporate income tax — 5.5%

Florida does impose a **5.5% corporate income tax** under **Fla. Stat. Ch. 220** on entities taxed as C corporations under federal law. The first **$50,000** of net income is exempt. Pass-through entities (S corporations, partnerships, LLCs taxed as partnerships) are not subject to corporate income tax — their income flows to owners' federal returns and pays no Florida tax there either. Most Florida contractors structure as LLCs or S corporations to avoid the 5.5% rate.

### 4.3 Sales tax — 6% state, up to 1.5% local surtax, 7.5% combined cap

**Statutory authority:** Fla. Stat. § 212.05 (state rate); Fla. Stat. Ch. 212 (Limited Sales, Excise, and Use Tax); Fla. Stat. § 212.054 (local discretionary sales surtax) [42][43][44].

- **State sales tax rate: 6%** under § 212.05(1)(a)1.a [42][43].
- **Local discretionary sales surtax:** counties may impose up to **1.5%** under § 212.055 (per current Florida Department of Revenue Form DR-15DSS) [44]. A handful of counties impose **2%** surtaxes under specific statutory authority (school capital outlay, charter county transit, indigent care), with most surtaxes capped at 1.5% in aggregate. The Florida Department of Revenue publishes the current per-county surtax rate annually in Form DR-15DSS [44].
- **Combined maximum (typical metropolitan): 7.5%** in counties such as Miami-Dade (1% transit surtax + 6% state, but combined caps and surtax-eligibility rules vary).
- **$5,000 surtax cap on single-item sales.** Under § 212.054(2)(b), the local discretionary surtax applies only to the first **$5,000 of any single sale of tangible personal property** — a structural break for large equipment, vehicle, and machinery purchases. The 6% state rate applies to the full sales price.

**Administrator:** Florida Department of Revenue (FDOR), Tax Information Services, 5050 W. Tennessee Street, Tallahassee, FL 32399 [43][44].

### 4.4 Contractor sales-tax treatment — Florida Administrative Code Rule 12A-1.051

Florida's sales-tax treatment of construction contractors is governed by **Fla. Admin. Code Rule 12A-1.051** ("Sales to or by Contractors Who Repair, Alter, Improve and Construct Real Property") [45][46][47]. The rule is among the most detailed in the country and turns on the type of contract.

**The default rule — contractor is the ultimate consumer of materials.** Under Rule 12A-1.051(3), a contractor performing a **lump-sum, cost-plus, fixed-fee, guaranteed-price, or time-and-materials contract** to improve real property is the **ultimate consumer of the materials** incorporated into the improvement [45][46][47]. The contractor pays sales tax on the materials at the time of purchase from the supplier (or remits use tax on out-of-state purchases), and **charges no sales tax to the customer** on the finished improvement.

This is the most common Florida construction-tax structure. A residential remodeler buying lumber, drywall, paint, and fixtures pays 6% (plus local surtax up to $5,000 per item) at Home Depot or the local distributor and invoices the homeowner for a lump-sum amount with **no sales tax line**.

**The exception — retail sale plus installation.** Under Rule 12A-1.051(3)(b), a contractor whose contract is structured as a **"retail sale plus installation"** — meaning all materials to be incorporated are **itemized and priced in the contract before work begins**, with installation labor priced separately — is **not the consumer**. Instead, the contractor is a **dealer** that buys materials tax-free under a resale certificate and **collects sales tax from the customer on the materials portion** (installation labor is not taxable) [45][46][47].

The retail-sale-plus-installation election is **opt-in** and requires upfront contract structuring. A contractor that elects this treatment must register with FDOR as a dealer, collect tax on the materials portion of each invoice, and remit. The retail-sale-plus-installation route is rarely the default in Florida residential and small-commercial work — it generates additional paperwork without materially changing the customer's after-tax cost (because the customer pays the same 6% one way or the other) — but is occasionally used by large commercial flooring, cabinetry, and equipment installers.

**Fixtures vs. tangible personal property.** Rule 12A-1.051 also distinguishes between **fixtures** (items that become a permanent part of the realty — windows, doors, built-in cabinetry, integrated HVAC equipment) and **tangible personal property** (items that retain their separate identity — standalone appliances, free-standing equipment). Fixtures are taxed under the real-property-improvement rule above (contractor as consumer); free-standing tangible personal property installations are taxed under **Rule 12A-1.006** (the contractor as dealer charging tax on the property; labor taxability depends on whether it constitutes installation or remodeling) [48].

**Mixed contracts and Technical Assistance Advisements.** Florida contracts that mix real-property improvement with tangible-personal-property installation are evaluated under the **predominant-purpose** test [45][47]. FDOR routinely issues Technical Assistance Advisements (TAAs) — public guidance on specific contractor scenarios. TAA 15A-019, for example, addressed the lump-sum-vs.-itemized question for a plumbing-system installer [46]. Contractors structuring large or unusual jobs should consult FDOR or counsel on the predominant-purpose analysis before contract signing; once a contract is signed, the sales-tax treatment is generally fixed and not retroactively reformable.

### 4.5 Use tax on out-of-state materials

Under **Fla. Stat. § 212.06**, materials purchased out of state and brought into Florida for use in a Florida real-property improvement are subject to **Florida use tax** at the combined state-plus-local rate [42][43]. The contractor must file a Florida Sales and Use Tax Return (Form DR-15). Multi-state contractors importing steel, prefabricated assemblies, equipment, or specialty fixtures into Florida should expect FDOR audit attention — Florida use-tax compliance is a routine audit target for HVAC, mechanical, and steel contractors.

### 4.6 Sales and use tax permit

Any Florida contractor making sales of tangible personal property — including retail-sale-plus-installation contractors and free-standing equipment installers — must register with FDOR and obtain a **Sales and Use Tax Certificate of Registration** under § 212.18. The certificate is free; failure to register before making taxable sales is a misdemeanor under § 212.14 and subjects the contractor to back-tax assessments plus a **25% penalty** and interest.

### 4.7 Documentary stamp tax

Florida imposes a **documentary stamp tax** under **Fla. Stat. Ch. 201** on (a) deeds and other instruments transferring real-property interests (typically **$0.70 per $100** of consideration outside Miami-Dade; $0.60 plus local surtax in Miami-Dade), and (b) promissory notes and written obligations to pay money (**$0.35 per $100**). For construction contractors, the documentary stamp tax most commonly applies to **construction loans and mortgages** (the borrower pays $0.35 per $100 of mortgage proceeds) rather than to the construction work itself. ContrPro contractors involved in development financing should budget for documentary-stamp-tax exposure on the financing tranche, but no documentary stamp applies to the construction-services invoice itself.

## 5. Hurricane and storm-related provisions

Florida's geography makes hurricane-related construction regulation unavoidable. The Florida Building Code (FBC), the licensing rules, and the lien-law administrative regime all carry hurricane-specific overlays.

### 5.1 The Florida Building Code — Eighth Edition, 2023

The current Florida Building Code is the **Eighth Edition (2023)**, in effect since **December 31, 2023**, with code updates approved by the Florida Building Commission and codified in Fla. Admin. Code Ch. 61G20 [49][50]. The FBC is adopted by **Fla. Stat. § 553.73** and is enforceable as the minimum standard statewide; counties and municipalities may not adopt less restrictive codes but may adopt local technical amendments more restrictive than the statewide standard.

The 2023 FBC integrates the IBC 2021 and ASCE 7-22 wind-load standard, with **Florida-specific HVHZ (High-Velocity Hurricane Zone) overlay** for Miami-Dade and Broward Counties [49][50][51].

### 5.2 High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) — Miami-Dade and Broward

Under **Fla. Stat. § 553.842** and the FBC's Chapter 16 wind-load provisions, **Miami-Dade County and Broward County** are designated the High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) [49][50][51]. HVHZ construction requirements are the strictest in the United States and include:

- **Design wind speed:** Miami-Dade: **175 mph** (3-second gust) for Risk Category II structures; Broward: **170 mph** [50][51].
- **Mandatory Exposure Category C** for all structures (no Exposure B reduction for sheltered urban sites).
- **Miami-Dade Notice of Acceptance (NOA)** required for every component of the exterior building envelope — every window, door, garage door, shingle, roof underlayment, exterior wall system, soffit, and fascia must hold an active NOA from the Miami-Dade County Product Control Division [50][51]. Broward operates an analogous Broward County Product Control approval system.
- **TAS 201/202/203 large-missile and cyclic-pressure impact testing** required on the entire building envelope (not just glazed openings) [51]. Material substitutions during construction require NOA-equivalent product approvals.

For ContrPro contractors operating in Miami-Dade or Broward, **HVHZ compliance is an embedded scope of work in every exterior-affecting project**, including HVHZ-specific permit submittals, NOA documentation, and licensed-inspector field verification.

### 5.3 Wind-borne-debris regions (statewide outside HVHZ)

Under **FBC § 1609.2** and **§ 1626** (HVHZ), wind-borne debris regions extend along essentially the entire Florida coast outside HVHZ. Within these regions, **glazed openings must be impact-resistant or protected by impact-resistant shutters** rated for at least the design wind speed. Wind speeds along the Florida Atlantic and Gulf coasts range from approximately **130 mph to 170 mph** (3-second gust) under ASCE 7-22 [49][50]. The interior of the Florida peninsula (parts of central Florida away from both coasts) drops to wind-speed minima of 120–130 mph.

### 5.4 Post-disaster permit and inspection regime

Following any **declared state of emergency** under Fla. Stat. Ch. 252 (Emergency Management), several construction-regulatory overlays activate:

- **Registered contractor jurisdiction expansion (§ 489.117(4)).** A registered contractor may work outside its primary jurisdiction for up to **24 months following the emergency declaration** without local re-registration, provided the contractor obtains a temporary permit from the host jurisdiction [7].
- **Unlicensed-contractor felony enhancement (§ 489.127(2)(c)).** Performing contracting work without a license **during a declared state of emergency is a third-degree felony on a first offense**, with no misdemeanor step. Florida's Attorney General and local State Attorney offices prosecute unlicensed storm-chasers aggressively post-hurricane [9].
- **Roofing-specific consumer-protection provisions (§ 489.147).** Following a series of post-storm insurance abuses, Florida tightened roofing contractor obligations regarding solicitation, insurance assignment-of-benefits, and contractual disclosures. These provisions are most relevant to roofing trades, but contractors performing **storm-related repair work that includes roofing scope** must comply with the § 489.147 disclosure and solicitation rules.

### 5.5 Notice of Commencement re-recording after damage

When a covered project is interrupted by storm damage and resumed under the original Notice of Commencement, the Notice may need to be **re-recorded** if the resumption date is more than 90 days after the original commencement (or more than the Notice's stated expiration period). Recording a new Notice of Commencement is the safer course; many Florida title insurers require re-recording before approving a post-storm construction draw.

### 5.6 Practical takeaway

For ContrPro contractors with HVHZ exposure (Miami-Dade, Broward, or coastal Palm Beach), HVHZ compliance is a **scope-of-work and product-procurement item** on every project. For contractors performing post-storm work, the **24-month emergency carve-out** for registered contractors is a meaningful expansion of work geography, but the **felony enhancement** for unlicensed practice during a state of emergency is a hard floor — every employee, every sub, every job-site representative must verify licensure before performing storm work.

## 6. Recent changes (2024–2026)

The Florida Legislature meets in **annual 60-day regular sessions** beginning in January (with a March start in election years). Session bills become effective on dates specified in the legislation, with most construction-relevant bills timed for **July 1** or **October 1** to align with the state's fiscal year and CILB biennial-renewal cycle.

### 6.1 Senate Bill 346 (2023) — public-works prompt-payment and retainage overhaul, effective July 1, 2023

**SB 346 (Ch. 2023-134, Laws of Florida)**, sponsored by **Sen. Ed Hooper** (substituted CS/CS/SB 346 by Senators DiCeglie and committee), was approved by Governor Ron DeSantis on **May 25, 2023** and took effect **July 1, 2023** [38][39][52]. SB 346 comprehensively rewrote the public-works portions of:

- **§ 218.735** — Local Government Prompt Payment Act
- **§ 218.76** — Improper payment requests and dispute resolution
- **§ 255.073** — Timely payment for construction services (state)
- **§ 255.074** — Payment due-date calculations
- **§ 255.077** — Project closeout and retainage
- **§ 255.078** — Public construction retainage
- **§ 255.0992** — Public works project restrictions

**Headline changes:**

- **Retainage cap reduced to 5% throughout the project** on local-government works and state public works. Prior law had permitted graduated structures with 10% retainage in the first half of construction stepping down to 5% in later phases; SB 346 imposed a flat 5% from the first progress payment forward [33][35][38][39].
- **Punch-list mechanic standardized.** Punch lists must be developed within **30 days of substantial completion** for projects ≤ $10M (45 days for > $10M); retainage release is due within **20 business days** of punch-list finalization, less 150% of the estimated cost to complete punch-list items [33][39].
- **Dispute-resolution timelines compressed.** Local-government disputes: 30 days to commence resolution (down from 45); 45 days to issue a final decision (down from 60) [33][38].
- **Exemption preserved for contracts ≤ $200,000.** The 5% cap does not apply to contracts at or below $200,000 [33][35].

**Practical impact:** SB 346 was the most consequential Florida prompt-pay/retainage reform in over a decade. ContrPro contractors performing local-government or state public works after July 1, 2023, should expect 5% retainage flat, with retainage release tied to the punch-list mechanic rather than to the broader "substantial completion" event.

### 6.2 Senate Bill 360 (2023) — construction defect statute of repose reduced from 10 to 7 years, effective April 13, 2023

**SB 360 (Ch. 2023-22, Laws of Florida)**, signed by Governor DeSantis on **April 13, 2023**, amended **Fla. Stat. § 95.11(3)(c)** to **reduce the statute of repose for construction defect claims from 10 years to 7 years** [53][54][55]. The bill also:

- **Changed the trigger date** for both the statute of limitations and statute of repose. The accrual date is now the **earliest of**: (a) issuance of a temporary certificate of occupancy, (b) issuance of a certificate of occupancy, (c) issuance of a certificate of completion, or (d) date of abandonment if construction is incomplete. The prior triggers — date of actual possession by owner, date of design or construction contract completion or termination — were removed [53][54].
- **One-year grace period.** Claims that would have been timely under the prior 10-year repose but are barred under the new 7-year window had until **July 1, 2024** to be filed. That grace period has now expired; claims accruing after April 13, 2023 are governed by the 7-year repose [54][55].

**Practical impact:** Florida contractors' tail exposure for latent-defect claims has been **shortened by 3 years**. For ContrPro contractors entering project records into accounting and document-retention systems, the document-retention horizon for project files should be at least 7 years from the earliest of the four triggers above, plus the underlying 4-year statute of limitations. Insurance tail coverage (claims-made GL policies) should be reviewed against the new 7-year repose for adequacy.

### 6.3 Senate Bill 658 (2025) — lien waiver and release form standardization, effective July 1, 2025

**SB 658 (Ch. 2025-XX, Laws of Florida)** [56][57][58], signed by Governor DeSantis in 2025 and effective **July 1, 2025**, amended **Fla. Stat. § 713.20** ("Waiver or release of liens") to:

- **Mandate exact form compliance.** Previously, § 713.20 allowed lien waivers and releases "substantially similar" to the statutory form. SB 658 removed the substantial-similarity safe harbor; **the forms must now match the statutory templates exactly** [56][57][58]. A non-conforming waiver is unenforceable as a lien release.
- **Recognize electronic and alternative-payment methods.** SB 658 expanded acceptable payment methods for triggering a conditional-release-on-payment waiver from "check" to electronic funds transfer (EFT), ACH, and other verifiable payment systems. The lienor's right to condition the waiver on actual receipt of funds is preserved [56][57][58].
- **Updated the statutory waiver-form text** for both progress-payment and final-payment partial and unconditional releases.

**Practical impact for ContrPro:** Every contractor and subcontractor operating in Florida after July 1, 2025 must update its lien waiver templates to the new statutory form verbatim. ContrPro contract templates and lien-release packets in use before July 1, 2025 are likely non-conforming and should be refreshed against the post-SB 658 statutory text published on the Florida Senate site [22][56].

### 6.4 Senate Bill 102 (2023) — the "Live Local Act," effective July 1, 2023

**SB 102 (Ch. 2023-17, Laws of Florida)**, the **Live Local Act**, was signed by Governor DeSantis on **March 29, 2023** and took effect (largely) on **July 1, 2023** [59][60]. The Live Local Act primarily addresses **affordable and workforce housing development incentives** through property-tax exemptions, sales-tax refunds, and statewide preemption of local zoning rules for qualifying affordable-housing developments. For ContrPro contractors, the Live Local Act is consequential not as a direct contracting regulation but as a **demand-side driver for affordable multifamily and mixed-use construction** in Florida's urban cores. The Act does **not** modify Chapter 489 licensing, Chapter 713 lien law, or Chapter 218/255 prompt-pay law — those changes came via the separate SB 346 (2023) and other bills.

A common secondary-source error conflates SB 102 (Live Local Act, affordable-housing zoning and tax incentives) with SB 346 (2023, construction prompt-pay/retainage) [59][60]. The two are distinct bills with distinct legislative histories and distinct effective-date provisions. ContrPro materials referencing "SB 102 retainage changes" are confusing the two and should be corrected to "SB 346."

### 6.5 House Bill 1203 (2024) — community association reforms, effective July 1, 2024

**HB 1203 (Ch. 2024-XX, Laws of Florida)** [61], effective **July 1, 2024**, amended Chapter 720 (Homeowners' Associations) and Chapter 718 (Condominiums) to impose new disclosure, governance, and document-retention requirements on community associations. For ContrPro contractors, HB 1203 is most relevant where the contractor's customer is a community association or HOA — disclosure obligations regarding contracts above certain thresholds, mandatory competitive bidding for HOA contracts above statutory floors, and contractor-of-record reporting requirements may apply. HB 1203 does **not** amend Chapter 489 licensing, Chapter 713 lien law, or the prompt-pay regime; it amends community-association governance rules that affect construction procurement by HOAs.

### 6.6 Bills proposed but not enacted (verification cross-reference)

A review of the 2024 and 2025 Regular Session legislative trackers via flsenate.gov and the Florida Online Sunshine identifies several construction-relevant bills that did **not** pass and should not be relied upon:

- **Proposed expansion of the 5% retainage cap to private works.** Several bills introduced in 2024 and 2025 would have extended the public-works 5% retainage cap into the private-works arena. None passed; **private-works retainage in Florida remains uncapped and contract-driven**.
- **Proposed statewide rebar inspection rule.** A bill proposing mandatory third-party rebar inspection for residential foundation pours did not advance beyond committee in 2025.
- **Proposed contractor reciprocity expansion.** Bills proposing automatic reciprocity for Tennessee, Georgia, and South Carolina contractor licenses did not pass; **Florida certification by examination remains the only path for out-of-state contractors**, subject to limited examination-waiver provisions for Business and Finance test-takers from specific reciprocal states.
- **Proposed restoration of 10-year construction-defect statute of repose.** Bills introduced in 2024 to roll back SB 360's 7-year repose to the historical 10-year repose did not pass; **the 7-year repose remains the law**.

### 6.7 Discrepancy resolutions

Several persistent secondary-source errors worth flagging for ContrPro materials:

- **Some commentary still describes retainage on Florida public works as "10% to 50% complete, then 5%."** This was the rule before SB 346 (2023). The post-July 1, 2023 rule is **5% throughout the project**, with the punch-list-based release mechanic at substantial completion [33][35][38].
- **Some commentary describes the construction-defect statute of repose as 10 years.** This was the law before SB 360 (April 13, 2023). The current repose is **7 years** [53][54].
- **Some commentary describes Notice to Owner as required within 45 days of contract signing.** The statute runs 45 days from **first furnishing**, not from contract signing [23][24]. A subcontractor that signs a contract in January and first furnishes materials in March has until **April 45 days later** — not February — to serve Notice to Owner.
- **Some commentary describes the registered-contractor jurisdiction restriction as a "courtesy" or "voluntary."** It is not. A registered contractor performing work outside its registered jurisdiction commits unlicensed practice under § 489.127 — a Class A misdemeanor on first offense, third-degree felony during a declared emergency [9][7].
- **Some commentary describes Florida lien waivers as enforceable in "substantially similar" form.** This was the law before **SB 658 (effective July 1, 2025)**. After July 1, 2025, the waiver and release forms must match the statutory templates exactly [56][57][58].

## 7. Practical compliance checklist for a Florida contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**

- Confirm the **state-license status** for each self-performed trade:
  - General/Building/Residential — CILB Certified (CGC/CBC/CRC) for statewide work, or Registered (RG/RB/RR) for jurisdiction-limited work.
  - HVAC — CILB Certified Air Conditioning Class A (CAC) for unlimited capacity, or Class B (CAC limited to 25 tons / 500,000 BTU per system).
  - Plumbing — CILB Certified Plumbing Contractor (CFC) or Registered (RF).
  - Mechanical — CILB Certified Mechanical Contractor (CMC) or Registered (RM).
  - Electrical — ECLB Certified Electrical Contractor (CEC) or Registered (ER).
  - Structural steel — no dedicated state credential; verify CGC umbrella scope plus AISC and AWS qualifications, or local specialty registration.
- Confirm **public liability and property damage insurance** to DBPR minimums: **$300,000/$50,000 for Division I**; **$100,000/$25,000 for Division II**; **$300,000/$50,000 for ECLB Certified Electrical**.
- Confirm **workers' compensation coverage** for every employee. **Construction-industry threshold is 1 employee**. Verify every subcontractor's WC certificate of insurance at every contract execution and at every renewal; the prime contractor is the statutory employer under § 440.10(1)(b).
- Confirm **FICO score ≥ 660** for the qualifier of record, or have **financial-responsibility course completion** plus **surety bond** in place ($20,000 Division I; $10,000 Division II; $20,000 ECLB).
- Confirm **municipal permit and registration** in each county/city where work will be performed. Even Certified contractors must register and pull permits locally; Registered contractors must hold local competency credentials in each jurisdiction.

**Before commencing work — Notice of Commencement (§ 713.13):**

- **Owner records the Notice of Commencement** in the county where the property is located, **before commencing improvement**, for any direct contract > $2,500 ($7,500 for residential roofing repairs).
- **Post a certified copy at the jobsite** before commencement.
- Confirm the Notice's **effective period** (1 year unless otherwise specified) and **expiration date**. **Re-record before expiration** if the project will extend.
- Verify the **designated agent for service** on the recorded Notice — this address controls Notice-to-Owner service.

**Within 45 days of first furnishing — Notice to Owner (§ 713.06(2)):**

- **Every subcontractor, sub-subcontractor, materialman, and design-professional sub** serves the statutory Notice to Owner on the owner, the designated agent, the contractor, and the contractor's surety (if a payment bond exists).
- Service by **certified mail with return receipt** or **traceable private courier** (FedEx, UPS); preserve proof of delivery.
- **The 45 days runs from first furnishing, not from contract signing.**
- **Failure to serve = complete defense to lien**. No grace period, no substantial-compliance shield.

**During the work — payment cycle:**

- **Private works (§ 715.12):** Owner pays prime within **14 days** of properly invoiced amount with required releases. Cascade to subs on receipt-and-release. Interest on overdue: legal rate (§ 55.03) + **12% per annum** = approximately **19% APR** in 2026.
- **Local-government works (§ 218.735):** Local government pays prime within **25 business days** (with architect/engineer approval; 20 business days without). Prime pays subs within **10 days** of receipt; sub-subs within **7 days**. Interest on overdue: **2% per month (24% APR)** or contract rate, whichever is greater.
- **State public works (§§ 255.073/255.077):** Schedules vary by procurement statute; 25 business days typical. Retainage cap **5%** flat throughout project (post-SB 346).
- **Pay-if-paid clauses:** Enforceable if unambiguous in the subcontract (*Peacock Construction* line of authority). Florida has no statutory limitation analogous to Texas's Chapter 56 — clause drafting controls.

**After substantial completion — punch list and retainage release:**

- **Punch list within 30 days** of substantial completion (projects ≤ $10M) or **45 days** (> $10M).
- **Retainage release within 20 business days** of punch-list finalization, less **150% of estimated cost to complete punch-list items**.
- Subcontractor retainage must be cascaded promptly upon receipt by the prime.

**Within 90 days of last furnishing — Claim of Lien (§ 713.08):**

- **Record the Claim of Lien** in the county where the property is located **not later than 90 days after final furnishing**. Punch-list items, warranty work, and trivial return trips do **not** extend the 90 days.
- **Serve the recorded Claim of Lien on the owner** before recording or within **15 days after recording**.
- Include the mandatory **1-year warning** and all § 713.08(3) required content.

**Before suing on the lien — Final Contractor's Affidavit (§ 713.06(3)(d)) (prime contractors only):**

- The prime delivers the **Final Contractor's Affidavit** to the owner **at least 5 days before filing suit**.
- The affidavit lists all lienors who served Notice to Owner and confirms payment in full, or names unpaid lienors with amounts due.
- **No Affidavit = no lien**: "The contractor shall have no lien or right of action against the owner... while in default for not giving the owner the affidavit."

**Watch for the owner's Notice of Contest (§ 713.22(2)):**

- After a Claim of Lien is recorded, the owner may file a **Notice of Contest of Lien** shortening the lienor's foreclosure deadline from **1 year to 60 days from service**.
- Be prepared to **file suit within 60 days** of receiving a Notice of Contest. The lien is automatically extinguished if not.
- Alternatively, the lien may be **transferred to a cash deposit or surety bond** under § 713.24 (lien amount + 3 years' interest + greater of $5,000 / 25%); the lien attaches to the bond.

**Construction-funds discipline (§ 713.345):**

- Treat construction payments as **fund earmarked for labor, services, and materials**. Diverting these funds is a **felony** — third degree (< $1,000), second degree ($1,000–$99,999), or **first degree ($100,000+, up to 30 years prison)**.
- **Officers and agents are personally criminally liable**, regardless of corporate form.

**Florida Building Code compliance:**

- Confirm the current FBC edition (Eighth Edition, 2023) and any subsequent supplements adopted by the Florida Building Commission.
- **HVHZ projects (Miami-Dade, Broward):** every exterior-envelope component requires an active **NOA (Miami-Dade) or Product Control approval (Broward)**. Mandatory TAS 201/202/203 testing for envelope products.
- **Wind-borne-debris regions (coastal statewide):** impact-resistant glazed openings or impact-resistant shutters per FBC § 1609.2.
- **Post-storm work:** unlicensed contracting during declared emergency is a third-degree felony — verify licensure of every employee, sub, and field representative.

**After every storm event in your jurisdiction:**

- Confirm the **state of emergency declaration** has been formally declared by the Governor.
- If working outside your registered jurisdiction: **register temporary work with the host jurisdiction** under § 489.117(4); the 24-month emergency-jurisdiction window applies from declaration date.
- Watch for **roofing-specific consumer-protection rules under § 489.147** if storm-related work includes roofing scope.

**Sales-tax compliance (Rule 12A-1.051):**

- **Default treatment: contractor is the consumer.** Pay 6% state + local surtax (up to 1.5%) at materials purchase; charge no sales tax to the customer on lump-sum, cost-plus, fixed-fee, guaranteed-price, or time-and-materials contracts.
- **Retail-sale-plus-installation election:** itemize materials with prices in the contract before work begins; register as a dealer with FDOR; collect sales tax from the customer on materials portion (installation labor not taxable).
- **Use tax** on out-of-state materials at combined state + local rate.
- File **Sales and Use Tax Return (DR-15)** monthly or quarterly depending on volume.

**Biennial / annual compliance:**

- **Renew Certified or Registered license biennially** with DBPR by **August 31** (even-numbered license numbers in even-numbered years; odd in odd). Submit **14 hours of CE** (1 hour each of safety, WC, business, laws, FBC, advanced FBC; 8 elective hours).
- **Renew workers' compensation coverage** annually or per policy term; **submit WC waiver re-elections every 2 years** for exempt corporate officers (Department of Financial Services).
- **File corporate income tax return** (Form F-1120) annually if C-corporation, with first $50,000 of net income exempt.
- **File annual report** with Florida Department of State, Division of Corporations, by **May 1**.
- **Verify FICO** and update credit reports as part of any qualifier-of-record change.
