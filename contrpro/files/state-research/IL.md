## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Illinois structure — no statewide general contractor license; licensure is by trade or by municipality

Illinois is one of a small minority of U.S. states (alongside Texas, Pennsylvania, Maine, and a few others) that does **not** require a statewide general contractor license [1][2][3]. There is no Illinois state agency, board, or department charged with licensing general contractors. A person or entity may operate as a general contractor in Illinois without obtaining any state-level credential, subject to:

1. **Trade-specific state licensure** — Plumbing (225 ILCS 320) and Roofing (225 ILCS 335) are licensed at the state level. Plumbing licensure is administered by the Illinois Department of Public Health (IDPH); Roofing licensure is administered by the Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation (IDFPR) [4][5][6][7].
2. **Municipal contractor registration and licensure** — Most Illinois cities and many counties require general contractors and trade contractors to register with the local building department or to hold a local license. The City of Chicago operates the most prominent municipal licensing regime under Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-36 (general contractors), Chapter 4-292 (supervising electricians), Chapter 4-290 (electrical contractors), Chapter 4-200 (plumbers), and others [8][9][10][11].
3. **Workers' compensation insurance** — Effectively universal for construction employers under 820 ILCS 305 (see §5 below) [12][13].

For ContrPro's five mechanical and electrical trades, the practical map is:

| Trade | Statewide license? | Authority | Statutory authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| General contracting | **No state license** — municipal only | City/county building departments; Chicago Dept. of Buildings is the dominant example | None statewide; Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-36 [9][10] |
| Plumbing | **Yes** — Apprentice, Plumber, and Plumbing Contractor registrations | Illinois Department of Public Health (IDPH) | 225 ILCS 320 [4][14][15] |
| Electrical | **No state license** — municipal only | Local building departments (Chicago, Aurora, Rockford, Naperville, Springfield, etc., each operate independently) | None statewide; Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-290, 4-292 [11][16] |
| HVAC / Mechanical | **No state license** — municipal only | Local building departments (Chicago Mechanical Contractor License; Cook County; municipal jurisdictions) | None statewide; Chicago Municipal Code [10][17] |
| Structural steel erection | **No state license** | Local building department; AISC/AWS by contract | Industry standard, building code |

**Roofing** — though not in ContrPro's principal five trades — is licensed statewide under 225 ILCS 335, administered by IDFPR; the Act has Limited (residential, 8 units or fewer) and Unlimited (any roofing work) tiers and was renewed in 2025 by Public Act 104-0427 through January 1, 2031 [5][6][7].

### 1.2 The absence of a statewide general contractor license — implications

The absence of a statewide GC license has three significant consequences a contractor (and ContrPro as a tooling platform serving them) must understand:

1. **Unenforceable-contract risk is municipal, not statutory.** Unlike Georgia (where § 43-41-17(b) renders an unlicensed contract unenforceable as a matter of state statute) or California (where Business & Professions Code § 7031 bars recovery by unlicensed contractors), Illinois has **no statewide unenforceable-contract penalty** for unlicensed general contracting. Enforcement is creatures of local municipal code, and the principal "you cannot sue for the contract" exposure arises only where a municipality has expressly so provided — most prominently the City of Chicago (Chicago Municipal Code § 4-36-100, prohibiting performance of work without the required license and authorizing fines, but not, by its terms, voiding the contract) [9][10][18].
2. **Project-by-project verification matters more than in licensed states.** Because state licensure does not confirm competency, owners, lenders, and bonding companies routinely impose **contract-based qualification** requirements — proof of prior similar work, financial statements, bonding capacity, insurance certificates — at much higher specificity than they would in a licensed state. ContrPro contractors operating across multiple Illinois jurisdictions must maintain a flexible municipal-registration profile and a per-jurisdiction tracking system for permit-pulling credentials.
3. **The trade-license layer is binding regardless.** Even though general contracting is not licensed at the state level, **plumbing and roofing work performed by a general contractor self-performing those trades requires the state-level plumbing license (225 ILCS 320) or roofing license (225 ILCS 335)**. There is no "general contractor exemption" from the state trade-license statutes. A GC that attempts to self-perform plumbing or roofing without the proper license is operating in violation of those statutes and exposes itself to civil penalties under 225 ILCS 320/40 (plumbing) or 225 ILCS 335/9.1 (roofing) [4][5][14].

### 1.3 City of Chicago — General Contractor License (Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-36)

The most economically significant municipal licensing regime in Illinois is the **City of Chicago General Contractor License**, established under **Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-36** and administered by the **Chicago Department of Buildings** [9][10][18][19].

**Required for** any person or entity that engages in the business of general contracting in the City of Chicago, where "general contracting" is defined to include construction, alteration, repair, removal, or demolition of any structure, OR oversight of work performed by other contractors, where the work requires a building permit issued by the Chicago Department of Buildings [9][10][18].

**License classes** (Chicago Municipal Code § 4-36-030) [9][10][20]:

| Class | Project value cap | GL insurance minimum (per occurrence) |
|---|---|---|
| **Class A** | **Unlimited** project value | **$5,000,000** |
| **Class B** | Up to **$10,000,000** per project | **$3,000,000** |
| **Class C** | Up to **$5,000,000** per project | **$1,000,000 / $2,000,000 aggregate** |
| **Class D** | Up to **$2,000,000** per project | **$1,000,000 / $2,000,000 aggregate** |
| **Class E** | Up to **$500,000** per project | **$1,000,000** per occurrence |

All Chicago GC license classes require **workers' compensation insurance** at statutory levels for any employer that has employees in Illinois (effectively a universal requirement under 820 ILCS 305 for construction; see §5 below) [10][12][13].

**Eligibility requirements** (Chicago Municipal Code § 4-36-040; Department of Buildings General Contractor License webpage) [9][10][20][21]:

1. **Age:** At least **18 years** old; must hold either a Social Security Number or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN).
2. **Examination:** Pass the **Continental Testing Services General Contractor Examination** for the applicable class. The exam is open-book and covers the Chicago Construction Codes (which adopt the 2018 IBC, IRC, IECC, IPMC, IPSDC, and other I-Codes with Chicago Amendments), Chicago Municipal Code requirements relevant to contractors, business and project-management content, and OSHA/safety basics [9][10][20].
3. **Business-entity disclosures:** Specify business structure (LLC, sole proprietor, partnership, corporation); identify all members, partners, and officers. Out-of-state entities must register with the Illinois Secretary of State and appoint a registered agent in Illinois before applying.
4. **Insurance certificates:** Certificate of commercial general liability insurance at the class-required minimum, with the **City of Chicago listed as additional insured**, on file at the time of application and continuously thereafter; certificate of workers' compensation insurance [9][10][20][21].
5. **Fees:** Application fee approximately $250–$1,000 depending on class; license fee approximately $1,000–$4,000 depending on class; exam fee approximately $80–$200 administered by Continental Testing Services [10][20][21].

**Renewal cycle.** The Chicago GC License is renewed **biennially** (two-year cycle), with renewal fees scaled to license class. Late renewals incur restoration fees and may require re-examination [9][10][20][21].

**Continuing education.** Chicago does **not** currently impose a formal continuing-education requirement on GC license-holders parallel to the state-imposed CE in Georgia or Florida. Continuing-education-style content is folded into the renewal-examination preparation if a license has lapsed.

**No state reciprocity.** Because Illinois has no statewide GC license, there is no state reciprocity available. A general contractor licensed in, say, Indiana, Wisconsin, or Michigan must qualify for the Chicago GC license on the standard application terms.

**Penalty for unlicensed work.** Performing general contracting work in Chicago without the appropriate Class A through E license is a violation of Chicago Municipal Code § 4-36-020. Fines under § 4-36-100 range from **$1,000 to $5,000 per offense**, with each day of operation treated as a separate offense. Repeat violators may be barred from future licensure and from pulling permits as a related-party entity. Chicago Building Inspectors are authorized to issue stop-work orders that remain in force until the licensure violation is cured [9][10][18].

### 1.4 City of Chicago — Supervising Electrician and Electrical Contractor (Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-292 and 4-290)

Because Illinois has no statewide electrical license, electrical work in Chicago is governed entirely by **Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-290 (Electrical Contractor)** and **Chapter 4-292 (Supervising Electrician)**, plus the **Chicago Electrical Code** (currently the 2020 NEC with Chicago Amendments, formally adopted as Title 14E of the Chicago Municipal Code) [11][16][22][23].

**Two separate credentials** [11][16][22]:

| Credential | Held by | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Supervising Electrician License** | An **individual** | Qualifies the holder to design, install, alter, repair, or supervise electrical work and to act as the technical "qualifier" for an Electrical Contractor entity |
| **Electrical Contractor License** | A **business entity** (LLC, corporation, partnership, sole proprietor) | Permits the entity to bid for and contract electrical work; must designate one or more Supervising Electricians as the entity's qualifier(s) |

**Supervising Electrician License — eligibility** (Chicago Municipal Code § 4-292; Continental Testing Services profession code 303) [11][16][22][23]:

1. **Age:** At least **21 years** old.
2. **Experience:** At least **5 years** of practical electrical work experience for the General Supervising Electrician exam (verified by signed letters from prior supervisors); **2 years** for the Supervising Low-Voltage Electrician exam (covering Class 1, 2, and 3 circuits, signaling, power-limited, and communications wiring).
3. **Examination:** Continental Testing Services administers two exams:
   - **General Supervising Electrician Exam:** 150 questions, open-book, covering the Chicago Electrical Code (2020 NEC with Chicago Amendments), Chicago Building Code references, electrical theory, calculations, motors, transformers, and the Chicago Municipal Code Title 14E. Minimum passing score 70% [16][22][23].
   - **Supervising Low-Voltage Electrician Exam:** 100 questions, open-book, same code base, restricted scope. Minimum passing score 70% [16][22][23].
4. **Application fee:** approximately $350; exam fee approximately $80 (CTS) [22][23].
5. **Identification:** Government-issued photo ID required at exam.

**Electrical Contractor License — eligibility** (Chicago Municipal Code § 4-290) [11][16]:

1. **Designated qualifier:** Must designate a licensed Supervising Electrician (typically an officer or full-time employee of the entity) as the technical qualifier. If the qualifier leaves the entity, a replacement must be designated within 60 days or the contractor license is suspended.
2. **Workers' compensation insurance** for all employees.
3. **GL insurance** at minimum $1,000,000 per occurrence (Class C/D-equivalent), with the City of Chicago as additional insured.
4. **Surety bond:** $20,000 to the City of Chicago, conditioned on faithful compliance with the Chicago Electrical Code and all related municipal regulations.

**Renewal:** Both credentials renew biennially. Supervising Electrician CE is offered through approved providers; CE is not statutorily mandatory for renewal but is strongly recommended for code updates.

**Other Illinois municipal electrical regimes.** Aurora, Rockford, Naperville, Joliet, Champaign-Urbana, Springfield, and Peoria each administer their own electrical contractor licensure. Reciprocity between Illinois municipal regimes is rare; a contractor working across multiple Illinois cities typically holds multiple municipal credentials.

### 1.5 Illinois statewide plumbing license — 225 ILCS 320

Plumbing is the principal Illinois construction trade licensed at the **state** level. The **Illinois Plumbing License Law (225 ILCS 320)**, administered by the **Illinois Department of Public Health (IDPH)** through its **Plumbing Program** under Title 77 Illinois Administrative Code Part 750, governs all plumbing work in Illinois [4][14][15][24][25].

**Statutory scope** (225 ILCS 320/3) [4][14]: "Plumbing" means the actual installation, repair, maintenance, alteration or extension of a plumbing system by any person, including the connection thereof to the public water system. "Plumbing system" includes water supply distribution piping, plumbing fixtures and traps, drain, soil, waste, and vent piping, the building drain, and the building sewer to the connection at the public sanitary sewer or private waste-treatment system. No person is permitted to perform plumbing work in Illinois except as the holder of an IDPH-issued plumbing license [4][14][24].

**License types** [4][14][15][24][25]:

| License | Scope | Path |
|---|---|---|
| **Apprentice Plumber** | Plumbing work under direct supervision of a licensed Plumber, while completing an approved apprenticeship | Register with IDPH; complete 4-to-6-year apprenticeship under an approved Joint Apprenticeship Committee program or a sponsoring Plumbing Contractor |
| **Plumber (Journey-equivalent)** | Plumbing work under the responsibility of a Plumbing Contractor; permitted to install, alter, repair, and extend plumbing systems | Successful completion of an apprenticeship (4–6 years) and passage of the IDPH Plumber Licensing Examination |
| **Plumbing Contractor** | Engages in the business of plumbing — bids, contracts, employs other Plumbers; permits issuance pulled in the Contractor's name | Hold a valid Plumber license; register annually as a Plumbing Contractor with IDPH; carry required bond and insurance |

**Apprentice Plumber — registration** (Title 77 Ill. Adm. Code Part 750) [14][15][25]:

- Minimum age **16**.
- Sponsorship by an Illinois-licensed Plumber or an IDPH-approved apprenticeship program.
- Apprenticeship period: **48 to 72 months** (4 to 6 years) of supervised on-the-job training, with classroom instruction in the Illinois Plumbing Code (Title 77 Ill. Adm. Code Part 890), water supply, drainage, venting, and fixtures.
- After a **maximum of 6 years**, the apprentice must either pass the Plumber Licensing Examination or lose apprentice-license renewal eligibility [14][15].

**Plumber Licensing Examination** [14][15][24]:

- Administered by IDPH (with proctoring outsourced).
- Closed-book exam covering the Illinois Plumbing Code (Part 890), backflow prevention, water supply and distribution, drain/waste/vent, gas piping (joint authority with the Office of the State Fire Marshal on fuel-gas), and Illinois plumbing-business rules.
- Minimum passing score 75%.
- Application fee approximately $200; exam fee approximately $150.

**Plumbing Contractor Registration** — separate annual registration filed by every Plumber who performs work "for another person" — i.e., every Plumber who contracts to install or service plumbing other than as a direct employee of a single Plumbing Contractor [4][14][15][24][25]. Required of:

- Sole-proprietor Plumbers operating their own businesses.
- Partnerships, LLCs, or corporations that engage in plumbing contracting (must designate a licensed Plumber as the registered technical qualifier).

**Plumbing Contractor — registration requirements** (Title 77 Ill. Adm. Code Part 750.330; 225 ILCS 320/13) [4][14][15][26]:

1. **Surety bond:** **$20,000** to the State of Illinois conditioned on compliance with the Illinois Plumbing Code, the Illinois Plumbing License Law, and IDPH rules.
2. **Continuous public liability insurance** of at least **$100,000 per occurrence**.
3. Each licensed Plumber working for the contractor must be currently licensed; the contractor is responsible for documenting plumber-license currency.
4. Annual registration fee, approximately $200.

**No "Master Plumber" designation.** Illinois does **not** issue a separate "Master Plumber" license — the Plumbing Contractor registration plus the underlying Plumber license is the functional equivalent. The "Master Plumber" terminology used in Chicago refers to the **Chicago Plumber's License** under Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-200, which is a municipal credential layered on top of the state license, **not** a separate state license [4][14][26].

**Continuing education.** Plumbers and Plumbing Contractors must complete **4 hours of approved CE per year** to maintain licensure, covering Illinois Plumbing Code updates, backflow prevention, water-supply protection, and rules updates [14][15][25]. CE providers are approved by IDPH.

**Penalties for unlicensed plumbing.** Under 225 ILCS 320/40, performing plumbing work without an IDPH license is a **Class A misdemeanor** for a first offense and a **Class 4 felony** for subsequent offenses. Civil penalties of up to **$5,000 per violation** may also be assessed [4][14][26].

**IDPH publishes approximately 8,900 licensed plumbers and 2,000 registered apprentice plumbers as of recent reporting cycles** [15][25].

**Backflow prevention.** A separate **Cross-Connection Control Device Inspector** certification is required to test backflow-prevention assemblies on Illinois public water systems. Administered by IDPH under Title 77 Ill. Adm. Code Part 890.

### 1.6 City of Chicago — Plumbing Contractor License (Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-200)

A licensed Illinois Plumbing Contractor working in the City of Chicago must **also** hold the **City of Chicago Plumbing Contractor License** (commonly called the "Chicago Master Plumber" license) under **Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-200** [27][28]. Requirements layer on top of the state license:

1. **Hold a current IDPH Plumber and Plumbing Contractor registration** (the state license is a prerequisite).
2. **Pass the Chicago Plumber Examination** administered by Continental Testing Services, covering the Chicago Plumbing Code (a Chicago-amended version of the Illinois Plumbing Code with municipal-specific water-distribution and sewer requirements) and Chicago Municipal Code provisions [28].
3. **GL insurance** at minimum $1,000,000 per occurrence; **$50,000 surety bond** to the City of Chicago [27][28].
4. **Workers' compensation insurance** as required by 820 ILCS 305.
5. Annual or biennial Chicago license fee, approximately $1,400.

The Chicago Plumbing Contractor license is **non-transferable** and must be held by the individual designated as the firm's qualifier; if the qualifier leaves, the contractor license is suspended pending designation of a replacement within a short window (typically 60 days).

### 1.7 City of Chicago — Mechanical Contractor (Refrigeration / HVAC)

HVAC contracting in Chicago is licensed at the municipal level — there is no statewide HVAC license in Illinois. The **City of Chicago Mechanical Contractor License**, administered by the Chicago Department of Buildings, is required for any entity that installs, alters, repairs, or maintains heating, ventilation, air-conditioning, refrigeration, or mechanical ventilation systems in Chicago [17][29].

Eligibility parallels the Electrical Contractor pattern:

1. **Designated qualifier** — a licensed Stationary Engineer or designated equivalent with at least 5 years of mechanical-system experience.
2. **Examination** through Continental Testing Services covering the Chicago Mechanical Code (which adopts the IMC with Chicago Amendments), the Chicago Fuel Gas Code, refrigeration safety, and the Chicago Municipal Code.
3. **GL insurance** at minimum $1,000,000 per occurrence; **$10,000 surety bond** to the City of Chicago.
4. **Workers' compensation** insurance as required by 820 ILCS 305.

Outside Chicago, HVAC contracting in Illinois municipalities follows widely varying patterns — some cities (Aurora, Joliet) require a local Mechanical Contractor registration; many smaller municipalities simply rely on the building-permit process without a separate trade license.

### 1.8 Structural steel erection — no state license; AISC/AWS by contract

As in Georgia, Illinois does **not** license structural-steel erectors at the state level [30][31]. Competency on commercial and DOT projects is established through:

1. **AISC Certified Erector / AISC Certified Fabricator** under AISC 207/303 — required by most large commercial specifications and by Illinois Department of Transportation (IDOT) bridge specifications.
2. **AWS welder qualification** under AWS D1.1 (building steel) or AWS D1.5 (bridge welding); IDOT requires AWS D1.5 for state bridge welds.
3. **AWS Certified Welding Inspector (CWI)** for inspection on commercial and IDOT projects.
4. **OSHA 10/30** as contract requirement.
5. **Special inspections** — Illinois adopts the IBC's Chapter 17 special-inspection regime through the Illinois Capital Development Board's State Capital Construction Standards and through local building-code adoptions; commercial steel typically requires owner-engaged special inspectors.

A structural-steel contractor with no self-performed plumbing, electrical, or HVAC scope may operate in most Illinois municipalities under a building permit issued in the GC's name, without a separate trade license — but Chicago, Aurora, and several other cities require the steel contractor to register as a "subcontractor" affiliated with the permit-holder GC.

### 1.9 Roofing — 225 ILCS 335 (Illinois Roofing Industry Licensing Act)

Although roofing is not in ContrPro's primary five trades, the **Illinois Roofing Industry Licensing Act (225 ILCS 335)** is the second major statewide trade license in Illinois (after plumbing), administered by **IDFPR** [5][6][7]. Two license tiers exist:

| Tier | Scope | Bond |
|---|---|---|
| **Limited Roofing License** | Residential roofing on structures of **8 units or fewer** | **$10,000** continuous surety bond |
| **Unlimited Roofing License** | All roofing work, residential and commercial | **$25,000** continuous surety bond |

Both tiers require GL insurance of at least **$250,000 per occurrence (public liability)** and **$500,000 per occurrence (personal injury / bodily harm)** [5][6][7]. The Act was renewed by **Public Act 104-0427 (SB 2503)**, signed August 15, 2025, extending the licensure regime through **January 1, 2031**, and raising the maximum civil penalty for unlicensed-roofing violations to **$15,000** per violation [5][6][7].

GCs that self-perform roofing on Illinois projects (residential or commercial) must hold the appropriate IDFPR roofing license. Subcontracting roofing to a properly licensed Limited or Unlimited Roofing contractor is the standard practice for GCs that do not specialize in roofing.

### 1.10 Workers' compensation — automatic coverage for construction; statutory employer exposure

Illinois workers' compensation is at **820 ILCS 305 et seq.**, administered by the **Illinois Workers' Compensation Commission (IWCC)** [12][13][32][33]. Construction is the most heavily regulated industry segment.

**Construction-industry automatic coverage** — **820 ILCS 305/3** [12][32]:

"The provisions of this Act shall apply automatically and without election to all employers and all their employees, engaged in any of the following enterprises or businesses which are declared to be extra hazardous, namely: 1. The building, maintaining, repairing, removing, remodeling, altering or demolishing of any structure; ... Every person, firm, public or private corporation, including municipal corporations, township, incorporated village or school district, body politic, or quasi-municipal corporation, who has any person in service or under any contract for hire, express or implied, oral or written, in any business, enterprise, or activity hereinabove enumerated, shall provide and pay compensation to such person... ."

Unlike non-construction industries (which require WC coverage above a 1-employee threshold for most employments under § 305/3 generally), **construction employers have no minimum-employee threshold** — coverage is automatic and required regardless of headcount, including for sole-proprietor employers who hire any helper, day laborer, or 1099 contractor whom the IWCC may reclassify as an employee.

**Statutory employer rule — 820 ILCS 305/1(a)(3)** [12][32][33]:

A general contractor that engages a subcontractor who does not carry the required workers' compensation insurance is liable to pay workers' compensation benefits to that subcontractor's employees as if the GC had directly employed them. Illinois's statutory-employer rule is structurally identical to Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 34-9-8 [33].

Two practical consequences:

1. **Always require Certificates of Insurance evidencing WC coverage** from every sub before site access. The COI should name the GC as certificate holder and be currency-verified at minimum quarterly.
2. **Sole-proprietor subs with no employees** may be exempt from carrying WC for themselves, but any helper or day laborer triggers the requirement — and the GC inherits liability when the sub fails to carry. Illinois has aggressive worker-classification enforcement: independent-contractor designations are reviewed under the **Illinois Employee Classification Act (820 ILCS 185)**, which presumes construction workers are employees unless the contracting entity proves all three prongs of an "ABC test" [34].

**Sole-proprietor / corporate-officer election.** Sole proprietors, partners, and corporate officers may elect into or out of coverage under their own policy. The exclusion is filed via the IWCC and reflected on the WC policy as an officer-exclusion endorsement [12][32].

**Construction-industry classification rates.** Illinois is an NCCI advisory-loss-cost state. The construction classifications (5538 Heating/AC, 5183 Plumbing, 5190 Electrical, 5057 Iron/Steel Erection, 5645 Carpentry/Single-Family, 5474 Painting, 5022 Masonry) carry the state's highest base rates and experience-modification volatility.

**Penalties for failure to carry WC.** Under 820 ILCS 305/4(a), an employer that fails to carry required WC is subject to:

- **Civil penalty of $500 per day**, with a minimum penalty of **$10,000** per uninsured employer [12][32][35].
- **Class 4 felony** for knowing or willful failure to carry coverage [12][32].
- Personal liability for corporate officers who knowingly cause the corporation to fail to carry coverage.
- **Stop-work order** authority — the IWCC may issue an emergency order ceasing all operations until coverage is obtained.

The Illinois Workers' Compensation Commission maintains the **IWCC Insurance Compliance Division**, which audits employers and may obtain default judgments against uninsured employers on behalf of injured workers, then pursue collection from corporate officers personally.

### 1.11 Worker classification — 820 ILCS 185 (Illinois Employee Classification Act)

The **Illinois Employee Classification Act (820 ILCS 185)**, also known as the "ECA," is a construction-industry-specific statute that **presumes any individual performing services for a construction-industry contractor is an employee**, not an independent contractor, unless the contractor proves all three prongs of the statute's ABC test [34][36]:

**The ABC test** (820 ILCS 185/10) [34][36]:

A worker is **not** an employee under the ECA if, and only if, the contractor proves all three:

(A) The individual has been and will continue to be free from control or direction over the performance of the service for the contractor, both under the individual's contract of service and in fact; AND

(B) The service performed by the individual is outside the usual course of services performed by the contractor (NOTE — this is the killer prong for most construction-trade subcontractor relationships); AND

(C) The individual is engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, profession, or business.

**Penalties** (820 ILCS 185/40, 45) [34][36]:

- Civil penalties of **$1,000 to $1,500 per worker per violation** (first offense), rising to **$2,500 per worker** for subsequent offenses.
- **Double damages** for misclassified workers' lost wages, benefits, and tax-withholding.
- Personal liability for corporate officers who knowingly misclassify.
- Cross-referral to the Illinois Department of Labor for wage-and-hour claims.
- Cross-referral to the Illinois Department of Revenue for unpaid withholding.
- Cross-referral to the IWCC for unpaid workers' compensation premiums.

The Illinois ECA is one of the most aggressive worker-classification statutes in the nation and is a frequent source of enforcement actions against general contractors who treat trade subcontractors' on-site workers as "independent contractors" rather than as W-2 employees of the sub. The GC's safest practice is to require every subcontractor entity to evidence W-2 employment of all on-site workers (or, if 1099 use is unavoidable, to require the sub to indemnify the GC against ECA exposure and to maintain a robust paper trail under the three-prong ABC test).

### 1.12 GL insurance — Illinois statewide minimums

Illinois imposes no statewide GL minimum for general contracting (because there is no statewide GC license). GL minimums arise from:

| Source | Minimum |
|---|---|
| Chicago GC License Class A | **$5,000,000 per occurrence** |
| Chicago GC License Class B | **$3,000,000 per occurrence** |
| Chicago GC License Class C/D | **$1,000,000 / $2,000,000 aggregate** |
| Chicago GC License Class E | **$1,000,000 per occurrence** |
| Chicago Electrical Contractor License | **$1,000,000 per occurrence** |
| Chicago Plumbing Contractor License | **$1,000,000 per occurrence** |
| Chicago Mechanical Contractor License | **$1,000,000 per occurrence** |
| Illinois Plumbing Contractor (state) | **$100,000 per occurrence** |
| Illinois Roofing Contractor (Limited) | **$250,000 / $500,000** (public liability / personal injury) |
| Illinois Roofing Contractor (Unlimited) | **$250,000 / $500,000** + $25,000 bond |

Project owners, lenders, and bonding companies routinely require GL limits well above any of these statutory minimums — $1M/$2M is the typical commercial floor on private work; $2M/$4M is common on larger commercial and institutional jobs; bonded public-works projects routinely require $5M to $10M aggregate, particularly when the project involves an existing public facility or sensitive use.

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — 770 ILCS 60 (Mechanics Lien Act)

The Illinois Mechanics Lien Act, **770 ILCS 60 et seq.**, is one of the oldest mechanic's-lien statutes in the country, with origins in the 1873 statute and substantial modernization in 1987 (subcontractor notice rewrite), 2007 (residential consumer protections), and most recently 2024–2025 (notice-service modernization by Public Act 103-0827, effective January 1, 2025) [37][38][39][40][41].

### 2.1 Who may claim a lien — 770 ILCS 60/1

Under **770 ILCS 60/1** [37][39][40]:

Any person who shall by any contract or contracts, express or implied, or partly expressed or implied, with the owner of a lot or tract of land, or with one whom the owner has authorized or knowingly permitted to contract, to improve the lot or tract of land or for the purpose of improving the tract of land, or to manage a structure under construction thereon, is known under this Act as a **contractor** and has a lien upon the lot or tract of land and upon adjoining or adjacent lots or tracts of land of the owner constituting the same premises and occupied or used in connection with such lot or tract of land as a place of residence or business; and in the case of the construction of any houses, the contractor has a lien on all such lots or tracts of land and improvements thereon for the amount due to him or her for the material, fixtures, apparatus, machinery, services, or labor.

The Act protects "any person" who performs services or furnishes material under contract with the owner, owner's agent, or anyone the owner knowingly permits to contract. Subcontractors and suppliers at any tier (sub, sub-sub, materialman) are afforded similar but **separately-perfected** lien rights under § 60/21 (subcontractor liens) [37][41][42][43].

### 2.2 Lien-claim aggregate cap — 770 ILCS 60/21

A subcontractor's lien is limited under **770 ILCS 60/21** to **what is justly due** to the subcontractor by the contractor, but in no event greater than the amount due from the owner to the contractor at the time of receipt of the subcontractor's 90-day notice, less any subsequent payments properly made [37][41][42][43]. This is the Illinois analog of Georgia's contract-price cap and protects the owner from double liability.

If the owner has already paid the contractor in full before receiving the sub's 90-day notice, the sub's lien is effectively zero — though the sub retains contract and equitable remedies against the contractor directly.

### 2.3 Subcontractor 90-day notice — 770 ILCS 60/24

The most important subcontractor procedural step under Illinois law is the **90-day notice** required by **770 ILCS 60/24** [37][41][42][43][44]. A subcontractor, supplier, or other person not in direct privity with the owner who has performed work or furnished materials on an owner's property must serve a notice of claim on:

1. The **owner of record** (and the owner's agent or architect, or the superintendent in charge); AND
2. **The lender** of record (if known).

**Deadline:** Within **90 days** of the last date on which the subcontractor performed work or delivered material to the project [37][41][42][43].

**Method of service** (as amended by Public Act 103-0827, effective January 1, 2025) [44][45]:

- Registered or certified mail with return receipt requested; **OR**
- Personal service; **OR**
- A nationally recognized commercial delivery company **with tracking service** (FedEx, UPS, DHL) [44][45].

**The Public Act 103-0827 (HB 4660) amendment, effective January 1, 2025**, was a significant modernization [44][45]:

1. **FedEx, UPS, and DHL with tracking are now recognized** as valid carriers (previously, only USPS certified mail or personal service satisfied the strict statutory text).
2. **No specific recipient's signature is required** — digital tracking confirmation is sufficient proof of delivery.
3. **Notice is legally served on the date of mailing or pickup**, not on the date of arrival — this fixes a long-standing trap where mail delays could push delivery past the 90-day window even though the mailing was timely.

**Contents of the 90-day notice.** A statutory form is provided at 770 ILCS 60/24. Required elements include claimant identity, project address, identity of the person with whom claimant contracted (i.e., the upstream party), description of labor or materials, total contract value, and amount claimed unpaid. The notice must be sworn (notarized) and substantially in the statutory form [37][41].

**Effect of failure to serve within 90 days** [37][41][42][43]:

- Failure to serve the **owner** within 90 days — the subcontractor's lien is **invalid as against the owner**.
- Failure to serve the **lender** within 90 days — the subcontractor's lien is **inoperable as against the lender** (but may remain enforceable against the owner if the owner was timely served).
- A subcontractor who is included in a **sworn statement** filed by the general contractor under 770 ILCS 60/5 (see §2.5 below) may not be required to serve the 90-day notice as a precondition to the lien — but the cautious practice is to serve regardless, because (a) the sworn-statement coverage is often disputed at trial and (b) the sworn statement may not include the actual amount owed.

### 2.4 Residential 60-day owner notice — 770 ILCS 60/5(a) and § 60/7

For owner-occupied residential property (single-family residence, owner-occupied condominium unit, or multi-unit residential property of 4 or fewer units that is owner-occupied), Illinois imposes a **separate** notice on the **general contractor** under **770 ILCS 60/5(a)** [37][41][46][47]:

The contractor must furnish the owner a **written notice of the rights of subcontractors and suppliers to file mechanic's liens** before any payment is due. This is a consumer-protection disclosure rather than a sub-side notice; it ensures the residential owner understands that paying the GC may not extinguish lien rights of sub-tier claimants who go unpaid.

**Failure to provide the residential notice** may bar the contractor from recovering under the lien (and may, in some cases, be defended against under the Illinois Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act, 815 ILCS 505).

A parallel **60-day owner notice** under **770 ILCS 60/7** applies to lien-rights preservation against subsequent third-party purchasers in certain residential contexts; the deadlines and form should be reviewed against the most current statutory text for any residential project.

### 2.5 Sworn statement under 770 ILCS 60/5

Both the lien statute and Illinois title-insurance practice rely heavily on the **Sworn Statement** procedure under **770 ILCS 60/5** [37][41][46][47]. Before receiving a payment from the owner, the contractor must furnish the owner a written, sworn statement listing:

- Every subcontractor, supplier, or materialman that has furnished or will furnish labor or materials;
- The amount due to each;
- The amount paid to each to date;
- The balance remaining unpaid.

The owner is entitled to **withhold payment** from the contractor pending receipt of the sworn statement; the owner is **protected** in making payments in reliance on the sworn statement, in the sense that payments properly made consistent with the statement reduce the pool against which subs may later claim [37][41][46].

A sub omitted from the sworn statement retains lien rights notwithstanding the omission — but only if (a) the sub serves the 90-day notice timely, or (b) the contractor is found to have misrepresented the sworn statement.

### 2.6 Filing the Claim of Lien — 770 ILCS 60/7

For lien claims that must bind **third parties** (purchasers, encumbrancers, or the lender of record), the lien must be **recorded with the recorder of deeds of the county where the property is located** within **4 months** after the last day on which the claimant performed work or delivered material [37][41][42][43][48][49].

**Two distinct timing tracks** [42][43][48][49]:

| Deadline | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Recording the Claim of Lien within 4 months** of last day of furnishing | Lien binds **owner**, **third-party purchasers**, **encumbrancers**, and **the lender of record** |
| **Recording the Claim of Lien within 2 years** of last day of furnishing | Lien binds **owner only** — third parties who acquired interests in the property between 4-month and 2-year mark take **free** of the lien |

**The 4-month clock is strict.** Illinois courts have repeatedly held that warranty visits, punch-list returns, and de minimis return trips do **not** restart the 4-month clock if the original work was substantially complete. The clock restarts only if additional work was **new, distinct work** within the scope of the original contract [42][43][48][49].

**Contents of the Claim of Lien** (770 ILCS 60/7) [37][41][42]:

- A brief statement of the contract;
- The dates of commencement and completion;
- A description of the property sufficient to identify it;
- The total amount due (and not the gross contract price);
- Whether the lien is sought against the owner, contractor, or other party;
- Verification by oath (notarization).

**Service of the Claim of Lien.** Under 770 ILCS 60/7 (as practitioner-standardized), within **10 days** of recording the Claim of Lien with the recorder of deeds, the claimant should serve a copy on the owner and the lender of record by certified mail or by a tracking-capable carrier (FedEx/UPS/DHL under the Public Act 103-0827 amendment). Service of the filed claim is best practice and minimizes defenses based on lack of notice [41][44][45].

### 2.7 Two-year suit-to-enforce deadline — 770 ILCS 60/9 and § 60/7

A lien claimant must **commence suit to enforce the lien** within **2 years** of the last day of furnishing labor or materials [37][41][42][43][48][50]. The 2-year deadline is jurisdictional — Illinois courts have no equitable authority to extend it.

The 2-year deadline applies to **suit against the owner** under the lien itself. As noted above, the lien is unenforceable against third parties who acquired interests after the 4-month recording window but before the 2-year mark.

**No tolling.** Illinois case law is clear that the 4-month and 2-year deadlines under § 60/7 and § 60/9 are not tolled by negotiations, partial payments, owner promises, or contractor's continued presence at the project performing punch-list. Equitable tolling has been consistently rejected by the Illinois Supreme Court and intermediate appellate courts [42][43][48].

### 2.8 Demand to commence suit — 770 ILCS 60/34

The owner of property burdened by a recorded mechanics' lien may, under **770 ILCS 60/34**, serve a **written demand on the lienor** to commence suit on the lien within **30 days** [37][41][50]. If the lienor fails to commence suit within 30 days of service of the demand, the lien is **forfeited**.

**Section 34 demand** is the principal tool for clearing speculative or stale liens at the front end of a refinance, sale closing, or bond-discharge proceeding. The Section 34 demand is the Illinois analog of Georgia's Notice of Contest of Lien (which collapses the 365-day suit-on-debt deadline to 60 days) but is even more aggressive — 30 days versus 60.

### 2.9 Public works lien — 770 ILCS 60/23

Public property cannot be liened in Illinois. Under **770 ILCS 60/23**, however, an unpaid subcontractor or supplier on a **public works project** has an analog procedural route: a **Notice and Claim for Lien on Public Works** [37][41][51][52].

The Public Works Lien is filed with the **public agency** (the state, county, municipality, school district, or other public body that contracted for the work), and creates a lien on **funds owed by the public agency to the prime contractor**, rather than on the public property itself. The lien attaches only to monies in the public agency's hands; once the public agency disburses to the prime, the lien is exhausted.

**Deadlines** [51][52]:

- The Notice and Claim must be filed **within 90 days** of the claimant's last work or delivery of materials.
- The lien claim is enforced through suit within **90 days** of filing the Notice and Claim with the public agency.

The Illinois Public Construction Bond Act (30 ILCS 550 — see §3.6 below) provides a parallel payment-bond claim route for public projects above the bonding threshold, and bond claims are the principal recovery vehicle for unpaid subs on public works.

### 2.10 Bonding off a lien — 770 ILCS 60/38.1

The owner of property burdened by a recorded mechanics' lien may **bond off** the lien under **770 ILCS 60/38.1**, transferring the lien from the property to the bond and clearing the property record [37][41][53].

**Bond amount:** **175% of the lien claim amount** (compared to Georgia's standard 200% / 100%-for-homestead and Texas's 1.5x rule) [53].

**Bond form:** Surety bond from a corporate surety qualified to do business in Illinois, or a cash bond. The bond must be filed with the **circuit court of the county** where the lien was recorded; the court issues an order substituting the bond for the lien and directs the recorder of deeds to release the lien from the property records.

**Surety defenses.** The surety on a bond-discharge bond is entitled to assert any defenses that would have existed in an action on the lien itself — statute-of-limitations defenses, failure to file timely notice, defective contents, and underlying contract defenses [53].

### 2.11 Pre-paid lien waivers and waiver enforceability — 770 ILCS 60/1.1 and § 60/35

Illinois lien waivers are governed by **770 ILCS 60/1.1** (general rule) and by the strict-form requirements of related provisions and Illinois case law [37][41][54]:

**Pre-paid (advance) waivers are largely unenforceable.** Like Georgia (§ 44-14-366(a)) and California (Civ. Code § 8122), Illinois courts have consistently held that an advance waiver of lien rights — given before any payment is received, in exchange for the right to be a sub on the project — is unenforceable as against public policy [54][55].

**Conditional and unconditional waivers.** Illinois practice recognizes two waiver categories paralleling the California Civil Code § 8132–§ 8138 statutory forms:

1. **Conditional waiver upon progress payment** — releases lien rights only upon actual receipt of the stated payment.
2. **Unconditional waiver upon final payment** — releases lien rights without further condition.

Unlike California, Illinois has **no statutory waiver form** — meaning that the form of the waiver is contract-driven. Best practice in Illinois is to use waiver language modeled on the California § 8132–§ 8138 forms (which the title-insurance industry treats as the de facto standard) and to include clear language: (a) describing the specific payment received; (b) describing the work and project; (c) reserving lien rights for any extra or change-order work not described; and (d) — for conditional waivers — explicit conditioning on actual payment clearing.

**Notice of nonpayment.** Where a sub has executed a conditional waiver on a payment that subsequently fails (bounced check, NSF, short payment), the sub should immediately revoke the waiver in writing to the owner, contractor, and lender. There is no Illinois statutory "Affidavit of Nonpayment" filing parallel to Georgia § 44-14-366(f); the revocation is contract- and equity-based.

### 2.12 No statutory "construction trust fund" — but Wage Payment Act and ECA exposure

Illinois does **not** have a dedicated construction-trust-fund statute analogous to Texas Property Code Chapter 162 or New York Lien Law Article 3-A [56][57][58]. Construction-payment misapplication, in Illinois, is addressed through:

1. **Common-law fraud and constructive-trust claims** — courts may impose a constructive trust on contractor receipts to the extent of unpaid subs and suppliers, particularly where the contractor has acted in bad faith or has comingled funds.
2. **Illinois Wage Payment and Collection Act (820 ILCS 115)** — protects employees (including site labor) for unpaid wages; corporate officers face personal liability for knowing nonpayment.
3. **Illinois Employee Classification Act (820 ILCS 185)** — see §1.11 above; misclassified workers' civil claims and double damages.
4. **Theft by deception** under 720 ILCS 5/16-1 — criminal statute potentially reaching contractors who divert progress payments through fraudulent representations to owners.
5. **Illinois Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act (815 ILCS 505)** — for residential customers, expansive remedies including attorneys' fees against contractors who divert payments or otherwise engage in deceptive practices.

The absence of a dedicated trust-fund statute is a **gap** compared to Texas and New York; unpaid subs in Illinois rely primarily on the lien remedy (§ 770 ILCS 60), bond claims (§ 30 ILCS 550), and contract / fraud theories.

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

Illinois has three principal prompt-payment statutes, each applying to a different project type [59][60][61][62][63]:

| Statute | Applies to | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **815 ILCS 603 — Contractor Prompt Payment Act (CPPA)** | **Private construction projects** | Excludes residential of 12 or fewer units; excludes public projects |
| **50 ILCS 505 — Local Government Prompt Payment Act** | **Local government projects** (cities, counties, school districts, park districts, etc.) | Some bid-threshold rules; broadly applicable |
| **30 ILCS 540 — State Prompt Payment Act** | **State agency projects** | Broadly applicable to state contracts |

Public-bond claims and retainage on public projects are governed by the **Public Construction Bond Act, 30 ILCS 550**, and the **Public Works Preference Act** and related procurement statutes.

### 3.1 Private projects — Contractor Prompt Payment Act, 815 ILCS 603

The **Contractor Prompt Payment Act (CPPA)**, codified at **815 ILCS 603**, governs payment timing and retainage on private construction projects above the residential-12-units exemption [59][60][61][64].

**Owner-to-contractor payment deadline — § 603/10:**

The owner shall approve or disapprove a contractor's pay request within **25 days** of receipt. Upon approval, payment shall be made within **15 days** thereafter [60][61][64]. The combined window from pay-request submission to required payment is thus **40 days** at the outer bound (25 review + 15 pay).

**Contractor-to-subcontractor pass-through — § 603/15:**

A contractor shall pay each subcontractor and supplier the proportionate share of any payment received from the owner, less retainage withheld, within **15 days** after the contractor's receipt of the owner payment [60][61][64]. The 15-day pass-through cascades down each tier.

**Interest on late payment.** A contractor or subcontractor entitled to payment under the CPPA may collect **interest at a rate equal to 10% per annum** on amounts wrongfully withheld after the statutory deadlines, plus reasonable attorneys' fees in any enforcement action [60][61][64]. The 10% interest figure is a meaningful contrast with Georgia's 12% (1% per month).

**Stop-work right.** Under § 603/20, a contractor may, after at least **7 days' written notice** to the upstream party, **suspend performance** if a properly submitted pay request remains unpaid beyond the statutory deadline. The right to suspend is not automatic and is conditioned on the proper notice; misuse exposes the contractor to breach-of-contract claims.

### 3.2 Private retainage cap — 815 ILCS 603/20 (10% / 5% / 5%)

The CPPA's retainage cap, set by **Public Act 101-0383**, effective **August 20, 2019**, is one of the strictest private-works retainage statutes in the country [60][62][64][65]:

| Project completion | Maximum retainage |
|---|---|
| **Before 50% completion** | **10%** of each payment |
| **At 50% completion** | Aggregate retainage **reduced to no more than 5%** |
| **After 50% completion** | **No more than 5%** of subsequent payments may be withheld as retainage |

**Coverage** [62][65]:

- **Applies to** private construction contracts between owner-and-prime, and between prime-and-sub.
- **Does not apply to** residential construction projects of **12 or fewer units** (small-builder exemption).
- **Does not apply to** public-funded projects (covered by the parallel local-government and state retainage statutes).
- **Effective date:** Contracts entered into **on or after August 20, 2019**.

**Pass-through retainage release.** The 50%-completion threshold applies at each tier — the prime is responsible for reducing each sub's retainage in the same percentage and timing as the owner reduces the prime's retainage, consistent with the pass-through pattern under § 603/15.

**No statutory carve-out for substantial-completion punch-list.** Unlike Georgia (which allows 200%-of-incomplete-item withholding) and Texas (which permits punch-list-specific withholding under contract), the Illinois CPPA's 5% cap applies cleanly through final payment. Owners that wish to retain additional amounts for incomplete punch-list items must structure the withholding as a back-charge or correction-cost reduction rather than as additional retainage.

### 3.3 Local government projects — Local Government Prompt Payment Act, 50 ILCS 505

The **Local Government Prompt Payment Act (LGPPA)**, codified at **50 ILCS 505 et seq.**, applies to construction contracts let by Illinois local governmental units — counties, townships, municipalities, school districts, park districts, fire-protection districts, sanitary districts, and other quasi-municipal bodies [63][66][67].

**Owner-to-prime payment deadline:**

The local government must approve or disapprove a pay request within **30 days** of receipt and pay an approved request within **60 days** of receipt (or by the date specified in the contract, whichever is earlier) [63][66][67].

**Late-payment interest:** Interest accrues at **1% per month** on undisputed amounts not paid within the statutory window. The 1%/month rate parallels Georgia's prompt-payment interest [63][66].

**Retainage cap (effective January 1, 2024):**

The LGPPA was amended by **Public Act 103-0252**, effective **January 1, 2024**, to import the same 10%/5%/5% retainage cap that applies to private projects under the CPPA [62][66][67][68]:

| Project completion | Maximum retainage |
|---|---|
| **Before 50% completion** | **10%** of any payment |
| **At 50% completion** | Reduced to **no more than 5%** |
| **After 50% completion** | **No more than 5%** of subsequent payments |

The 2024 amendment was the **second** of three sequential Illinois retainage reforms (private 2019, local-government 2024, state-agency 2027 — see §5.1 below).

**Coverage.** All local-government construction contracts. The amendment carved exceptions for certain road-maintenance contracts where federal-funding rules pre-empt state retainage caps, but the principal exclusion is for projects funded with federal dollars where federal procurement rules apply differently.

### 3.4 State agency projects — 30 ILCS 540 and pending HB 1224 (effective June 1, 2027)

The **State Prompt Payment Act (30 ILCS 540)** governs state-agency procurement; the principal rule is that state agencies must pay contractors within **60 days** of receipt of a proper invoice, with interest at **1% per month** thereafter on undisputed amounts [69][70].

**State retainage** has historically been set by individual state-agency procurement rules rather than by a unified statute. **HB 1224 (104th General Assembly)**, signed by Governor JB Pritzker on **August 15, 2025**, will extend the 10%/5%/5% retainage cap to most Illinois state agencies effective **June 1, 2027** [68][71][72]:

- **Covered agencies:** All state agencies **except IDOT** (Illinois Department of Transportation, which is exempt because its projects rely on federal funding and generally do not withhold retainage) [68][71][72].
- **Retainage cap:** Same 10%/5%/5% as private and local-government projects.
- **Eliminate-when-satisfactory provision:** HB 1224 contains language requiring state agencies to **eliminate retainage altogether** when "satisfactory progress" has been achieved, unless project-specific concerns warrant continued retention. This is a more aggressive provision than the local-government or private-works equivalents [68][71][72].

**Effective date:** **June 1, 2027** for contracts entered on or after that date. Contracts entered before the effective date remain under prior procurement rules.

### 3.5 Public Construction Bond Act — 30 ILCS 550

The **Illinois Public Construction Bond Act (30 ILCS 550)** is Illinois's "Little Miller Act" — the public-works payment-and-performance-bond statute that protects subs and suppliers on public projects [73][74][75].

**Bonding threshold (current 2026):**

- **General threshold:** Performance and payment bonds required on public-works contracts above **$150,000**, effective for contracts let after **January 1, 2024** [73][74][75].
- **IDOT and Illinois State Toll Highway Authority threshold:** Bonds required above **$500,000** (recognizing the volume and complexity of highway and transit projects under federal funding).
- **Sunset:** The current threshold framework expires **January 1, 2029**, after which the General Assembly is expected to revisit the thresholds.

**Bond amount.** Performance bond and payment bond each in the amount of the contract price.

**Payment bond claim deadlines** [73][74]:

- **Notice to contractor** of intent to file a bond claim: within **180 days** of last work or delivery (no privity required for sub-tier claimants).
- **Suit on the bond:** must be commenced within **1 year** after the last work or delivery (rather than within 4 months as under the lien act); the 1-year deadline is jurisdictional.

The Public Construction Bond Act is the **principal recovery vehicle** for unpaid subs and suppliers on Illinois public projects, because public property cannot be liened. The bond claim process is procedurally more forgiving than the lien act on timing (1 year vs. 4 months) but bond surety defenses (suretyship law, notice, bond conditions) can complicate recovery.

### 3.6 Coverage gaps and contractual override

Illinois prompt-payment law has several gaps a contractor must understand:

1. **Residential 12-units-or-fewer exemption** under the CPPA — small-builder projects (single-family, small multifamily) are not covered by the 10%/5%/5% retainage cap or the 25-day/15-day approval-and-pay deadlines. Residential consumer rights derive from the lien act (770 ILCS 60/5(a) notice) and from the Illinois Consumer Fraud Act, not from the CPPA.
2. **Contractual override** — parties may agree by contract to **modify** payment timing (longer pay-app cycles, for example) but **may not** waive the statutory retainage caps. The retainage limits are not subject to private waiver.
3. **Attorneys' fees** — the CPPA expressly authorizes prevailing-party attorneys' fees in enforcement actions [60][61][64]. The LGPPA does not.
4. **No federal preemption issues** on the CPPA itself, but federally funded projects (FAA, FTA, FHWA grant projects) may carry federal procurement rules that override state retainage limits — verify project-by-project.

## 4. Tax structure for contractors

### 4.1 State and local sales tax rates

- **State sales tax rate:** **6.25%** under **35 ILCS 120/2** (Retailers' Occupation Tax) and 35 ILCS 105 (Use Tax) [76][77][78][79]. The 6.25% is composed of 5.00% to the state general fund, 1.00% to local governments (distributed by the Illinois Department of Revenue), and 0.25% to a transportation/highway use-tax fund [76][77].
- **Local sales tax:** Counties, municipalities, special-purpose taxing districts, and home-rule units may add local sales tax. Combined rates range from **6.25%** (state-only floor in non-home-rule areas) to approximately **11%** in **Chicago/Cook County** [76][77][78][79][80].
- **Chicago combined sales tax (general merchandise, 2026):** **10.25%** — composed of: 6.25% Illinois state + 1.75% Cook County + 1.25% City of Chicago Home Rule + 1.00% Regional Transportation Authority (RTA) [80][81]. Restaurant tax adds further.
- **Administered by:** Illinois Department of Revenue (IDOR).

The applicable combined rate depends on the **location of delivery or installation**, not the contractor's office. Multi-jurisdiction Illinois contractors must track combined rates by ZIP code or by IDOR's MyTax Illinois Tax Rate Finder [76][81].

### 4.2 Contractor sales-tax treatment — contractor as end user / consumer

Illinois follows the **"contractor as consumer of materials"** model for real-property improvement [82][83][84][85][86]. The rule is articulated in **35 ILCS 120 (Retailers' Occupation Tax Act)**, refined in **86 Ill. Adm. Code § 130.1940 (Construction Contractors and Real Estate Developers)** and **§ 130.2075 (Sales To Construction Contractors)** [85][86][87].

**Core rule.** When a construction contractor purchases tangible personal property (lumber, steel, copper, concrete, conduit, wire, fixtures, fittings, HVAC equipment) and **permanently incorporates** it into real property in the performance of a construction contract, the contractor is the **end user of the materials**. The contractor:

- **Pays sales tax** (or accrues use tax) on the **cost price** of the materials at the time of purchase from the supplier; AND
- **Does NOT collect sales tax** from the customer on the contract price [82][83][84][85][86].

**Statement from IDOR Q&A 149** [83][84]: "A construction contractor does not need to collect sales tax when incorporating tangible personal property into real estate under a construction contract. In this scenario, Illinois law considers the construction contractor the end user of the items permanently incorporated into real estate. The construction contractor must either pay tax on the cost price of these items directly to its supplier or remit the tax directly to the Illinois Department of Revenue."

**Labor is not taxed as a service.** Unlike Texas (where commercial repair-and-remodel labor is taxable as a service under § 151.0048) or some other states, **Illinois does not tax construction labor as a service**. Labor is outside the sales-tax base entirely.

**Lump-sum vs. separated contracts.** Because Illinois taxes the contractor's input materials (not the contractor's output sale), the lump-sum vs. separated distinction does not have the same significance it does in Texas. A contractor can operate under a lump-sum contract without recharacterizing — the contractor pays the use tax on materials, recovers the cost (including tax) in the contract price, and never collects sales tax from the customer.

### 4.3 Use tax on out-of-state materials

Materials purchased outside Illinois for use in an Illinois construction project are subject to **Illinois Use Tax** under **35 ILCS 105** at the combined state-plus-local rate that would have applied had the purchase occurred in Illinois at the point of delivery/use [76][82][86][88]. The contractor must file a Use Tax return reporting the liability. A credit is allowed for like sales taxes properly paid in the state of purchase under "like-tax-against-like-tax" reciprocity (state credited against state; local credited against local) [82][88].

The IDOR routinely audits use-tax compliance on out-of-state purchases for Illinois jobsites — steel fabricators, modular suppliers, and out-of-state specialty equipment installers are particular audit targets. The IDOR audit period is **3 years** under 35 ILCS 735/3-2, extended to **6 years** for substantial under-reporting and unlimited for fraud [82][88].

### 4.4 Sales-tax registration — IBT number required for all contractors

All Illinois construction contractors who purchase materials in Illinois (whether subject to sales-tax-paid-at-purchase or use-tax-self-remittance) should obtain an **Illinois Business Tax (IBT) number** from IDOR via the MyTax Illinois portal [82][86]. While a contractor that pays sales tax to suppliers and never resells need not file regular sales-tax returns, the IBT number is required for use-tax accruals on out-of-state purchases and for sales-tax exemption claims on materials that are not real-property improvements (e.g., reusable construction equipment, tools).

### 4.5 Exemptions — manufacturing, enterprise zones, high-impact business

Illinois has several construction-specific sales-tax exemptions that ContrPro contractors may encounter [82][89][90]:

- **Manufacturing machinery and equipment exemption** under 35 ILCS 120/2-5(14) — applies to machinery and equipment that becomes part of a manufacturing or assembling process; some related building materials qualify when installed as components of qualifying manufacturing facilities.
- **Enterprise Zone Building Materials Exemption** under 35 ILCS 120/5k — building materials physically incorporated into a building or structure within an Illinois Enterprise Zone are exempt from state sales tax (and from most local sales taxes) when the contractor obtains a Certificate of Eligibility for Sales Tax Exemption from the Enterprise Zone administrator [89][90].
- **High Impact Business Building Materials Exemption** under 35 ILCS 120/5l (and 86 Ill. Adm. Code § 130.1952) — building materials incorporated into real estate at a designated High Impact Business site are exempt from state and most local sales taxes.
- **River Edge Redevelopment Zone Building Materials Exemption** — analogous to the Enterprise Zone exemption for designated River Edge zones (Aurora, East St. Louis, Elgin, Peoria, Rockford).
- **Hospital, religious, school, governmental, and not-for-profit exemptions** — purchases by qualifying exempt entities are exempt; the contractor may purchase materials free of tax when acting under contract with such an entity and producing a valid IDOR exemption identification number.

The Enterprise Zone, High Impact Business, and River Edge exemptions are particularly valuable on large commercial and industrial projects within designated zones; a contractor that overlooks these exemptions effectively gives the IDOR 6.25%+ of project material costs unnecessarily.

### 4.6 Corporate income, replacement income, and net-worth taxes

**Corporate income tax — 35 ILCS 5/201** [91][92]:

- **Rate (TY 2025):** **7.0%** flat (corporate income tax base rate of 7.0%).
- **Plus Personal Property Replacement Income Tax (PPRT) — 35 ILCS 5/201(c):** **2.5%** for C-corporations. The combined corporate effective rate is **9.5%** on Illinois-apportioned income.
- **Pass-through entities (S-corp, partnership, LLC taxed as partnership):** Subject to PPRT at **1.5%**; underlying income flows to owners' personal returns at 4.95% personal rate.
- **Applies to:** Domestic and foreign corporations doing business in Illinois.

**Personal income tax — 35 ILCS 5/201(b):**

- **Rate (TY 2025):** **4.95%** flat for individuals (including pass-through partners, sole proprietors, and S-corp owners) [91][92].
- Illinois moved to a flat tax in 1969; voter referenda to adopt graduated brackets have been defeated, most recently in 2020.

**Net-worth/franchise tax — Illinois franchise tax (805 ILCS 5/15.35 and 5/15.65):**

- Illinois enacted a **phase-out** of the franchise tax beginning in 2020 under SB 689 (Public Act 101-0009). Effective for reports filed after December 31, 2023, the **first $30 million in liability** is exempt; the franchise tax is **eliminated entirely** for reports due after December 31, 2024 [93][94]. Illinois domestic and foreign corporations no longer owe franchise tax for years beginning January 1, 2025.

### 4.7 Withholding for nonresident contractors

Illinois does not impose a state-level mandatory withholding regime on payments to nonresident contractors comparable to Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 48-7-129 (2% on payments over $250,000). Out-of-state subs working on Illinois projects are subject to Illinois income tax on Illinois-source income (apportioned), and the prime contractor may be required to verify the out-of-state sub's Illinois nexus and tax compliance — but there is no automatic flat-percent withholding obligation imposed on the prime.

### 4.8 Chicago-specific taxes

Contractors operating in Chicago face several city-level taxes beyond the combined sales-tax base [80][81][95]:

- **Chicago Use Tax for Nontitled Personal Property** under Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 3-27 — applies to nontitled tangible personal property purchased outside Cook County and used in Chicago (a Chicago analog to state use tax with a 1.00% rate).
- **Chicago Lease/Transaction Tax** — applies to leases of personal property in Chicago at 9.0% (for cloud computing and software-as-a-service, the rate is 11.0% as of recent amendments). Tools and equipment rented for use on Chicago job sites can fall within the lease tax.
- **Chicago Restaurant Tax** at 0.50% applies to job-site catered meals over the relevant threshold; minor but cumulatively significant on long projects.
- **City of Chicago Real Property Transfer Tax** — applies to transfers; relevant to developer-contractor entities that take title to property pending construction.

## 5. Workers' compensation and labor

Illinois workers' compensation is summarized in §1.10 above. Additional Illinois-specific labor compliance items contractors must observe:

### 5.1 Prevailing wage — 820 ILCS 130 (Illinois Prevailing Wage Act)

The **Illinois Prevailing Wage Act, 820 ILCS 130**, requires payment of prevailing-wage rates to laborers, mechanics, and other workers on public-works projects [96][97][98]. The Act is administered by the **Illinois Department of Labor (IDOL)**.

**Coverage** [96][97]:

- All public-works contracts let by Illinois state agencies, local governments, public-school districts, public-university construction, park districts, and other public bodies.
- All construction trades: laborers, carpenters, electricians, ironworkers, masons, operating engineers, painters, plasterers, plumbers, roofers, sheet-metal workers, etc.

**Wage rates.** Prevailing wage rates are published monthly by the IDOL by county and by craft, and incorporate base wage plus fringe benefits (health, pension, training contributions). Contractors must pay both base and fringe — fringe may be paid into qualifying benefit plans or paid as additional cash wages.

**Certified payroll.** Every contractor on a covered public-works project must submit **certified payroll reports** monthly to the public body and to the IDOL via the IDOL Certified Transcript of Payroll portal [96][97][98].

**Penalties.** Violations are subject to **debarment** (1-year first offense; 5-year second offense), restitution to underpaid workers, civil penalties of **20% to 50%** of the wage underpayment, and personal liability for corporate officers who knowingly cause underpayment [96][97][98].

### 5.2 Illinois Wage Payment and Collection Act — 820 ILCS 115

The **Illinois Wage Payment and Collection Act (IWPCA), 820 ILCS 115**, protects employees on all Illinois worksites for unpaid wages, regardless of public or private context [99][100]. Key features:

- Wages must be paid at the **agreed regular intervals** (typically at least semi-monthly for non-executive employees).
- Final wages must be paid on the **next regular payday** after termination.
- **Personal liability** of corporate officers, directors, and agents who knowingly cause the corporation to fail to pay wages.
- Treble damages and attorneys' fees in successful actions.

The IWPCA is widely used in Illinois construction-payment disputes, particularly where small subs cannot meet the lien-act timing deadlines and instead seek personal recovery against the prime's corporate officers.

### 5.3 Day and Temporary Labor Services Act — 820 ILCS 175

Illinois amended the **Day and Temporary Labor Services Act (820 ILCS 175)** through Public Act 103-0437 (2023), expanding "labor broker" / temporary-staffing-firm regulation and imposing co-employer liability on contractors that engage staffing firms for site labor. Contractors using temp-staffing firms must verify the firm's IDOL registration and observe equal pay-for-equal-work rules (after 90 days of placement, temp workers must receive pay equivalent to that of the contractor's directly-employed workers in comparable jobs) [101][102].

## 6. Recent changes (2024–2026)

The Illinois General Assembly meets in two-year cycles. The current 104th General Assembly began January 8, 2025. Each cycle has produced consequential construction-law amendments.

### 6.1 Public Act 103-0252 — Local government retainage reform, effective January 1, 2024

**Public Act 103-0252**, signed by Governor JB Pritzker in 2023, amended **50 ILCS 505** to extend the 10%/5%/5% retainage cap to local government construction projects, effective **January 1, 2024** [62][66][67][68]. This was the second of three sequential retainage reforms (private 2019, local 2024, state 2027).

The reform applies to construction contracts entered into by counties, townships, municipalities, school districts, park districts, fire-protection districts, sanitary districts, and quasi-municipal bodies on or after **January 1, 2024**.

### 6.2 Public Act 103-0827 (HB 4660) — Mechanics lien notice modernization, effective January 1, 2025

**Public Act 103-0827 (HB 4660)**, signed by Governor JB Pritzker on **August 9, 2024**, amended **770 ILCS 60/24** to modernize the methods by which the subcontractor 90-day notice may be served, effective **January 1, 2025** [44][45][103].

Key changes:

1. **Nationally recognized commercial carriers (FedEx, UPS, DHL) with tracking** are now valid service methods, in addition to USPS registered/certified mail and personal service.
2. **No specific signature required** — digital tracking confirmation suffices as proof of delivery.
3. **Notice is legally served on the date of mailing or carrier-pickup**, not the date of arrival.

This amendment closes a recurring source of disputes: prior law's strict requirement of "registered or certified mail with return receipt requested" had been interpreted by some Illinois courts to invalidate notice served by FedEx Express, UPS Next Day, or similar carriers even where the carrier had a tracking number and a delivery confirmation. The 2025 amendment recognizes the modern reality of commercial delivery and reduces the trap for subs who used a carrier other than USPS [44][45][103].

### 6.3 Public Act 104-0427 (SB 2503) — Roofing Industry Licensing Act renewal, effective August 15, 2025

**Public Act 104-0427**, signed August 15, 2025, renewed the **Illinois Roofing Industry Licensing Act (225 ILCS 335)** through **January 1, 2031**, and raised the maximum civil penalty for unlicensed roofing to **$15,000 per violation** [5][6][7]. The renewal locks in the existing Limited / Unlimited tier structure and bond requirements ($10,000 / $25,000).

### 6.4 HB 1224 (104th General Assembly) — State agency retainage reform, effective June 1, 2027

**HB 1224**, signed by Governor JB Pritzker on **August 15, 2025**, extends the 10%/5%/5% retainage cap to most Illinois state agencies (excluding IDOT), effective **June 1, 2027** [68][71][72]. HB 1224 completes the trio of sequential retainage reforms (private 2019, local-government 2024, state agency 2027). The bill also requires non-exempt state agencies to **eliminate retainage** when "satisfactory progress" has been achieved, except on a project-specific basis where retention is warranted.

### 6.5 Public Act 103-0437 — Day and Temporary Labor Services Act expansion, effective in stages 2023–2024

**Public Act 103-0437**, signed in 2023, substantially expanded the Day and Temporary Labor Services Act (820 ILCS 175) [101][102]. Key changes affecting construction contractors:

- **90-day equal-pay rule:** Temp workers placed with a contractor for more than 90 calendar days must receive pay (including benefits) equivalent to that of the contractor's directly-employed workers in comparable roles.
- **Safety training:** Labor brokers must provide general safety training before placement; specific-jobsite training is the responsibility of the contracting end-user.
- **Co-employer liability:** Contractors that engage labor brokers share liability for wage-and-hour, OSHA, and IDOL claims.
- **Registration:** Labor brokers must hold a current IDOL Day and Temporary Labor Services registration; engaging an unregistered broker is itself a violation.

### 6.6 Bills proposed but not enacted (cross-reference)

A review of the 103rd and 104th General Assembly bill trackers identifies several construction-relevant bills that did not become law or remain pending:

- Proposed legislation to create a **statewide General Contractor License** has been introduced repeatedly but has not advanced — Illinois remains a no-statewide-GC-license state.
- Proposed legislation to create a **statewide Electrician License** has been similarly proposed but has not passed; electrical contracting remains municipally licensed.
- Proposed legislation to create a **statewide HVAC/Mechanical License** under IDFPR has been proposed in successive sessions; the most recent proposals (HB 2438 (102nd GA), SB 2459 (102nd GA)) did not pass.

The persistent failure of statewide-license bills reflects the political reality that Illinois's municipal-licensing patchwork is protected by local-government interest groups, the Chicago Department of Buildings, and the trade unions that control journeyman-card issuance in major cities.

### 6.7 Discrepancy resolutions

Several persistent secondary-source errors should be flagged:

- Some third-party "Illinois contractor licensing guides" continue to describe a statewide General Contractor license. **Illinois has no statewide General Contractor license** — licensure is by trade (plumbing and roofing only at the state level) and by municipality otherwise [1][2][3].
- Some commentary continues to report a **10%** retainage cap on private projects. **The CPPA caps retainage at 10% only before 50% completion**; the cap drops to **5%** at 50% completion and remains at 5% thereafter under Public Act 101-0383 (effective August 20, 2019) [62][64][65].
- Some commentary still reports a strict **USPS-only** service rule for the subcontractor 90-day notice. **Public Act 103-0827 (effective January 1, 2025)** added FedEx, UPS, and DHL as recognized service methods [44][45][103].
- Some Illinois plumbing-licensing summaries reference a "Master Plumber" state license. Illinois does **not** issue a separate "Master Plumber" — the state issues a Plumber license with a separate Plumbing Contractor registration; "Master Plumber" terminology in Illinois practice refers to the **Chicago** municipal credential, not a state license [4][14][27].
- Some references describe Illinois as a "Texas-style trust fund state" for construction payments. **Illinois has no construction trust fund statute analogous to Texas Chapter 162 or New York Lien Law Article 3-A.** Civil recovery for misapplied payments proceeds under common-law fraud, constructive trust, the lien act, the wage payment act, the ECA, or contract theories [56][57][58].

## 7. Practical compliance checklist for an Illinois contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**

- **Determine the project's municipal jurisdiction** and confirm that the contractor holds the appropriate municipal license:
  - Chicago — Class A through E GC license under Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 4-36; appropriate GL insurance per class ($5M for Class A; $3M Class B; $1M/$2M Class C/D; $1M Class E).
  - Other municipalities — confirm local contractor-registration requirements (Aurora, Naperville, Rockford, Joliet, Springfield, Peoria, Champaign-Urbana, etc., each operate independently).
- **Confirm the state trade licenses** for any self-performed plumbing or roofing scope:
  - Plumbing — IDPH Plumber license plus Plumbing Contractor registration; $20,000 bond; $100,000 public liability insurance.
  - Roofing — IDFPR Roofing license (Limited for 8-units-or-fewer residential; Unlimited for all); $10,000 or $25,000 bond; $250,000/$500,000 public liability / personal injury insurance.
- **Electrical / HVAC** — confirm the municipal licensure for the specific jurisdiction (e.g., Chicago Supervising Electrician + Electrical Contractor; Chicago Mechanical Contractor; Aurora trade licenses; etc.). Illinois has **no** state electrical or HVAC licensure.
- **Confirm workers' compensation coverage** for all employees; require COIs from every subcontractor before site access (the GC is statutory employer under 820 ILCS 305/1(a)(3) for any uninsured sub).
- **Verify Illinois Employee Classification Act compliance** — every site worker should either be W-2 employed by a sub entity or rigorously documented under the 820 ILCS 185 ABC test as an independent contractor.
- **Obtain or verify Illinois Business Tax (IBT) number** with IDOR; required for use-tax reporting on out-of-state material purchases.
- **For Chicago projects:** Verify Chicago Use Tax for Nontitled Personal Property and Chicago Lease Tax exposure if renting tools or equipment for the project.

**At project commencement:**

- **No Notice of Commencement requirement.** Unlike Georgia (which requires a 15-day NOC filing) or Florida (which requires NOC), Illinois does not require a Notice of Commencement on private projects. The sub-side 90-day notice procedure under 770 ILCS 60/24 operates without an NOC predicate.
- **Residential 770 ILCS 60/5(a) consumer notice** — the GC on owner-occupied residential work must furnish the owner a written notice of subcontractors' and suppliers' lien rights **before any payment is due**. Failure can bar GC lien recovery and trigger Consumer Fraud Act exposure.

**For subs and suppliers — within 90 days of last work or delivery:**

- **Serve the 90-day notice under 770 ILCS 60/24** on the owner and on the lender of record, by:
  - Registered or certified mail with return receipt requested; OR
  - Personal service; OR
  - **FedEx, UPS, or DHL with tracking** (recognized as of January 1, 2025 by Public Act 103-0827).
- **Notice is legally served on the date of mailing or carrier-pickup**, not the date of arrival. Document the mailing/pickup date with proof.
- **Including the sub in the GC's Sworn Statement under 770 ILCS 60/5** technically alleviates the 90-day notice obligation as to the owner, but the safer practice is to serve regardless — the sworn-statement coverage is frequently disputed at trial.

**During the work — payment cycle:**

- **Owner pays prime within 25 + 15 = 40 days** of properly submitted pay app on private projects (CPPA, 815 ILCS 603/10); **60 days** on state projects (30 ILCS 540); **30-day-approval + 60-day-pay** on local government projects (50 ILCS 505).
- **Prime pays subs within 15 days** of receipt of owner payment, less retainage, under § 603/15.
- **Interest at 10%/year** on late payments under CPPA; **1%/month** on late local-government payments under LGPPA.
- **Retainage capped at 10% before 50% complete; reduces to 5% at 50%; 5% maximum thereafter** for private projects (CPPA), local government projects (LGPPA effective January 1, 2024), and state agency projects (HB 1224 effective June 1, 2027). **Residential 12-units-or-fewer is exempt** from the CPPA retainage caps.

**Lien filing (against third parties — including lender):**

- **Within 4 months** of last day of furnishing labor or materials, file the **Claim of Lien** with the **recorder of deeds of the county** where the property is located (770 ILCS 60/7). This is the deadline for binding third parties.
- **Within 2 years** of last day of furnishing, the lien may still be filed and remain effective as against the **owner**, but is void as to third parties acquiring interests during the 4-month-to-2-year gap [42][43][48][49].
- **No tolling.** Warranty work, punch-list visits, and de minimis returns do **not** extend the 4-month clock; only new, distinct work under the original contract restarts it.

**Suit to enforce:**

- **Within 2 years** of last day of furnishing, commence suit to enforce the lien (770 ILCS 60/9). The 2-year deadline is jurisdictional and not subject to equitable tolling.
- **If owner serves a Section 34 demand to commence suit,** the lien claimant has **only 30 days** to file suit or forfeit the lien (770 ILCS 60/34).

**Public works:**

- **Bonding threshold:** $150,000 generally; $500,000 for IDOT/ISTHA (in effect until January 1, 2029).
- **Public works lien under 770 ILCS 60/23:** file with the public agency within 90 days of last work or delivery; lien attaches to funds in the public agency's hands.
- **Payment bond claim under Public Construction Bond Act (30 ILCS 550):** notice within **180 days** of last work; suit within **1 year**.
- **Prevailing wage and certified payroll:** required on all public-works projects under 820 ILCS 130; monthly certified payroll filings via IDOL portal.

**Conversion of payments and misapplication:**

- Illinois has **no dedicated construction trust fund statute**. Civil recovery for misapplied progress payments proceeds via:
  - Common-law fraud and constructive trust;
  - Wage Payment Act (820 ILCS 115) for unpaid worker wages, with personal-officer liability;
  - Employee Classification Act (820 ILCS 185) for misclassification penalties;
  - Theft by deception (720 ILCS 5/16-1) as a criminal alternative;
  - Consumer Fraud Act (815 ILCS 505) for residential consumers.
- The lien (770 ILCS 60) and the payment bond (30 ILCS 550) are the principal direct-recovery vehicles.

**Annual / biennial compliance:**

- **Illinois state sales/use tax** — file monthly (or quarterly for low-volume); pay sales tax on materials at the combined state-plus-local rate of the delivery/installation location; accrue and remit use tax on out-of-state purchases.
- **Illinois corporate income tax** — 7.0% corporate base + 2.5% PPRT = **9.5% effective combined** on C-corps; **4.95% personal** on pass-through income; PPRT 1.5% on pass-through entities.
- **Illinois franchise tax — eliminated effective for reports due after December 31, 2024** under Public Act 101-0009 (SB 689).
- **IDPH Plumber and Plumbing Contractor renewal** — annual; 4 hours of approved CE per year; bond and insurance maintained continuously.
- **IDFPR Roofing license renewal** — biennial; bond ($10,000 or $25,000) and insurance maintained continuously.
- **Chicago GC license renewal** — biennial; class-specific fees and insurance certificates on file.
- **Chicago Supervising Electrician, Electrical Contractor, Plumbing Contractor, Mechanical Contractor renewals** — biennial; CE recommended; bonds and insurance maintained continuously.
- **Workers' compensation policies** — verify currency on every sub's COI continuously; deny site access to subs without current COI showing WC.
- **Illinois Employee Classification Act compliance documentation** — maintain ABC-test paper trail for every 1099 site worker classification.
- **Prevailing wage certified payrolls** — monthly on every public-works project; IDOL portal submission.
