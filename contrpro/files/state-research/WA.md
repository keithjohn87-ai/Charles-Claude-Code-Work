## 1. Licensing — "Registration," Not Licensure

### 1.1 The Washington structure — RCW 18.27 (Contractor Registration Act)

Washington is a **registration** state, not a licensure state, for general and specialty contractors. This is a meaningful distinction. Under the **Contractor Registration Act, Chapter 18.27 RCW**, every person or entity that holds itself out to perform construction work for compensation in Washington must register with the **Department of Labor & Industries (L&I)** before submitting any bid, executing any contract, or performing any work [1][2][3][4]. There is **no statewide competency examination** for general contractor registration — unlike California, Florida, Georgia, Tennessee, or Texas, Washington does not test a registrant's trade knowledge as a precondition to issuing the credential. Registration is, in substance, a *financial-responsibility-and-identity* filing: surety bond on file, insurance on file, business name and ownership disclosed, UBI number obtained from the Department of Revenue, and workers' compensation account opened with L&I [1][2][5].

The practical consequence is that Washington's gatekeeping function for contractor competence sits on three other pillars instead of an exam:

1. The **surety bond** required by RCW 18.27.040 (which exists to pay claims by injured consumers, unpaid subcontractors, suppliers, and L&I prevailing-wage assessments) [1][6][7].
2. The **public liability and property damage insurance** required by RCW 18.27.050 (to cover bodily injury and property damage caused by the registrant's work) [1][8].
3. The **trade-specific licensure** layered on top of registration for electrical (RCW 19.28) and plumbing (RCW 18.106) work, which **are** examined trades administered by L&I as competency-based programs [9][10][11][12].

Because Washington's gatekeeping is financial-responsibility based, a Washington contractor who is "registered and in good standing" has merely cleared the financial hurdle — they have not been certified as competent at any trade. Owners, GCs, and bonding agents should not infer technical qualification from the registration record alone.

### 1.2 The two classifications — General Contractor and Specialty Contractor

RCW 18.27.010 establishes a **two-tier classification** [1][2][13]:

| Classification | Scope | Statutory authority |
|---|---|---|
| **General Contractor** | A contractor whose business operations require the use of more than two unrelated building trades or crafts for the performance of work | RCW 18.27.010(11) [1] |
| **Specialty Contractor** | A contractor whose operations as such do not fall within the foregoing definition of "general contractor" — i.e., a single-trade contractor | RCW 18.27.010(13) [1] |

A registrant must designate one classification at the time of application. A specialty registrant may not act as a general contractor; a general registrant may perform specialty work within its declared trades but, where the specialty is electrical or plumbing, the underlying RCW 19.28 (electrical) or RCW 18.106 (plumbing) license is still required regardless of the general-contractor registration [1][9][11].

**Practical implication for ContrPro.** A registrant's classification is recorded in the L&I contractor look-up database (the "Contractor Verify" tool at lni.wa.gov), which is the authoritative public-facing source for verification by owners, GCs, and lenders. ContrPro's onboarding should pull and persist the classification along with the registration number, bond status, and insurance status — not just the registration number — because mis-bidding work outside the declared classification is a registration-act violation under RCW 18.27.020 [1][2][14].

### 1.3 The surety bond — RCW 18.27.040 — the **HB 1534 increase**

For more than two decades, Washington's contractor bond was **$12,000 (general)** and **$6,000 (specialty)**. Those amounts had been set in 2001 and remained unchanged until the **2023 Legislature passed Engrossed Substitute House Bill 1534**, signed by Governor Inslee on May 1, 2023, raising the bond floors and creating a Homeowner Recovery Program. The contractor-bond provisions took effect **July 1, 2024**; the Homeowner Recovery Program takes effect **July 1, 2026** [6][7][15][16][17].

The current statutory bond requirement under **RCW 18.27.040(1)** is [1][6][7][15][16][17]:

| Classification | Bond amount (effective July 1, 2024) | Prior amount (2001 – June 30, 2024) |
|---|---|---|
| **General Contractor** | **$30,000** | $12,000 |
| **Specialty Contractor** | **$15,000** | $6,000 |

The bond must be a **continuous surety bond** to the State of Washington for the benefit of any person harmed by the registrant's breach of contract or violation of RCW 18.27, and for the benefit of L&I for any unpaid taxes, fees, or assessments. The bond is filed with L&I and remains in effect until canceled, with prepaid coverage required for renewal periods [1][6][7].

**Claim priority on the bond** (RCW 18.27.040(3)) [1]:

1. Residential homeowners with a contract directly with the registrant (first-priority claimants under the Homeowner Recovery Program changes).
2. Employees for unpaid wages.
3. L&I for unpaid prevailing-wage assessments, taxes, and penalties.
4. Subcontractors, materialmen, equipment lessors, and other claimants in the order their claims accrue.

**Claim window.** A claimant must bring suit on the bond within **one year** from the date the claimant ceased work or the date the registrant's registration was canceled, whichever occurs first (RCW 18.27.040(4)) [1].

**Bond aggregation.** The bond is a *pool*, not a per-claimant ceiling. If multiple claimants prevail and the aggregate of judgments exceeds the bond's face value, payments are prorated. The bond does not insulate the registrant from individual liability; a successful claimant whose recovery is prorated on the bond may still pursue the registrant directly for the unpaid balance [1][7][15].

**Cash deposit in lieu of bond.** RCW 18.27.040(7) permits a cash deposit or assignment of savings to the State of Washington in lieu of a surety bond, in the same amount. In practice, virtually all registrants use a commercial surety bond because the cash-deposit-in-lieu option ties up working capital for the duration of the registration plus the one-year tail [1][6][15].

**Bond cancellation triggers re-registration.** If a surety cancels the bond, L&I sends a 90-day cancellation notice. The registrant must replace the bond within the cancellation period or registration is **automatically suspended**, and any contracting performed thereafter is unregistered contracting subject to RCW 18.27.020 penalties [1][2][14].

### 1.4 Public liability and property damage insurance — RCW 18.27.050

Beyond the bond, every registrant must maintain **public liability and property damage insurance**, with proof on file at L&I, in **minimum** amounts of [1][8]:

| Coverage | Minimum |
|---|---|
| **Property damage** (per occurrence) | **$50,000** |
| **Personal injury — one person** | **$100,000** |
| **Personal injury — two or more persons (per occurrence)** | **$200,000** |

The certificate of insurance must name the State of Washington, Department of Labor & Industries, as the certificate holder. Lapse of coverage is grounds for suspension. **These are floors, not market norms.** A general contractor bidding commercial work in Seattle, Tacoma, or Spokane will typically carry $1M–$5M per-occurrence general liability with $2M–$10M aggregate; bidding requirements on commercial work routinely exceed the RCW 18.27.050 statutory minimum by an order of magnitude [1][8].

The statutory minimums are a *registration floor*, not a *prudence floor*. ContrPro should communicate this distinction to users: clearing the RCW 18.27.050 minimum does **not** mean an owner or GC will accept the certificate.

### 1.5 Workers' compensation — the State Fund (RCW Title 51)

Washington is one of only four monopolistic-state workers'-compensation jurisdictions in the United States (alongside North Dakota, Ohio, and Wyoming). Under **RCW Title 51 (Industrial Insurance)** and administered by **L&I's Industrial Insurance Division**, every Washington employer must either pay premiums into the **State Fund** or, if eligible, **self-insure** with L&I approval under RCW 51.14 [18][19][20][21][22]. **Private workers' compensation insurance is not lawful** in Washington for the standard employer market — the State Fund is the only insurer (and self-insurance, the only alternative, requires audited financial capacity that typically excludes all but the largest employers and is rarely used by general or specialty construction contractors).

**Mandatory participation for construction.** RCW 51.12.020 catalogs the narrow exemptions from mandatory coverage; no exemption applies to commercial or residential construction work performed by a registered contractor. Every employee of a Washington construction contractor — including casual labor — must be covered under the State Fund (or under an approved self-insurance plan) [18][19][22].

**Premium rates.** Premiums are computed **per-hour-worked** by class code (a Washington-specific approach, in contrast to the per-$100-of-payroll convention used in private-insurance states like Texas, Florida, and Georgia). Construction-trade hourly rates are among the highest in the State Fund schedule. As examples of the order of magnitude:

- **Construction labor — framing carpentry (class 0510)** and similar carpentry trades: **typical 2025 composite rate in the $1.50–$2.20 per hour worked range**, with rate variation by experience modifier and retrospective rating election [20][21][23].
- **Roofing (class 0507)**: among the highest construction rates, **frequently above $4.00 per hour worked**.
- **Plumbing (class 0306)**: composite rates frequently in the **$1.10–$1.70 per hour** range.
- **Electrical (class 0601 inside / 0602 outside)**: composite rates often in the **$0.50–$1.20 per hour** range; outside-line electrical is materially higher than inside-residential.

L&I publishes the **Schedule of Premium Rates** annually under WAC 296-17 and WAC 296-17A. Composite rate = base rate + supplemental pension fund + supplemental medical-aid fund, with an employee deduction portion permitted under RCW 51.32.073 [20][21][23]. The rates above are *order-of-magnitude examples for context only*; the controlling rate for any specific risk class is the rate published by L&I for the current fiscal year.

**Owner / officer / partner exemption.** Sole proprietors, partners in a partnership, members of an LLC, and corporate officers may elect to **exempt themselves** from mandatory coverage under RCW 51.12.020 and WAC 296-17-31022 (an "owner-elected coverage" exemption). The election is filed with L&I; exempt owners forgo State Fund benefits (no time-loss compensation, no medical coverage through the State Fund) and must understand they are uninsured against on-the-job injury unless they carry private accident or disability insurance [18][22][24]. The exemption is widely used by single-owner specialty registrants but does **not** extend to any employees — every non-owner employee must still be reported and premiums paid [18][22][24].

**Quarterly reporting.** Every covered employer must file a **Quarterly Report** with L&I (Form F212-055-000) reporting hours worked by class code and remitting premium. Late filings carry penalties of $10 plus 1% of premium per month (RCW 51.48.150). Audit underreporting can result in retroactive premium assessment for up to **three years** plus penalties up to **100% of the underreported premium** under RCW 51.48.103 [22][25].

**Independent contractor / personal-labor test.** L&I applies a stringent **six-part personal-labor test** under RCW 51.08.180 and RCW 51.08.195 to determine whether a worker is an "employee" for State Fund premium purposes regardless of how the parties have characterized the relationship. Failing the test results in retroactive premium assessment — a frequent and expensive surprise for construction registrants who use "1099 subs" without satisfying every prong [22][26]. The six prongs require, in summary: (1) the worker is free from control over the manner of performance; (2) the work is outside the usual course of the principal's business or performed outside the principal's place of business; (3) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established business of the same nature; plus a parallel three-prong test under RCW 51.08.195 for residential remodeling. Failing any one prong defaults the worker into employee status. Washington's test is materially stricter than the federal IRS twenty-factor / common-law test [22][26].

**Certificate of Coverage.** L&I issues a Certificate of Coverage to the registrant upon proof of State Fund account and current premium payment. The certificate is required documentation on every L&I prevailing-wage public-works project (RCW 39.12) and on every project requiring a building permit in most Washington jurisdictions [11][27][28].

### 1.6 The Homeowner Recovery Program — effective July 1, 2026 (HB 1534, 2023)

The 2023 Legislature created a new **Homeowner Recovery Program** within L&I, codified primarily in amendments to RCW 18.27 and new sections of Chapter 18.27 RCW, effective **July 1, 2026** [15][16][17]. The program creates a pool funded by an additional surcharge on contractor registration fees from which qualifying homeowners may recover for financial losses caused by a registered contractor's work on the homeowner's primary residence, in cases where:

1. The homeowner has obtained a judgment against the contractor; and
2. The homeowner has been unable to collect on the judgment or on the contractor's RCW 18.27.040 surety bond; and
3. The work was on the homeowner's primary residence; and
4. The application is filed within the statutory window after the failure to collect.

The program functions as a **state-administered last-resort recovery fund**, analogous in concept to California's Contractor's State License Board Recovery Fund (Business & Professions Code §7071.11) and Maryland's Home Improvement Guaranty Fund, but with a Washington-specific funding mechanism (registration-fee surcharge) and a residential-only scope [15][16][17][29]. L&I rulemaking for program administration was finalized in early 2026 under WAC 296-200A (new program rules); the per-claim recovery cap and per-fund-period aggregate limit are set by rule under L&I's RCW 18.27 rulemaking authority.

**Practical implication.** Beginning July 1, 2026, Washington registered contractors face a **second-tier financial backstop** beyond their RCW 18.27.040 bond and RCW 18.27.050 insurance — the registration-fee surcharge that funds the Homeowner Recovery Program. ContrPro should treat the surcharge as part of total cost-of-registration in any "what does it cost to operate in Washington" calculation.

### 1.7 Registration term, fees, and renewal

Registrations under RCW 18.27 are **two-year** credentials [1][5]. Renewal is biennial and must be accompanied by:

1. **Continuous proof of bond** on file (RCW 18.27.040).
2. **Continuous proof of insurance** on file (RCW 18.27.050).
3. **UBI number** in good standing with the Department of Revenue (the Unified Business Identifier; obtained at business formation through the Department of Revenue's Business Licensing Service).
4. **L&I workers' compensation account in good standing** (RCW Title 51).
5. **Payment of the registration fee** (RCW 18.27.075 — fee set by rule; L&I adopted a 6.51% fee increase effective in 2024 across the Contractor Registration, Electrical, Elevator, Factory Assembled Structures, and Plumber programs).

If the registrant lapses for **more than 30 days** without renewal, the registration is canceled and the registrant must re-apply (which under post-July-1-2024 rules requires the new $30,000 / $15,000 bond level) [1][5][17].

### 1.8 Disclosure statement — RCW 18.27.114 — residential 1-4 unit projects

Under **RCW 18.27.114**, before commencing residential repair, alteration, or construction on **four or fewer dwelling units**, or commercial work where the bid or contract price is **$1,000 or more but less than $60,000**, the registered contractor must furnish the customer with a **Model Disclosure Statement** drafted by L&I [30][31]. The statement explains the bond's role and limits, the customer's lien rights and recourse, and recommends verifying contractor registration at lni.wa.gov.

The statutory threshold is **$1,000**. Below $1,000, no disclosure is required; at or above $1,000, the disclosure is mandatory for residential 1-4-unit projects [30][31]. The contractor must retain a signed copy of the disclosure statement for a minimum of **three years** and produce it on L&I request [30][31].

The disclosure form contains the statutory caution: "THIS BOND OR DEPOSIT MIGHT NOT BE SUFFICIENT TO COVER A CLAIM THAT MIGHT ARISE FROM THE WORK DONE UNDER YOUR CONTRACT" — a clear warning that the RCW 18.27.040 bond is a *minimum financial-responsibility instrument*, not a guarantee of full recovery [30][31]. **Failure to deliver the disclosure** does not by itself void the contract, but it forfeits the contractor's right to recover **attorney's fees and costs** in any subsequent collection action against the customer [30][31]. On a residential remodel that ends in litigation, this is a meaningful operational sanction.

### 1.9 Penalties for unregistered contracting — RCW 18.27.020 and RCW 18.27.340

Performing work that requires RCW 18.27 registration **without being registered** is a violation of RCW 18.27.020. The statute provides [1][2][14]:

1. **First offense** — an **infraction** under RCW 18.27.200 with a base penalty of **$1,000**, plus L&I administrative costs and any consumer-restitution component.
2. **Subsequent offenses, or any offense committed while the contractor's registration is suspended or revoked** — a **gross misdemeanor**, with each day of work and each worksite treated as a separate offense. Under Washington's gross-misdemeanor sentencing (RCW 9A.20.021), each count carries up to **364 days in jail and a $5,000 fine**. The "separate offense per day" and "separate offense per worksite" multipliers make sustained unregistered contracting an extremely expensive risk [1][14].
3. **Unenforceability of contract for collection.** Under **RCW 18.27.080**, an unregistered contractor may not maintain any action in any court of Washington to collect compensation for unregistered work [1][32][33]. The contractor must plead and prove registration at the **time of contract**, **at the time the work was performed**, and **at the time the action is commenced** [32][33]. This is a powerful affirmative defense for owners and GCs against unregistered claimants — analogous in concept to California's B&P §7031 bar, though Washington's RCW 18.27.080 bar is somewhat narrower (it bars *suit*, not recovery of any funds already paid). Washington courts have repeatedly enforced RCW 18.27.080 strictly; lapses of registration during the contract performance, even brief ones, have caused contractors to forfeit suit rights [32][33][34].
4. **Anti-collusion** — RCW 18.27.030 makes it unlawful for any person to advertise, offer to act, or act as a contractor without being registered. Yellow-page, online, and yard-sign advertising by an unregistered contractor is itself an actionable violation, independent of any consummated contract [1][2].

The combination of (1) infraction-then-misdemeanor escalation, (2) per-day-per-worksite multipliers, and (3) the RCW 18.27.080 suit bar makes Washington's unregistered-contracting penalties among the more aggressive enforcement regimes in the country.

### 1.10 Trade-specific licensure overview — electrical and plumbing are real exams

Even with a valid RCW 18.27 registration, a contractor performing **electrical** or **plumbing** work must hold the underlying trade license issued by L&I as a competency-based credential. These are *real exams*, not just financial filings [9][10][11][12].

| Trade | Statutory authority | Issuing authority | Examination required? | Statutory minimum experience |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Electrical** | RCW 19.28, WAC 296-46B | L&I Electrical Program | **Yes** — Journey-Level (01) and Specialty Electrician exams plus Administrator exam | Journey-Level: 8,000 hours under journey-level supervision (≈ 4 years) per RCW 19.28.191 [9][10] |
| **Plumbing** | RCW 18.106, WAC 296-400A | L&I Plumber Program | **Yes** — Journey Plumber and Specialty Plumber exams | Journey: typically 6,000–8,000 hours of qualifying work plus four years' classroom and on-the-job training [11][12] |
| **HVAC / refrigeration** | Generally **not statewide-licensed**; covered by RCW 19.28 if the work involves electrical | L&I Electrical Program, locally for mechanical permits | Electrical portion only | Same as electrical for the electrical scope |
| **Boilers / pressure systems** | RCW 70.79, WAC 296-104 | L&I Boiler Program | Boiler operator certifications | Varies by class |

Two operational consequences for ContrPro:

1. **An RCW 18.27 general or specialty registration is *not* a license to do electrical or plumbing work.** A registered general contractor self-performing wiring or piping without the underlying RCW 19.28 or RCW 18.106 credential is in violation of those chapters, with separate civil and criminal penalties [9][10][11][12].
2. **The L&I look-up tools** for the contractor registry, the electrical license registry, and the plumber license registry are *three separate databases*. ContrPro's verification workflow for an HVAC-electrical-plumbing-multi-trade GC will need to query all three (or use the L&I combined look-up that surfaces all credentials by business name) to confirm full credentialing across declared scopes.

### 1.11 Electrical licensure — RCW 19.28 — the load-bearing detail

Washington's electrical licensure regime under **RCW 19.28** and **WAC 296-46B** is one of the more developed in the country, structured around three credential tiers [9][10][35][36]:

1. **Electrical Contractor License** — issued to a *firm* (sole proprietor, partnership, LLC, corporation) that engages in the business of electrical contracting. Required under RCW 19.28.041.
   - **Bond:** $4,000 in favor of the State of Washington, conditioned on faithful compliance with RCW 19.28 (RCW 19.28.041(3)).
   - **Insurance:** $50,000 property damage and $200,000 personal injury minimums.
   - **Administrator:** Every Electrical Contractor License must designate a certified **Electrical Administrator** (RCW 19.28.061) who is responsible for ensuring code compliance and signing electrical work permits. The administrator must hold a current administrator certificate, which requires passing the Administrator Examination (a code-and-business exam administered by L&I).
2. **Journey-Level Electrician (01) Certificate of Competency** — issued to an *individual* under RCW 19.28.191. Eligibility requires:
   - **8,000 hours** of work experience under journey-level supervision (≈ four years), of which a substantial portion must be in commercial / industrial settings; or
   - Completion of an approved apprenticeship program; and
   - Passage of the Journey-Level (01) examination administered by L&I.
3. **Specialty Electrician Certificates** — RCW 19.28.211 — including but not limited to: Residential (02), Pump and Irrigation (03), Sign (04), Limited Energy (06), Nonresidential Maintenance (07), Restricted Industrial (08), Appliance Repair (09). Each has its own work-hour eligibility and examination requirement, generally 2,000–4,000 hours of qualifying experience.

**Apprentice / trainee.** RCW 19.28.161 establishes the trainee (apprentice) framework — a person learning the trade may work only under direct supervision of a journey-level electrician, with worker-ratio limits (typically 1:1 trainee-to-journey on the same crew).

**Continuing education.** Journey-level (01) electricians must complete **24 hours of approved continuing education every three years** under WAC 296-46B-940, of which at least 8 hours must be on the National Electrical Code (current adopted edition).

**EV infrastructure provisions.** Recent amendments to RCW 19.28 (RCW 19.28.281 and 19.28.285) authorize specific rulemaking for electric vehicle supply equipment installation and certification — a 2020s-era policy overlay reflecting Washington's transportation electrification goals [9][10].

**Penalties.** Performing electrical work without the required credential is a violation subject to L&I citations starting at **$500 per first offense** and escalating to **$5,000+** for repeat or willful violations; severe willful violations may trigger criminal prosecution and revocation of registration. Worked permits issued in the absence of credentials are voidable, and certificates of occupancy can be withheld [9][10].

### 1.12 Plumbing licensure — RCW 18.106 — competency-based

Washington's plumbing licensure under **RCW 18.106** is administered by L&I's Plumber Program. Three credential tiers exist [11][12]:

1. **Plumber Trainee** — entry-level credential allowing the holder to work under direct supervision of a Journey Plumber. RCW 18.106.020.
2. **Journey Plumber Certificate of Competency** — RCW 18.106.040, .050. Eligibility requires substantial qualifying experience (typically 6,000–8,000 hours over four years) under journey-plumber supervision, completion of an approved apprenticeship or equivalent training, and passage of the Journey Plumber examination administered by L&I.
3. **Specialty Plumber** — limited-scope credentials including:
   - **Residential Service Plumber** — limited to residential service work.
   - **Backflow Assembly Tester (BAT)** — limited to backflow-prevention-assembly testing under RCW 18.106.310 and WAC 246-290-490, jointly administered with the Department of Health's drinking-water program.
   - **Medical Gas Piping Installer Endorsement** — RCW 18.106.075, an add-on endorsement on a Journey Plumber or specialty plumber credential for installation of medical gas systems under NFPA 99.

**Continuing education.** Plumbers must complete **16 hours of approved continuing education per three-year renewal period** under WAC 296-400A.

**Penalties.** Performing plumbing work without the required certificate is an L&I citation matter starting at **$500 per first offense** and escalating; willful or repeat violations carry escalating civil penalties and, in egregious cases, criminal prosecution under RCW 18.106.180 [11][12].

### 1.13 Local jurisdiction overlay — Seattle, Tacoma, and others

Although Washington's contractor regime is largely state-administered (registration + trade licensure + State Fund WC, all through L&I), several Washington cities impose their own **building-permit, business-license, and contractor-disclosure** requirements:

1. **City of Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections (SDCI)** issues building permits and enforces the Seattle Building Code (a Washington-amended version of the IBC with Seattle Amendments). Seattle requires a **City of Seattle Business License** for any contractor performing work in the city limits; the business license tax is separate from the state B&O tax and is administered by Seattle's Department of Finance and Administrative Services.
2. **City of Tacoma Planning and Development Services** similarly issues building permits and imposes a Tacoma business license requirement. Tacoma also imposes a city B&O tax with rates separate from (and additive to) the state B&O.
3. **Smaller jurisdictions** (Bellevue, Spokane, Vancouver, Everett, Olympia, Renton, Kent, Yakima, Tri-Cities) generally rely on the state RCW 18.27 registration for contractor qualification but issue their own building permits and business licenses.

Because Washington does **not** preempt local business-license or B&O regimes (unlike states that occupy the field entirely), a contractor working in Seattle, Tacoma, Bellevue, and Spokane on the same job year may owe city-level B&O and business-license fees in **all four cities** plus the state B&O on the consolidated activity. ContrPro should treat the city-level business-license-and-B&O layer as a real operational cost in the Washington geographic footprint.

## 2. Mechanics' Liens — RCW 60.04

Washington's lien law sits in **Chapter 60.04 RCW (Mechanics' and Materialmen's Liens)**. It is a comprehensive, codified regime with five load-bearing deadlines, an unusually intricate pre-claim notice, a fast 90-day filing window, an eight-month foreclosure window, and a well-developed bonding-off mechanism [37][38][39][40][41][42].

### 2.1 Who has lien rights — RCW 60.04.021

Under **RCW 60.04.021**, every person furnishing **labor**, **professional services**, **materials**, or **equipment** for the improvement of real property at the instance of the owner or the owner's agent or construction agent has a lien upon the improvement [37][38][43]. "Person" is defined broadly to include suppliers, equipment lessors, registered architects and engineers (RCW 18.43 / 18.08), and laborers. "Improvement" is broadly defined in RCW 60.04.011(5) and includes constructing, altering, repairing, remodeling, demolishing, clearing, grading, filling, or landscaping.

**Pre-condition: registration.** Under **RCW 60.04.011(11)**, a "potential lien claimant" must be **registered or licensed if registration or licensure is required** under Washington law. A contractor who is not registered under RCW 18.27 (or, where applicable, not licensed under RCW 19.28 or RCW 18.106) **has no lien rights** for the unregistered work [37][43][44]. This is one of the strongest single integrations between Washington's registration regime and its lien regime: lapsing registration during work doesn't merely expose the contractor to the RCW 18.27.080 suit bar — it strips lien rights as well.

### 2.2 Pre-claim notice — RCW 60.04.031 — the 10-day-vs-60-day distinction

Washington's pre-claim notice rule (the "Notice to Owner" / "Notice of Right to Claim Lien") under **RCW 60.04.031** is one of the more nuanced in the country [37][41][45][46][47]. Two timing rules apply, depending on the project type and the claimant's role:

**Rule 1 — New single-family residence:** The pre-claim notice, when timely given, **only protects the right to claim a lien for labor, professional services, materials, or equipment supplied after a date that is *ten days* before the notice is given** [37][45][46][47]. The 10-day "look-back" on a new single-family residence is unusual; in effect, the lien claimant on a new home build has only ten days of retrospective lien protection from the moment notice is delivered.

**Rule 2 — All other projects (commercial, multi-family, repair, remodel):** The notice protects a lien for **labor, professional services, materials, or equipment furnished after a date that is *sixty days* before the notice is given** to the owner [37][45][46][47]. This is the standard 60-day look-back found in the typical notice regime.

**Who must give notice:** Every potential lien claimant **except**:

1. The prime contractor (a contractor contracting directly with the owner) [37][45].
2. A laborer performing only labor (no materials, no equipment, no professional services) [37][45].
3. A subcontractor who has a contract directly with the prime contractor and whose work is on a residential repair or alteration on the owner's primary residence (subject to limited exceptions) [37][45].
4. A supplier whose total contract value with the contractor is less than the de minimis threshold (rarely triggers in practice).

**Method of delivery:** Certified or registered mail to the owner or reputed owner, with proof of mailing; **or** personal delivery with documented evidence of delivery [37][45][46]. Regular first-class mail is **not** sufficient. The notice must be in the statutory form (RCW 60.04.031(5)) — a particular notice to owner that mirrors the form prescribed by statute.

**Residential repair-or-remodel limitation — RCW 60.04.031(3):** For repair, alteration, or construction of an owner-occupied **single-family residence**, any lien by a person not contracting directly with the owner is **limited to amounts not yet paid to the prime contractor by the owner at the time the notice was received**. Translation: even with perfect notice, a subcontractor's residential remodel lien cannot reach owner funds that have already flowed downstream to the prime; the homeowner cannot be made to pay twice [37][45][46].

This is a significant departure from the lien rules in California, Texas, and New York, all of which permit subcontractor liens to reach the owner regardless of disbursement timing. A Washington residential subcontractor must therefore time the pre-claim notice not just to capture the lookback window but to **interrupt the owner-to-prime payment flow before draws have left the owner's hands**. Failure to do so is functionally fatal to the lien on a fast-paying job.

### 2.3 Claim of lien — RCW 60.04.091 — the 90-day filing deadline

A registered lien claimant must **record** a **Notice of Claim of Lien** in the county where the property is located **not later than 90 days after the claimant ceased to furnish labor, professional services, materials, or equipment** [37][39][40][42][48]. The 90-day window is a strict period of limitation: **"no action to foreclose a lien shall be maintained unless the claim of lien is filed for recording within the ninety-day period stated"** [37][48].

**Contents** of the Notice of Claim of Lien — RCW 60.04.091 [37][48]:

1. Claimant's name, phone number, and address.
2. First and last dates labor, services, materials, or equipment were furnished.
3. Name of the person owing payment.
4. Property's street address, legal description, or other identifying information.
5. Owner's name (or statement that the name is unknown).
6. Principal amount claimed.
7. **Signature under penalty of perjury** and acknowledgment under RCW 64.08.

**Service on the owner — 14-day rule.** Within **14 days of filing**, the claimant must serve a copy of the claim of lien on the owner or reputed owner, by certified/registered mail or personal service. **Failure to serve within 14 days forfeits the right to recover attorney's fees and costs** in the foreclosure action [37][39][48]. The deadline does not invalidate the lien itself, but it converts a fee-shifting lien into a cost-bearing one. In a contested lien foreclosure, that difference can be the entire economic case.

**No on-the-clock for change orders.** Washington treats "ceased to furnish" strictly. If the contract ended on April 1 and a punch-list visit occurred on April 25, the cease-date is *April 25*. But "trivial" work — particularly work undertaken purely to revive the 90-day clock — does not restart the period. Washington courts have rejected "warranty visit" liens where the work was not bona fide part of the contract scope [37][39][41][42].

**Practical implication for ContrPro:** The lien deadline calendar should run from the **last calendar day of bona fide contract work** at each project, not the last invoice date. ContrPro's project closeout module should prompt the user to confirm the actual last-work date and trigger a 90-day countdown for lien-claim consideration.

### 2.4 Foreclosure deadline — RCW 60.04.141 — 8 months

Under **RCW 60.04.141**, a lien is valid for **eight calendar months after the claim of lien has been recorded** unless an action to foreclose is filed in the superior court of the county where the property is located within that eight-month period [37][49][50]. The statute is direct: "*No lien created by this chapter binds the property subject to the lien for a longer period than eight calendar months after the claim of lien has been recorded unless an action is filed by the lien claimant within that time*" [37][49].

**Additional rules of the foreclosure period** [37][49]:

1. **Service on the owner within 90 days of filing the action.** The lien claimant must obtain service of summons and complaint on the owner within 90 days of filing the foreclosure action. Failure to do so means the action is treated as not commenced for lien-preservation purposes — and if the 8-month period expires in the interim, the lien dies on the vine. This is a frequent malpractice trap.
2. **Two-year want-of-prosecution dismissal.** If the foreclosure action is filed but not prosecuted to judgment within two years of commencement, the court may dismiss for want of prosecution. Dismissal cancels the lien.
3. **Credit extension exception.** If credit was extended and the terms are stated *in the claim of lien itself*, the eight-month period runs from the expiration of the credit, not from the recording date.

**Practical implication.** Washington's combined 90-day claim window plus 8-month foreclosure window is on the **fast** end of the national lien-deadline distribution. A claimant who recorded a lien on January 15 must commence foreclosure by **September 15** and obtain service by **December 14** (90 days after filing). ContrPro should track three dates per lien: (i) record date, (ii) record-date + 8 months for foreclosure, (iii) foreclosure-filing date + 90 days for service. Missing any of the three forfeits the lien.

### 2.5 Bonding off the lien — RCW 60.04.161

A property owner or interested party may **bond off** a recorded lien under **RCW 60.04.161** by recording a bond in an amount equal to **1.5 times the lien amount (or $5,000, whichever is greater)** [37][51]. The bond is conditioned on payment of any amount the claimant ultimately recovers and operates as a substitution: the bond replaces the property as security; the lien is released as a cloud on title; the foreclosure action continues against the bond rather than against the real property.

The 1.5x multiplier matches the multiplier used in most western states (e.g., Idaho, Oregon) but is lower than Illinois' 175% or California's 125%. The economic effect: a $100,000 lien costs the owner approximately $1,500–$3,000 in surety premium to bond off (rates vary), which is often the cheapest path to lifting a lien-induced title cloud on a refinance or sale.

### 2.6 Frivolous lien — RCW 60.04.081 — fast-track relief

Washington provides a **fast-track procedure** under **RCW 60.04.081** for an owner or interested party to obtain release of a **frivolous or clearly excessive** lien [37][52]. The procedure: file an application in superior court; the court must conduct a hearing within **14 days** of filing; if the court finds the lien is frivolous (made without reasonable cause) or clearly excessive in amount, the court orders release of the lien and may award the prevailing party reasonable attorney's fees and costs [37][52].

The standard is reasonably high — "frivolous" means no reasonable basis in fact or law — and the procedure is not a substitute for a full merits trial of the lien claim. But for clearly defective liens (e.g., filed by an unregistered contractor, filed after the 90-day deadline, filed for amounts that grossly exceed any plausible contract value), the RCW 60.04.081 procedure is fast and effective.

**Practical implication for contractors.** Washington's frivolous-lien procedure is a meaningful disincentive against weak liens. Filing a $50,000 lien on a $10,000 contract dispute exposes the claimant to attorney's-fee liability under RCW 60.04.081, which can rapidly exceed any plausible recovery. The remedy reinforces the discipline of accurate lien amounts.

### 2.7 Lien waivers — RCW 60.04.071

Washington permits lien waivers under **RCW 60.04.071** but treats them strictly. A waiver of lien rights is unenforceable to the extent of unpaid sums and is generally enforceable only with respect to amounts actually paid. Pre-payment waivers (releases conditional on receipt of payment) are common practice on public and large private projects [37][53].

Washington has **no statutory waiver form** — unlike California (Civil Code §8132 et seq.) or Texas (Property Code §53.281 et seq.) where the legislature has prescribed exact waiver forms. The result is contract-driven: waiver forms vary by GC, lender, and owner, and a Washington subcontractor's lien-waiver discipline depends entirely on internal review at the time of signing. ContrPro should flag lien-waiver-form review as a discrete onboarding item for Washington subs and suppliers.

### 2.8 Public-works retainage and bonds — RCW 60.28 and RCW 39.08

Liens **cannot attach to public property** in Washington (a uniform rule across all states). Instead, public-works claimants are protected by two parallel mechanisms:

1. **Retainage** — **RCW 60.28.011** — discussed in detail in §3 below — the public body retains up to 5% of the contract sum as a fund against which lien-style claims may be asserted.
2. **Payment-and-performance bond** — **Chapter 39.08 RCW** — every public works contract over a threshold value (currently $35,000 for state agencies and most municipalities, with some variation by entity type) must be bonded for **100% of the contract price** by the prime contractor, conditioned on payment of subcontractors and suppliers. Public-works claimants assert claims on the **payment bond** rather than (or in parallel with) the retainage [54][55].

The two-track public-works protection means a subcontractor or supplier on a Washington public job has a **bond claim under RCW 39.08** plus a **retainage claim under RCW 60.28**, both with their own filing deadlines: payment bond claims must be filed within **30 days after acceptance** of the public work (RCW 39.08.030), and retainage claims must be filed within **45 days after acceptance** (RCW 60.28.021). Missing either deadline forfeits that protection track [54][55].

## 3. Prompt Payment and Retainage

### 3.1 Public-works retainage — RCW 60.28.011

Washington imposes a statutory **5% retainage maximum** on public-improvement contracts under **RCW 60.28.011(1)(a)**: the public body retains "**not to exceed five percent of the moneys earned by the contractor**" as a trust fund for the payment of subcontractor, supplier, and tax claims [56][57][58]. The 5% retainage cap is among the more contractor-favorable in the country — many states permit up to 10% retainage on public works, and federal procurement under the FAR permits retainage at the contracting officer's discretion (typically up to 10%).

**Retainage release — RCW 60.28.011(3)** [56][57]:

1. **Contractor request after completion of work other than landscaping** (RCW 60.28.011(3)(a)): The contractor may request release after substantial completion; the public body must release upon completion of statutory release procedures.
2. **Mandatory release 60 days after completion** (RCW 60.28.011(3)(b)): "Sixty days after completion of all contract work the public body must release and pay in full the amounts retained" [56][57]. The 60-day window assumes no unresolved claims; outstanding lien-style claims under RCW 60.28.021 or unpaid tax warrants stay the release until resolved.

**Bond in lieu of retainage — RCW 60.28.011(6).** The contractor may submit a **retainage bond** in form acceptable to the public body in lieu of cash retainage; the public body **must accept** the bond unless it can demonstrate good cause for refusal or the bond is commercially unavailable [56][57]. This option lets the prime contractor get the 5% retainage off the books (improving working-capital cash flow) at the cost of paying a small surety premium on the bond. For larger contractors on multiple public jobs simultaneously, the retainage-bond option is essentially universal.

**Federal-transportation-funded contracts.** Under RCW 60.28.011(1)(b), public-improvement contracts funded by federal transportation funds use the **contract bond under Chapter 39.08 RCW** for claims protection in lieu of retainage. This aligns Washington with federal-aid highway practice where retainage is generally not permitted.

**Foreclosure of retainage claims — RCW 60.28.030.** Subcontractors and suppliers asserting claims against the retainage must file a notice of claim with the public body within 45 days after acceptance of the public work (RCW 60.28.021) and commence an action to foreclose within four months. Failure to comply forfeits the retainage claim, though the bond claim under RCW 39.08 may still be available.

### 3.2 Private-works prompt payment — practice and contract

Washington does **not** have a comprehensive private-works prompt-payment statute analogous to Texas Property Code §28 or Illinois 815 ILCS 603. The closest provisions are:

1. **RCW 39.04.250** — public-works prompt payment from contractor to subcontractor: when a contractor or subcontractor receives payment on a public works contract, the contractor or subcontractor must pay each subcontractor **within 10 days of receipt** to the extent of the subcontractor's interest in the payment, less amounts withheld for disputes (up to 150% of the disputed amount may be withheld). Wrongful withholding is subject to interest at the statutory rate and reasonable attorney's fees [59][60]. **This is a public-works statute, not a private-works statute.**
2. **RCW 19.86 (Consumer Protection Act).** Egregious payment-withholding by a contractor may give rise to a Consumer Protection Act claim by a consumer (residential homeowner) — but the CPA is not a generally available payment-acceleration remedy for B2B disputes.
3. **Contract-based prompt-payment terms.** Most Washington commercial construction contracts include AIA-style or similar pay-when-paid / pay-if-paid clauses, statutory interest-on-late-payment language, and termination-for-non-payment provisions. Washington courts have generally enforced pay-if-paid clauses where clearly stated, but pay-when-paid clauses are treated as timing mechanisms only.

The upshot for ContrPro users: **Washington has no statutory backstop for private-works payment timing**. The remedy is the lien (RCW 60.04) plus contract-based interest and termination rights. Compared to Illinois, New York, Texas, or California — all of which provide statutory prompt-payment rights with interest accrual on late payments — Washington private-works payment discipline is essentially contractually enforced.

### 3.3 Prevailing wages on public works — RCW 39.12

Washington has a **state-level prevailing-wage statute (Chapter 39.12 RCW)** that applies to all public works contracts, including state, county, municipal, and special-district contracts, regardless of dollar threshold [61][62][63]. The prevailing-wage rates are published by **L&I's Prevailing Wage Program** at the county and trade-classification level.

**Statement of Intent / Affidavit of Wages Paid.** Before commencing public work, the prime contractor and every subcontractor must file an **Intent to Pay Prevailing Wages** with L&I. Upon project completion, each must file an **Affidavit of Wages Paid**. Filings are made through L&I's online prevailing-wage portal; filing fees apply per filing [61][62]. Failure to file is grounds for L&I citation and exclusion from future public bids.

**Apprenticeship utilization — RCW 39.04.310 et seq.** State and major-municipal public works contracts above specified thresholds (typically $1M for state-funded buildings, lower for transportation) must include **state-approved apprentice labor at minimum percentages** of total trade hours — currently 15% of trade hours statewide, with city-specific higher targets in Seattle and certain other jurisdictions [63][64]. Apprentice-utilization shortfalls are subject to liquidated damages under contract.

**Penalties.** Underpayment of prevailing wages exposes the contractor to back-wage liability plus an L&I civil penalty up to **$5,000 or 50% of the wage underpayment, whichever is greater**, per violation, plus debarment from public bidding for up to three years for repeat or willful violations (RCW 39.12.050).

## 4. Tax Structure — Washington's Construction-as-Retail Trap

### 4.1 The headline — no state income tax, but **construction is a retail sale**

Washington is one of nine states with **no state personal income tax** and one of eight with no state corporate income tax. The state's revenue comes primarily from three sources [65][66][67]:

1. The **Business and Occupation (B&O) tax** on gross receipts (Chapter 82.04 RCW) — assessed on every Washington business at varying rates by classification.
2. The **Retail Sales Tax** (Chapter 82.08 RCW) at **6.5% state** plus local rates that bring total combined rates to **8.5% – 10.6%** depending on jurisdiction.
3. The **Use Tax** (Chapter 82.12 RCW) — the complement of sales tax, owed on tangible personal property used in Washington when sales tax was not collected at the time of purchase.

The single most important construction-tax fact in Washington — and **the single biggest trap** for an out-of-state or new-to-Washington contractor — is the treatment of construction on real property as a **retail sale**. This is set out in **RCW 82.04.050(2)(b)** [68][69][70]:

> *"Retail sale" includes the constructing, repairing, decorating, or improving of new or existing buildings or other structures under, upon, or above real property of or for consumers, including the installing or attaching of any article of tangible personal property therein or thereto, whether or not such personal property becomes a part of the realty by virtue of installation, and also includes the sale of services or charges made for the clearing of land and the moving of earth excepting the mere leveling of land used in commercial farming or agriculture.*

The practical consequence: a Washington contractor performing work for a "consumer" (an owner who will use, occupy, or sell the improvement to a final user) charges **retail sales tax on the entire contract price — labor and materials combined**. The contractor purchases materials from suppliers **using a Reseller Permit** under RCW 82.04.470 / RCW 82.32.780 (avoiding sales tax at the supplier counter) and collects sales tax from the consumer on the total contract sum at the rate applicable to the project location [65][68][69][70][71][72].

**This is the inverse of how most states treat construction.** In Texas, California, Florida, New York, Illinois, Tennessee, and Georgia (and most other states), the contractor is treated as the **end consumer** of materials: the contractor pays sales tax on materials at the supplier counter, and the contract price to the owner is **not** subject to sales tax (it is a service, not a sale). In Washington, that paradigm is **flipped**: materials flow through the contractor sales-tax-free under the reseller permit, and sales tax attaches when the **completed improvement** is delivered to the consumer [65][68][69][70][71][72].

The size of this trap is hard to overstate. A contractor relocating to Washington from California, Texas, or any other "materials-only" state who continues to operate on the old playbook will:

1. **Pay sales tax at the supplier counter** that should have been deferred via the reseller permit (overpaying upfront).
2. **Fail to collect sales tax from the owner on the labor component** (underpaying downstream).
3. **Underreport sales tax remittances** to DOR by the labor-tax delta.

The DOR audit recovery, when discovered, is the full labor-component sales tax for **up to four years back** (RCW 82.32.050 statute of limitations) plus interest at the statutory rate plus penalties up to **25% of the tax due** for late payment plus a **5% assessment penalty** plus, in egregious cases, a **substantial-underpayment penalty of 5%** stacking on top. A contractor on $5M of annual Washington revenue with a 60/40 labor/material split could face $5M × 60% × ~9% sales tax × 4 years = **~$1.08M** in unremitted tax exposure, plus interest and penalties. **This is the Washington trap.**

### 4.2 The four construction-tax classes — get this right

Washington Department of Revenue's *Construction Industry Tax Guide* identifies **five tax-treatment categories** for construction contractors, each with different B&O classification, sales-tax behavior, use-tax behavior, and reseller-permit treatment [65][68][72][73][74]:

| Category | B&O classification | Sales tax | Use tax on materials | Reseller permit usable? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Custom construction** (the most common — work for a consumer on the consumer's land) | **Retailing** at **0.471%** | **Charged on total contract (labor + materials + markup)** to the consumer | No — materials bought tax-free for resale | **Yes** |
| **Speculative building** (contractor builds on land it owns for sale to a future consumer) | **No B&O on the construction activity itself**; B&O is on the sale of real property which is generally exempt | **Not charged on the eventual sale of real property** | **Yes — use tax owed on materials** at the time of installation | **No — supplier-counter sales tax (or use tax accrual)** |
| **Public road construction** (work on roads owned by federal, state, or local government) | **Public Road Construction** at **0.484%** | **Not charged** — public road work is not a retail sale | No — materials bought tax-free under reseller permit | **Yes** |
| **Government contracting** (work for a federal, state, or local government entity on real property owned by the government, not public road) | **Government Contracting** at **0.484%** | **Not charged** to the government entity | **Yes — use tax owed on materials** at installation | **No — supplier-counter sales tax (or use tax accrual)** |
| **Wholesale construction** (subcontracting to another contractor who will resell the work to a consumer; subcontractor possesses the prime's reseller permit) | **Wholesaling** at **0.484%** | **Not charged** — the prime collects from the consumer | No — materials bought tax-free under reseller permit | **Yes (the prime's permit)** |

The structure is intricate. Speculative building and government contracting are particularly counterintuitive because the contractor pays the **use tax** on materials (despite being a registered contractor with a reseller permit) — the reseller permit is **not** available to defer materials tax on those two activity types [65][68][72][73][74].

**Custom construction — the dominant private-work category.** Most Washington residential remodels, commercial tenant-improvement jobs, new commercial buildings on consumer-owned land, and similar work fall under custom construction. Under custom construction:

1. Materials purchases from suppliers: present **Reseller Permit**; supplier does not charge sales tax (RCW 82.04.470, WAC 458-20-102).
2. Labor performed: no special tax treatment at the labor-purchase level (workers are employees, not vendors for sales-tax purposes).
3. Invoice to owner: charge **state sales tax + local sales tax + state Retailing B&O on the gross contract**, separately stated to the customer. The local rate is the rate at the **point of delivery** — i.e., the project location, *not* the contractor's office location (the destination-based sourcing rule under RCW 82.32.730).
4. Remit collected sales tax + Retailing B&O to DOR on the monthly, quarterly, or annual filing schedule appropriate to the contractor's volume.

**Speculative building — the developer trap.** A contractor who **owns the land** and builds for **eventual sale to an unknown future buyer** is treated as a "speculative builder." The construction activity is **not** subject to Retailing B&O (because the contractor is not selling a service to a consumer — the contractor is building for its own future sale). Instead [65][68][72][73]:

1. The speculative builder owes **use tax on materials** at the time of installation, at the project-location rate. The Reseller Permit is **not available** for speculative building.
2. The eventual real-property sale to a buyer is generally exempt from sales tax (real property sales are not "retail sales").
3. The Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) applies to the eventual sale (state rate 1.28%, with graduated municipal additions for higher-value parcels under the 2020 REET reform; see RCW 82.45).

The speculative-building characterization is fact-driven and contestable. A "build-to-sell" home built on land the contractor doesn't own — i.e., the land is owned by the eventual buyer at the time construction begins — is **not** speculative; it's custom construction. The line is whether the contractor or the future owner holds title to the land at the time work begins [65][68][72][73].

**Government contracting — public buildings, not roads.** Construction on real property owned by federal, state, or local government — courthouses, schools, fire stations, parks (not roads) — is **Government Contracting**. The B&O rate is 0.484%. No sales tax is charged to the government entity. But the contractor owes **use tax on materials at the project-location rate**, with the reseller permit **not available** [65][68][72][73].

**Public road construction — a special class.** Work on highways, streets, and roads owned by federal, state, or local government is **Public Road Construction**. B&O rate is 0.484%. No sales tax is charged. The reseller permit **is** available for materials, and use tax is generally **not** owed on materials installed in the road. Washington's special treatment of public road construction reflects the federal-aid-highway funding regime and is fundamentally different from government building work [65][68][72][73].

**Wholesale construction — subcontractors to a prime.** A subcontractor working for a prime contractor on the prime's custom-construction job is a **wholesaler** for B&O purposes. The B&O rate is 0.484% (Wholesaling). The sub does **not** charge sales tax to the prime (the prime is not a consumer; the prime will resell to the consumer who pays sales tax on the prime's full contract). The sub uses **the prime's reseller permit** for materials purchases — or its own permit, with documentation that the work is wholesale [65][68][72][73][75].

The two "use-tax-on-materials" categories — speculative building and government contracting — are the most common audit traps in Washington construction. A contractor who has been operating in custom-construction mode (reseller permit, sales tax on total contract) and then takes a government-contracting job must remember to switch behavior on that job: **stop using the reseller permit on materials for that job; accrue use tax on materials installed; charge B&O at Government Contracting (not Retailing).** The fact-pattern switch is not optional.

### 4.3 The B&O tax — gross receipts, no deductions

Washington's **Business and Occupation tax (Chapter 82.04 RCW)** is a tax on **gross receipts** with virtually **no deductions** — no deduction for cost of goods sold, no deduction for labor, no deduction for materials. This makes the B&O behave economically like a small-percentage tax on **revenue**, not on **income or profit** [65][66][67][76].

For construction, the B&O classifications are [65][68][76]:

| Classification | Rate | Applies to |
|---|---|---|
| **Retailing** | **0.471% (0.00471)** | Sale of construction services to consumers (custom construction) and retail sale of TPP |
| **Wholesaling** | **0.484% (0.00484)** | Subcontracting and wholesale sales |
| **Government Contracting** | **0.484% (0.00484)** | Construction on government-owned real property (not roads) |
| **Public Road Construction** | **0.484% (0.00484)** | Construction on public roads, streets, highways |
| **Service & Other Activities** | Graduated: **0.015 / 0.0175 / 0.021** (tiered by prior-year revenue) | Architecture, engineering, surveying, consulting (RCW 18.43 / 18.08 work) — separate B&O on professional services |

The rates are small in absolute terms — Retailing at 0.471% looks trivial against a 9% sales tax — but because the B&O applies to **gross receipts with no deductions**, a contractor with a 5% net margin pays effectively (0.471% / 5%) = **9.4% of net** in Retailing B&O alone. For low-margin contractors, the B&O is a meaningful drag on profitability that does not exist in income-tax-only states. **The B&O is not optional and is not deductible from the same revenue under any other tax.**

**Small Business B&O Tax Credit — RCW 82.04.4451.** Washington provides a small-business B&O credit that fully or partially offsets B&O liability for very small businesses, phased out for businesses above approximately $48,000 / quarter in B&O liability. The credit makes truly small businesses effectively B&O-free, but most active registered contractors exceed the threshold and pay full B&O.

**Filing frequency.** B&O and sales tax are reported on the **same Excise Tax Return** with DOR, filed monthly (most active businesses), quarterly (smaller businesses), or annually (very small businesses). DOR assigns filing frequency based on volume; mis-filing or late filing carries penalties starting at 9% of the tax for the first month late and escalating [65][67][76].

### 4.4 Sales tax — state rate, local rates, and destination sourcing

Washington's **state retail sales tax** is **6.5%** under **RCW 82.08.020** [65][66][67][77]. **Local sales tax** is added on top, with city, county, regional transit (e.g., Sound Transit in the Seattle area), and special-purpose-district increments combining to total local rates of **1.0% – 4.1%**. Combined state-plus-local rates currently span approximately **6.5% (in unincorporated areas with no local add-ons, rare) to 10.6% (Seattle, Tacoma, several others)**. The DOR publishes current combined rates by location at the **Tax Rate Lookup tool** on dor.wa.gov.

**Destination-based sourcing.** Sales tax is sourced to the **point of delivery** — i.e., the project location for construction — under **RCW 82.32.730 and the Streamlined Sales Tax Agreement**. A contractor headquartered in Spokane (combined rate ~9.0%) performing work in Seattle (combined rate ~10.35%) charges the **Seattle rate** on that Seattle job. ContrPro must track project-location tax rates per job, not contractor-office rates [65][67][77].

**Reseller permit — RCW 82.04.470 / RCW 82.32.780 / WAC 458-20-102.** Washington's Reseller Permit is the document that lets a registered contractor purchase materials from suppliers **without** paying sales tax at the counter, on the theory that the materials will be resold (incorporated into a retail-sale construction project). DOR issues reseller permits to contractors who:

1. Are registered with DOR for B&O / sales tax;
2. Have filed Excise Tax Returns timely;
3. Have a Washington business location and a UBI; and
4. Engage in qualifying retail sale or wholesale activity.

Reseller permits are issued **for a four-year term** under WAC 458-20-102 (with an automatic renewal review by DOR). DOR may decline to renew a permit for a contractor with delinquent filings, audit deficiencies, or compliance issues. **A contractor without a valid reseller permit must pay sales tax at the supplier counter on every purchase — including for custom-construction jobs where the work will ultimately be sales-taxed to the consumer.** That double-taxation can be recovered as a refund or B&O deduction in some cases, but the procedural friction is real. ContrPro should treat reseller-permit renewal as a calendar-tracked compliance item [65][68][71][72][73].

**Speculative-building / government-contracting exception.** As noted above, the reseller permit is **not available** for speculative building and government contracting. On those activity types, the contractor must pay sales tax at the supplier counter (or accrue use tax on tax-free purchases). This is the single biggest "internal switch" a Washington construction business must operate: same supplier, same materials, but the tax treatment differs by the activity type of the project the materials will be installed in.

### 4.5 Use tax — Chapter 82.12 RCW — the compensating tax

The **Use Tax (Chapter 82.12 RCW)** is the complement of the sales tax: it applies to tangible personal property used in Washington where sales tax was not collected at the time of acquisition. The use tax rate is the same as the local sales tax rate at the place of use. Use tax applies to [65][66][67]:

1. Equipment and supplies purchased out of state and used in Washington (e.g., tools bought in Idaho and used on a Spokane job).
2. Materials transferred from inventory to a non-retail-sale-activity job (e.g., speculative-building or government-contracting jobs, where the reseller permit is not available but materials originally purchased on the reseller permit are being installed).
3. Self-construction (a contractor building improvements for its own use — e.g., a shop or office on the contractor's land).

**The "withdrawal from inventory" rule.** If a contractor uses its reseller-permit purchasing to acquire materials and then installs those materials on a speculative-building or government-contracting job, the contractor owes **use tax** on the cost basis of the materials at the time of installation, at the project-location rate. Forgetting to accrue this is a common audit hit [65][67][68][72].

### 4.6 City B&O — Seattle, Tacoma, Bellevue, and others

Washington permits cities to impose **municipal B&O taxes** in parallel with the state B&O. The cities with active municipal B&O regimes that affect construction include:

- **Seattle** — Seattle B&O administered by the Seattle Department of Finance and Administrative Services. Service & Other Activities rate around 0.222% – 0.427% depending on classification; retailing rate around 0.215%. The Seattle rates are *additive* to state B&O.
- **Tacoma** — Tacoma B&O with rates in the 0.10% – 0.50% range by classification.
- **Bellevue** — Bellevue B&O in the same range.
- **Spokane, Everett, and others** — vary; many smaller municipalities do not impose B&O.

A contractor operating across multiple Washington cities files separate municipal B&O returns at each city's threshold; the cities have adopted a **Municipal Business and Occupation Tax Model Ordinance** under chapter 35.102 RCW that standardizes definitions and apportionment rules across participating cities, but the rates remain city-set. ContrPro's Washington module should treat city-level B&O as a separate filing track per city of activity.

### 4.7 The Washington tax fact sheet — for ContrPro's prompt-engineering use

For a Washington construction registrant on private custom work, the **operative tax stack** per dollar of revenue is approximately:

1. State Retailing B&O **0.471%**.
2. State sales tax **6.5%** + local sales tax **1% to 4.1%** = combined ~8.5%–10.6% on total contract (passed through to consumer; not the contractor's net cost — but a cash-flow management item).
3. City B&O **0.10% – 0.50%** for activity in Seattle, Tacoma, Bellevue, or similar.
4. State workers' compensation premium (hourly rate by trade class, see §1.5).
5. **No** state income tax (saves 5%–10% versus most other states).
6. Federal income tax + federal payroll tax (FICA, FUTA, etc.) — same as every other state.

The combined operating tax load on a Washington contractor with broad geographic activity is **comparable to a high-income-tax state**, despite the absence of an income tax. The B&O-plus-WC premium structure compensates the state for forgone income tax revenue. ContrPro should not let users believe Washington is a "low-tax" state for construction — it isn't.

## 5. Workers' Compensation — Deeper Detail

(See §1.5 for the basic structure; this section addresses the operational consequences.)

### 5.1 The State Fund monopoly

Washington's **State Fund** is the sole permissible workers'-compensation insurer for the vast majority of Washington employers. Self-insurance under RCW 51.14 is available only to large employers (typically $25M+ in audited net worth, with specific liquidity demonstrations); fewer than 400 Washington employers self-insure. **There is no admitted private insurance market for State-Fund-eligible employers.** This makes Washington a tightly controlled-rate environment: every contractor pays State-Fund-published rates by class code, with experience-rating modifiers and retrospective rating program elections being the only meaningful levers [18][19][20][21][22][23].

### 5.2 Class codes and hourly-rate structure

L&I classifies industries into ~300 **risk classifications** published in **WAC 296-17A**. Construction trades have their own classifications (the 05xx and 06xx ranges, with some variation). Each class has [20][21][23]:

1. A **base hourly rate** for accident-fund premium.
2. A **medical-aid-fund rate** (separate component).
3. A **supplemental pension fund rate** (cost-of-living adjustment for older permanent-total-disability claims).
4. A **stay-at-work** fund rate (light-duty / return-to-work incentive).

The **total composite hourly premium rate** is the sum, often expressed as "Employer Hours Rate" and "Employee Hours Rate" (the latter is permitted as a payroll deduction from the employee under RCW 51.32.073, generally for the medical-aid and supplemental-pension components only).

**Construction rate volatility.** Construction class rates fluctuate annually based on the State Fund's claims experience for the class. A class with a major recent claim trajectory (e.g., roofing falls) can see double-digit-percentage rate increases year-over-year. ContrPro should treat the State Fund rate as an annually re-baselined cost driver — not a stable fixed cost.

### 5.3 Experience rating and retrospective rating

L&I applies **experience-rating modifiers** to each employer's premiums based on three years of historical claims experience. The modifier is calibrated so an industry-average claims history yields a 1.00 modifier; better-than-average histories yield modifiers below 1.00 (premium discount); worse-than-average yield modifiers above 1.00 (premium surcharge). A contractor's modifier can swing premiums by **50%+** in either direction over a three-year window [20][22][24].

Larger employers may elect into **retrospective rating** ("Retro") under RCW 51.18 — a program administered by L&I and (more commonly) by industry-association group sponsors (e.g., AGC Retro, Master Builders Retro). Retro returns a portion of premiums based on the group's collective claims performance over a multi-year adjustment period. Retro participation is among the most effective premium-reduction tools available to Washington construction employers but requires multi-year commitment to the group and ongoing safety-program performance.

### 5.4 Reporting and audit

Quarterly Reports under RCW 51.16 are filed with L&I on Form F212-055-000 within one month after the end of each calendar quarter. The report includes:

1. Hours worked by each employee, allocated to the correct risk classification.
2. Total premium computed at the applicable rates.
3. Employee deduction component.

**Classification disputes.** A frequent audit point: assigning the right risk class to each worker. A construction laborer who spends 80% of time framing and 20% of time on cleanup is classified at the **highest applicable rate** for the work performed (the "highest rated job" rule), not the average. Worker timekeeping must support classification splits if multiple classes are reported. Failed audits typically reclassify all hours to the highest-rated class for the period.

**Audit lookback.** L&I premium audits look back up to three years (RCW 51.48.103); longer for fraudulent misreporting. Audit assessments include unpaid premium + interest + penalty up to 100% of underpayment for willful violations.

### 5.5 The "owner-elected coverage" exemption — operational detail

A sole proprietor, partner, LLC member, or corporate officer who elects out of coverage under RCW 51.12.020 / WAC 296-17-31022 must file the election with L&I. The election applies to that owner only — every other employee remains covered. Important operational points [18][22][24]:

1. **The election does not reduce premiums for covered employees.** Premium on every covered worker's hours is still owed.
2. **The owner forgoes State Fund medical and time-loss coverage** for any on-the-job injury. Owners frequently substitute private disability or accident insurance to fill the gap.
3. **The election is per-business-entity.** An owner of two LLCs must elect separately for each.
4. **GC's may require sub's WC coverage by contract** regardless of the owner-elected exemption. A sole-proprietor specialty sub working on a Seattle commercial job will frequently be required by the GC's prime contract to maintain coverage on the owner anyway. The election is a state-law minimum; contracts can require more.

## 6. Recent Changes — 2023 to Present

### 6.1 The HB 1534 trifecta — bond increase + Homeowner Recovery Program + L&I rulemaking

**Engrossed Substitute House Bill 1534**, signed by Governor Inslee **May 1, 2023**, was the most significant overhaul of Washington's contractor-registration regime in 22 years [15][16][17][29]:

1. **Bond increases (effective July 1, 2024).** General contractor bond from $12,000 to $30,000; specialty contractor bond from $6,000 to $15,000. First increase since 2001.
2. **Homeowner Recovery Program (effective July 1, 2026).** A new last-resort recovery fund for homeowners with uncollected judgments against registered contractors. Funded by a registration-fee surcharge. L&I adopted implementing rules under WAC 296-200A finalized in early 2026.
3. **Disclosure and consumer-information enhancements.** Updates to the Model Disclosure Statement under RCW 18.27.114 to incorporate Homeowner Recovery Program information.

### 6.2 L&I fee increase — 6.51% in 2024

In 2024, L&I adopted a **6.51% across-the-board fee increase** for the Contractor Registration, Electrical, Elevator, Factory Assembled Structures (FAS), and Plumber programs — the first multi-program fee adjustment in several years. The increase applies to new applications, renewals, and certification fees [17][29]. Combined with the bond increase under HB 1534, the cost of being a Washington registered contractor in 2024–2026 is meaningfully higher than in 2022–2023.

### 6.3 Workers' comp rate adjustments

State Fund rates are adjusted annually. For policy year 2025 and 2026, L&I has continued its post-COVID claims-experience recalibration; construction-trade composite rates in 2025 were broadly flat to slightly down on most building trades and slightly up on roofing and demolition. The annual rate-adoption notice and the WAC 296-17A schedule are published each fall for the following policy year.

### 6.4 Lien-law amendments — RCW 60.04 updates

The 2024 Legislature did not enact substantive changes to RCW 60.04's 90-day-claim or 8-month-foreclosure windows. The 2025 legislative session reviewed proposed amendments (HB 1428 and similar) but no enacted changes to the lien deadlines have taken effect. The lien deadlines as described in §2 above remain the law as of May 2026.

### 6.5 Prevailing wage — apprentice utilization expansion

The 2023 Legislature expanded apprentice-utilization requirements on certain transportation-funded projects and on state-funded buildings, with implementation rules under RCW 39.04.310 et seq. and WAC 296-127 adopted in 2024. ContrPro users bidding state public works in 2025–2026 should expect strict apprentice-utilization audits on projects above the threshold.

### 6.6 Tax changes — capital gains tax and the JumpStart B&O

Two tax developments affect Washington construction businesses indirectly:

1. **Capital Gains Tax — Chapter 82.87 RCW** (effective January 1, 2022, after multiple court challenges including *Quinn v. Washington*, 230 Wn.2d 711 (2023)). A 7% tax on Washington-source long-term capital gains above an annual exclusion ($262,000 for 2023, indexed). Construction-business owners realizing capital gains on sale of a business or on appreciated business assets may have liability. The tax is **not** assessed on real estate sales (which remain under REET).
2. **Seattle JumpStart Tax — Seattle Municipal Code 5.38** (effective January 1, 2021). A payroll-expense tax on Seattle businesses with payroll above $7M annually and on individual employees earning $174,337+ (2024 threshold, indexed). Most construction-trade workers fall below the per-employee threshold, but project-management and executive salaries can trigger liability for larger firms operating in Seattle.

### 6.7 City of Seattle Building Code and Permitting changes

Seattle adopted the **2021 Seattle Building Code suite** (2021 IBC, IRC, IECC, IMC, IPC, and IFC, each with Seattle Amendments) effective **March 15, 2024**. The transition included structural-amendment changes for low-rise residential and energy-code stringency increases beyond the state baseline. Contractors operating in Seattle should verify which code version controls each project's design (typically the code in effect at permit application).

## 7. Practical Compliance Checklist

### 7.1 The Washington-specific gotchas a casual contractor will miss

1. **Sales tax on the total contract — not just materials.** The single biggest trap. Under RCW 82.04.050(2)(b), construction labor is a **retail sale**. Washington contractors charge sales tax on the **gross contract price (labor + materials + markup)** to the consumer. Materials flow through tax-free under the reseller permit. **A contractor coming from California, Texas, Florida, New York, Illinois, Tennessee, or Georgia who applies the old "tax on materials only" model will be wrong by the full labor-component sales tax — potentially 60% of every job × 9% sales tax × four-year audit lookback.**
2. **The reseller permit is not always available.** Speculative building and government contracting both require **sales tax at the supplier counter** (or **use-tax accrual** on materials installed). Custom construction, public road construction, and wholesale construction *do* permit reseller-permit purchases. The activity-class determines the supplier-counter behavior.
3. **B&O on gross receipts — no deductions.** Retailing B&O at 0.471% on gross applies to every custom-construction dollar. For a 5%-margin contractor, this is ~9.4% of net. Plus Wholesaling B&O for sub work, Government Contracting for public buildings, Public Road Construction for roads — each at its own rate. The B&O is the silent tax — small in percentage, real in dollars, and not negotiable.
4. **State Fund monopolistic WC.** No private workers' comp insurance market for Washington employers. Premiums are hourly-rate-based (not payroll-percentage-based). Construction trades are among the highest-rated classes. Retro programs through industry associations are the principal premium-reduction lever.
5. **Bond increased July 1, 2024 — $30K general / $15K specialty.** Renewal after that date triggers the new bond level. Contractors who renewed in 2023 at the old level had to step up at next renewal.
6. **Registration is required for everyone — but it's a financial filing, not a competency exam.** Holding an "active and in good standing" Washington registration means the contractor has bond, insurance, UBI, and a WC account. It says *nothing* about competence. Trade-specific competence (electrical, plumbing) is a separate L&I credential and is exam-based.
7. **Trade licenses are real exams.** Electrical (RCW 19.28) Journey-Level (01) and Specialty Electrician certifications are exam-based and require 4,000–8,000 hours of qualifying experience. Plumbing (RCW 18.106) is the same. An RCW 18.27 general contractor registration does **not** authorize the registrant to perform electrical or plumbing work without the underlying license.
8. **Lien deadlines are fast.** 90 days from cessation to record (RCW 60.04.091). 8 months from recording to commence foreclosure (RCW 60.04.141). 14 days from filing to serve owner — or forfeit attorney's fees and costs. 90 days from filing the foreclosure action to serve owner. The 14-day fee-shifting rule is the most-missed deadline; the 90-day claim rule is the most-fatal-when-missed.
9. **Pre-claim notice — 60 days commercial, 10 days new SFR.** Pre-claim notice under RCW 60.04.031 protects different lookback windows depending on project type. The 10-day window on a new single-family residence is among the shortest in the country.
10. **Residential remodels — owner can't be made to pay twice.** Under RCW 60.04.031(3), subcontractor liens on residential remodel of an owner-occupied SFR are limited to amounts not yet paid to the prime at the time notice is received. Time the notice to interrupt cash flow, or the lien collapses.
11. **RCW 18.27.080 suit bar — register continuously.** A registration lapse during work strips the right to sue for unpaid sums. Track registration status as a project compliance check, not just an onboarding check.
12. **The pre-residential disclosure — $1,000 threshold.** RCW 18.27.114 requires the Model Disclosure on residential 1-4-unit work at or above $1,000. Forget it and forfeit attorney's fees on collection.
13. **Public-works retainage at 5% — bondable.** RCW 60.28.011 caps public-works retainage at 5%. Submit a retainage bond to release the cash retainage at any time. This is a meaningful working-capital lever for sub and prime contractors.
14. **Public-works payment-bond claim has 30-day acceptance deadline.** RCW 39.08.030. Don't confuse it with the 45-day retainage-claim deadline. Both are different from the 90-day lien deadline (which doesn't apply to public works at all because public property is non-lienable).
15. **Apprentice utilization on public works.** RCW 39.04.310 et seq. — 15% statewide apprentice-hours requirement on state-funded projects above threshold. Failing this is a liquidated-damages event under most public-works contracts.
16. **City-level B&O and business licenses.** Seattle, Tacoma, Bellevue, and several other cities impose B&O and business-license fees on top of state B&O. Multi-city operations file in every city.
17. **The "personal labor" test for 1099s.** Washington's six-prong test under RCW 51.08.180/195 is stricter than federal. Most construction "subs" who are 1099-paid will fail and trigger retroactive State Fund premium assessment. Use registered, bonded, insured subs with documented separate businesses — not casual labor labeled 1099.
18. **Homeowner Recovery Program (effective July 1, 2026).** Registration-fee surcharge begins funding the program; consumer recoveries begin July 1, 2026. Factor the surcharge into the cost-of-registration model.

### 7.2 ContrPro's Washington-onboarding data model

For a Washington contractor entering ContrPro, the minimum viable record should include:

1. **L&I Contractor Registration Number** (the "WAC###" or "###LL###" depending on entity type) — pulled and verified against L&I's "Contractor Verify" API/page.
2. **Classification** (General or Specialty) — from L&I.
3. **Bond status and amount** ($30K or $15K, post-July-1-2024) — from L&I.
4. **Insurance status and amounts** ($50K property / $100K-$200K personal injury minimums or higher) — from L&I.
5. **UBI Number** (9-digit Unified Business Identifier) — from DOR's Business Licensing Service.
6. **L&I Workers' Comp Account Number** and current Certificate of Coverage — separate L&I database.
7. **Reseller Permit Number and expiration date** (4-year permits) — from DOR.
8. **DOR Excise Tax filing frequency** (monthly / quarterly / annual) — from DOR.
9. **Electrical and Plumbing license numbers** (if applicable, with expiration dates) — from L&I.
10. **City business licenses** for cities of activity (Seattle, Tacoma, Bellevue, Spokane, etc.) — per-city.
11. **City B&O account numbers** for cities of activity.
12. **Prevailing-wage prior-public-work history** — from L&I PWP portal (if bidding public work).

Verification should be **periodic** (at minimum semi-annually for active registrants), not one-time. A registration that was active at onboarding can lapse mid-engagement.

### 7.3 ContrPro's Washington-tax engine data model

For Washington tax compliance, each project record should carry:

1. **Activity classification** — custom construction / speculative building / public road / government contracting / wholesale — drives all downstream tax behavior.
2. **Project location** (full address) — drives sales-tax rate at delivery.
3. **Project value, labor/material split, and markup** — drives invoice tax computation.
4. **Customer entity type** (consumer / government / prime contractor) — drives retail/wholesale/government characterization.
5. **Reseller permit applicability flag** for materials purchases.
6. **B&O classification** — Retailing / Wholesaling / Government Contracting / Public Road Construction — drives B&O liability.
7. **Use tax accrual** flag and basis — for spec-build and government-contracting materials.
8. **City of activity** — drives municipal B&O reporting.
9. **Prevailing-wage applicability** — drives apprentice utilization and Intent/Affidavit filings.
10. **Excise Tax Return filing window** — monthly / quarterly / annual.

The data model differs materially from a "general state contractor" model used in income-tax states. Washington's construction-as-retail framework forces a tax engine that operates at the **project-classification level**, not just the customer-invoice level.
