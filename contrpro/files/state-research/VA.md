## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 The Virginia structure — DPOR statewide, three license classes by project value, classification + specialty on top

Virginia is **structurally distinctive** among East Coast construction states in maintaining a **single statewide contractor licensing scheme** administered by the **Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR)** through the **Board for Contractors** under **Va. Code § 54.1-1100 et seq.** [1][2][3][4][5]. Unlike Ohio (which devolves general-contractor licensing entirely to home-rule municipalities), New York (no statewide GC license), or Pennsylvania (registration-only Home Improvement Contractor regime), Virginia operates a comprehensive, tiered, statewide license tied to **project value bands** (Class A / Class B / Class C), layered with **classifications** (the type of work — RBC, CBC, H/H, ELE, PLB, HVA, etc.), and further subdivided by **specialty designations** (more than 30 specialties under 18 VAC 50-22-30, covering everything from billboards to fire suppression to landscape irrigation) [1][3][6][7][8][9][10].

The Virginia structure is, in one sense, the cleanest in the Mid-Atlantic — one statute, one agency, one license number per contractor — and in another sense, the most error-prone, because **the same contractor can hold a license at the wrong class, the wrong classification, or with the wrong specialty designations and still be unlicensed for a given project under Va. Code § 54.1-1115**, with the punishments running up to **Class 1 misdemeanor** criminal exposure, **$500-per-day** civil penalties under § 54.1-111, and **contract unenforceability** denying the contractor the right to sue for unpaid amounts [11][12][13][14].

**Statutory authority — Va. Code § 54.1-1100 et seq.** Chapter 11 of Title 54.1 — entitled "Contractors" — sets the entire framework: definitions (§ 54.1-1100), Board composition and powers (§§ 54.1-1101 through 54.1-1105), Class A / B / C licensure (§§ 54.1-1106 through 54.1-1108), exam and net-worth provisions (§§ 54.1-1106 and 54.1-1108), tradesman certification (§§ 54.1-1129 through 54.1-1132), prohibited acts and penalties (§§ 54.1-1111 through 54.1-1115), and the Virginia Contractor Transaction Recovery Fund (§§ 54.1-1118 through 54.1-1127) [1][3][6][7][9][11][12][15]. The implementing regulations sit at **18 VAC 50-22** (Board for Contractors Regulations) and **18 VAC 50-30** (Individual License and Certification Regulations for Tradesmen) [6][8][9][16][17].

**Project-value threshold — the $1,000 floor and the three license classes.** Under Va. Code § 54.1-1100 and 18 VAC 50-22-20, the three license classes are defined by the **total value of a single contract or project** and by the **annual aggregate value of work undertaken in any 12-month period** [1][3][6][7][8][9][18][19]:

| Class | Single-project value | Annual aggregate value | Net-worth requirement | Bond alternative |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Class C** | More than **$1,000** but less than **$10,000** per single contract or project | Less than **$150,000** annually | None | None |
| **Class B** | **$10,000 or more** but less than **$120,000** per single contract or project | Less than **$750,000** annually | **$15,000** minimum equity | **$50,000 surety bond** in lieu of net worth |
| **Class A** | **$120,000 or more** per single contract or project, **or any single project** equal to or exceeding the Class B annual cap | **$750,000 or more** annually | **$45,000** minimum equity | **$50,000 surety bond** in lieu of net worth |

**Note on the historic $30,000 / $150,000 thresholds.** Older secondary sources and some commercial training materials refer to the older Class A threshold of $120,000 single project, Class B threshold of $7,500 single project, and Class A annual aggregate of $1 million. The General Assembly amended Va. Code § 54.1-1100 in recent sessions, and the current 18 VAC 50-22-20 definitions control. Practitioners should consult the current statute and regulation directly; specialty CLE materials and training-site summaries lag the statutory updates by 6 to 24 months [1][3][6][8][9][18][19][20].

**The $1,000 floor — Va. Code § 54.1-1101.** Below the $1,000 single-project / annual-volume threshold, no DPOR contractor license is required for that work — though tradesman certification (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, gas fitter) may still apply by trade, and municipal building permits and inspections continue to apply regardless of dollar amount [1][3][6][9][12][18]. Above $1,000, the contractor **must** hold the correct class (C, B, or A) for the specific contract or project.

**The single-project + annual aggregate trap.** The two halves of the class definition are **disjunctive** — a contractor crosses up the class ladder if **either** the single-project value **or** the annual aggregate value crosses a threshold [1][3][6][9][18][19]. A Class C contractor doing forty-five $5,000 jobs in a year ($225,000 aggregate) has exceeded the Class C annual cap and is operating outside the class on every job after the threshold was crossed, regardless of whether each individual job was under the $10,000 single-project ceiling. A Class B contractor whose single $135,000 contract goes through the closing into a Class A scope is similarly outside the class. The class-mismatch trap is the most common DPOR violation and is the principal reason DPOR's Compliance and Investigations Division pursues administrative cases against contractors who held a license but operated outside it [11][12][13][14].

### 1.2 Classifications — the type of work, layered on the class

Under **18 VAC 50-22-20** and **18 VAC 50-22-30**, the Board for Contractors grants licenses in named **classifications** that describe the type of work the licensee is qualified to perform, with classifications available within each license class (A, B, or C) [6][7][8][9][10]:

**Major classifications (broad scope):**

| Classification | Code | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Residential Building Contractor** | **RBC** | Construction, remodel, repair, improvement, removal, or demolition of dwellings and townhouses as defined in the USBC, including accessory buildings and structures. Does **not** include electrical, plumbing, HVAC, or gas fitting services. Does **not** permit work on commercial, industrial, institutional, or governmental-use structures [6][7][8][9][10][20]. |
| **Commercial Building Contractor** | **CBC** | Construction, remodel, repair, improvement, removal, or demolition of structures **not** defined as dwellings or townhouses in the USBC — commercial, industrial, institutional, governmental, and multifamily 4+ unit structures. Does **not** include electrical, plumbing, HVAC, or gas fitting services [6][7][8][9][10]. |
| **Highway/Heavy Contractor** | **H/H** | Construction, repair, improvement, or removal of roads, bridges, tunnels, airports, dams, levees, and other heavy civil structures [6][7][8][9][10]. |
| **Electrical Contractor** | **ELE** | All electrical work — installation, repair, improvement, removal of electrical wiring and equipment, in residential, commercial, and industrial structures [6][7][8][9][10][16][17]. |
| **Plumbing Contractor** | **PLB** | All plumbing work — water supply, drain, waste, vent, fixtures, gas piping — in residential, commercial, and industrial structures [6][7][8][9][10][16][17]. |
| **Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning Contractor** | **HVA** | All HVAC work — installation, repair, improvement, removal of heating, cooling, and ventilation systems [6][7][8][9][10][16][17]. |
| **Gas Fitter Contractor** | **GFC** | Installation, repair, improvement, or removal of gas piping and gas appliances. Often held jointly with PLB or HVA [6][7][8][9][16][17]. |
| **Fire Sprinkler Contractor** | **FSC** | Installation, repair, improvement, or removal of automatic fire sprinkler systems and standpipe systems [6][7][8][9][10]. |

**Specialty designations (narrow scope).** In addition to the major classifications, the Board grants **specialty designations** under **18 VAC 50-22-30** for narrower scopes of work, each with its own qualification and exam requirement. The current specialty list runs to more than 30 designations and includes [6][7][8][9][10][21]:

- **Asbestos Contracting (ASB)** — installation/repair/removal of asbestos-containing material
- **Billboard / Sign Contracting (BSC)** — installation, repair, improvement, or dismantling of any billboard or structural sign permanently annexed to real property
- **Blast/Explosive Contracting (BEC)** — use of explosive charges for repair, improvement, alteration, or demolition
- **Commercial Improvement Contracting (CIC)** — repair or improvement to structures not defined as dwellings/townhouses in the USBC (narrower than CBC)
- **Concrete Contracting (CEM)** — installation, repair, improvement, or removal of concrete in any form
- **Drywall Contracting (DRY)** — installation, repair, improvement, or removal of drywall and related finish work
- **Electronic/Communication Service Contracting (ESC)** — telephone, computer cabling, sound systems, data, TV/cable wiring, antenna, fiber optics, and other systems operating at 50 volts or less
- **Elevator/Escalator Contracting (EEC)** — elevators and escalators permanently annexed to real property
- **Environmental Monitoring Well Contracting (EMW)** — construction of wells to monitor hazardous substances in the ground
- **Excavation Contracting (EXC)** — excavation of earth, rock, and other materials in connection with construction
- **Fire Suppression Contracting (FSP)** — gas systems (halon, etc.), dry chemical systems, carbon dioxide systems (distinct from wet-pipe sprinkler covered by FSC)
- **Flooring and Floor Covering Contracting (FLR)** — installation, repair, improvement, or removal of materials common in the flooring industry
- **Home Improvement Contracting (HIC)** — alterations, additions, repairs, or remodeling of detached and attached residential structures
- **Landscape Irrigation Contracting (LIC)** — installation of underground sprinkler/irrigation systems
- **Landscape Service Contracting (LSC)** — landscape grading, planting, and improvements
- **Lead Abatement Contracting (LAC)** — removal or encapsulation of lead-based paint and lead-containing material
- **Marine Facility Contracting (MFC)** — boat docks, piers, bulkheads, and other waterfront structures
- **Masonry Contracting (BRK)** — brick, block, and stone masonry
- **Painting and Wallcovering Contracting (PTC)** — paint, wallpaper, and related finishes
- **Radon Mitigation Contracting (RMC)** — installation of radon mitigation systems
- **Recreational Facility Contracting (RFC)** — swimming pools, hot tubs, recreational structures
- **Refrigeration Contracting (REF)** — commercial refrigeration systems
- **Residential Building Maintenance and Repair Contracting (RBM)** — maintenance and minor repair on dwellings (narrower than RBC)
- **Roofing Contracting (ROC)** — installation, repair, removal of roofs
- **Sewage Disposal System Contracting (SDS)** — septic tanks, drain fields, alternative onsite sewage systems
- **Steel Erection Contracting (SEC)** — structural steel erection
- **Stone Contracting (STC)** — quarry stone, dimensional stone, and stone veneer
- **Vessel Construction Contracting (VCC)** — boats and ships
- **Water Well/Pump Contracting (WWP)** — water well drilling, well pump installation, and related work

A contractor may hold **multiple classifications and specialty designations** under a single license number, but **must pass the trade examination for each classification or specialty** and must establish qualification for each through the designated Qualified Individual under § 54.1-1106 [6][7][8][9][22].

### 1.3 Application requirements — Class A, B, C (Va. Code §§ 54.1-1106, 54.1-1108)

**Class A application — Va. Code § 54.1-1106 and 18 VAC 50-22-40.** A Class A license requires [1][3][6][8][9][18][22][23]:

1. **Designated Employee (DE) — exam-passed qualifier.** The Designated Employee must be a member of responsible management of the firm (officer, director, or principal employee) and must pass the **Class A Business and Project Management Examination** administered by **PSI Services LLC** under contract with DPOR. The exam covers business management, accounting, contract law, OSHA safety, lien law, project management, and Virginia-specific topics; passing score is 70%, exam fee is approximately $85 to $115 per sitting [18][23][24].
2. **Qualified Individual (QI) — trade-experience qualifier.** The Qualified Individual must have at least **five (5) years of experience** in the relevant classification or specialty designation within the preceding ten years; must pass the trade examination for each classification or specialty (e.g., the RBC, CBC, ELE, PLB, HVA exam) administered by PSI; and must be a member of responsible management of the firm or be the firm's qualifying agent [22][23][24].
3. **Pre-license education — eight (8) hours.** All Class A and Class B applicants must complete an **eight-hour pre-license education course** administered by a Board-approved provider; the course covers Virginia contractor law, lien law, business management, and ethics. The DE or other member of responsible management must complete the course [18][22][23][25].
4. **Financial responsibility — $45,000 net worth or $50,000 surety bond.** Class A applicants must demonstrate **a net worth or equity of at least $45,000** through a reviewed or audited financial statement, **or** file a **$50,000 surety bond** with DPOR (the Va. Code § 54.1-1106 contractor license bond, written by a corporate surety licensed in Virginia and approved by the Attorney General) [18][26][27][28].
5. **Application fee.** Approximately $385 (subject to periodic adjustment by Board regulation) plus exam fees.
6. **Renewal.** Class A licenses are issued for a **two-year cycle** and must be renewed before expiration; renewal includes **continuing education** for any trade designations (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, gas fitter) and re-attestation of financial responsibility [18][22][23][24][25].

**Class B application — Va. Code § 54.1-1108 and 18 VAC 50-22-50.** Class B requirements parallel Class A with reduced thresholds [1][3][6][8][9][18][22][23]:

1. **Designated Employee — Class B Business and Project Management Examination** (separate exam from Class A; same vendor, narrower scope).
2. **Qualified Individual — three (3) years of experience** in the classification/specialty; must pass trade exam.
3. **Pre-license education — eight (8) hours** (same as Class A).
4. **Net worth — $15,000** or **$50,000 surety bond** in lieu of net worth [26][27][28].
5. **Application fee.** Approximately $385 plus exam fees.
6. **Renewal.** Two-year cycle.

**Class C application — Va. Code § 54.1-1106 and 18 VAC 50-22-60.** Class C is the lowest tier and has the most reduced requirements [1][3][6][8][9][18][22][23][24]:

1. **No business / project management exam.** Class C applicants are not required to pass the DE business exam; instead, a member of responsible management must complete the pre-license education course.
2. **Qualified Individual — one (1) year of experience** in the classification/specialty; must pass the trade exam for each classification or specialty.
3. **Pre-license education — eight (8) hours** (same as Class A and B; must be completed by a member of responsible management).
4. **No net worth requirement and no bond required.**
5. **Application fee.** Approximately $235 plus exam fees.
6. **Renewal.** Two-year cycle.

### 1.4 Tradesman certification — DPOR Tradesmen Program, Va. Code § 54.1-1129 et seq.

Independent of the Board for Contractors' Class A/B/C contractor licensing, Virginia separately licenses **individual tradesmen** through the **DPOR Tradesmen Program** under **Va. Code § 54.1-1129 through § 54.1-1132** and **18 VAC 50-30** [16][17][29][30][31][32]. The Tradesmen Program regulates the individuals who perform the work in regulated trades, distinct from the firm-level contractor license.

**Regulated trades.** The Board for Contractors regulates individual tradesmen in the following disciplines [16][17][29][30]:

- **Electrical** (Master Electrician, Journeyman Electrician, Residential Electrical Mechanic)
- **Plumbing** (Master Plumber, Journeyman Plumber, **Residential Plumber** — effective April 1, 2025)
- **Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC)** (Master HVAC, Journeyman HVAC, **Residential HVAC Mechanic** — effective April 1, 2025)
- **Gas Fitting** (Master Gas Fitter, Journeyman Gas Fitter)
- **Water Well Construction**
- **Elevator Mechanic**
- **Backflow Prevention Device Worker**
- **Building Energy Analyst**

**Journeyman requirements.** Journeyman applicants must satisfy one of the qualifying experience-and-training combinations under 18 VAC 50-30, all premised on documented hands-on practical experience in the trade [16][17][29][30][31]:

- **Four (4) years of practical experience and 240 hours of formal vocational training**, or
- **Five (5) years of practical experience and 160 hours of vocational training**, or
- **Six (6) years of practical experience and 80 hours of vocational training**, or
- **Seven (7) years of practical experience and 40 hours of vocational training**, or
- **Ten (10) years of practical experience in the trade** without formal vocational training.

In each case the applicant must pass the journeyman examination for the trade (administered by PSI) with a 70% minimum score.

**Master requirements.** Master applicants must hold a journeyman license for at least **one (1) year** in the trade plus pass the master examination (broader scope, covering supervisory responsibilities and code interpretation) [16][17][29][30][31].

**Residential tradesman tier — effective April 1, 2025.** Under recent DPOR rulemaking (effective April 1, 2025) the Board created **Residential HVAC Mechanic** and **Residential Plumber** licenses as an **intermediate tier** between unlicensed worker and full Journeyman [16][17][29][30][33]. The residential tier requires less experience and vocational training than journeyman licensure and authorizes work **only on residential structures** (one- and two-family dwellings, townhouses). The residential tier was created in response to industry workforce shortages and is intended as a stepping stone toward journeyman licensure. Residential tradesmen who attempt commercial work are operating outside the license and are exposed to misdemeanor charges under § 54.1-1115 [16][17][29][30][33].

**Continuing education for journeyman and master tradesmen.** As a condition of renewal, all journeyman and master tradesmen must complete a **continuing education course** in each discipline in which they hold a license; the CE course is approximately three hours per discipline per renewal cycle [16][17][29][30][31].

**The interaction between Tradesman certification and the Class A/B/C contractor license.** This is one of the most error-prone interactions in Virginia construction law. A firm holding a **Class A Electrical Contractor (ELE) license** authorizes the **firm** to enter into electrical contracts above $120,000; but the **individuals who actually perform the work** must each hold their own journeyman or master electrician tradesman license [1][16][17][29][30]. The firm-level license does not "cover" the individual workers — a Class A ELE-licensed firm employing an unlicensed worker to perform electrical work is exposed to the unlicensed-worker violation under § 54.1-1115. Both the firm-level Class A/B/C license and the individual tradesman license must be in place.

**Universal License Recognition — Va. Code § 54.1-119 and tradesman reciprocity.** Effective 2020 and expanded since, Virginia recognizes out-of-state professional and occupational licenses on a Universal License Recognition (ULR) basis. A tradesman holding a substantially equivalent license in another state may apply to DPOR for recognition of that license in Virginia, subject to background, experience documentation, and Virginia-law-specific examination requirements. The ULR pathway is administered by DPOR's License Recognition Section and applies to tradesman licenses; it does **not** waive the Class A/B/C contractor license requirement [29][30][34].

### 1.5 Qualified Individual and Designated Employee — § 54.1-1106 and § 54.1-1108

The **Qualified Individual (QI)** and the **Designated Employee (DE)** are statutory roles that personally bind the firm to the regulatory scheme [1][3][22][23][24].

**Qualified Individual — trade-side anchor.** The QI must have the experience and exam credentials in the **classification or specialty** the firm is licensed to perform. For a firm licensed in multiple classifications (e.g., RBC + CBC + ELE), the QI must either (a) hold the qualifying experience and exam for each classification, or (b) the firm must designate multiple QIs, one per classification. The QI must have a genuine employment or ownership relationship with the firm — fabricated or "rented" QI arrangements expose both the licensee and the QI to license revocation and Class 1 misdemeanor charges under § 54.1-1115 [1][22][23].

**Designated Employee — business-side anchor.** The DE applies only to Class A and Class B firms and is the member of responsible management who has passed the **Business and Project Management Examination**. The DE need not be a tradesperson; the role is business-side accountability for the firm's contractual, financial, and compliance practices. A small contractor whose owner-operator passes both the trade exam (as QI) and the business exam (as DE) may hold both roles personally [22][23].

**Separation of QI or DE.** When the QI or DE separates from the firm (resignation, termination, death), the firm has **60 days** to designate a replacement QI or DE under 18 VAC 50-22-70 [22]. Failure to designate within 60 days suspends or revokes the firm's license. This is among the most-missed compliance obligations in practice — the QI's departure from the firm, without prompt replacement, terminates the firm's right to perform regulated work even if the firm continues to operate under the same DPOR license number.

### 1.6 Surety bond and Recovery Fund — Va. Code §§ 54.1-1106, 54.1-1120.1, 54.1-1118 et seq.

**The $50,000 contractor license bond — Va. Code § 54.1-1106 and § 54.1-1120.1.** A Class A or Class B applicant who cannot meet the net-worth requirement ($45,000 Class A; $15,000 Class B) may post a **$50,000 surety bond** in lieu of the net-worth showing [18][26][27][28]. The bond is issued by a corporate surety licensed in Virginia and approved by the Attorney General, runs to the benefit of any party harmed by the contractor's improper or dishonest conduct, and is filed with DPOR. The bond limit — $50,000 aggregate liability across all claimants and across the years the bond is in force — is fixed; the surety's exposure cannot exceed $50,000 regardless of the number of claims [26][28].

**Recovery on the bond — § 54.1-1120.1.** A person harmed by the licensee's improper or dishonest conduct may sue on the bond after obtaining a final judgment against the contractor. Recovery is limited to **actual monetary loss, court costs, and attorney fees** assessed against the contractor as part of the underlying judgment — punitive damages and pre-judgment interest are excluded [26][27][28].

**Virginia Contractor Transaction Recovery Fund — § 54.1-1118 through § 54.1-1127.** Separately from the contractor's individual bond, Virginia operates the **Contractor Transaction Recovery Fund** — a statutory pool funded by assessments on all DPOR contractor licensees that compensates consumers harmed by licensee misconduct when the contractor's own assets are insufficient [35][36][37][38].

**Per-transaction claim limit — Va. Code § 54.1-1123.** The Recovery Fund will pay a maximum of **$30,000 per claimant per single transaction** involving contracting [35][36][37]. The $30,000 cap is **inclusive of** any amount paid from the contractor's surety bond under § 54.1-1120.1 — i.e., a claimant receiving $20,000 from the bond may claim only an additional $10,000 from the Fund, not a fresh $30,000.

**Per-contractor aggregate cap.** The Board limits aggregate claims against the Fund based on unpaid judgments arising out of misconduct by any one contractor to **$100,000 per biennium** [35][36][37]. After $100,000 has been paid out on a single contractor's account within a biennium, additional claimants must wait until the next biennium or pursue alternative remedies.

**Excluded amounts.** The Recovery Fund will not pay interest, punitive damages, or other amounts that do not constitute actual monetary loss. Court costs and reasonable attorney fees are eligible [35][36][37].

**Funding mechanism.** The Fund is replenished through periodic assessments on contractor licensees; when the Fund balance falls below a statutory minimum, the Board may impose an assessment of up to $50 per licensee per biennium to restore the Fund [35][36].

### 1.7 Reciprocity — limited and trade-by-trade

Virginia does **not** maintain blanket NASCLA-based reciprocity for Class A/B/C contractor licenses. Out-of-state contractors must apply for a Virginia license through the standard process — Designated Employee exam, Qualified Individual exam (per classification/specialty), pre-license education, and net-worth or bond posting [1][22][23][34]. DPOR may, by regulation, accept a substantially equivalent out-of-state license for examination purposes — i.e., waive the trade exam if the applicant has passed a substantially equivalent exam in another state — but the Virginia Business and Project Management Examination and the pre-license education course are universal and are **not** waived by reciprocity [22][23][34].

For tradesmen, the **Universal License Recognition** pathway under § 54.1-119 is broader and more forgiving — a tradesman holding a journeyman license in another state with substantially equivalent experience and training may obtain Virginia tradesman recognition without retaking the journeyman exam, subject to background check and Virginia-specific code-update training [29][30][31][34].

### 1.8 Unlicensed contractor penalties — Va. Code § 54.1-111 and § 54.1-1115

Operating without the correct DPOR contractor license — wrong class, wrong classification, missing specialty designation, or no license at all — exposes the contractor to layered penalties under **Va. Code § 54.1-111** (the chapter-wide unlawful-acts statute) and **§ 54.1-1115** (the Contractors-Chapter prohibited-acts statute) [11][12][13][14].

**Criminal — Class 1 misdemeanor.** Under § 54.1-111(A) and § 54.1-1115(A), a contractor who willfully engages in regulated contracting without a license, or with the wrong class of license, or in a classification or specialty for which the contractor is not licensed, is guilty of a **Class 1 misdemeanor** — up to **12 months in jail and a $2,500 fine per offense** [11][12][13][14]. Under § 54.1-111(B), the **third or any subsequent conviction** for violating the section during a **36-month period** constitutes a **Class 6 felony** (up to 5 years in prison) [11][12].

**Civil — $500 per day.** Under § 54.1-111(E), in addition to the misdemeanor punishment, an unlicensed contractor is liable for a civil penalty of up to **$500 per day** for each day in violation [11][12][13].

**Consumer Protection Act exposure.** Under § 54.1-111(F), unlicensed contracting that involves a consumer transaction also constitutes a prohibited practice under the **Virginia Consumer Protection Act (Va. Code § 59.1-196 et seq.)**, exposing the contractor to that Act's remedies, including actual damages, attorney fees, and (for willful violations) treble damages [11][12].

**Contract unenforceability — § 54.1-1115(C).** A construction contract entered into by a person performing work without a valid Virginia contractor's license **shall not be enforceable by the unlicensed contractor**, with limited statutory exceptions [12][13][14]. The unlicensed contractor cannot sue to collect the contract price — meaning a homeowner who hires an unlicensed contractor, refuses to pay, and faces a collection suit can defend on the licensure defense and prevail. The unenforceability is **mandatory and unwaivable** — the parties cannot agree by contract to override the statute.

**The class-mismatch / classification-mismatch trap.** The unlicensed-contractor remedies under § 54.1-1115 apply not only to entirely unlicensed work but also to **work performed outside the contractor's license class or classification** [13][14]. A Class C contractor who takes a $40,000 project is operating outside the Class C scope and is "unlicensed" for purposes of § 54.1-1115 — even though the contractor holds a valid Class C DPOR license. A Class A RBC contractor who takes a commercial-building project is operating outside the RBC classification and is similarly "unlicensed" for purposes of that classification. The class-mismatch trap is particularly severe because the resulting contract is unenforceable, meaning even if the work is fully and competently performed the contractor cannot legally collect [13][14].

### 1.9 General liability insurance and workers' compensation as license conditions

Virginia does **not** impose a single statewide GL minimum as a precondition to the Class A/B/C license. GL insurance is contractually required by owners, lenders, and bonding companies but is not a statutory licensure prerequisite. Typical commercial floors are $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate; institutional and bonded public-works projects routinely require $2M/$4M to $5M/$10M aggregate [3][9][12].

**Workers' compensation.** Under Va. Code § 65.2-101, workers' compensation coverage is required for any Virginia employer with **three or more employees**, regardless of industry — see § 5 below for full treatment. Workers' compensation coverage is a condition of obtaining and maintaining the Class A/B/C contractor license; DPOR routinely verifies coverage at the time of license issuance and renewal [3][9][12][39][40].

### 1.10 Municipal building permits and short-term registration

Beyond the DPOR Class A/B/C license, Virginia contractors must obtain **building permits** from the local jurisdiction (county or city) for the work itself, and in some localities must complete a short **contractor registration** with the local permit office. The Uniform Statewide Building Code (USBC, 13 VAC 5-63) administered by the Virginia Department of Housing and Community Development (DHCD) sets the technical-code framework, with local building-permit administration by the locality's building official [3][9][41]. Unlike Ohio's municipal-license patchwork, Virginia localities **do not** issue substantive contractor licenses (because DPOR preempts the field); they administer permits and inspections under the USBC.

**Examples of local administrative overlay.** Fairfax County, Loudoun County, and Prince William County operate online permit-application portals through their respective departments of land development and code compliance; the City of Virginia Beach, the City of Norfolk, and the City of Richmond operate similar permit portals. Each requires the DPOR license number, certificate of insurance, and project-specific application; **none** issues a substantive contractor license parallel to or independent of DPOR.

**Business license tax — Business, Professional and Occupational License (BPOL) tax.** Most Virginia localities impose a **BPOL tax** on gross receipts of businesses operating in the locality. The BPOL tax is an annual tax administered by the local commissioner of the revenue, with rates varying by locality (typically 0.05% to 0.36% on construction gross receipts) and with filing/payment deadlines synchronized to the local fiscal year [42]. The BPOL is a local-tax obligation independent of the DPOR license and is the single most-missed compliance item for out-of-state contractors entering Virginia.

## 2. Mechanic's lien law — Va. Code § 43-1 et seq.

Virginia mechanic's lien law is codified at **Title 43 of the Code of Virginia (Mechanics' and Certain Other Liens)** and is, by reputation among practitioners, one of the most procedurally treacherous lien regimes in the United States [43][44][45][46][47]. The Virginia regime is distinctive in three respects:

1. **General contractors enjoy automatic lien rights** without any preliminary notice obligation — there is **no equivalent** of California's 20-day preliminary notice, North Carolina's 15-day Notice to Lien Agent, or Ohio's 21-day Notice of Furnishing for the prime contractor [43][44][45][46][47].
2. **The 90-day filing deadline runs from the last day of the month in which work was last performed** — not from the last day of work itself — a quirk that often gives the lien claimant an extra few days but also creates calculation traps [43][45][48][49].
3. **The 150-day "look-back" rule** caps the amount of any single lien memorandum to labor and materials furnished within the **150 days before the claimant's last day of work** — a substantive limitation independent of the 90-day filing deadline that has invalidated entire liens in Virginia Supreme Court cases [43][45][50][51].

### 2.1 Lien rights by claimant type — § 43-3, § 43-7, § 43-9

**Va. Code § 43-3** creates the mechanic's lien for "all persons performing labor or furnishing materials of the value of $150 or more, including the reasonable rental or use value of equipment, for the construction, removal, repair or improvement of any building or structure permanently annexed to the freehold" [43][44][45][46]. The lien attaches to the structure and to the land on which the structure stands, with the value of the lien limited to the contracting party's interest in the property.

**General contractor — § 43-4.** A general contractor (a contractor in **direct privity with the owner**) perfects a lien by filing a **Memorandum of Mechanic's Lien** with the clerk of the circuit court of the county or city where the property lies, not later than **90 days from the last day of the month in which** the GC last performed labor or furnished materials, and in no event later than 90 days from the time the building or structure is completed or the work otherwise terminated [43][45][48][49]. The GC has **no preliminary notice obligation** — the GC's direct contractual relationship with the owner provides constructive notice of the GC's involvement, and the GC's lien rights vest automatically [44][45].

**Subcontractor — § 43-7.** A subcontractor (a contractor with whom the GC has contracted) may perfect a lien by (i) giving **written notice to the owner** of the amount and character of the subcontractor's claim, and (ii) filing the same form of Memorandum of Mechanic's Lien with the clerk of the circuit court [44][45][46][52][53]. The subcontractor's lien is limited to **the amount the owner owes the GC** at the time the subcontractor's notice is given — a substantive cap that distinguishes Virginia from states (like North Carolina) that protect subs against waiver-of-lien-by-payment-to-prime [52][53].

**Sub-subcontractor or supplier — § 43-9.** A person furnishing labor or materials to a subcontractor (i.e., a sub-sub or supplier without privity to the GC) must give **written notice to both the owner AND the general contractor** of the amount and character of the claim, and file the Memorandum [44][45][46][52][53]. The sub-sub's lien is limited to **the amount the subcontractor could itself claim under § 43-7** — i.e., bounded by what the owner owes the GC at the time of the sub-sub's notice [52][53].

**Preliminary notice — § 43-11 (the optional "personal liability" upgrade).** Under § 43-11, a subcontractor or supplier **may** (but is not required to) give an early written **preliminary notice** to the owner, stating the nature and probable amount of the claimant's prospective claim. Upon receipt of the preliminary notice, the owner becomes **personally liable** to the claimant for amounts the owner thereafter pays to the GC without first ensuring payment to the noticing sub or supplier — a personal-liability upgrade that materially strengthens the claimant's leverage against the owner [44][45][52][53]. The § 43-11 preliminary notice is **not required** for lien rights; it is a separate, optional tool that triggers personal liability of the owner on top of the lien against the property.

### 2.2 The 90-day filing deadline — "last day of the month" quirk — § 43-4

The single most-litigated and most-misunderstood deadline in Virginia lien practice is the **90-day filing window** under **Va. Code § 43-4** [43][45][48][49][54][55].

**Statutory language.** Under § 43-4, the Memorandum of Mechanic's Lien must be recorded **"at any time after the work is commenced or material furnished, but not later than 90 days from the last day of the month in which he last performs labor or furnishes material, and in no event later than 90 days from the time such building, structure, or railroad is completed, or the work thereon otherwise terminated"** [43][48][49].

**The "last day of the month" mechanic.** Unlike most states (Ohio: "last furnishing date"; North Carolina: "last furnishing date"; Texas: "last furnishing date"), Virginia's 90-day clock starts from the **last day of the calendar month** in which the claimant last performed labor or furnished material — **not** from the actual last day of work itself [43][45][48][49][54]. Example: A subcontractor who completes work on **April 7, 2026** has 90 days from **April 30, 2026** (the last day of April) to file the memorandum — **not** 90 days from April 7. The deadline is **July 29, 2026** (90 days after April 30), giving the claimant approximately **113 days** from the actual last day of work to file [45][48][49][54]. A subcontractor whose last day of work is April 29, 2026, has the same July 29, 2026 deadline — 91 days from actual last work.

**The 90-day outer limit — "in no event later than 90 days from completion / termination."** The 90-day clock is **capped** at 90 days from the date the building, structure, or railroad is **completed** or the work is **otherwise terminated** — even if the claimant's own work continued or might have continued [43][45][48][49]. If the project is shut down on May 15 and the claimant's last day of work was May 10, the claimant's outer deadline is **August 13** (90 days from May 15 termination), not the later of (a) 90 days from May 31 (end of month of last work) or (b) the 90-day-from-termination cap. The completion / termination cap controls if it falls earlier than the end-of-month + 90.

**Strict construction by Virginia courts.** Virginia courts strictly construe the 90-day deadline. A memorandum filed one day late is invalid; there is no doctrine of substantial compliance, no equitable extension, and no good-faith exception. The Supreme Court of Virginia has repeatedly held that mechanic's lien filings are creatures of statute and that strict statutory compliance is jurisdictional [44][45][49][55][56].

### 2.3 The 150-day look-back rule — § 43-4

The second distinctive feature of Virginia lien practice — and a frequent invalidator of otherwise-timely liens — is the **150-day look-back rule** under § 43-4 [43][45][50][51][57].

**Statutory language.** "The lien claimant may file any number of memoranda but no memorandum filed pursuant to this chapter shall include sums due for labor or materials furnished more than 150 days prior to the last day on which labor was performed or material furnished to the job preceding the filing of such memorandum" [43][50].

**Practical effect.** The amount of any single lien memorandum is capped to labor and materials furnished within the **150-day window** that ends on the claimant's last day of work preceding the memorandum filing [45][50][51][57]. A subcontractor who performed work continuously from **January 1 through August 1** and files a memorandum on **October 1** can claim only labor and materials furnished within the 150 days before August 1 — i.e., from approximately **March 4 through August 1** [45][50][51]. The January and February labor — outside the 150-day window — is **not lienable** through that memorandum.

**Retainage and unbilled-amount carve-outs.** Two amounts are excluded from the 150-day cap [50][51][57]:

1. **Retainage** — sums withheld as retainage with respect to labor performed or materials furnished at any time before the memorandum filing, not to exceed **10% of the total contract price**. Retainage that has been earned but withheld may be claimed even if the underlying labor was performed more than 150 days before the memorandum date.
2. **Pay-when-received amounts** — sums not yet due because the party with whom the claimant contracted has not yet received funds from the owner or another third party. This carve-out interacts with the post-2023 ban on pay-if-paid clauses (see § 3 below) but continues to apply to amounts that are contractually withheld pending owner payment.

**Consequence of including pre-150-day amounts — entire lien invalid.** The Supreme Court of Virginia has held that **an entire mechanic's lien is invalid if the memorandum includes dollar amounts outside the 150-day window** — not partially invalid, not reformable, but wholly invalid [44][45][51]. The all-or-nothing rule means a claimant who reflexively claims the full contract balance in the memorandum, including amounts billed for work performed more than 150 days before last furnishing, loses the entire lien — even the amounts that **were** within the 150-day window. The remedy is to file **multiple successive memoranda**, each capped at the 150-day window before its own last-furnishing date, with the 90-day filing deadline applied separately to each [44][45][50][51].

**The interaction between 90-day filing and 150-day look-back.** Together, these two rules require Virginia lien claimants to **monitor their own first-and-last-furnishing dates monthly** and file periodic memoranda whenever the unbilled or unpaid amount approaches the 150-day window's outer edge. Practitioners advise clients to file a memorandum within the 90-day window after each calendar quarter of substantial work, both to preserve lien rights for that quarter and to avoid inadvertent inclusion of pre-150-day amounts [45][51][57].

### 2.4 Memorandum form and contents — § 43-5 and § 43-10

The Memorandum of Mechanic's Lien must be substantially in the statutory form provided by **§ 43-5** (general contractor's lien) or **§ 43-10** (subcontractor's, sub-subcontractor's, or supplier's lien) [44][45][58]. Required content includes:

1. **Name of the lien claimant** and the claimant's address.
2. **Name of the owner** of the property (and lessee or part-owner if applicable).
3. **Property description** sufficient to identify the property — typically legal description plus street address.
4. **Amount claimed** — the principal sum due, not including interest unless interest is itemized separately.
5. **Type of work** performed or material furnished.
6. **First and last dates** of labor or material furnished by the claimant.
7. **For § 43-7 and § 43-9 claimants**: the name of the contractor or subcontractor with whom the claimant contracted.

The memorandum must be **sworn under oath** before a notary or other officer authorized to administer oaths, and must be **acknowledged** for recording purposes [45][58].

**Certification of mailing — § 43-4 (general contractor).** A lien claimant who is a general contractor must file, along with the Memorandum of Mechanic's Lien, a **certification of mailing of a copy of the memorandum on the owner of the property at the owner's last known address** [43][44][45]. The certification-of-mailing requirement is unique to the general contractor's filing and does **not** apply to subcontractor or sub-subcontractor filings (because those claimants must already give notice to the owner under § 43-7 or § 43-9 as a precondition of perfection).

**Recording — clerk of the circuit court.** The memorandum is recorded with the **clerk of the circuit court** of the county or city where the property is located (Virginia does not have a separate county recorder; the clerk of the circuit court maintains the deed and lien book). Recording fees are set by Va. Code § 17.1-275 and run approximately $20 to $30 for the first page plus modest per-page additions [45][58].

### 2.5 Foreclosure suit — § 43-17 (six months or sixty days)

Under **Va. Code § 43-17**, no suit to enforce a perfected mechanic's lien shall be brought after **the later of**:

1. **Six (6) months** from the time the Memorandum of Mechanic's Lien was recorded, **or**
2. **Sixty (60) days** from the time the building, structure, or railroad was completed or the work thereon otherwise terminated [44][45][59][60].

The "whichever shall last occur" mechanic gives the claimant the more generous of the two periods. In the ordinary case — where the work was completed before the lien was filed — the six-month-from-recording deadline controls. In the rare case where the project is still under construction at the time of lien filing and substantial completion occurs more than five months after lien recording, the 60-days-from-completion alternative may extend the deadline beyond the six-month-from-filing date.

**Strict construction.** The 6-month / 60-day suit deadline, like the 90-day filing deadline, is strictly construed and is jurisdictional — failure to file the foreclosure complaint within the deadline **terminates the lien by operation of law** [44][45][59][60]. Virginia does not recognize equitable tolling or any good-faith exception.

**Suit filing fee and form.** Foreclosure suits are filed in the circuit court of the county or city where the property is located. The complaint joins the owner, all parties claiming an interest in the property (including lenders, other lienors, and tenants), and the claimant's contracting party. The court may enter a decree of sale subject to prior-perfected interests; in practice, most lien suits are settled or bonded off well before sale.

### 2.6 Bonding off the lien — Va. Code § 43-71

Under **Va. Code § 43-71**, a property owner (or other party with an interest) may **bond off** a recorded mechanic's lien by depositing with the clerk of the circuit court a **cash deposit or surety bond** in an amount equal to the lien claim plus court-determined costs and interest [45][61]. Upon bond posting, the lien is **released from the real property** and the bond stands as substitute security; the claimant's foreclosure suit then proceeds against the bond rather than the property. Bonding off is the standard owner-side mechanism to close a sale or refinancing without lien encumbrance and is routinely used in commercial transactions [45][61].

### 2.7 Priority — relation back to commencement of work

Virginia mechanic's liens generally **relate back** to the **date the lien claimant first performed labor or furnished materials** on the project, with priority running from that date relative to other recorded interests [44][45]. The relation-back doctrine differs from many states (which use the date of recording for priority) and means that a lien recorded late in the project may still have priority over a deed of trust recorded after the claimant's first day of work — even if the deed of trust was recorded before the lien memorandum.

**Construction loan priority.** Construction lenders mitigate the relation-back exposure through standard practices: requiring lien waivers from the GC at each draw, requiring the GC to provide sworn lien statements identifying all subs and suppliers, and obtaining title insurance with mechanic's-lien coverage. Title insurance underwriters in Virginia commonly require pre-loan inspections to verify that no visible work has commenced before the deed of trust is recorded.

### 2.8 Public works — no lien rights against publicly owned property; bond claim instead

Subcontractors and suppliers on Virginia **public construction projects** have **no mechanic's lien rights against publicly owned real property** — the Commonwealth and its political subdivisions cannot be subjected to a mechanic's lien. The recovery vehicle for unpaid subs and suppliers on public works is a claim on the prime contractor's **payment bond** under the **Virginia Public Procurement Act** (Va. Code § 2.2-4337) or the **Little Miller Act** for federal projects.

**Va. Code § 2.2-4337 — Payment Bond Requirement.** Public construction contracts above statutory threshold values must include a **payment bond** in the full amount of the contract, posted by the prime contractor and running to the benefit of subcontractors and suppliers. Subcontractors and suppliers may sue on the bond within **one year** of the day on which the person bringing the action last performed labor or furnished material [62].

**Notice and demand procedures.** Sub-subcontractors and suppliers without privity to the prime must give the prime contractor **written notice** of the claim within **90 days** of last labor or material before suing on the bond. The 90-day notice is a separate prerequisite from the 1-year suit deadline and is strictly enforced.

### 2.9 Lien waivers — enforceability and limitations

Virginia enforces written lien waivers when they are clear, knowing, and supported by consideration. Both **conditional progress-payment waivers** (effective upon receipt of payment) and **unconditional final waivers** (effective immediately) are valid. **Advance lien waivers** — those executed before labor or materials are furnished — are subject to more scrutiny and have been struck down in some cases as unconscionable or as contrary to the statutory lien framework.

**Effect of the 2023 pay-if-paid ban.** The 2022 amendment to Va. Code § 11-4.6 (effective January 1, 2023) banned pay-if-paid clauses in construction contracts (see § 3 below). The pay-if-paid ban does **not** directly invalidate lien waivers but materially shifts the bargaining dynamic — contractors and owners can no longer use pay-if-paid clauses to make lien rights effectively waivable by contractual conditioning of payment [63][64][65][66][67][68][69][70].

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

### 3.1 Statutory framework — 2022 SB 550 and 2023 HB 2500 / SB 1313

Virginia's prompt-payment regime was **dramatically rewritten** by **Senate Bill 550** of the 2022 General Assembly (effective **January 1, 2023**) and further refined by **House Bill 2500** and its Senate companion **SB 1313** of the 2023 General Assembly (effective **July 1, 2023**) [63][64][65][66][67][68][69][70][71]. The 2022-2023 reforms (i) imposed fixed payment deadlines on both public and private construction contracts, (ii) **banned pay-if-paid and pay-when-paid clauses** in most construction contracts, and (iii) created direct contractor liability to subcontractors for unpaid amounts even where the higher-tier party has not been paid by the owner. The current framework sits at **Va. Code § 2.2-4354** (public construction) and **Va. Code § 11-4.6** (private construction) [63][64][65][66][67][68][69][70][71].

### 3.2 Public construction prompt payment — Va. Code § 2.2-4354

Under **Va. Code § 2.2-4354**, every contract awarded by a state agency, and every contract awarded by a local public body in accordance with Va. Code § 2.2-4352, must include a payment clause that [63][64][65][66][67][72]:

1. **Obligates the prime contractor to pay each subcontractor** the proportionate share of any payment received from the public body for the subcontractor's work, by the earlier of:
   - **Seven (7) days** after receipt by the prime of payment from the public body for the subcontractor's work, or
   - **Sixty (60) days** after the subcontractor's invoice is received by the prime — even if the prime has not yet received payment from the public body for the subcontractor's work.

2. **Imposes joint-and-several liability** on the prime to the subcontractor for the entire amount owed, even if the prime has not received payment from the public body.

3. **Renders any pay-if-paid or pay-when-paid clause unenforceable** in the public construction context, with the limited exception below.

**The narrow contracting-party-insolvency exception.** A pay-if-paid or pay-when-paid clause remains enforceable **only if** the party contracting with the contractor (i.e., the public body or higher-tier party) is **insolvent or a debtor in bankruptcy** as defined in Va. Code § 50-73.79 [64][65][66][70][71]. The insolvency exception is the only remaining safe harbor for pay-if-paid clauses in Virginia public construction.

**Retainage carve-out for subcontract retainage.** The 60-day / 7-day payment deadlines and the pay-if-paid ban do **not apply to retainage** if the subcontract expressly provides for retainage withholding [63][66][72]. Retainage in subcontracts is governed by the subcontract terms (subject to the public-contract 5% maximum at the prime-to-public-body level under Va. Code § 2.2-4333 — see § 3.4 below).

**Professional services carve-out.** The deadlines and pay-if-paid ban do not apply to **professional services** contracts where the state agency has contracted directly with an architectural or engineering firm [66][68][72].

### 3.3 Private construction prompt payment — Va. Code § 11-4.6

For private construction, **Va. Code § 11-4.6** (as amended by SB 550 in 2022 and HB 2500 / SB 1313 in 2023) imposes parallel obligations [63][64][65][66][67][68][69][70][71]:

**Owner to prime contractor.** The owner on a private construction project must pay the prime within **sixty (60) days of receiving an invoice** following satisfactory completion of the work invoiced [64][65][66][68].

**Prime to subcontractor.** The prime contractor on a private project must pay each subcontractor by the earlier of:
- **Seven (7) days** after the prime receives payment from the owner for the subcontractor's work, or
- **Sixty (60) days** after the subcontractor's invoice is received by the prime [64][65][66][68].

**Pay-if-paid ban.** Va. Code § 11-4.6(D) makes pay-if-paid and pay-when-paid clauses **unenforceable** on private construction contracts, with the same narrow exception for contracting-party insolvency or bankruptcy under Va. Code § 50-73.79 [64][65][66][70][71].

**Interest on late payment.** Failure to pay within the statutory window triggers interest at the rate of **one percent (1%) per month** (i.e., 12% per annum) on the unpaid balance, unless a higher rate is provided by contract [66][68][72].

**No express attorney-fees provision in private context.** Unlike the public-construction context where Va. Code § 2.2-4354 and § 2.2-4356 provide for interest plus attorney fees in certain disputes, the private-construction § 11-4.6 does not include a fee-shifting provision; private prompt-pay disputes are governed by the parties' contract attorney-fee clauses, if any, plus the general American Rule [66][68].

**Wage theft overlay.** The 2023 amendments to § 11-4.6 added connections to Virginia's **wage theft law** (Va. Code § 40.1-29) — a contractor that willfully fails to pay subcontractor wages (which the subcontractor would owe to its own employees) may be exposed to **wage theft penalties**, including civil penalties, triple damages in some circumstances, and joint liability across the contracting chain. The wage theft connection is among the most aggressive enforcement features in the United States [66][67][68].

### 3.4 Retainage — public works at 5% maximum under Va. Code § 2.2-4333

For Virginia public construction contracts that provide for **progress payments in installments** based on estimated percentage of completion, **Va. Code § 2.2-4333** caps retainage at **five percent (5%)** of each progress payment [73][74][75]:

**Statutory language — § 2.2-4333(A).** "In any public contract for construction that provides for progress payments in installments based upon an estimated percentage of completion, the contractor shall be paid at least ninety-five percent of the earned sum when payment is due, with no more than five percent being retained to ensure faithful performance of the contract" [73][74][75].

**Application to subcontracts.** Under § 2.2-4333(B), "any subcontract for a public project that provides for similar progress payments shall be subject to the provisions of this section" — subcontractor retainage on public projects is also capped at 5% [73][74][75].

**Inclusion in final payment.** All amounts withheld may be included in the final payment after substantial completion and acceptance.

**No statutory escrow trigger.** Unlike Ohio's 50%-completion escrow trigger under ORC § 153.13, Virginia does not require a statutory escrow or interest-bearing deposit of retained funds. Retainage held by the public body sits on the public body's books pending release.

### 3.5 Retainage — private works (contract-based)

Private construction retainage in Virginia is **contractually negotiated** and is not statutorily capped — unlike the public-contract 5% maximum [73][74][75]. Owners and primes typically negotiate retainage of 5% to 10% on private commercial projects, with various release triggers (substantial completion, 50% completion, punchlist completion). The prompt-pay deadlines in § 11-4.6 govern payment timing once retainage is contractually due, with the 60-day / 7-day rule and the 1%-per-month interest penalty applying [63][66][68][72].

### 3.6 The interaction — pay-if-paid ban, mechanic's lien, and bond claims

The 2023 reforms interact with mechanic's lien and bond-claim rights in a way that materially strengthens subcontractor remedies in Virginia:

1. **Pay-if-paid clauses can no longer be used to suspend a sub's bond-claim or lien-claim rights pending owner payment to the prime.** Under § 11-4.6(D) and § 2.2-4354, the sub's right to payment from the prime accrues independently of the owner's payment to the prime — meaning the sub's right to invoke lien and bond remedies is not contractually deferred [66][67][68][70][71].
2. **The 150-day lien look-back under § 43-4 includes a carve-out for "sums not yet due" pending owner payment** — but the post-2023 prompt-pay regime now treats the sub's amounts as due at the 60-day / 7-day deadline regardless of owner payment, narrowing the practical scope of the carve-out.
3. **Sub claims accumulate clearly enforceable rights** — pay-when-paid cannot suspend the sub's lien recording deadline, the sub's 1-year bond suit deadline (public works), or the sub's prompt-pay claim under § 11-4.6.

## 4. Tax structure

### 4.1 Retail Sales and Use Tax — Va. Code § 58.1-600 et seq.

**State base sales tax rate:** Virginia imposes a state-level retail sales and use tax of **4.3%** under **Va. Code § 58.1-603** [76][77][78][79]. The state rate is among the lowest in the country and has been stable at 4.3% since 2013 (it increased from 4.0% to 4.3% under the 2013 transportation funding package).

**Mandatory statewide local 1% add-on — Va. Code § 58.1-605.** Every locality in Virginia imposes a **1.0% local option sales tax** under Va. Code § 58.1-605 [76][77][78][79]. The 1% local add-on is uniform across all 95 counties and 38 independent cities, yielding a **combined statewide minimum rate of 5.3%** for sales-tax-eligible transactions.

**Regional add-ons — Va. Code § 58.1-603.1.** Two regions impose an additional **0.7% regional sales tax** under Va. Code § 58.1-603.1 [78][79][80]:

1. **Northern Virginia Region** — Cities of Alexandria, Fairfax, Falls Church, Manassas, Manassas Park; Counties of Arlington, Fairfax, Loudoun, Prince William.
2. **Hampton Roads Region** — Cities of Chesapeake, Franklin, Hampton, Newport News, Norfolk, Poquoson, Portsmouth, Suffolk, Virginia Beach, Williamsburg; Counties of Isle of Wight, James City, Southampton, York.

In these regions, the combined rate is **6.0%** (4.3% state + 1.0% local + 0.7% regional).

**Additional historic transit and special-purpose surcharges.** Several localities have voter-approved additional local sales taxes for specific purposes (e.g., Williamsburg-James City County / York County tourism, Charlottesville, Halifax County), pushing combined rates up to **7.0%** in some jurisdictions [76][77][78][79][80].

| 6.0% | Northern Virginia (Alexandria, Arlington, Fairfax, Falls Church, Loudoun, Manassas, Manassas Park, Prince William); Hampton Roads (Chesapeake, Franklin, Hampton, Isle of Wight, James City, Newport News, Norfolk, Poquoson, Portsmouth, Southampton, Suffolk, Virginia Beach, Williamsburg, York) |
| 7.0% | Historic Triangle (Williamsburg, James City County, York County) with the additional 1.0% tourism tax; certain other special-tax localities |

Contractors must compute sales tax on materials at the **delivery / use location** rate — not the contractor's office rate — and must verify the rate in effect on the delivery date through the Virginia Department of Taxation's published rate tables (which list combined rates by county and city) [76][77][78][79].

### 4.2 Construction contractor as consumer — Va. Code § 58.1-610 and 23 VAC 10-210-410

Virginia treats the construction contractor as the **consumer** of tangible personal property incorporated into real property — the standard "contractor as ultimate consumer" model shared with most states [81][82][83][84][85].

**Statutory definition — Va. Code § 58.1-610.** Under § 58.1-610(A): "Any person who contracts orally, in writing, or by purchase order, to perform construction, reconstruction, installation, repair, or any other service with respect to real estate or fixtures thereon, and in connection therewith to furnish tangible personal property, shall be deemed to have purchased such tangible personal property for use or consumption" [81][82][83][84]. The contractor pays sales tax at the supply house (or use tax on out-of-state purchases) at the rate in effect at the delivery / use location.

**Regulatory implementation — 23 VAC 10-210-410.** Virginia Administrative Code § 23 VAC 10-210-410 (Contractors respecting real estate) provides the implementing rules [82][83][85]:

1. The construction contractor is the **user or consumer** of tangible personal property furnished in connection with real property construction, reconstruction, installation, repair, and similar contracts.
2. The contractor's purchase of materials is a **retail sale** subject to tax at the time of purchase by the contractor; the contractor cannot purchase materials on resale and pass the sales-tax burden to the property owner.
3. The contractor's labor and service component — the value added to the materials in the contracted-for service of installing them in real property — is **not separately taxed** as a service.

**Tax Bulletin 17-8 (Real Property Contractor Sales and Use Tax Guidelines).** The Department of Taxation has published comprehensive guidance in **TB 17-8**, covering the contractor-as-consumer treatment, special rules for retailers who also perform installation services (the "dual operator" rules), and exemption-certificate procedures for tax-exempt projects [82][83][84].

**Exemption for government and certain non-profit projects.** A contractor performing real-property construction for a state agency, federal agency, or qualifying non-profit may purchase materials free of sales tax if the materials would otherwise be exempt if purchased directly by the exempt entity. The exemption is administered through **Form ST-11A** (Sales and Use Tax Certificate of Exemption) or project-specific exemption letters from the Tax Commissioner.

**Effective July 1, 2024 — erroneous-collection correction mechanism.** A 2024 statutory amendment allows contractors who **erroneously collected sales tax from customers** (treating themselves as retailers rather than consumers) to have the erroneously remitted tax applied to a **use tax assessment for the same transaction** [82]. This is a procedural relief mechanism that eliminates double-tax exposure when a contractor's classification has been misapplied.

**The "dual operator" trap — retailers who also install.** A merchant who both **sells tangible personal property at retail** (e.g., an appliance store) **and** **installs it as part of a service contract** is a "dual operator" and must apply different sales-tax treatments depending on the transaction. Sales of tangible personal property to consumers (without installation) are retail sales, with the retailer collecting tax from the consumer. Sales-with-installation are treated as real-property construction, with the retailer paying tax on its acquisition cost as consumer. The dual-operator treatment requires careful invoicing and recordkeeping; a retailer who fails to separate the two transaction types may face audit assessments [82][83][84].

### 4.3 Business, Professional and Occupational License (BPOL) tax

Most Virginia localities impose a **BPOL tax** on business gross receipts under **Va. Code § 58.1-3700 et seq.** [42]. The BPOL tax is an annual locality-level tax administered by each locality's commissioner of the revenue. Rates and structure vary substantially:

- **Rates.** Most jurisdictions impose BPOL on contractor gross receipts at rates between **$0.05 and $0.36 per $100 of gross receipts** (i.e., 0.05% to 0.36%). Rates vary by locality and by business category — contractors are typically categorized separately from retail, services, and professionals.
- **Filing.** Annual return due in the early spring (most jurisdictions: March 1) covering the prior calendar year's gross receipts.
- **Apportionment.** Contractors operating in multiple localities must allocate gross receipts to each locality where work was performed; the locality-by-locality filing burden is one of the heavier compliance items for multi-locality contractors.
- **Out-of-locality contractors.** A contractor with no fixed place of business in a Virginia locality but performing work there is generally subject to a flat BPOL fee (often $30 to $50) plus the gross-receipts component on receipts attributable to work performed in the locality.

The BPOL is the single most-missed compliance item for out-of-state contractors entering Virginia for short-duration work — the contractor may register with DPOR, obtain the building permit, and complete the job without ever registering with the locality's commissioner of the revenue, only to receive a BPOL assessment notice months later [42].

### 4.4 Other taxes — overview

- **Virginia Corporate Income Tax (Va. Code § 58.1-400 et seq.).** Virginia imposes a flat 6% corporate income tax on net income apportioned to Virginia. Pass-through entities (partnerships, S corporations, LLCs taxed as partnerships) pass income to owners reporting on personal returns.
- **Virginia Individual Income Tax.** Virginia imposes a graduated personal income tax (2% to 5.75%) on individuals, applicable to construction-industry sole proprietors, partners, and S-corp shareholders.
- **Virginia Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET — Va. Code § 58.1-390.1 et seq.).** Effective for tax years 2021 and forward (and amended several times since), Virginia permits pass-through entities to elect to be taxed at the entity level at 5.75%, with a corresponding refundable credit at the owner level — designed as a workaround for the federal SALT cap.
- **Property tax — local.** Real and tangible personal property are taxed at the local level by each county and city; commercial construction equipment is subject to tangible personal property tax in most jurisdictions, with rates varying widely (typically $1.50 to $4.50 per $100 of assessed value annually).
- **Withholding and unemployment taxes.** Virginia imposes employer withholding on employee wages (administered by the Department of Taxation) and unemployment insurance contributions (administered by the Virginia Employment Commission) under Va. Code Title 60.2.

## 5. Workers' compensation — Virginia Workers' Compensation Commission

Virginia is a **private-market workers' compensation state** — unlike Ohio, Washington, North Dakota, or Wyoming (monopoly states), Virginia employers obtain workers' compensation coverage from competitive private-market carriers (Travelers, Hartford, Liberty Mutual, CNA, Berkshire Hathaway, AmTrust, etc.) [39][40][86][87]. The system is administered by the **Virginia Workers' Compensation Commission (VWCC)** at workcomp.virginia.gov, with statutory framework at **Va. Code Title 65.2**.

### 5.1 Statutory framework — Va. Code Title 65.2

Virginia workers' compensation law is at **Va. Code Title 65.2** (Workers' Compensation), administered by the Virginia Workers' Compensation Commission [39][40][86][87]. Title 65.2 covers definitions (§ 65.2-101), coverage scope (§ 65.2-300 et seq.), benefits (§§ 65.2-500 through 65.2-708), procedure (§ 65.2-700 et seq.), and special provisions for construction-industry contractors (§§ 65.2-302 and 65.2-309).

### 5.2 The three-employee threshold — Va. Code § 65.2-101

Under **Va. Code § 65.2-101**, the definition of "employee" excludes employees of any person, firm, or private corporation that "has regularly in service less than three employees in the same business within this Commonwealth, unless such employees and their employers voluntarily elect to be bound by this title" [39][40][87][88][89]. The practical effect: a Virginia employer must obtain workers' compensation coverage if it **regularly employs three (3) or more employees** in the Commonwealth.

**"Regularly in service" — case-law gloss.** The "regularly in service" standard is not a strict head count at any single moment but considers whether the employer routinely operates with three or more employees during the year. A contractor with two full-time employees plus a part-time worker who works 20 hours per week, year-round, is "regularly" employing three or more and is subject to coverage. A contractor who occasionally hires a third worker for a one-week busy season may fall below the threshold, but VWCC enforcement is fact-specific and the threshold is interpreted generously in favor of coverage.

**Voluntary election.** An employer below the three-employee threshold may **voluntarily elect coverage** under § 65.2-101 by obtaining a policy and filing notice with the Commission. Election is irrevocable for the policy period.

**Construction-industry exception — Va. Code § 65.2-101 and § 65.2-302.** The "less than three employees" exemption is **substantially narrowed for construction-industry employers** through the **statutory employer** doctrine of **Va. Code § 65.2-302** and the **subcontractor coverage** rules of **§ 65.2-309** [39][40][88][89][90]. See §§ 5.4 and 5.5 below.

### 5.3 Construction-industry employer obligations

**Construction industry — special rules.** Virginia treats the construction industry distinctively in its workers' comp scheme [39][40][86][87][88][89][90]:

1. **The statutory employer doctrine — Va. Code § 65.2-302.** A general contractor (or any party that contracts work to another party) is the **statutory employer** of the contracted party's employees for workers' comp purposes, and is liable to provide coverage if the contracted party fails to do so. The statutory-employer liability runs upward from the lowest-tier sub through the chain to the prime, making the prime liable for an uninsured sub-sub's employee's injury.
2. **Subcontractor coverage — Va. Code § 65.2-309.** A prime contractor that engages a subcontractor must verify the sub's workers' compensation coverage; failure to verify exposes the prime to direct statutory-employer liability under § 65.2-302.
3. **Effective lower threshold.** Through the statutory-employer doctrine, the practical effect for construction primes is that **any** sub or sub-sub on a Virginia construction site is treated as part of the prime's "employee" population for premium-recapture and injury-claim purposes if the sub lacks its own coverage. The three-employee threshold protects only the bottom of the construction food chain (the smallest single-employee sub), and even then, only if the upstream chain is properly covered.

### 5.4 The statutory employer doctrine — Va. Code § 65.2-302

**Va. Code § 65.2-302(A).** "When any person (referred to as 'owner') undertakes to perform or execute any work which is a part of his trade, business, or occupation and contracts with any other person (referred to as 'subcontractor') for the execution or performance by or under such subcontractor of the whole or any part of the work undertaken by such owner, the owner shall be liable to pay to any worker employed in the work any compensation under this title which he would have been liable to pay if the worker had been immediately employed by him" [88][89].

**Trade, business, or occupation test.** The statutory-employer doctrine applies only when the contracted work is part of the prime's **trade, business, or occupation** — not merely incidental work outside the prime's core activities. Construction-industry primes are routinely held to be statutory employers of their subs' employees because subcontracted construction work is core to the prime's business.

**Section 65.2-309 enforcement.** Under § 65.2-309, a prime contractor that fails to require workers' comp coverage from its subcontractors is liable for the unpaid premium and for any uncovered injury claim arising on the project.

### 5.5 Sole proprietors, partners, and corporate officers

Under **Va. Code § 65.2-101** and 16 VAC 30-50 et seq., the following are excluded from the default definition of "employee" [39][40][87][88]:

- **Sole proprietors** (the individual owner).
- **Partners** of a partnership.
- **Members of an LLC** classified as a partnership for federal tax purposes.
- **Corporate officers** in certain narrow circumstances (executive officers electing to be excluded under specific conditions).

**Elective coverage.** An excluded individual may elect coverage by including the individual's anticipated remuneration in the employer's payroll reporting and obtaining the elective coverage endorsement on the workers' comp policy. Election is policy-specific and must be properly documented.

**Construction-industry sole proprietor pitfall.** A construction-industry sole proprietor who personally performs work on a Virginia jobsite and engages no employees is exempt from the three-employee threshold — but if the sole proprietor sub-contracts work or hires day laborers, the sole proprietor becomes the statutory employer of those workers under § 65.2-302 and faces full premium and injury exposure.

### 5.6 Premium structure — NCCI classifications and competitive private market

Virginia premium rates are set through the competitive private market, with each carrier filing rates with the Virginia Bureau of Insurance and rating each employer through National Council on Compensation Insurance (NCCI) experience modifications [86][87]. Construction-industry rates are among the higher classifications in the NCCI catalog:

- **5022** — Masonry
- **5057** — Iron and steel erection
- **5183** — Plumbing
- **5190** — Electrical (interior wiring)
- **5403** — Carpentry (light)
- **5474** — Painting
- **5538** — HVAC, sheet metal work
- **5645** — Carpentry, residential
- **5704** — Carpentry, heavy

Carriers apply experience modifiers based on the employer's three-year loss history; modifiers above 1.0 increase premium, modifiers below 1.0 (loss-controlled employers) decrease premium. Construction-industry contractors with active safety programs routinely achieve EMRs of 0.75 to 0.90 through claims management.

### 5.7 Penalties for failure to carry coverage

Failure of a Virginia employer subject to the three-employee threshold (or to the construction-industry statutory-employer doctrine) to maintain workers' comp coverage exposes the employer to [39][40][87][89]:

- **Civil penalties** of up to **$250 per day** for each day of uninsured operation, plus the cost of any uncovered claim that arises during the uninsured period.
- **Class 1 misdemeanor** for willful violations under Va. Code § 65.2-805 — up to 12 months in jail and $2,500 fine per offense.
- **Loss of the workers' compensation exclusive-remedy defense** — an injured employee may sue the employer directly in tort with no statutory cap on damages.
- **DPOR licensing exposure** — DPOR considers workers' comp non-compliance grounds for license suspension or revocation under § 54.1-1106 and § 54.1-1108.

## 6. Recent legislative changes (2024-2026)

### 6.1 2022 SB 550 — Prompt payment overhaul and pay-if-paid ban (effective January 1, 2023)

The most consequential single change to Virginia construction law in recent years was **SB 550 of the 2022 General Assembly**, which amended Va. Code §§ 2.2-4354 and 11-4.6 to (i) impose fixed payment deadlines on owner-to-prime, prime-to-sub, and sub-to-lower-tier payment chains; (ii) **ban pay-if-paid and pay-when-paid clauses** in public and private construction contracts; and (iii) impose joint-and-several liability on primes for subcontractor amounts regardless of whether the prime has been paid by the owner [63][64][66][67][68][69][70][71]. SB 550 became effective **January 1, 2023** and put Virginia among the minority of jurisdictions (with New York, North Carolina, Illinois, South Carolina, and a handful of others) that prohibit pay-if-paid clauses by statute [63][64][65][66][67].

### 6.2 2023 HB 2500 / SB 1313 — Prompt payment refinements (effective July 1, 2023)

The 2023 General Assembly enacted **HB 2500 and SB 1313**, signed by Governor Glenn Youngkin on March 26, 2023 and effective **July 1, 2023**, amending Va. Code §§ 2.2-4377, 2.2-4354, and 11-4.6 to refine the prompt-pay regime [63][64][65][66][67][68][69][70][71]. Key 2023 refinements:

1. **Clarified the 7-day / 60-day pay deadlines** — payment is due from prime to sub by the **earlier of** 7 days after prime receives owner payment **or** 60 days after the sub's invoice, regardless of whether the prime has been paid for the sub's specific work.
2. **Added retainage carve-out** — the 7-day / 60-day deadlines and pay-if-paid ban do not apply to retainage if the subcontract expressly provides for retainage.
3. **Added professional services carve-out** — exempting architectural and engineering professional services from the pay-if-paid ban.
4. **Tied wage theft liability** (Va. Code § 40.1-29) to the prompt-pay framework — a contractor failing to pay subcontractor wages is exposed to wage theft penalties including triple damages in some circumstances.

### 6.3 2024 — Sales tax procedure for erroneously collected tax (effective July 1, 2024)

A 2024 amendment to Va. Code § 58.1-625 / § 58.1-625.1 allowed contractors who erroneously collected sales tax from customers (treating themselves as retailers rather than consumers) to have the erroneously remitted tax applied to a use tax assessment for the same transaction — eliminating double-tax exposure in misclassified contractor transactions [82][83]. Effective **July 1, 2024**.

### 6.4 2025 — Residential trades licensing tier (effective April 1, 2025)

Effective **April 1, 2025**, DPOR created the **Residential HVAC Mechanic** and **Residential Plumber** license tiers as an intermediate pathway between unlicensed worker and full Journeyman in those trades [16][17][29][30][33]. The residential tier requires less experience and vocational training than journeyman licensure (specifically: a reduced experience requirement, paired with less formal vocational training) and authorizes work **only on residential structures** (one- and two-family dwellings, townhouses). Residential tradesmen who attempt commercial work are operating outside their license and are exposed to misdemeanor charges under § 54.1-1115. The residential tier was created in response to workforce shortages and is intended as a stepping stone toward journeyman licensure.

### 6.5 2025 — Contractor Recovery Fund — claim limit increases

A 2025 General Assembly amendment (Acts of Assembly 2025, ch. 127) increased the per-transaction Contractor Recovery Fund claim limit from $20,000 to **$30,000** and the per-contractor biennium aggregate cap from $40,000 to **$100,000** under Va. Code § 54.1-1123 [35][36][37]. The increases recognize inflation in construction-claim values since the prior 2010s-era caps and materially strengthen consumer protection for victims of contractor misconduct.

### 6.6 2025 Universal License Recognition expansion — SB 1188

**SB 1188 of the 2025 General Assembly** directed the boards within DPOR (including the Board for Contractors) to develop regulations providing a **pathway to licensure for individuals with equivalent qualifications from another country**, expanding the Universal License Recognition pathway under Va. Code § 54.1-119 to include international credentials [34]. Implementing regulations were under development through 2025 and 2026.

### 6.7 2026 — Public works legislation (varies; HB 569 / SB 518, HB 1046 / SB 324)

The **2026 General Assembly** considered several pieces of legislation affecting public works contracting, including HB 569 (Feggans) / SB 518 (Rouse) and HB 1046 (Carr) / SB 324 (McPike), addressing aspects of public works procurement, prevailing wage requirements, and project labor agreements. The bills were amended during the legislative process and the scope and effective dates of any enacted versions should be verified through lis.virginia.gov [91][92]. Contractors should monitor the 2026 session's enacted statutes for changes effective July 1, 2026 (the default effective date for non-emergency legislation).

### 6.8 Sales tax — Northern Virginia and Hampton Roads regional rates (unchanged)

The 6.0% combined sales tax in Northern Virginia and Hampton Roads (4.3% state + 1.0% local + 0.7% regional) has been in place since 2013 (Northern Virginia) and 2013-2014 (Hampton Roads) and was unchanged in 2024-2026 sessions [78][79][80]. Contractors should continue to compute tax at the delivery / use location rate.

## 7. Practical compliance checklist

### 7.1 The Class A/B/C tier — easy to misclassify and operate outside the class

The most common DPOR violation is operating outside the license class — a Class C contractor taking a $40,000 single project, a Class B contractor crossing the Class A single-project or annual-aggregate thresholds, or a Class A contractor whose net-worth attestation has lapsed during a renewal cycle [11][12][13][14][18][19]. The compliance discipline:

1. **Monthly aggregate tracking.** Every Class B and Class C contractor must track running annual aggregate of contracts entered into over the trailing 12 months — not just the calendar year — and must trigger a class upgrade application **before** crossing the threshold, not after.
2. **Single-project value at signing, not at billing.** The class threshold is keyed to the **value of the single contract or project**, not to the amount actually billed. A Class B contractor signing a $135,000 project crossed the Class B single-project ceiling at signing and is operating outside the class on day one — even if only $20,000 has been invoiced by month-end.
3. **Classification scope discipline.** A Class A RBC contractor cannot take a commercial-building project even within the dollar range of the RBC license — RBC excludes commercial structures. A Class A ELE contractor cannot take a plumbing scope. A specialty designation (e.g., HIC) carries narrower scope than a major classification.
4. **QI continuity.** If the firm's Qualified Individual separates from the firm (resignation, death, termination), the firm has **60 days** to designate a replacement under 18 VAC 50-22-70. Operating without a QI past the 60-day window suspends the license [22].

### 7.2 The 90-day "last day of the month" lien quirk

The 90-day lien filing window under Va. Code § 43-4 runs from **the last day of the month** in which the claimant last performed labor or furnished material — not from the actual last day of work [43][45][48][49][54][55]. The compliance discipline:

1. **Calendar the last day of the month, not the last day of work.** If a subcontractor's last day of work is April 7, the lien filing deadline is approximately July 29 (90 days from April 30). The 90-day clock starts at April 30, not April 7.
2. **The completion / termination cap also runs.** The 90-day clock is capped at 90 days from the date the project is **completed** or the work otherwise **terminated** — whichever falls earlier than the end-of-month-plus-90 calculation.
3. **Monthly successive memoranda for long-running projects.** Because the 150-day look-back caps each memorandum's lienable amount to labor and materials furnished within 150 days before that memorandum's last-furnishing date, long-running projects (more than 5 months) routinely require **multiple successive lien filings** to preserve the full unpaid balance [44][45][50][51].

### 7.3 The 150-day "look-back" rule — all-or-nothing

The 150-day look-back rule under § 43-4 caps each memorandum's lienable amount to labor and materials furnished within the 150 days before that memorandum's last-furnishing date [44][45][50][51]. The compliance discipline:

1. **Never reflexively claim the full contract balance.** A long-running subcontract may have unpaid amounts going back 12 months; including any amount outside the 150-day window invalidates the entire memorandum.
2. **File successive memoranda.** A claimant may file any number of memoranda under § 43-4. The correct practice on a long-running project is to file periodic memoranda each capped at the 150-day window, with the retainage and "not yet due" carve-outs separately considered.
3. **Retainage carve-out — 10% maximum.** Retainage of up to 10% of the contract price may be claimed in any single memorandum even if the underlying work was performed outside the 150-day window.

### 7.4 Prompt payment — 7-day / 60-day window post-2023

Under the post-2023 prompt-pay regime, primes must pay subs by the **earlier of 7 days after receipt of owner payment for that sub's work or 60 days after the sub's invoice** [63][64][65][66][67][68][72]. The compliance discipline:

1. **Invoice-tracking infrastructure.** The 60-day clock runs from invoice receipt, not from owner-payment receipt. A prime that delays invoice processing internally still accumulates 60-day exposure on each unpaid sub invoice.
2. **Pay-if-paid clauses are unenforceable.** Subcontract pay-if-paid clauses cannot be used to defer prime-to-sub payment past the 60-day mark; the prime is liable on the contract regardless of owner payment status (except in the narrow insolvency / bankruptcy exception).
3. **1% per month interest accrues.** Late payment triggers 1% per month interest (12% per annum) plus potential wage-theft liability under § 40.1-29 if the unpaid amount represents wages downstream.

### 7.5 BPOL — the locality-by-locality gross-receipts tax

Out-of-state contractors entering Virginia for short-duration projects routinely miss the BPOL tax in the work locality [42]. The compliance discipline:

1. **Register with the locality's commissioner of the revenue.** Each county or city where work is performed requires a BPOL registration — typically a flat fee ($30 to $50) plus annual gross-receipts return.
2. **Apportion gross receipts.** Contractors operating in multiple Virginia localities must apportion gross receipts to each locality.

### 7.6 Workers' compensation — three-employee threshold + construction statutory employer

Construction-industry contractors must understand both the three-employee threshold under § 65.2-101 and the statutory-employer doctrine under § 65.2-302 [39][40][88][89]. The compliance discipline:

1. **Three-employee threshold.** Any Virginia employer with three or more regularly employed workers must obtain workers' comp coverage; the construction-industry statutory employer doctrine effectively narrows the practical exemption to single-person sole-proprietor operations.
2. **Subcontractor verification.** Every Virginia construction prime must obtain a certificate of workers' comp coverage from each subcontractor before granting site access; the prime is liable as statutory employer for any uninsured sub's employee injury.
3. **Class C / small-contractor pitfall.** A Class C residential remodeler with two helpers (three total in service) crosses the threshold and must obtain coverage; many small contractors mistakenly believe the three-employee threshold counts only employees other than the owner.

### 7.7 Sales tax — contractor as consumer at delivery location rate

Construction contractors purchase materials as **consumers**, paying sales tax at the supply-house or delivery location rate at the time of purchase [81][82][83][84][85]. The compliance discipline:

1. **Verify delivery / use rate.** Northern Virginia (6.0%) and Hampton Roads (6.0%) carry a 0.7% regional add-on above the 5.3% statewide minimum; Historic Triangle (7.0%) carries an additional tourism tax.
2. **Do not charge sales tax separately on real-property construction.** Contractor labor and the installed cost of materials in a real-property construction contract are not separately taxable; charging sales tax to the customer on the contract price misclassifies the transaction and exposes the contractor to potential refund liability (though the 2024 statutory amendment provides a procedure for correcting erroneously collected tax against the corresponding use tax assessment).
