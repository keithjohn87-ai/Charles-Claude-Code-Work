## 1. Licensing

### 1.1 Licensing authority

The **Contractors State License Board (CSLB)** sits within the California Department of Consumer Affairs and is the sole state agency that issues, renews, suspends, and revokes contractor licenses [1][2]. The CSLB was established by Business and Professions Code § 7000.5 in 1929, making it among the oldest contractor-licensing authorities in the United States.

- **Headquarters:** 9821 Business Park Drive, Sacramento, CA 95827
- **Mailing:** P.O. Box 26000, Sacramento, CA 95826
- **Phone:** 1-800-321-CSLB (2752)
- **Web:** https://www.cslb.ca.gov

The CSLB's sunset date — the date after which the Legislature must reauthorize the Board — was most recently extended to **January 1, 2029** by Senate Bill 1455 (2024), enacted as part of the broader contractor-licensing reform package discussed in §1.9 below [3].

### 1.2 Statutory authority

The licensing scheme is established by the **Contractors' State License Law** at **Business and Professions Code §§ 7000 through 7191** [1][4]. Substantive licensure provisions are at §§ 7065 through 7077.5; disciplinary provisions are at §§ 7090 through 7124.6; and unlicensed-practice and consumer-protection provisions are at §§ 7028 through 7059.1. The Board's own administrative rules are codified at **Title 16, California Code of Regulations (16 CCR) §§ 810 through 887**, with the primary classification rules at 16 CCR §§ 830 through 832.62 [5].

### 1.3 Project value threshold — **MATERIAL 2025 CHANGE**

Under **Business and Professions Code § 7048** (the "handyperson exemption"), unlicensed work is exempt from the licensure requirement of § 7028 **only if** the aggregate contract price for labor, materials, and all other items is less than the statutory threshold and the work is of a "casual, minor, or inconsequential" nature. **Assembly Bill 2622 (Stats. 2024, Ch. 248), effective January 1, 2025, raised this threshold from $500 to $1,000**, the first increase since 1939 [6][7]. The exemption applies only when:

1. The total aggregate contract price (labor + materials) is **less than $1,000**.
2. The work does **not** require a building permit.
3. The work is performed by the unlicensed person alone, **without employees, subcontractors, or other workers**.
4. The work is not part of a larger or major operation that would, in the aggregate, exceed $1,000 — splitting a larger job into sub-$1,000 pieces to evade licensure is itself a violation of § 7048 [6][7].

Performing work that requires a license without holding one is a **misdemeanor** under **Business and Professions Code § 7028(a)**, punishable by up to six (6) months in county jail and a fine of up to **$5,000** on a first conviction. A **second conviction** mandates a fine of 20% of the contract price (or $5,000, whichever is greater) and a minimum 90-day jail term; a third or subsequent conviction mandates a fine between $5,000 and $10,000 (or 20% of the contract price, if greater) and 90 days to one year of jail [8][9]. In addition, § 7031(a) prohibits an unlicensed contractor from suing to collect compensation for any unlicensed work, and § 7031(b) entitles the project owner to **disgorgement of every dollar paid** to the unlicensed contractor regardless of whether the work was performed satisfactorily [4][8].

### 1.4 License classifications relevant to ContrPro's five trades

CSLB classifications are codified at **16 CCR §§ 832.01 et seq.** [5][10] and detailed in the CSLB's *Description of Classifications* publication [10]. The classifications and trade-exam requirements relevant to ContrPro's five trades are:

| Trade | CSLB Classification | Trade Exam | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| General building (commercial / multi-trade) | **B — General Building Contractor** (requires structures whose construction uses ≥ 2 unrelated trades) | Required | 16 CCR § 832.02 [10] |
| General engineering (heavy / civil) | **A — General Engineering Contractor** (fixed works for which specialized engineering knowledge is required) | Required | 16 CCR § 832.01 [10] |
| Mechanical — HVAC | **C-20 — Warm-Air Heating, Ventilating and Air-Conditioning** | Required | 16 CCR § 832.20 [10] |
| Mechanical — refrigeration | **C-38 — Refrigeration Contractor** | Required | 16 CCR § 832.38 [10] |
| Electrical | **C-10 — Electrical Contractor** | Required | 16 CCR § 832.10 [10] |
| Plumbing | **C-36 — Plumbing Contractor** | Required | 16 CCR § 832.36 [10] |
| Structural steel | **C-51 — Structural Steel Contractor** (fabrication and erection of structural steel shapes and plates, with related riveting, welding, rigging, and metal roofing) | Required | 16 CCR § 832.51 [10][11] |

Three classification rules trip up out-of-state and multi-trade contractors:

1. **Class B requires two unrelated trades.** A contractor performing only one trade (even a "general"-sounding scope like framing or drywall) cannot use the B classification — it must hold the specialty C classification for that trade [10][11].
2. **A Class B prime cannot self-perform plumbing, HVAC, or electrical above the $500/$1,000 incidental threshold without holding the corresponding C-36 / C-20 / C-10 classification.** It must either obtain the C classification or subcontract the work to a properly licensed C-classified contractor (B&P Code § 7059) [4][10].
3. **C-10, C-36, C-20, C-38, and C-51 are all stand-alone classifications.** A C-36 plumbing license does not authorize HVAC or refrigeration work; a separate C-20 or C-38 is required [10].

### 1.5 Experience and examination

Per **16 CCR § 825** and the CSLB's *Application for Original Contractor's License* instructions [12], each applicant (or its qualifying individual) must:

1. Provide documented experience of **at least four (4) years**, within the immediately preceding **ten (10) years**, performing work at a **journey level, foreman, supervising employee, contractor, or owner-builder level** in the classification(s) for which the license is sought (B&P Code § 7068; 16 CCR § 825) [12][13]. At least one year must be hands-on practical experience. Apprenticeship credit, technical-school credit, and college-degree credit are available in limited tiers.
2. Pass the **Law and Business Exam**, a multiple-choice test of approximately 115 questions covering the Contractors' State License Law, employment and labor law, safety, insurance, lien law, contracts, and financial management. The exam is administered by **PSI Services LLC** at PSI testing centers [14][15].
3. Pass the **trade exam** for the requested classification, also administered by PSI. Trade exams range from 100 to 115 questions and reference classification-specific code books [14][15].

Both exams require a 73% passing score. An applicant who fails either exam may re-test for a fee; applications expire after **eighteen (18) months** if the applicant has not passed both exams [12][14].

### 1.6 Bond requirements — **Updated by SB 607 (effective Jan. 1, 2023)**

Under **Business and Professions Code § 7071.6** and **16 CCR § 815** [16][17], every active CSLB license must be supported by a **contractor's bond in the amount of $25,000**, executed by an admitted surety, issued on a form approved by the Attorney General, and received at CSLB headquarters within 90 days of the bond's effective date. The bond runs in favor of the State of California for the benefit of consumers, employees (for unpaid wages), and the State (for unpaid taxes).

- **Prior bond amount:** $15,000.
- **Effective date of $25,000 increase:** January 1, 2023, per **Senate Bill 607 (Stats. 2022, Ch. 376)** [17][18]. This was the first bond increase since 2007 and applies equally to (a) the contractor's bond, (b) the bond of a qualifying individual (required when the qualifying individual is not the licensee personally), and (c) the disciplinary bond imposed by the Registrar in certain enforcement proceedings.

**No further bond increase is scheduled for 2024–2026 as of the verification date.** Industry reporting that has occasionally circulated about a further bond increase has been traced to draft versions of unrelated reform bills that did not pass.

### 1.7 Workers' compensation — **MATERIAL 2026 TRANSITION, FURTHER DELAYED BY SB 1455 (2024)**

**Labor Code § 3700** requires every California employer to secure the payment of workers' compensation by (a) being insured by an authorized insurer, or (b) obtaining a certificate of self-insurance from the Department of Industrial Relations [19][20]. **Business and Professions Code § 7125** makes proof of workers' compensation insurance (or a valid exemption) a precondition to issuance, reactivation, or renewal of an active contractor's license, and § 7125.4 makes failure to maintain coverage grounds for **automatic license suspension** [4][20].

**Pre-2023 baseline:** A sole proprietor or non-employer contractor could file an Exemption from Workers' Compensation form (CSLB form 13E-49) and operate without a policy until hiring the first employee.

**SB 216 of 2022 (Stats. 2022, Ch. 978)** restructured this regime in two phases [21][22]:

- **Phase 1 — effective January 1, 2023:** Every licensed contractor in the **C-8 (Concrete), C-20 (HVAC), C-22 (Asbestos Abatement), and D-49 (Tree Service)** classifications, and every active **C-39 (Roofing)** licensee (which was already subject to a separate prior mandate), must carry workers' compensation insurance **regardless of employee status** [21][22].
- **Phase 2 — originally effective January 1, 2026:** All remaining active licensed contractors and applicants for licensure, regardless of classification, were to be required to carry workers' compensation coverage even without employees, with a narrow joint-venture exemption.

**SB 1455 (Stats. 2024, Ch. 999) delayed the Phase 2 universal mandate from January 1, 2026 to January 1, 2028** [3][22]. As of the verification date (May 13, 2026), the **operative rule** is therefore:

- Contractors holding **C-8, C-20, C-22, C-39, or D-49** classifications must carry workers' compensation regardless of employees.
- All other contractors must carry workers' compensation **if they have any employees** (Labor Code § 3700; B&P Code § 7125). Sole proprietors and certain corporate-officer-only entities without employees may file an exemption.
- **Effective January 1, 2028, the universal mandate will sweep in every remaining classification.** ContrPro should treat that date as a hard switch [3][22].

For ContrPro's five trades, this is consequential right now: a contractor holding a **C-20 HVAC** license must carry workers' compensation even without employees (Phase 1). A contractor holding C-10, C-36, C-38, C-51, or B may continue to file an exemption if it has no employees — until January 1, 2028.

License suspension for lapse in workers' compensation coverage is **automatic and self-executing** under B&P Code § 7125.4; the contractor receives no advance notice from CSLB before the suspension takes effect. Work performed during a suspension period is treated as unlicensed work and triggers § 7031 disgorgement liability [4][20].

### 1.8 General liability insurance

California **does not impose a statutory minimum** general liability limit for licensure as a precondition to issuing the license, but **Business and Professions Code § 7125.5** (as added by SB 1230, 2017) requires every licensed contractor to **disclose its general liability insurance** to the CSLB (carrier, policy number, limits) and certify that the licensee will maintain coverage. The Board publishes the licensee's GL information on its public license-lookup page [4][20]. Project-level GL minimums are set by individual owners, prime contractors, public-works specifications, and lenders, not by statute.

### 1.9 Renewal cycle and continuing education

- **Renewal cycle:** Two (2) years (biennial). Renewals are filed with CSLB approximately 60 days before expiration, and an acceptable renewal must be received at CSLB headquarters **before the expiration date** of the license (B&P Code § 7140; 16 CCR § 858) [4][23][24].
- **Active license renewal fee:** $450 (sole owner) / $700 (non-sole owner). Inactive renewal fee is lower. Late renewal incurs a 50% delinquency penalty [25].
- **Continuing education:** California **does not impose a statewide CE mandate** on most CSLB classifications. Asbestos abatement (C-22) carries certification requirements administered by DIR. **There is no general statewide CE mandate for B, C-10, C-20, C-36, C-38, or C-51 licensees as of May 2026** [23][24].

## 2. Mechanics' lien law (Civil Code §§ 8000–8848)

California's modern mechanics' lien statutes are at **Civil Code §§ 8000 through 8848** (Part 6 of Division 4 of the Civil Code), in effect in their current form since the comprehensive 2010–2012 recodification (former Civil Code §§ 3082–3267 were repealed). The statutory scheme distinguishes sharply between **direct contractors** (in privity with the owner — the prime) and **other claimants** (subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, material suppliers, equipment lessors, design professionals). The two have **very different** notice obligations [26][27][28].

### 2.1 Who can claim a lien

**Civil Code §§ 8400 and 8430** grant lien rights to any person who, at the request of the owner or a party in privity with the owner, furnishes labor, services, equipment, or materials for a work of improvement [27][28]. Eligible claimants include direct contractors, subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, material suppliers (to the level of the original contractor, subcontractor, or sub-subcontractor), equipment lessors, registered architects, licensed land surveyors, licensed engineers, and laborers (whose wages are protected separately) [27].

### 2.2 Preliminary 20-day notice — the trap

**Civil Code §§ 8200 through 8216** require a preliminary notice ("Preliminary 20-Day Notice" in legacy terminology) as a **precondition** to a valid mechanics' lien for nearly every claimant [29][30][31].

**Who must serve:** Any claimant **other than (a) a direct contractor and (b) a wage-claiming laborer** must serve a preliminary notice. A direct contractor (the prime) must serve a preliminary notice only on the construction lender, if any (§ 8200(e)) [29][30].

**On whom:** The notice must be served on (1) the owner, (2) the direct contractor, and (3) the construction lender, if any. Public works have analog requirements under § 9300 [29][30].

**When:** The notice must be given **not later than 20 days after the claimant has first furnished work or materials** to the work of improvement (§ 8204) [32][33]. The trap is in § 8204(b): a claimant who fails to serve within 20 days **does not lose all lien rights**, but the claimant is **entitled to a lien only for work or materials furnished within the 20-day window preceding the date of service, plus everything furnished thereafter**. Work performed before that 20-day lookback is irretrievably outside the lien. This is the single most-litigated trap in California construction collections — a sub that starts on the job January 1 and serves notice March 1 has lost January and most of February.

**How:** The notice must be in writing (§ 8102), state the required content (§ 8202 — name and address of claimant, name of person to or for whom labor/materials are furnished, description of labor or materials, estimated total price, description sufficient to identify the property, statement of statutory rights), and served by **(a) personal delivery, (b) registered mail, (c) certified mail, or (d) overnight courier** (§§ 8108, 8110, 8118) [29][34]. **Email and fax are not authorized methods of service** [34].

**Effect of non-service:** Without a timely preliminary notice (subject to the 20-day lookback), the claimant has no lien, no right to a stop payment notice (§ 8520), and no right to make a claim against a payment bond on a private work (§ 8612). Direct contractors are exempt as to the owner but must still serve the construction lender to make a payment-bond claim (§ 8200(e)) [29][30].

### 2.3 Recording deadlines for the claim of lien

After completion or cessation, a claim of lien (mechanics' lien) must be **recorded with the county recorder** in the county where the property is located, within a tight statutory window:

| Claimant | Deadline (no Notice of Completion / Cessation recorded) | Deadline (Notice of Completion / Cessation recorded by owner) | Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Direct contractor** (prime) | **90 days** after completion of the work of improvement | **60 days** after recording of the Notice of Completion or Notice of Cessation | Civil Code § 8412 [35][36] |
| **Other claimant** (sub, supplier, design professional, etc.) | **90 days** after completion of the work of improvement | **30 days** after recording of the Notice of Completion or Notice of Cessation | Civil Code § 8414 [35][37] |

Two facts to internalize: (1) the owner's recording of a Notice of Completion **accelerates** every downstream claimant's deadline; (2) the direct contractor gets a longer 60-day window than the subs' 30-day window after a Notice of Completion. ContrPro should treat receipt of a Notice of Completion or Cessation as an emergency deadline.

### 2.4 Notice of Completion and Notice of Cessation (Civil Code §§ 8180–8190)

**Civil Code § 8180** defines "completion" for private works to occur on the earliest of (a) actual completion of the work; (b) occupation or use by the owner accompanied by cessation of labor; (c) cessation of labor for a continuous period of **60 days**; or (d) recording of a notice of cessation after labor has ceased continuously for at least 30 days [38][39].

**Notice of Completion (§ 8182):** The owner may record a notice of completion **on or within 15 days after** the date of completion. The notice must be signed and verified by the owner, identify the property, identify the direct contractor(s), and state the date of completion [38][39].

**Notice of Cessation (§ 8188):** The owner may record a notice of cessation if there has been a continuous cessation of labor for **at least 30 days** that continues through the recording date [38].

**Owner's mandatory copy-service obligation:** Under § 8190, an owner that records either notice must, **within 10 days** of recording, give a copy to every direct contractor and to every claimant that has given a preliminary notice. Failure to give the required copies **invalidates** the notice as to that claimant — meaning the claimant retains the longer 90-day window [38][39]. ContrPro should always demand confirmation that the owner served the notice on it personally; absence of service is a defense to a "your time is up" argument.

### 2.5 Claim of lien — form and content (Civil Code §§ 8416, 8420)

The recorded claim of lien must be **verified by the claimant** and must include (§ 8416): (a) statement of the claimant's demand after deducting credits and offsets; (b) name of the owner or reputed owner; (c) general statement of the labor, services, equipment, or materials furnished; (d) name of the person by whom the claimant was employed; (e) description sufficient to identify the property; (f) the claimant's address; and (g) a proof of service affidavit confirming service on the owner. A notice of mechanics' lien in statutory form must be served on the owner (§ 8416(a)(7)) — the lien is **not valid** unless served [27][35].

### 2.6 Lien foreclosure — 90 days, hard cap (Civil Code § 8460)

Once recorded, the lien must be enforced by suit within **90 days after the date of recording the claim of lien**, or the lien expires and is unenforceable (§ 8460(a)) [40][41]. The 90-day enforcement window may be extended by recording a written agreement to extend credit between the claimant and owner (§ 8460(b)), but only up to **one year after completion** in any event. Missing the 90-day enforcement deadline is fatal; the lien is "expunged by operation of law" and the owner may petition for an order releasing the property under § 8480 [40][41].

### 2.7 Stop payment notice — separate remedy (Civil Code §§ 8500–8560)

A **stop payment notice** is California's distinctive supplemental remedy that allows a claimant (other than the direct contractor) to compel the owner or construction lender to **withhold funds otherwise payable to the prime contractor** to secure the subordinate claimant's claim [42][43].

- **Private works** — Civil Code §§ 8500 through 8560 [42][43].
- **Public works** — Civil Code §§ 9350 through 9510, with the operative public-works stop-notice section at § 9352 [42].

The stop payment notice is given to the owner (private works), to the public entity (public works), or to the construction lender (in either case if the lender is identified). To be effective as to the construction lender, a private-works stop payment notice must be **bonded** at 125% of the claim amount (§ 8532). The construction lender or owner that receives a properly served stop payment notice **must withhold** the noticed amount from progress payments to the prime; failure to withhold creates direct liability to the noticing claimant up to the amount of the claim (§§ 8536, 8538) [42][43].

The stop payment notice is **independent** of the mechanics' lien — a claimant may pursue both simultaneously. The action to enforce a stop payment notice must be commenced within **90 days after the expiration of the time within which to serve the stop payment notice** (§ 8550) [42]. The stop-notice service deadlines mirror the lien-recording deadlines: 90 days after completion (or 30/60 days after Notice of Completion).

### 2.8 Statutory waiver and release forms — Civil Code §§ 8132, 8134, 8136, 8138

California is one of a small handful of states that requires waivers and releases of mechanics' lien rights to be in **statutorily prescribed form** to be enforceable. Civil Code § 8122 makes any purported waiver of lien rights **void and unenforceable** unless it is in substantial compliance with one of four statutory forms [44][45]:

| Form | Purpose | Code Section |
|---|---|---|
| **Conditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment** | Given **before** a progress payment; becomes effective only when the payment clears the bank | Civil Code § 8132 [44][45] |
| **Unconditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment** | Given **after** a progress payment is received; immediate, unconditional release of lien rights up to the stated amount | Civil Code § 8134 [44][45] |
| **Conditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment** | Given **before** the final payment; effective only when payment clears | Civil Code § 8136 [44][45] |
| **Unconditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment** | Given **after** final payment; full and complete release of all lien, stop-payment, and bond claim rights | Civil Code § 8138 [44][45] |

Practical rule: **never sign an unconditional release unless the check has cleared.** A subcontractor that signs a § 8134 unconditional progress-payment release before the check clears, and then has the check bounce, has waived its lien for the released amount. CSLB consumer-affairs publications warn of this on every project [44].

### 2.9 Public works — payment bond and stop notice

On public works, mechanics' liens against the public property itself are not available; the claimant's remedies are (a) a **claim against the prime contractor's payment bond** (Civil Code § 9550 et seq.; "Little Miller Act" analog), and (b) a public-works stop notice served on the public entity (§ 9350 et seq.) [42][46]. The bond claim deadline is **15 days after recording of a notice of completion** or, absent a notice of completion, **90 days after acceptance or completion** (§ 9558). Action on the bond must be filed within **six (6) months after the period in which a stop payment notice may be given** (§ 9558).

## 3. Prompt payment and retainage

California's prompt-payment regime is **fragmented across at least eight statutes** — owners vs. primes vs. subs, private vs. public, progress vs. retention — but every payment relationship lands somewhere in the matrix below. Two major 2026 changes (SB 61 and SB 440) materially restructure the private-works portion.

### 3.1 Owner-to-prime payment — private works (Civil Code § 8800)

Under **Civil Code § 8800**, the owner shall pay the direct contractor within **30 days** after notice demanding payment pursuant to the contract is given, as to any progress payment not subject to a good-faith dispute [47][48]. If a good-faith dispute exists, the owner may withhold up to **150% of the disputed amount** (§ 8800(b)). Wrongful withholding is penalized at **2% per month** of the wrongfully withheld amount, in lieu of any contract or statutory interest, and the prevailing party in a collection action is entitled to costs and reasonable attorney's fees (§ 8800(c)) [47][48].

### 3.2 Prime-to-subcontractor payment — Bus. & Prof. Code § 7108.5

**Business and Professions Code § 7108.5** is the primary prompt-payment statute governing payments **from a prime contractor to subcontractors** on **both private and public** works:

- **Deadline:** Payment must be made to the subcontractor within **7 days** of receipt of a progress payment by the prime, unless otherwise agreed in writing [49][50].
- **Penalty:** Violation subjects the prime to a **2% of the amount due per month penalty payable to the subcontractor** for every month the payment is wrongfully withheld, plus **attorney's fees and costs** to the prevailing party in any collection action.
- **Disciplinary effect:** Violation is also "cause for disciplinary action" against the prime's CSLB license — a powerful collateral consequence.

The same 7-day window cascades down: a subcontractor that receives payment must pay its sub-subcontractors within 7 days (§ 7108.5(a)) [49][50].

### 3.3 Owner-to-prime payment — public works (Public Contract Code § 10261.5; § 20104.50 et seq.)

For **state public works** (state agencies, including the Department of General Services and Caltrans), **Public Contract Code § 10261.5** requires payment to the direct contractor **within 30 days** after receipt of an undisputed payment request (39 days for state universities) [51][52]. A payment request determined not to be proper must be returned within **7 days** of receipt. Late payments accrue interest at the statutory rate (currently **10% per annum** under Government Code § 685.010), or, where applicable, the higher Prompt Payment Act rate.

For **local public agencies**, **Public Contract Code § 20104.50** imposes parallel 30-day deadlines with 2% monthly interest on wrongfully withheld amounts.

### 3.4 Retention — **MATERIAL 2026 CHANGE for private works (SB 61)**

**Public works retention** has been statutorily capped at **5%** since the 2012 amendment to **Public Contract Code §§ 7201 and 9203** [53][54]:

- **PCC § 7201** caps retention on local-agency public works at **5%** of any progress payment; the cap also applies down the chain to retention withheld by primes from subs.
- **PCC § 9203** caps retention on local-agency public works of $5,000 or more at **5%**, with discretion to release further retention after 50% completion if satisfactory progress is being made.
- **PCC § 7107** governs **release of retention** on public works: the public entity must release retention within **60 days** after completion (90 days for state agencies in certain circumstances under § 7107(g)); the prime must pass through each subcontractor's share **within 7 days** of receiving its retention from the public entity; the prime may withhold only **150% of a genuinely disputed amount**; late payment is penalized at **2% per month** and attorney's fees to the prevailing party [53][54].

**Private works retention (pre-2026 baseline):** Civil Code §§ 8810–8824 governed retention timing on private works, with retention release due **45 days after completion** (§ 8812) and pass-through to subs within **10 days** of receipt (§ 8814). However, prior to January 1, 2026, **the retention percentage itself was unregulated on private works** — owners and primes could and routinely did withhold 10%, 15%, or higher on private projects.

**Private works retention (effective January 1, 2026) — Senate Bill 61, codified as new Civil Code § 8811:**

**SB 61 (Stats. 2025, Ch. 218)**, signed by Governor Newsom on **July 14, 2025** and effective for contracts entered into on or after **January 1, 2026**, creates **Civil Code § 8811** and caps retention on private works of improvement at **5% of any progress payment**, with a parallel cap on aggregate retention at **5% of the contract price** [55][56][57][58]. The cap applies at every tier — owner to prime, prime to sub, sub to sub-sub. The percentage of retention withheld at any tier may not exceed the percentage withheld at the tier immediately above; i.e., a prime that has 4% withheld by the owner cannot withhold 5% from its subs.

**Exceptions to the 5% cap (§ 8811):**
1. **Residential projects of 4 stories or less that are not mixed-use.** Single-family residential, small multifamily up to 4 stories, and similar wholly-residential projects remain unregulated as to retention percentage.
2. **Bond-default exception.** If the prime or subcontractor notified bidders, at or before bidding, that performance and payment bonds were required, and the successful subcontractor fails to furnish such bonds, the 5% cap does not apply to that subcontract.

**Effect on existing contracts:** SB 61 applies **only to contracts entered into on or after January 1, 2026**. Pre-existing contracts continue to be governed by the contract terms. ContrPro customers with rolling, multi-phase, or master-services agreements must review whether new task orders or work authorizations under a pre-2026 master constitute new "contracts" for SB 61 purposes — the safe answer is to assume yes for any new MSA task order issued in 2026 or later.

**Retention release deadlines (Civil Code §§ 8812, 8814, 8818) — unchanged by SB 61:**

- Owner must release retention to prime within **45 days after completion** (§ 8812) [59].
- Prime must release each sub's retention share within **10 days** of receipt from the owner (§ 8814) [59][60].
- Subs must pay sub-subs within **10 days** of receipt.
- Wrongful withholding penalty: **2% per month** in lieu of interest, plus attorney's fees to the prevailing party (§ 8818) [59][60].
- A direct contractor may withhold a subcontractor's retention pass-through **only if a good-faith dispute exists that relates directly to the subcontractor's performance**; the California Supreme Court in *United Riggers & Erectors, Inc. v. Coast Iron & Steel Co.* (2018) 4 Cal.5th 1082 narrowed the "good faith dispute" defense to disputes specifically connected to the retained work [60].

### 3.5 Change-order disputes — **NEW EFFECTIVE JANUARY 1, 2026 (SB 440)**

**Senate Bill 440 (Stats. 2025, Ch. 626) — the Private Works Change Order Fair Payment Act** — signed October 10, 2025, effective for contracts entered on or after **January 1, 2026** (and sunsets January 1, 2030 unless extended), imposes a mandatory dispute-resolution process for **change-order and time-extension claims on private works of improvement** [61][62][63]. The statutory framework, codified at new Civil Code provisions paralleling the public-works claim procedure, requires:

1. **Contractor submits a written claim** by registered or certified mail describing the requested change, time extension, or delay damages.
2. **Owner has 30 days** to provide a written statement identifying what portion of the claim is undisputed and disputed, and to meet and confer on the disputed portion.
3. **Within 10 business days** after the meet-and-confer, the owner must issue a second written statement reaffirming the undisputed and disputed amounts.
4. **Undisputed amounts must be paid within 60 days** of the owner's written statement.
5. **Failure to respond within the 30-day window is deemed a denial** that triggers the claim's right to escalate (and to suspend work — see below).
6. **Non-waivable stop-work right.** Any contract provision that purports to waive the contractor's right to suspend performance for nonpayment of an undisputed change-order amount is **void as against public policy**. This overrides the typical "contractor shall continue performance pending resolution" clause in standard form contracts.

SB 440's claim procedure is **modeled on Public Contract Code § 9204** (the public-works counterpart) but is somewhat more aggressive in its statutory penalties for owner non-response. ContrPro should treat any private-works owner's failure to respond to a written change-order claim within 30 days as an immediate trigger to escalate.

### 3.6 Right to suspend work for nonpayment

Outside SB 440's specific change-order context, **Civil Code § 8830** authorizes a direct contractor on a private work to give a 10-day written notice of nonpayment and, if not cured, suspend performance. **Business and Professions Code § 7108.5** does not authorize work suspension by a sub against a prime — the sub's remedy is the prompt-payment penalty, lien, stop notice, and (if applicable) bond claim.

## 4. Tax structure for contractors

### 4.1 State and local sales/use tax rates

- **Statewide base rate:** **7.25%** (Revenue and Taxation Code § 6051 — state-level component is 6.00%, plus a state-mandated 1.25% local component) [64][65].
- **District taxes:** Local jurisdictions (counties, cities, special districts) may impose additional **district taxes** in 0.10% to 1.00% increments, in some cases stacking to roughly 3.5% [64][65][66]. The **combined maximum rate as of May 2026 is 10.75%**, in effect in **Union City** (Alameda County). Cities with rates at or near the 10%+ threshold include parts of San Leandro, Hayward, Albany, Newark, and several cities in Los Angeles County.
- **Administered by:** **California Department of Tax and Fee Administration (CDTFA)** (formerly the Board of Equalization for sales/use tax functions through 2017) [65].

### 4.2 Contractor sales-tax treatment — the materials/fixtures bifurcation

California's contractor sales-tax rule is **idiosyncratic and trap-laden**. Unlike Tennessee's "contractor is the consumer" rule or the typical Southern-state rule, California distinguishes between **materials** and **fixtures** within the same construction contract:

- **Materials** (items that "lose their individual identity to become an inseparable part" of the real property — bricks, lumber, paint, concrete, drywall, roofing felt): The **contractor is the consumer** and pays sales/use tax on the cost of the materials at the time of purchase. The contractor does **not** collect sales tax from the customer. **Regulation 1521(b)(2)(A)** [67][68].
- **Fixtures** (accessory items that "do not lose their identity when installed" — HVAC units, plumbing fixtures, prefabricated cabinets, electrical panels, signs, built-in appliances): The **contractor is the retailer** and **collects sales tax from the customer** on the selling price of the fixture, separately stated from labor. If the fixture is contractor-manufactured, the taxable base includes materials, labor, overhead, and a reasonable profit (typically 5%). **Regulation 1521(b)(2)(B)** [67][68].
- **Machinery and equipment** (items that are not affixed or that retain a separate identity, such as freestanding production equipment): Treated as **retail sales**. The contractor collects sales tax from the customer on the retail selling price (excluding installation labor, which is not taxable). **Regulation 1521(b)(2)(C)** [67][68].

The line between **materials**, **fixtures**, and **machinery & equipment** is set in the long appendices to **Regulation 1521** and in **CDTFA Publication 9** (*Construction and Building Contractors*) [67][68]. ContrPro should expect ongoing classification disputes — for example, whether a particular HVAC chiller is a "fixture" (contractor charges tax to customer) or "machinery" (also taxable, but at retail price not contractor-cost) is a recurring CDTFA audit issue.

### 4.3 Contract-type effects

There are three principal contract types under California tax law, each with different sales-tax mechanics [67][68]:

1. **Lump-sum contract:** The customer pays a single agreed amount. The contractor must internally allocate the sales tax on materials (cost basis) and fixtures (retail basis) and remit. The customer is not separately invoiced for sales tax.
2. **Time-and-materials contract:** Sales tax on materials is included in the materials line item at cost; sales tax on fixtures is separately stated and charged on the fixture's retail price.
3. **Cost-plus-fee contract:** Treated analogously to time-and-materials. The customer reimburses the contractor's actual materials cost (including sales tax paid by the contractor) and is separately charged sales tax on fixtures.

### 4.4 Use tax on out-of-state materials

Materials purchased out-of-state and brought into California for incorporation into California real property are subject to **California use tax** at the combined state + local + district rate of the project location (Rev. & Tax. Code § 6201) [64][65]. Contractors that bring in materials from Nevada, Arizona, or Oregon (where the contractor may have paid lower or no sales tax) **owe California use tax** on the unpaid differential, and must file a CDTFA consumer use tax return. This is a routine CDTFA audit target for multi-state contractors.

### 4.5 Income / franchise / gross-receipts taxes

California income and franchise taxation, administered by the **Franchise Tax Board (FTB)** [69]:

- **C-corporations:** **8.84%** corporate income tax on California taxable income, with an **$800 minimum franchise tax** in every taxable year (Rev. & Tax. Code §§ 23151, 23153) [69][70].
- **S-corporations:** **1.5% of net income or $800, whichever is greater** (Rev. & Tax. Code § 23802).
- **LLCs taxed as partnerships:** **$800 annual minimum franchise tax** plus an **LLC fee tiered by total California-source income**:
  - $250,000–$499,999: **$900**
  - $500,000–$999,999: **$2,500**
  - $1,000,000–$4,999,999: **$6,000**
  - $5,000,000 or more: **$11,790**
  (Rev. & Tax. Code § 17942) [69][71].
- **LLCs taxed as corporations:** Subject to C-corporation rules above.
- **Sole proprietorships and general partnerships:** No entity-level franchise tax; partners report on personal returns at California's progressive personal income tax rates (top rate 13.3% including the 1% mental health surcharge above $1 million).
- **Personal income tax rates** (applicable to sole proprietors and pass-through partners): graduated from 1.0% to 12.3%, with the additional 1% mental health surcharge on income above $1 million (Cal. Const. Art. XIIIB; Rev. & Tax. Code § 17041) [69].

There is **no general California state-level gross-receipts tax on contractors**. Some cities (most notably Los Angeles and San Francisco) impose **local gross-receipts taxes** with separate registration and remittance — these are city-imposed under municipal codes, not state law.

## 5. Recent changes (2024–2026)

### 5.1 Assembly Bill 2622 (Stats. 2024, Ch. 248) — handyperson threshold raised to $1,000, effective January 1, 2025

The first amendment to the **Business and Professions Code § 7048** unlicensed-work threshold since 1939. Raised the dollar limit from $500 to **$1,000** for work that does not require a permit, is performed by an individual without employees, and is not part of a larger operation [6][7]. Practical effect for ContrPro: small repair work that was previously a licensure-trip-wire ($501–$999) is now permitted unlicensed if the other § 7048 conditions are met. The "casual, minor, or inconsequential" qualitative test still applies — splitting large jobs into sub-$1,000 chunks is still a violation.

### 5.2 Senate Bill 1455 (Stats. 2024, Ch. 999) — CSLB sunset extension and SB 216 Phase 2 delay

SB 1455 extended the CSLB sunset to January 1, 2029 and **delayed the SB 216 Phase 2 universal workers' compensation mandate from January 1, 2026 to January 1, 2028** [3][22]. As of May 13, 2026, the operative rule is: C-8, C-20, C-22, C-39, D-49 contractors must carry WC regardless of employees; all others must carry WC only if they have employees (or file an exemption). Universal mandate takes effect January 1, 2028.

### 5.3 Senate Bill 61 (Stats. 2025, Ch. 218) — 5% retention cap for private works, effective January 1, 2026

Created **Civil Code § 8811**. Capped retention on private works of improvement at **5% of each progress payment** and **5% of the aggregate contract price**, at every tier from owner to sub-sub. Applies to contracts entered into on or after **January 1, 2026**. Exceptions for residential ≤ 4 stories non-mixed-use and for bond-default situations [55][56][57][58]. This is the **single most consequential payment-law change** in California construction in over a decade — many private owners had historically withheld 10–15% retention on private projects.

### 5.4 Senate Bill 440 (Stats. 2025, Ch. 626) — Private Works Change Order Fair Payment Act, effective January 1, 2026

Established the mandatory 30-day / 10-business-day / 60-day change-order claim resolution procedure for **private works of improvement**, modeled on the public-works claim procedure under Public Contract Code § 9204. Includes a non-waivable right to suspend work for nonpayment of undisputed change-order amounts. Applies to contracts entered into on or after **January 1, 2026** and sunsets January 1, 2030 unless extended [61][62][63].

### 5.5 Senate Bill 607 (Stats. 2022, Ch. 376) — bond increase to $25,000, effective January 1, 2023

Although now three years old, the bond increase from $15,000 to $25,000 remains the operative bond amount and continues to surface in renewal cycles [17][18].

### 5.6 Senate Bill 216 (Stats. 2022, Ch. 978) — workers' comp expansion, Phase 1 effective January 1, 2023; Phase 2 delayed

See § 5.2 above. Phase 1 brought C-8, C-20, C-22, D-49 (and reaffirmed C-39) into mandatory WC regardless of employees. Phase 2 universal mandate now January 1, 2028 [21][22].

### 5.7 Discrepancy resolutions

Two persistent secondary-source errors worth flagging:

- Some third-party "California contractor licensing guides" continue to state the **$500 threshold for unlicensed work**. This was correct **through December 31, 2024**. As of January 1, 2025, the threshold is **$1,000** under AB 2622 [6][7].
- Some third-party guides report the SB 216 Phase 2 universal workers' comp mandate as effective **January 1, 2026**. SB 1455 (signed 2024) **delayed Phase 2 to January 1, 2028**. ContrPro should verify any "all licensed contractors must have WC by 2026" statement against the current Labor Code and SB 1455 [3][22].
- The 5% retention cap (SB 61, Civ. Code § 8811) is sometimes paraphrased as applying to "all construction projects." It applies only to **private works**; public works have a separate 5% cap under Public Contract Code §§ 7201 / 9203 that has been in effect since 2012 [53][54][55][56].

### 5.8 Bills proposed but not enacted (verification cross-reference)

A review of the 2025–2026 Regular Session bill tracker on leginfo.legislature.ca.gov [3] identifies several bills that were introduced but did not pass and that should not be relied upon:

- An earlier version of SB 61 had attempted to cap retention at 3%; the enacted version raised the cap to 5% to match public-works rules.
- Various "single-license universal contractor" reform bills introduced in 2024–2025 did not pass; CSLB classifications remain as listed in 16 CCR § 832.
- Bills proposing a 5% prevailing-wage threshold change did not pass; the current $1,000 (new construction) / $15,000 (alteration) thresholds under Labor Code § 1771 remain operative [72].

## 6. Practical compliance checklist for a California contractor

**Before the bid is signed:**
- Confirm the project value (labor + materials). If **≥ $1,000** and not within the handyperson exemption, the contractor must hold a current CSLB license **before bidding**. Unlicensed contracting is a misdemeanor under B&P Code § 7028 and triggers § 7031(b) disgorgement.
- Confirm the appropriate classification(s) on the license — Class B for multi-trade general building (requires ≥ 2 unrelated trades); the relevant specialty C classification(s) for each self-performed trade above the incidental threshold (C-10 electrical, C-20 HVAC, C-36 plumbing, C-38 refrigeration, C-51 structural steel, etc.).
- Confirm workers' compensation status: if holding C-8, C-20, C-22, C-39, or D-49, **must** carry WC regardless of employees. Other classifications may file an exemption if no employees — but the universal mandate begins **January 1, 2028**.
- Confirm the $25,000 contractor bond is in place and the qualifying individual bond, if applicable, is in place.

**Within 20 days of first furnishing labor or materials (every sub, every supplier, every design professional):**
- **Serve the Preliminary 20-Day Notice** (Civ. Code §§ 8200–8216) on (a) the owner, (b) the direct contractor, and (c) the construction lender (if any) by personal delivery, registered mail, certified mail, or overnight courier. **Email and fax do not satisfy the statute.**
- A late notice still preserves lien rights, but **only for work performed within the 20-day lookback preceding service, plus everything thereafter**. Work performed before that lookback is lost.

**During the work — payment cycle:**
- **Private works:** Owner pays prime within **30 days** of progress payment demand (Civ. Code § 8800); prime pays subs within **7 days** of receipt (B&P § 7108.5).
- **Public works:** Owner pays prime within **30 days** (PCC § 10261.5 / § 20104.50); prime pays subs within **7 days** (B&P § 7108.5).
- **Penalties:** **2% per month** on wrongfully withheld amounts (Civ. Code § 8800(c); B&P § 7108.5(b); PCC § 7107(f); PCC § 10261.5).
- **Good-faith dispute exception:** Owner/prime may withhold up to **150% of the disputed amount**, no more.

**For contracts entered on or after January 1, 2026 (SB 61):**
- **Retention cannot exceed 5%** of any progress payment or 5% of the aggregate contract price on **private works**, at every tier. Exception: residential ≤ 4 stories, non-mixed-use.
- Public-works retention remains capped at **5%** under PCC §§ 7201 / 9203 / 7107 (unchanged since 2012).

**For contracts entered on or after January 1, 2026 (SB 440):**
- Submit change-order claims by **registered or certified mail** with required content; owner has **30 days** to respond identifying disputed and undisputed portions, then **10 business days** after meet-and-confer to confirm.
- **Undisputed amounts must be paid within 60 days** of the owner's written statement.
- **Right to suspend work for nonpayment of undisputed change-order amounts is non-waivable** notwithstanding contract language to the contrary.

**Lien preservation — within 90 days of completion (or 60 / 30 days after Notice of Completion or Cessation):**
- **Direct contractor:** Record sworn claim of mechanics' lien within **90 days after completion**, or **60 days after recording of Notice of Completion / Cessation** if earlier (Civ. Code § 8412).
- **Sub / supplier / design professional:** Record within **90 days after completion**, or **30 days after Notice of Completion / Cessation** if earlier (Civ. Code § 8414).
- **Verify** that the owner served the contractor with a copy of the Notice of Completion within 10 days of recording (Civ. Code § 8190); failure to serve **invalidates** the notice as to that contractor and preserves the 90-day window.
- Serve the recorded claim of lien on the owner (Civ. Code § 8416(a)(7)) — the lien is **not valid** unless served.

**Lien enforcement — within 90 days of recording (HARD CAP):**
- File suit to foreclose the lien within **90 days after recording the claim of lien** (Civ. Code § 8460). Missing this deadline **extinguishes the lien by operation of law**.
- Extension by written credit agreement is permitted up to **one year after completion** (Civ. Code § 8460(b)), but must be **recorded** within 90 days of the lien recording for it to bind subsequent purchasers and encumbrancers.

**Stop payment notice (parallel remedy — Civ. Code §§ 8500–8560 private / §§ 9350–9510 public):**
- Service deadlines mirror lien deadlines: within **90 days** of completion (or 30/60 days after Notice of Completion).
- For service on the construction lender on private works, the notice **must be bonded at 125%** of the claim amount (Civ. Code § 8532).
- Action to enforce must be commenced within **90 days after the stop-notice service deadline** (Civ. Code § 8550).

**Waivers and releases:**
- Use only the four statutory forms (Civ. Code §§ 8132, 8134, 8136, 8138). Non-statutory waivers are **void and unenforceable** (Civ. Code § 8122).
- **Never sign an unconditional release until the check has cleared.**

**Annual / biennial compliance:**
- File and pay **sales/use tax** on materials throughout the year (CDTFA monthly or quarterly returns depending on volume); collect and remit sales tax on fixture sales separately stated to customers (Reg. 1521).
- File and pay **franchise tax** ($800 minimum) and any LLC fee (Rev. & Tax. Code §§ 17942, 23151).
- File **California corporate or pass-through income tax** returns (FTB Form 100, 100S, 568, etc.).
- Pay **federal employment taxes** (FICA, FUTA), state SDI, and EDD UI taxes.
- **Renew the contractor license** every 2 years before expiration; renew the $25,000 bond annually with the surety; renew workers' compensation policy annually.
- If working public works: maintain **DIR public works contractor registration** (Labor Code § 1725.5) and file **electronic certified payroll records** monthly via the DIR portal for projects subject to prevailing wage (Labor Code §§ 1771, 1776) [72].
- Maintain general liability insurance as disclosed under B&P § 7125.5.
